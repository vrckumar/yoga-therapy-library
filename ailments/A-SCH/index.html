Schizophrenia

1. Definition & Classification

Schizophrenia is a severe, chronic psychiatric disorder characterised by disruptions in thought, perception, emotion, language, sense of self, and behaviour. It is classified within the psychotic spectrum disorders and typically emerges in late adolescence or early adulthood (peak onset: males 15–25 years; females 25–35 years).

Positive symptoms (presence of abnormal experiences):

  • Hallucinations — typically auditory (hearing voices), though visual, tactile, and olfactory hallucinations occur
  • Delusions — fixed false beliefs (persecution, grandiosity, reference, control)
  • Disorganised thinking and speech (loose associations, tangentiality, word salad)
  • Grossly disorganised or catatonic behaviour

Negative symptoms (absence of normal function):

  • Blunted or flat affect
  • Alogia (poverty of speech)
  • Avolition (lack of motivation and goal-directed behaviour)
  • Anhedonia (inability to experience pleasure)
  • Social withdrawal

Cognitive symptoms: Impaired working memory, attention, processing speed, and executive function. These often persist despite antipsychotic treatment and are major determinants of functional outcome.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Dopaminergic Dysregulation

The dopamine hypothesis remains the most influential neurobiological framework:

  • Mesolimbic pathway hyperactivity (VTA → nucleus accumbens → limbic structures) — produces positive symptoms through excessive dopaminergic stimulation of limbic circuits
  • Mesocortical pathway hypoactivity (VTA → prefrontal cortex) — produces negative and cognitive symptoms through inadequate dopaminergic support of PFC function

This explains why D2 receptor antagonists (antipsychotics) reduce positive symptoms (blocking mesolimbic hyperactivity) but often worsen negative symptoms (further reducing already-deficient mesocortical function).

Structural Brain Changes

Neuroimaging consistently shows: enlarged lateral ventricles, reduced grey matter volume in the hippocampus, prefrontal cortex, and superior temporal gyrus, and reduced thalamic volume. These changes likely reflect neurodevelopmental disruption (not purely degenerative), involving aberrant synaptic pruning during adolescence.

Psychosis: The Perceptual Mechanism

Hallucinations arise from dysregulated internal signal generation — the brain misattributes internally generated neural activity (inner speech, imagery) as externally sourced perceptions, due to failure of predictive error signalling (mismatch between top-down predictions and bottom-up sensory signals).

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes

  • Genetic: Heritability approximately 80%; first-degree relatives have 10× increased risk; concordance in identical twins 40–50% (indicating non-genetic factors)
  • Neurodevelopmental: Prenatal exposure to infection, malnutrition, or obstetric complications disrupts neuronal migration and synaptic development
  • Environmental: Urban upbringing, migration, social adversity, cannabis use (particularly high-THC strains) in adolescence
  • Epigenetic: Stress-induced gene expression changes interact with genetic vulnerability

Prodromal Phase

The prodromal phase (months to years before psychotic break) includes: social withdrawal, deteriorating academic performance, odd beliefs, perceptual disturbances, and altered affect. Early intervention during this phase significantly improves prognosis.

4. Conventional Treatment

  • First-generation antipsychotics (FGAs): Haloperidol, chlorpromazine — strong D2 blockade; effective for positive symptoms; significant extrapyramidal side effects (EPS), tardive dyskinesia
  • Second-generation antipsychotics (SGAs): Risperidone, olanzapine, clozapine, quetiapine — combined D2/5-HT2A blockade; improved tolerability; metabolic side effects (weight gain, diabetes risk)
  • Clozapine: Most effective antipsychotic for treatment-resistant schizophrenia; requires regular blood monitoring (agranulocytosis risk)
  • Psychosocial: Cognitive behavioural therapy for psychosis (CBTp), family therapy, social skills training, supported employment
  • Rehabilitation: Supported housing, community mental health teams, crisis intervention services

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for schizophrenia is supportive and adjunctive — it does not replace antipsychotic medication. It works to reduce secondary distress, improve neurocognitive function, address negative symptoms, and support overall quality of life. Strict clinical supervision is mandatory.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Antipsychotic medications frequently cause metabolic side effects (weight gain, sedation, metabolic syndrome). Gentle, grounding physical practices address these without creating overstimulation:

  • Loosening exercises (Sithilikarana Vyayama) — joint mobilisation in a seated or slow standing sequence, with predictable movements that reduce disorientation
  • Ardhakati Chakrasana (lateral standing bend) — simple, grounding, bilateral; promotes body awareness without complexity
  • Slow Surya Namaskara — where tolerated; provides rhythmic movement with built-in breath coordination that grounds scattered attention
  • Simple supine asanas — Pavanamuktasana series for abdominal health (often disrupted by antipsychotic-induced constipation)

Avoid inversions, vigorous sequences, and postures that alter body boundaries in disorienting ways during active or subacute psychosis.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Breathing practices must be mild, stabilising, and predictable:

  • Diaphragmatic breathing awareness — simply attending to natural breath; provides a sensory anchor in the present moment without inducing hyperarousal
  • Nadi Suddhi (mild, without retention) — gentle autonomic balancing; reduces the anxiety and hyperarousal often associated with psychotic episodes
  • Bhramari (humming bee breath) — particularly valuable: the internal sound resonance provides a stable sensory focus that competes with hallucinatory experiences; vagal activation reduces arousal

Strictly avoid: Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Kumbhaka (breath retention), or any hyperventilatory practice — these increase sympathetic arousal and can destabilise mental state in psychosis.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) — the therapist's guided voice provides external grounding; progressive body relaxation reduces the muscular tension and hypervigilance associated with persecutory ideation
  • OM Meditation — the sustained, simple focus of OM chanting provides a structured mental object that crowds out intrusive thought; the vibration has grounding properties; practised at low volume in a group context with appropriate supervision
  • Group devotional practices — singing, chanting in a supervised group provides social connection (addressing negative symptom isolation) and a shared positive emotional experience

Avoid open-ended, unstructured meditation during active psychosis — the absence of structure can amplify internal noise.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

Psychoeducation appropriate to the patient's cognitive capacity:

  • Simple, concrete explanations of the relationship between stress, sleep, and symptom exacerbation
  • Identification of personal warning signs for relapse
  • Medication compliance support through understanding the mechanism

Avoid complex philosophical discourses during active illness; Vijnanamaya engagement is most appropriate in stable maintenance phases.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • Karma Yoga — structured, purposeful daily activity (garden work, art, community service) provides the sense of contribution and meaning that counters the anhedonia and purposelessness of negative symptom schizophrenia
  • Devotional music — passive listening or simple participation in group bhajan or kirtan provides positive affect without cognitive demand

6. Recommended Practices

Practice Duration Purpose
Loosening exercises (seated/slow) 5–8 min Grounding, metabolic side effect management
Ardhakati Chakrasana 1 min each side Simple bilateral grounding
DRT 10–15 min Core relaxation, reducing hypervigilance
Nadi Suddhi (no retention) 3–5 min Gentle ANS stabilisation
Bhramari (5–9 rounds) 3–5 min Sound grounding, vagal activation
OM Meditation (supervised) 5–10 min Structured mental focus
Group devotional music 10–15 min Social connection, positive affect

Session notes: All sessions conducted under supervision of mental health professionals who know the patient. Maximum session length 30–40 minutes in acute or subacute phases. Increase length and complexity only as stability allows.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Mandatory supervision: Yoga therapist must work within the psychiatric multidisciplinary team
  • No practice during acute psychosis — wait until acute symptoms stabilised on medication before introducing yoga
  • Avoid Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Bhastrika-type breathing, breath retention in all phases
  • Avoid complex visualisation or imaginative practices — can be incorporated into delusional material
  • Monitor for medication interactions: Clozapine causes sedation (fall risk); antipsychotics can cause orthostatic hypotension (change positions slowly)
  • Paranoid patients: Establish trust before introducing eye-closed practices; allow eyes-open or eyes-downcast as alternatives
  • Avoid strong physical exertion in patients on high-dose antipsychotics with cardiovascular effects
