Epilepsy

Epilepsy

1. Definition & Classification

Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterised by a predisposition to generate recurrent, unprovoked epileptic seizures — resulting from abnormal, excessive, or hypersynchronous neuronal activity in the brain. A single seizure is not epilepsy; the diagnosis requires two or more unprovoked seizures, or one seizure with a high risk of recurrence.

Seizure classification (ILAE 2017):

Focal onset (arises from a network in one hemisphere):

  • Focal aware (previously: simple partial) — consciousness preserved
  • Focal impaired awareness (previously: complex partial) — consciousness impaired; automatisms common
  • Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic — spreads to both hemispheres

Generalised onset (involves both hemispheres immediately):

  • Tonic-clonic (grand mal) — LOC, tonic stiffening then clonic jerking
  • Absence (petit mal) — brief (5–30 sec) interruption of consciousness; staring; child typically resumes activity
  • Myoclonic — brief, sudden muscle jerks; often in morning; may progress to tonic-clonic
  • Atonic (drop attacks) — sudden loss of muscle tone; fall risk

Unknown onset: When onset cannot be determined.

Epilepsy syndromes: Childhood absence epilepsy, juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (JME), temporal lobe epilepsy (most common focal epilepsy; often associated with hippocampal sclerosis), frontal lobe epilepsy, Lennox-Gastaut syndrome.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Neurophysiology of Seizures

A seizure occurs when normal excitation-inhibition balance in neural networks is disrupted in favour of excessive, synchronous excitation:

Ictal (seizure) mechanism:

  • Excessive glutamate release from presynaptic neurons → AMPA and NMDA receptor overactivation → calcium influx → sustained depolarisation of neuronal populations
  • Failure of GABAergic inhibition — either reduced GABA release, impaired GABA-A receptor function, or loss of inhibitory interneurons
  • Paroxysmal depolarisation shifts (PDSs) — sustained plateau depolarisations generating bursts of action potentials
  • Hypersynchronisation — populations of neurons that normally fire asynchronously lock into phase → "epileptic discharge" visible on EEG as spike-wave complexes

Postictal state: After a generalised tonic-clonic seizure, deep neuronal inhibition produces the postictal period — confusion, fatigue, sometimes temporary weakness (Todd's paralysis), typically lasting minutes to an hour.

The Inhibition-Excitation Balance

The concept of an excitation-inhibition (E-I) set-point is fundamental to understanding both epilepsy and yoga therapy's role:

  • GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) is the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter; benzodiazepines and barbiturates enhance GABAergic inhibition (basis of seizure management)
  • Glutamate is the primary excitatory neurotransmitter
  • Practices that reduce neuronal excitability and promote GABAergic tone (slow diaphragmatic breathing, meditation, yoga Nidra) are intrinsically anti-epileptic in tendency
  • Practices that increase neuronal excitability (hyperventilation, sustained breath retention) are pro-epileptic and absolutely contraindicated

Why Kapalabhati and Bhastrika are contraindicated in epilepsy: Both practices involve hyperventilation components. Hyperventilation produces hypocapnia (reduced CO₂) → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced cerebral perfusion → neuronal hyperexcitability. The "hyperventilation activation" test is actually used clinically to provoke absence seizures during EEG recording. This is the precise physiological reason these pranayamas are contraindicated.

Common Epileptic Substrates

  • Hippocampal sclerosis — most common substrate for temporal lobe epilepsy; associated with early childhood febrile seizures; loss of hippocampal inhibitory interneurons (CA1, CA3 loss)
  • Cortical malformations — focal cortical dysplasia; disrupted neuronal migration
  • Genetic channelopathies — mutations in sodium (SCN1A — Dravet syndrome), potassium, or calcium channel genes
  • Acquired: Post-traumatic, post-infectious, post-stroke, tumour-related

3. Causes & Symptoms

Seizure Triggers

Trigger Mechanism
Sleep deprivation Shifts E-I balance toward excitation
Alcohol withdrawal GABA receptor downregulation rebounds
Missed medication Loss of pharmacological seizure threshold
Psychological stress HPA activation; cortisol reduces GABA tone
Flickering lights (photosensitivity) Occipital cortex hyperexcitability
Fever Increased metabolic demand; Na+ channel changes
Hyperventilation Hypocapnia → cerebrovascular changes → excitability
Menstrual cycle (catamenial epilepsy) Oestrogen (pro-convulsant) rises; progesterone (anti-convulsant, GABAergic) falls

Social Impact

Epilepsy's seizure unpredictability creates unique psychosocial consequences: driving restrictions, swimming safety concerns, occupation limitations, stigma, anxiety about public seizures, and depression (30% prevalence). The psychosocial burden often exceeds the neurological burden in well-controlled epilepsy.

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Anti-seizure medications (ASMs): Over 20 available agents
    • Sodium channel blockers: Carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin
    • GABA-enhancers: Valproate (broad spectrum), benzodiazepines, gabapentin
    • Multiple mechanisms: Levetiracetam (most widely used first-line)
    • ~70% achieve complete seizure control with medication
  • Surgery: Temporal lobectomy for drug-resistant temporal lobe epilepsy (hippocampal sclerosis); very high success rate (60–70% seizure-free)
  • VNS (vagus nerve stimulation): Implanted device; reduces seizure frequency by 30–50%
  • Ketogenic diet: High-fat, low-carbohydrate diet; anti-seizure mechanism involves GABA production from ketone bodies and reduced neuronal glucose dependence
  • Lifestyle: Sleep regularity, alcohol avoidance, stress management, medication compliance — all crucial

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for epilepsy is one of the most clearly protocol-governed applications in the entire yoga therapy field — certain practices are directly beneficial and others directly harmful. Clear communication with the patient's neurologist is essential.

Core principle: Reduce neuronal excitability; enhance GABAergic inhibition; reduce seizure triggers (stress, sleep deprivation, emotional dysregulation).

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

  • Gentle loosening exercises (Sakti Vikasaka) — safe, non-provocative joint mobilisation; provides the physical activity benefits without any stimulating effect; improves physical wellbeing and confidence
  • Slow asanas — Tadasana, Ardhakati Chakrasana, Trikonasana; slow, deliberate movement enhances proprioceptive awareness and promotes cortical synchronisation at restful frequencies
  • Sethubandhasana (bridge pose) — mild inversion; gentle stimulation of the autonomic nervous system (vagal); calming in effect; one of the safest and most beneficial asanas for epilepsy
  • Savasana — fundamental; provides grounded, still, relaxed body baseline

Avoid all inversions: Sarvangasana, Halasana, headstand — fall injury during a seizure in inverted position is dangerous; also, these postures alter cerebral blood flow in ways that are unpredictable in epilepsy.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Safe and beneficial:

  • Bhramari (primary pranayama) — the most important pranayama for epilepsy; documented anti-epileptic effect through bilateral vagal stimulation; the humming tone produces calming brainwave entrainment (alpha wave promotion); GABA-enhancing effect of vagal activation; no contraindication
  • Nadi Suddhi (without Kumbhaka — no breath retention) — gentle, calming alternating nostril breathing; no hyperventilation component; normalises ANS; promotes balanced hemispheric activity; specifically beneficial for stress-provoked seizures
  • Diaphragmatic breathing (slow, gentle) — reduces respiratory rate and promotes CO₂ retention (mildly pro-GABAergic); safe for all epilepsy

ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATIONS:

  • Kapalabhati — produces hyperventilation → hypocapnia → seizure provocation. Absolutely contraindicated.
  • Bhastrika — same mechanism. Absolutely contraindicated.
  • Kumbhaka (any breath retention) — holds the breath; transiently alters CO₂/O₂ balance; increases neuronal excitability. Absolutely contraindicated.
  • Any form of hyperventilation — contraindicated.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

  • DRT (primary) — reduces the cortisol-mediated neuronal hyperexcitability that links stress to seizure provocation; regular DRT reduces seizure frequency in stress-sensitive epilepsy
  • Yoga Nidra — deep rest; promotes alpha and theta brainwave states (anti-epileptic frequency bands); improves sleep quality (sleep deprivation is a major seizure trigger); some studies show direct reduction in seizure frequency with regular Yoga Nidra practice
  • Cyclic Meditation — regular practice normalises cortisol; reduces the emotional dysregulation that triggers seizures in emotionally sensitive epilepsy variants

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Seizure trigger identification and management — understanding personal seizure triggers (stress, sleep deprivation, missed medication, specific activities) and implementing avoidance strategies
  • Medication compliance education — missing even a single dose significantly raises seizure risk; yoga therapy provides an integrated lifestyle framework that supports consistent medication-taking
  • Safety protocols — discussing epilepsy-safe living (swimming, driving, heights, cooking, bathing) pragmatically; yoga practice itself must follow safety protocols (see Contraindications)
  • Stigma and social functioning — many epilepsy patients restrict activity excessively out of fear; Vijnanamaya work supports informed, appropriate engagement with life activities

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • Group practice — the social inclusion of epilepsy patients in a yoga group (with appropriate safety awareness) counters the social withdrawal that seizure fear often causes
  • Karma Yoga — meaningful purposeful action within the constraints of epilepsy; finding contribution and engagement
  • The yogic perspective on seizures as physiological events, not moral failings or supernatural occurrences — particularly important in cultures where epilepsy carries strong stigma

Safety protocol for all sessions:

  • Seizure first-aid training for yoga therapist (mandatory)
  • Always practice with a second person present
  • No practice within 24 hours of a seizure
  • Non-slip mat; padded environment; remove sharp objects from vicinity
  • Inform participant's emergency contact; have contact information accessible
  • If seizure occurs during practice: guide to floor if possible, time the seizure, protect head, do not restrain, do not put anything in mouth, call ambulance if > 5 minutes
