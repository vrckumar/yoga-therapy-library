Acid Peptic Disease (Hyperacidity / Peptic Ulcer)

1. Definition & Classification

Acid Peptic Disease (APD) — formerly known primarily as peptic ulcer disease — is defined as an inflammation of the inner lining of the stomach or duodenum resulting from acid-peptic digestion. In essence, it represents a breakdown of the dynamic balance between the protective factors of the stomach lining (mucus barrier, bicarbonate layer, mucosal blood flow, rapid cell repair) and the aggressive factors (hydrochloric acid, pepsin, Helicobacter pylori infection, NSAIDs, alcohol).

The term "peptic" derives from pepsin — the acid-dependent protein-digesting enzyme of the stomach. The yogic understanding places APD firmly in the category of Adhija Vyadhi — a psychosomatic disease whose root is in the Manomaya Kosha (the stressed, reactive mind) that percolates through the Pranamaya Kosha (disrupting autonomic regulation of gastric secretion) and manifests at the Annamaya Kosha as physical mucosal damage.

Types / Classification

By anatomical location:

  • Gastric ulcer — ulcer occurring in the stomach wall proper
  • Duodenal ulcer — ulcer occurring in the duodenum (first section of small intestine); more common, typically associated with high acid secretion
  • Oesophageal ulcer — associated with GERD (gastro-oesophageal reflux disease)

By severity:

  • Acute gastritis — first stage: redness and swelling of the gastric lining; reversible
  • Chronic gastritis — prolonged inflammation; may progress to ulceration
  • Peptic ulcer — full erosion through the mucosa into submucosa or deeper layers

By cause:

  • H. pylori-associated (most common worldwide)
  • NSAID/aspirin-induced
  • Stress-induced (critically ill patients — Cushing's ulcer, Curling's ulcer)

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Stomach Structure and Gastric Protection

The stomach is a muscular J-shaped sac forming the widest part of the digestive tract. It serves as a storage container, an acid and enzyme factory, and a germicidal organ. The stomach wall contains specialised cells:

  • Parietal (oxyntic) cells — produce hydrochloric acid (HCl), maintaining gastric pH between 1.5–3.5
  • Chief cells — produce pepsinogen, which is activated to pepsin by HCl
  • Mucous cells — secrete a thick protective mucus lining
  • G cells (pylorus) — produce gastrin in response to vagal stimulation, which in turn stimulates acid secretion

The stomach normally protects itself through a multi-layer barrier:

  1. Mucus layer — viscous coating that traps bicarbonate close to the epithelial surface
  2. Bicarbonate layer — alkaline buffer under the mucus that neutralises any acid that penetrates
  3. Mucosal blood flow — carries oxygen, removes acid, and enables rapid cell replacement
  4. Tight cell junctions — resist acid back-diffusion
  5. Rapid cell renewal — the gastric epithelium replaces itself every 3–5 days

When this defence is overwhelmed — by H. pylori colonisation, NSAIDs (which inhibit prostaglandin-mediated mucus synthesis), excess acid, alcohol, or chronic stress — the balance tips towards the aggressor and ulceration develops.

How Emotions Increase Gastritis

The brain-gut axis operates through the autonomic nervous system. When emotional excitement (anger, fear, anxiety, tension) activates the limbic system, the signal passes to the hypothalamus — the controller of the stress response — and then bifurcates:

  • Parasympathetic (Vagus nerve): Directly stimulates parietal cells to increase HCl production; also stimulates G cells to release gastrin, further amplifying acid secretion.
  • Sympathetic channel: Causes vasoconstriction of the mucosal blood vessels, reducing mucus production, slowing cell repair, and impairing the mucosal immune response (allowing H. pylori to penetrate deeper).

The result: stress simultaneously increases acid and weakens every defensive factor. Long-standing uncontrolled emotional reactivity — the yogic Udvegas (six enemies: desire, anger, miserliness, infatuation, arrogance, jealousy) — produces the exact physiological conditions for ulceration.

Symptoms

Classic presentation:

  • Dyspepsia — burning, gnawing pain in the upper abdomen (epigastrium)
  • Rhythm: Pain worse on empty stomach; relieved by food or antacids for 1–2 hours; recurs as stomach empties
  • Nocturnal pain — pain at 2–3 AM when stomach is completely empty; characteristic of duodenal ulcer
  • Fullness, bloating, belching, nausea
  • Heartburn (GERD overlap — acid reflux into oesophagus)
  • Loss of appetite is uncommon in duodenal ulcer (appetite may actually be stimulated by eating)

Complications (if untreated):

  • Haemorrhage (haematemesis — vomiting blood; melaena — black tarry stool)
  • Perforation (peritonitis — surgical emergency)
  • Gastric outlet obstruction (repeated scarring across the pylorus)

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

  • Clinical history (classic pain pattern)
  • Upper GI endoscopy (OGD — gold standard; visualises mucosa, permits biopsy)
  • H. pylori testing: breath test (C-13 urea breath test), stool antigen test, or biopsy urease test
  • Barium meal X-ray (less common now)

Medical Management

Category Drugs Mechanism
Antacids Magnesium hydroxide, Aluminium hydroxide Neutralise already-secreted acid; symptomatic relief
H2-receptor antagonists Ranitidine, Famotidine Block histamine-stimulated acid secretion from parietal cells
Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) Omeprazole, Pantoprazole Directly inhibit the acid-secreting proton pump; most potent acid suppression
H. pylori eradication Triple therapy: PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin for 7–14 days Eliminates the primary infective cause
Cytoprotective agents Sucralfate, Misoprostol Protect mucosal lining; promote healing

Drug treatment alone does not address the stress-driven root cause of APD. High recurrence rates in patients who return to their previous stressful lifestyle underscore this limitation — the yogic argument for addressing the Manomaya Kosha root.

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

The digestive system is acutely responsive to the mind's state. "Just as the face turns red when one is angry, the stomach lining turns red too." (PGDYT 104, Block 4). APD is a model psychosomatic disease — the GIT is one of the most directly innervated organs in the autonomic nervous system, with the enteric nervous system containing as many neurons as the spinal cord. Healing requires simultaneous intervention at all five Koshas.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Cleanse the gastric mucosa; reduce acid load; reset the GI environment; strengthen abdominal organisation.

The primary physical intervention for APD is the Kunjal Kriya (Vamana Dhauti / Stomach wash) — voluntarily induced emesis by ingesting 4–6 glasses of warm saline water and then stimulating the gag reflex. This:

  • Washes excess acid and irritants from the stomach
  • Clears H. pylori from the mucosal surface
  • Stimulates the vagal reflex, promoting parasympathetic balance
  • Clears emotional tension stored in the solar plexus region

Laghu Shankhaprakshalana (LSP) — a gentle saline cleanse using 500–750 ml warm salt water with 5 yoga postures (Tadasana, Tiryaka Tadasana, Kati Cakrasana, Tiryaka Bhujangasana, Udarak Sakti Vikasaka) — clears the stomach and upper small intestine of irritants, resets the GI motility pattern, and improves mucosal health.

Vajrasana — the essential post-meal posture. Sitting on the heels in Vajrasana for 5–10 minutes after meals:

  • Diverts blood flow to the digestive organs (reduces peripheral circulation to limbs)
  • Promotes efficient gastric emptying
  • Reduces post-prandial acid load
  • Provides mechanical compression of the lower abdomen that stimulates vagal activity

Asanas (GID-specific): Postures that compress, twist, or stretch the abdominal area stimulate mucosal blood flow and reduce prana blocks in the GI tract. Key postures: Paschimottanasana, Ardha Matsyendrasana, Sashankasana.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Counteract the vagal-sympathetic dysregulation that drives excess acid secretion; restore autonomic balance.

  • Chandra Anuloma Viloma (Left Nostril Breathing): The primary pranayama for APD. Left nostril breathing activates the parasympathetic (calming) branch of the ANS while simultaneously cooling the system — reducing the "pitta" (fire) excess that is APD's yogic counterpart. Practice lying on the left side (Chandra Nadi) or sitting.
  • Nadi Suddhi (9–12 rounds): Performed in Vajrasana after meals. Balances sympathetic and parasympathetic activity; reduces excess vagal acid drive by introducing a balanced rhythm that calms the reactive nervous system.
  • Sectional breathing with abdominal emphasis: Full abdominal breathing in lying position (Shavasana or Viparita Karani) promotes Samana Vayu balance — the prana of digestion. When performed with AAA chanting, it clears prana blocks in the solar plexus.
  • Sitali and Sitkari (cooling pranayama): Cool the system; reduce excess acid generation; promote physical calm.
  • Bhramari in Viparita Karani: Performed lying with legs elevated — the combination of vagal resonance (sound) and gravity-assisted venous drainage provides a powerful parasympathetic shift; specifically mentioned in PGDYT 104 for IBS and inflammatory bowel disorders.
  • QRT (Quick Relaxation Technique) — 3-minute abdominal breath awareness practice recommended for GID patients multiple times daily, especially before meals and immediately after a bowel movement.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Slow the excessive speed of the reactive mind that drives the stress → acid cascade; dissolve stored emotional "aggressors" from the solar plexus.

  • Cyclic Meditation: Reduces cortisol and sympathetic reactivity; documented to reduce stress-related GI symptoms.
  • OM Meditation / Nadanusandhana: Training in focusing and defocusing on OM is specifically mentioned in PGDYT 104 as useful for digestive disorders. The training involves applying this focus/defocus to the navel region to diffuse prana and achieve balance.
  • Yoga Nidra: Accesses deep-seated emotional tension — the suppressed anger, fear, and frustration that are the psychological precursors of APD.
  • DRT: Closes each session with systematic neuro-muscular release, allowing the digestive organs to relax.
  • Devotional sessions: Bhakti Yoga as "emotional management training" — developing soft, accepting emotions of sharing rather than the competitive, acquisitive emotional patterns that generate gastric acid.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Challenge the notion that life requires striving, fighting, and aggressive pursuit — the cognitive template that drives chronic GI stress.

The Bhagavad Gita's dietary guideline (17.8) is relevant: Satvik food is fresh, agreeable, promoting life, vitality, strength, health, happiness, and cheerfulness. The intellectual work at this level involves understanding why food choices and emotional patterns affect the GI tract — moving from compliance to genuine comprehension. Counselling and satsang challenge the beliefs that money, fame, and position are the source of happiness, replacing them with awareness of inner contentment.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Replace the agitated pursuit of external pleasures (which drives over-eating, drinking, smoking, and stress) with recognition of inherent inner contentment.

Karma Yoga principles — working with a sense of duty, without compulsive attachment to outcomes — dissolve the root of the acquisitive drive that creates chronic GI stress. The Gita's Yuktahara (moderation in food) and Yuktaviharasya (moderation in recreation) are practical Anandamaya prescriptions.

Dietary Guidance

  • Eat small, frequent meals (every 3–4 hours) — avoids the empty-stomach acid concentration
  • Cold milk (room temperature — not refrigerated) to neutralise excess acid temporarily
  • Avoid: spicy, fried, acidic foods; alcohol; caffeine; cigarettes; NSAIDs
  • Satvik diet: fresh, mild, well-cooked, adequate fibre
  • Never skip meals; heavy meals predispose to reflux

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Active bleeding peptic ulcer: No yoga therapy until medically stabilised. All physical practices contraindicated during active haemorrhage.
  • Kunjal Kriya: Contraindicated in oesophageal varices, recent abdominal surgery, severe hypertension, pregnancy, and active upper GI bleeding. Must be taught by a qualified yoga therapist.
  • Kapalabhati: Avoid in acute gastritis or active ulcer — the forceful abdominal pumping may aggravate mucosal irritation. Appropriate only in remission.
  • Strong forward bends: Avoid Halasana or Paschimottanasana in acute phase — abdominal compression increases intra-gastric pressure.
  • Inversions: Avoid in acute GERD or oesophagitis — gravity-assisted reflux worsens symptoms. Viparita Karani (mild inversion) is acceptable only when symptoms are controlled.
  • Breath retention (Kumbhaka): Avoid in active ulcer — increases intra-abdominal pressure.
  • NSAIDs: If pain management requires NSAIDs, ensure they are always taken with food; consider PPIs concurrently.
