Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's Disease

1. Definition & Classification

Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterised by loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta, producing the classic motor triad of tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia (slowness of movement). It is the second most common neurodegenerative disease after Alzheimer's (affecting approximately 10 million people globally).

Diagnostic criteria (MDS clinical diagnostic criteria): Bradykinesia + at least one of: muscular rigidity, rest tremor (4–6 Hz). Supportive criteria include: levodopa responsiveness, olfactory loss, asymmetric onset.

Classification:

  • Idiopathic Parkinson's disease — 90%+ of cases; unknown aetiology; diagnosis is clinical
  • Drug-induced parkinsonism — antipsychotics (D2 blockade), metoclopramide; reversible on withdrawal
  • Parkinson-plus syndromes — Multiple System Atrophy (MSA), Progressive Supranuclear Palsy (PSP), Corticobasal syndrome — motor parkinsonism with additional features; levodopa-resistant; worse prognosis

Stage (Hoehn and Yahr scale):

  • Stage 1: Unilateral involvement; no functional disability
  • Stage 2: Bilateral involvement; postural stability intact
  • Stage 3: Bilateral + mild postural instability; independent
  • Stage 4: Severe disability; still able to walk/stand unassisted
  • Stage 5: Wheelchair-bound or bedridden without assistance

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Nigrostriatal Pathway — The Dopamine Deficiency

The substantia nigra (SN, in the midbrain) sends dopaminergic projections via the nigrostriatal pathway to the striatum (caudate nucleus + putamen). This pathway is essential for the fine-tuning of voluntary movement:

Normal function: Striatal dopamine excites the "direct pathway" (promotes movement) and inhibits the "indirect pathway" (suppresses movement), resulting in smooth, intended movement initiation and execution.

In Parkinson's: Loss of SN dopamine neurons → striatum dopamine depletion → excessive inhibition of motor cortex output via the indirect pathway → difficulty initiating movement (akinesia/bradykinesia), rigidity, festination (involuntary acceleration), and postural instability.

Symptoms appear when approximately 60–80% of SN neurons have been lost — indicating that the brain maintains extensive compensatory reserves, providing a window for neuroprotective and rehabilitative interventions.

Alpha-Synuclein and Lewy Bodies

The pathological hallmark of PD is the intraneuronal accumulation of alpha-synuclein protein aggregates (Lewy bodies). Alpha-synuclein pathology spreads in a stereotyped pattern (Braak staging):

  • Stages 1–2: Dorsal vagal nucleus and olfactory bulb → olfactory loss, autonomic dysfunction (early prodromal symptoms)
  • Stages 3–4: Substantia nigra → motor symptoms appear
  • Stages 5–6: Neocortex → dementia in late-stage PD (Parkinson's Disease Dementia, PDD)

This staging explains why non-motor symptoms (constipation, anosmia, REM sleep behaviour disorder) often precede motor symptoms by years.

Non-Motor Features

Non-motor symptoms increasingly dominate the clinical picture:

  • Autonomic: Orthostatic hypotension, constipation, urinary urgency, sialorrhoea (drooling), seborrhoeic dermatitis
  • Neuropsychiatric: Depression (40%), anxiety (40%), apathy, dementia (30% late-stage), impulse control disorders (from dopaminergic medications)
  • Sleep: REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD — acting out dreams; may precede motor PD by decades), insomnia, excessive daytime somnolence
  • Sensory: Anosmia (reduced sense of smell — often first symptom), pain, fatigue
  • Voice/speech: Hypophonia (soft voice), dysarthria, dysphagia

3. Causes & Symptoms

Motor Symptoms

Tremor: Resting tremor (4–6 Hz); "pill-rolling" quality in hands; decreases with intentional movement; often asymmetric at onset. Distinguished from essential tremor (which is action tremor, bilateral, affects hands).

Rigidity: Cogwheel or lead-pipe increased tone in all muscle groups; contributes to the characteristic flexed posture.

Bradykinesia: Slowness and poverty of movement; reduced arm swing; small steps (micrographia in writing); difficulties with fine motor tasks; freezing of gait (sudden temporary inability to move feet forward — "feet stuck to floor").

Postural instability: Impaired righting reflexes → falls; typically appears later than the other features; pull test (patient pushed from behind) — inability to take 1–2 steps to recover indicates significant postural instability.

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Levodopa + carbidopa/benserazide — gold standard; converts to dopamine in the brain (carbidopa prevents peripheral conversion); most effective motor treatment; long-term: wearing-off, dyskinesias
  • Dopamine agonists — pramipexole, ropinirole, rotigotine patch; delay levodopa initiation; impulse control disorder risk
  • MAO-B inhibitors — rasagiline, selegiline; reduce dopamine breakdown; mild benefit; possible neuroprotective role
  • COMT inhibitors — entacapone, tolcapone; extend levodopa effect
  • Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS) — high-frequency stimulation of subthalamic nucleus or globus pallidus internus; dramatically reduces motor fluctuations in selected patients
  • Physiotherapy, speech therapy, occupational therapy — essential non-pharmacological components
  • Yoga therapy — growing evidence base; complements pharmacological and conventional rehabilitation

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for Parkinson's disease has a developing evidence base, with particular evidence for improvement in balance, gait, quality of life, mood, and voice function. The practice principles align with the broader rehabilitation science: amplitude-based training, rhythmic movement, voice practice, and balance challenge.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Balance asanas with support — graduated challenge:

  • Garudasana (Eagle Pose) — arm component (seated or standing with support): The bilateral arm crossing and uncrossing provides proprioceptive challenge and bilateral coordination; arms only is feasible even with advanced PD
  • Vrikshasana (Tree Pose) with wall/chair support: Single-leg balance; graduated challenge from fingertips on chair → light touch → no support; directly trains the postural reflex impairment central to PD falls
  • Tandem standing and walking: Heel-to-toe; challenges the medio-lateral stability that is specifically impaired in PD

Tai Chi-style slow movements: The Tai Chi evidence base in PD is the most robust of any movement intervention — improving balance, gait, and falls reduction. Yoga therapy can incorporate slow, flowing, deliberate movements with the same characteristics: large amplitude (counters micrographia/bradykinesia), rhythmic (uses preserved procedural timing circuits), bilateral symmetrical (activates mirror neuron circuits).

Large amplitude movement emphasis (LSVT BIG principle): Parkinson's produces movement that is progressively smaller (micrographia, shuffling gait, reduced arm swing). Yoga therapy should deliberately practise large, exaggerated movements — "big" arm raises, wide strides, full range of motion — to counter the neurological tendency toward movement reduction.

Asanas addressing PD posture: The characteristic PD posture is forward flexion — head and trunk bowing forward. Counteracting this:

  • Bhujangasana — prone backbend; extends the spine against the PD flexion tendency
  • Ustrasana — camel pose; full anterior trunk extension; counteracts spinal flexion
  • Tadasana — mountain pose; conscious upright posture; uses visual feedback and proprioceptive cuing

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Bhramari (primary) — voice and respiratory rehabilitation:

    • PD produces hypophonia (reduced voice volume) and dysarthria through rigidity of the laryngeal muscles; Bhramari directly exercises these muscles through sustained, vibrated exhalation
    • The humming sound requires exactly the vocal mechanism that PD impairs — adequate breath support, sustained phonation, resonant projection
    • Group Bhramari normalises voice volume expectations (social reinforcement)
    • LSVT LOUD equivalent: The yoga principle of "think loud, practise loud" for voice mirrors the LSVT LOUD programme's evidence base for PD dysarthria

  • Nadi Suddhi — ANS balance; particularly relevant for PD autonomic dysfunction (orthostatic hypotension, constipation); reduces anxiety and depression

  • Diaphragmatic breathing re-education — PD produces thoracic rigidity and reduced respiratory excursion; diaphragmatic breathing restores ventilatory efficiency and voice breath support

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

  • DRT — addresses PD depression and anxiety; provides the experience of muscular relaxation that PD's rigidity systematically removes; demonstration that the body can release is itself therapeutic
  • Yoga Nidra — sleep disturbance is nearly universal in PD (RBD, nocturia, rigidity-related); Yoga Nidra before sleep improves sleep initiation and quality; reduces the dream-enactment behaviour of RBD (by deepening the pre-sleep relaxation state)
  • Cyclic Meditation — reduces the fatigue that is a major non-motor symptom of PD and significantly impairs quality of life

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Understanding PD as a dynamic condition — symptoms fluctuate with medication cycles (on-off phenomenon); yoga therapy timing relative to levodopa dose should be planned (practise during "on" periods when motor function is optimal)
  • Falls prevention education — PD-specific hazard awareness; home modification; freezing management strategies (visual cues — use a line on the floor; rhythmic counting; shift weight before stepping)
  • Medication compliance — levodopa timing is critical; missing doses risks immobility; yoga therapy sessions reinforce the importance of consistent medication-taking

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • OM chanting and Nadanusandana (sound meditation): Voice practices are particularly important for PD — they train the vocal system, provide auditory feedback, and engage the patient's capacity for sound at a deep, meaningful level. Nadanusandana — the exploration of sound as a path to inner silence — is both therapeutically and spiritually significant for PD patients
  • Group practice — PD is profoundly isolating; the physical limitations and the social stigma of visible tremor and slowed movement often lead patients to withdraw from social life. The yoga group provides belonging, normalisation, and collective joy
  • Karma Yoga — finding ways to contribute within changed physical capacity; volunteering in PD support groups; mentoring newly diagnosed patients

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Freezing of gait: Plan session areas without narrow doorways or crowded paths; use rhythmic verbal cueing ("one-two-one-two") to assist with transitions; never hurry the patient
  • Postural instability (late PD): Non-negotiable chair/wall support for all balance practices; mat on the floor only with assistance to get up
  • Dyskinesias (levodopa-induced involuntary movements — "on" state): Reduce asana intensity during peak dyskinesia periods; schedule yoga during stable "on" period before peak medication effect
  • Dementia (late PD): Simplify instructions; maintain consistent routine; see intellectual disability adaptations for communication strategies
  • Orthostatic hypotension: Rise slowly; Sethubandhasana before standing
  • Dysphagia: Monitor for coughing or aspiration during chanting; ensure upright posture
