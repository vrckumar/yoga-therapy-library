Sleep Apnea

Sleep Apnea

1. Definition & Classification

Sleep apnea is a sleep-related breathing disorder characterised by repeated episodes of partial or complete cessation of breathing during sleep, resulting in oxygen desaturation, sleep fragmentation, and daytime consequences including excessive sleepiness, cognitive impairment, and cardiovascular risk.

Types:

  • Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) — most common type (90%+ of cases): upper airway collapse during sleep despite continued respiratory effort; the airway physically obstructs
  • Central Sleep Apnea (CSA) — respiratory drive from the brainstem temporarily ceases; no respiratory effort during the apneic episode; less common; associated with heart failure, opioid use, brainstem pathology
  • Mixed/Complex Sleep Apnea — combination of obstructive and central components

Severity grading (by Apnea-Hypopnea Index — AHI: events per hour):

  • Mild: AHI 5–15
  • Moderate: AHI 15–30
  • Severe: AHI > 30

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Upper Airway Anatomy

The upper airway extends from the nasal passages to the glottis. During wakefulness, dilator muscles (genioglossus, tensor palatini, geniohyoid, and others) maintain airway patency. During sleep, muscle tone falls — and in OSA patients, anatomical factors predispose to collapse:

  • Narrow pharyngeal airway — due to enlarged tonsils/adenoids, macroglossia, retrognathia, or excess parapharyngeal fat deposition (obesity)
  • Reduced airway soft tissue compliance — the airway wall yields too easily to the negative pressure generated by respiratory effort during inspiration
  • High loop gain — unstable ventilatory control system that overresponds to CO₂ changes

Collapse mechanism: During sleep → muscle tone falls → airway narrows → inspiratory effort generates negative intraluminal pressure → airway collapses → apnea → hypoxia and hypercapnia → arousal response (amygdala + brainstem activation) → muscle tone restored → airway reopens → snorting/snoring → brief awakening (often unremembered) → return to sleep → cycle repeats, potentially hundreds of times per night.

Oxygen Desaturation

Each apneic episode causes progressive oxygen desaturation (SpO₂ may fall from 98% to below 80% in severe OSA). Repeated desaturation-reoxygenation cycles produce:

  • Oxidative stress — reactive oxygen species generated during reperfusion damage endothelial cells
  • Sympathetic activation — each arousal triggers a catecholamine surge; sustained nocturnal sympathetic hyperactivity causes hypertension (OSA is the most common secondary cause of hypertension)
  • Systemic inflammation — elevated CRP, IL-6, TNF-α
  • Endothelial dysfunction — directly accelerating atherosclerosis

Sleep Architecture Disruption

Normal sleep cycles through NREM stages (N1 → N2 → N3 slow-wave sleep) and REM sleep in approximately 90-minute cycles. Slow-wave sleep (N3) is the most restorative phase; REM sleep is essential for memory consolidation. In OSA, arousals prevent sustained N3 and REM sleep → sleep deprivation despite lying in bed for 8 hours → excessive daytime somnolence, cognitive impairment, mood disturbance, metabolic dysregulation.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Risk Factors

Risk Factor Mechanism
Obesity (especially central) Parapharyngeal fat deposition narrows airway; reduced respiratory muscle compliance
Male sex Narrower airway; testosterone reduces upper airway muscle tone
Age Progressive loss of upper airway muscle tone and compliance
Anatomical factors Retrognathia, macroglossia, enlarged tonsils/adenoids
Supine sleeping position Tongue and soft palate fall posteriorly under gravity
Alcohol Relaxes upper airway muscles, reduces arousal threshold
Smoking Airway inflammation, increased mucus production, upper airway oedema
Nasal congestion Increases inspiratory effort, worsening negative pressure on pharynx

Symptoms

Nocturnal: Loud snoring (almost universal), witnessed apneas, gasping/choking arousals, nocturia, night sweats, insomnia.

Daytime: Excessive sleepiness (Epworth Sleepiness Scale), morning headache (CO₂ retention), cognitive impairment ("brain fog"), depression, reduced libido, irritability.

4. Conventional Treatment

  • CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure) — gold standard; provides a pneumatic splint maintaining airway patency during sleep; highly effective but adherence is often poor (50% long-term use)
  • Mandibular advancement device (MAD) — for mild-moderate OSA; protrudes the mandible to enlarge the retroglossal space
  • Positional therapy — for position-dependent OSA (worse in supine); devices or training to maintain lateral sleep position
  • Weight loss — most effective non-CPAP intervention; 10% weight loss can reduce AHI by 30%
  • Surgery — uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP), tonsillectomy (paediatric), maxillomandibular advancement
  • Central sleep apnea: Treat underlying cause; ASV (adaptive servo-ventilation) if needed

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy addresses OSA through multiple complementary mechanisms: strengthening and toning the upper airway muscles (Annamaya), improving nasal breathing and respiratory control (Pranamaya), addressing obesity (multi-kosha), improving sleep quality (Manomaya), and motivating sustained weight management behaviour (Vijnanamaya/Anandamaya).

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Neck and throat exercises — direct muscle toning of the upper airway:

  • Neck rolls and cervical Sakti Vikasaka — mobilise the cervical spine and activate cervical musculature
  • Simhasana (Lion Pose) — wide mouth opening with tongue protrusion exercises the genioglossus muscle (the primary tongue retractor); reduced genioglossus activity is a key OSA mechanism
  • Sarvangasana — inversion drains dependent nasal oedema and tonifies the pharyngeal muscles through the inverted gravitational position
  • Weight management asanas (Surya Namaskara, dynamic sequences) — obesity being the primary modifiable risk factor, vigorous physical practice is a core component for overweight/obese OSA patients

Lateral sleeping position (left lateral decubitus) — enforced through sleep positioning training — can reduce AHI by 30–50% in position-dependent OSA patients.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Ujjayi pranayama (throat toning) — the partial glottic closure required for Ujjayi directly exercises the pharyngeal and glottic muscles; the proprioceptive feedback from this muscular effort trains awareness of the upper airway. Regular Ujjayi practice may improve pharyngeal muscle tone
  • Nadi Suddhi — restores nasal breathing; nasal breathing (as opposed to mouth breathing) is associated with significantly reduced OSA severity (nasal airflow maintains more stable airway pressure gradient)
  • Jala Neti — saline nasal irrigation reduces nasal congestion and inflammatory load; improves nasal patency for nocturnal breathing
  • Bhramari — sound-generated vibration in the pharynx; the humming tone produces muscle vibration in the soft palate and posterior pharyngeal wall, potentially maintaining some dilator muscle tone

Note on pranayama and OSA: Breath retention practices (Kumbhaka) are generally avoided — they may mimic the hypoxic episodes of OSA and are inappropriate in patients with significant oxygen desaturation.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

  • DRT and Yoga Nidra — poor sleep quality in OSA contributes to anxiety, depression, and hyperarousal that paradoxically further disrupts sleep architecture; DRT practised before bedtime reduces pre-sleep autonomic arousal and improves sleep initiation
  • Nadi Suddhi before sleep — shifts ANS balance toward parasympathetic dominance at bedtime; reduces the sympathetic hyperarousal that OSA-related sleep deprivation perpetuates during daytime
  • Cyclic Meditation — addresses the daytime consequences of poor sleep (fatigue, cognitive fog, irritability) through parasympathetic restoration

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Weight management education: understanding that 10% weight loss can halve AHI provides powerful motivation
  • Sleep hygiene counselling: consistent sleep times, avoiding alcohol within 4 hours of sleep, lateral positioning, reducing large evening meals
  • CPAP adherence support: yoga therapy can improve CPAP adherence by reducing the claustrophobic distress that leads to device abandonment (DRT-based desensitisation to the mask sensation)

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Karma Yoga motivation for weight management: patients who connect their self-care to their capacity to serve family, community, and larger purpose demonstrate better adherence to lifestyle change than those motivated solely by fear of disease. The restorative benefits of improved sleep — deeper rest, clearer mind, greater equanimity — are themselves Anandamaya experiences that reinforce the practice.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Severe OSA with significant hypoxaemia: Medical CPAP is mandatory; yoga therapy is adjunctive, not a replacement
  • Kumbhaka (breath retention): Contraindicated — mimics hypoxic episodes; avoid in all OSA patients
  • Inversions (Sarvangasana): Caution in severe obesity, uncontrolled hypertension, and cervical spine disease
  • Yoga Nidra inducing sleep: Monitor patients who fall asleep during Yoga Nidra in class (common in severely sleep-deprived patients); this is not harmful but should be noted
  • Post-CPAP removal distress: In patients recently diagnosed, sensitivity required regarding body image and adjustment to the diagnosis
