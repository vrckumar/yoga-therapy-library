Cancer (Medical Oncology — Supportive/Palliative Yoga Therapy)

1. Definition & Classification

Cancer is a heterogeneous group of diseases characterised by uncontrolled proliferation of cells with the potential for local invasion and distant metastasis. Over 100 distinct cancer types are recognised, classified by tissue of origin (carcinoma — epithelial; sarcoma — connective tissue; lymphoma — lymphoid; leukaemia — haematopoietic; glioma — neural) and histological grade.

In yoga therapy, cancer is approached from a supportive and palliative perspective — yoga is not proposed as a cancer treatment but as a powerful tool for improving quality of life, managing treatment side effects, reducing psychological distress, and potentially supporting immune function during and after oncological treatment.

Cancer stages:

  • Stage I: Localised; high curability
  • Stage II: Locally advanced
  • Stage III: Regional lymph node involvement
  • Stage IV: Distant metastases; palliative intent

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Carcinogenesis: The Hallmarks of Cancer

Cancer develops through the accumulation of genetic and epigenetic alterations in normal cells (carcinogenesis), involving:

  1. Self-sufficiency in growth signals — oncogene mutations (Ras, c-Myc) provide constitutive growth stimulation
  2. Insensitivity to growth inhibition — tumour suppressor gene loss (p53, Rb) removes normal brakes on proliferation
  3. Evasion of apoptosis — anti-apoptotic gene overexpression (Bcl-2) prevents programmed cell death
  4. Limitless replicative potential — telomerase reactivation maintains telomere length; cells bypass the Hayflick limit
  5. Angiogenesis — VEGF secretion induces new blood vessel formation to supply the growing tumour
  6. Invasion and metastasis — loss of cell adhesion molecules (E-cadherin), matrix metalloproteinase secretion enable invasion of basement membrane and vascular entry

The Immune System and Cancer

Normal immunosurveillance eliminates nascent cancer cells through NK cells (recognising loss of MHC-I), cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs — recognising tumour antigens), and the innate inflammatory response. Cancer cells evade this through:

  • PD-L1 expression — binds PD-1 on CTLs, inhibiting their killing activity (the target of modern immunotherapy checkpoint inhibitors)
  • Downregulation of MHC-I — becomes "invisible" to CTLs
  • Secretion of immunosuppressive cytokines (TGF-β, IL-10) creating an immunosuppressive tumour microenvironment

Psychological stress and immunosurveillance: Sustained cortisol and catecholamine elevation suppress NK cell activity, reduce CTL function, and promote tumour-promoting inflammation — providing a plausible mechanism for the clinical observation that psychosocial support improves cancer outcomes.

Treatment-Related Physiology

Chemotherapy effects:

  • Bone marrow suppression → anaemia (fatigue), neutropenia (infection risk), thrombocytopenia (bleeding risk)
  • Peripheral neuropathy (taxanes, platinum compounds)
  • Cardiotoxicity (anthracyclines — doxorubicin)
  • Nephrotoxicity (cisplatin)
  • Cancer-related fatigue (CRF) — the most prevalent and distressing symptom; multifactorial (anaemia, inflammation, HPA dysregulation, sleep disruption, depression, inactivity)

Key Symptoms Across Cancer Types

Alarm symptoms (require urgent evaluation): unexplained weight loss, unexplained persistent fatigue, blood in urine/stool/sputum, persistent lump, change in skin or mole, unexplained pain.

Common functional symptoms managed in yoga therapy:

  • Cancer-related fatigue (CRF)
  • Pain (from tumour, neuropathy, post-surgical)
  • Nausea and vomiting (chemotherapy-induced)
  • Anxiety and depression (prevalent in 30–40% of cancer patients)
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Cognitive impairment ("chemo brain")
  • Lymphoedema (post-surgical, post-radiotherapy)

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Surgery — primary treatment for solid tumours; curative or palliative
  • Chemotherapy — cytotoxic agents targeting rapidly dividing cells; systemic
  • Radiotherapy — ionising radiation targeting localised disease
  • Hormonal therapy — for hormone-responsive cancers (breast — tamoxifen/aromatase inhibitors; prostate — androgen deprivation therapy)
  • Targeted therapy — agents targeting specific molecular drivers (imatinib/CML, trastuzumab/HER2+ breast, erlotinib/EGFR+ NSCLC)
  • Immunotherapy — checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab targeting PD-1/PD-L1), CAR-T cell therapy
  • Palliative care — symptom management, quality of life, psychosocial support; complementary to disease-directed treatment

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy in oncology is a supportive/palliative intervention — improving quality of life, reducing treatment side effects, and providing psychosocial support. It is practised with respect for: current treatment phase, current performance status, specific toxicities, and the patient's own goals (which may include cure, prolonged survival, or peaceful dying, depending on stage).

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Cancer and its treatment rapidly produce physical deconditioning. Gentle movement practice counters this:

  • Gentle asanas — modified based on current performance status; bed-based practices if bedridden; chair yoga if ambulatory but limited; standing sequences when health allows
  • Specific concerns:
    • Post-mastectomy: Avoid arm-raising over head until surgical clearance; gentle shoulder mobilisation (Sakti Vikasaka) prevents post-surgical adhesive capsulitis
    • Post-colostomy: Avoid strong abdominal compression
    • Bone metastases: Strict contraindication to weight-bearing at affected sites; no twists or bending if spinal mets
    • Neutropenia: Avoid communal practice during nadir (infection risk); individual home practice
    • Lymphoedema: Avoid constricting positions; gentle pumping exercises with elevation support lymphatic drainage

Walking meditation (mindful walking) — one of the most evidence-supported interventions for cancer-related fatigue; accessible even to significantly deconditioned patients.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • PET (Pranayama Energy Technique) — gentle, accessible breathing practice designed for significantly fatigued or deconditioned patients; uses breath as an energy-management tool rather than a vigorous exercise
  • MSRT (Mind Sound Resonance Technique) — systematic relaxation using the internal resonance of OM; does not require physical touch (important for patients with pain on touch, lymphoedema, or post-surgical sensitivity) and is deeply restful; particularly suitable for palliative care settings
  • Nadi Suddhi — regular practice supports sleep quality, immune function, and emotional regulation
  • Bhramari — rapid anxiety management; useful pre-chemotherapy and pre-procedure

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

The psychological burden of cancer is immense: existential threat, disrupted identity, treatment-related anxiety, anticipatory nausea, grief for lost function, and often clinical depression and PTSD.

  • DRT — provides the experience of profound rest independent of cancer diagnosis; demonstrates to the patient that the observing awareness is undisturbed even when the body is deeply ill
  • Yoga Nidra — particularly valuable for cancer-related pain management (endorsed by the International Association for the Study of Pain as complementary); reduces opioid requirements; improves sleep; addresses existential distress
  • MIRT (Mind-body Integration and Relaxation Training) — rebuilds positive relationship with the body after disfiguring surgery or treatment; reintegrates body image

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Lifestyle modification for survivorship: Diet (anti-inflammatory, plant-rich), exercise, smoking cessation, alcohol reduction — all within yoga therapy's scope for cancer survivors post-treatment
  • Navigating information overload: Helping patients develop Viveka (discernment) about the vast amount of cancer information (and misinformation) they encounter
  • Understanding complementary vs. alternative: Yoga therapy is supportive of (not a replacement for) conventional oncological treatment — this distinction must be clearly established

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body) — CENTRAL IN ONCOLOGY

Cancer confronts patients with their mortality in a way that few other diagnoses do. The Anandamaya dimension of yoga therapy offers what conventional medicine cannot: a framework for dying and for living well until death.

  • Karma Yoga framing: Reframing the cancer journey as an opportunity for the deepest human inquiry — into what truly matters, into what is most fully oneself beyond the physical body
  • Quality of life as a primary goal: In palliative intent, Anandamaya wellbeing — moments of joy, beauty, love, connection, peace — becomes the therapeutic objective, not survival statistics
  • Group practice: The community of shared practice provides belonging that counters the isolation of serious illness; the collective energy of group devotional practice is a genuinely restorative force documented in qualitative oncology research

6. Recommended Practices (Adapted by Performance Status)

Performance Status Practices
ECOG 0–1 (fully active/mildly restricted) Full gentle programme: loosening exercises, asanas (modified as needed), Surya Namaskara (slow), pranayama, DRT/Yoga Nidra, meditation
ECOG 2 (ambulatory, >50% of waking hours up) Chair yoga + bed asanas; Nadi Suddhi, Bhramari; DRT; MSRT; group devotional
ECOG 3–4 (limited self-care, bedridden) Bed-based breath practices; MSRT; Yoga Nidra; devotional music listening; family presence in practice

Note: Neutropenic patients (ANC <0.5) practice individually at home; return to group on immune recovery.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Active bone metastases: No weight-bearing at affected sites; no twisting or bending if spinal involvement; no Sarvangasana if cervical or thoracic mets
  • Thrombocytopenia: Avoid inversions, breath retention, vigorous practices when platelet count <50,000/μL
  • Neutropenia: Individual practice only; no communal mats; hand hygiene
  • Brain metastases/increased ICP: No inversions; no breath retention; no Kapalabhati
  • Post-mastectomy/axillary clearance: Avoid arm-raising until surgical clearance; specific lymphoedema precautions
  • Cardiotoxic chemotherapy (anthracyclines): Baseline ECG/ECHO; avoid vigorous practice
  • Active nausea/vomiting: Modify timing of practices; DRT and MSRT can be used during moderate nausea
