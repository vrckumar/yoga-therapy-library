Allergic Rhinitis

1. Definition & Classification

Allergic Rhinitis (AR) is a chronic inflammatory disorder of the nasal mucosa triggered by IgE-mediated hypersensitivity to airborne allergens. It is characterised by the classic tetrad of nasal symptoms: sneezing, rhinorrhoea (runny nose), nasal blockage, and nasal itch — often accompanied by itchy, watery eyes (allergic conjunctivitis). AR is among the most common chronic conditions worldwide, affecting 10–30% of adults and up to 40% of children.

In the yogic framework, AR represents an Annamaya Kosha manifestation of immune hypersensitivity — related to the Pranamaya Kosha disturbance at the nasal level (disrupted nasal breathing patterns, impaired nasal defences) and the Manomaya Kosha pattern of hypersensitivity that predisposes to immune overreaction. Yoga treats AR with specific nasal cleansing practices (Shatkarmas) that directly address the nasal mucosal dysfunction.

Classification

By trigger pattern:

  • Seasonal AR (Hay Fever): Triggered by seasonal pollens (tree pollen — spring; grass pollen — summer; mould spores — autumn). Symptoms correlate with pollen season.
  • Perennial AR: Year-round exposure to indoor allergens: house dust mites (Dermatophagoides pteronyssinus), cockroaches, animal dander (cats, dogs), moulds. Symptoms present throughout the year with variable intensity.
  • Occupational AR: Triggered by workplace allergens (flour dust in bakers, latex in healthcare workers, animal allergens in laboratory staff).

By severity:

  • Intermittent: Symptoms fewer than 4 days/week or fewer than 4 weeks/year
  • Persistent: Symptoms ≥4 days/week and ≥4 weeks/year
  • Mild / Moderate-Severe (impact on sleep, daily activities, work/school)

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The Nasal Mucosa and Its Defence Functions

The nose performs four critical functions: olfaction, humidification (warming and moistening air to body temperature and 100% humidity before it reaches the lungs), filtration (large particles trapped in nasal hair; smaller particles in the mucus layer), and immunological defence (IgA antibodies in nasal secretions; mucociliary clearance).

The nasal mucosa contains:

  • Ciliated columnar epithelium — cilia beat at 12–15 Hz, moving the mucus blanket posteriorly to the nasopharynx
  • Goblet cells — secrete mucus
  • Submucosal glands — additional secretory capacity
  • Venous sinusoids — capacious blood vessels responsible for nasal turbinate engorgement (congestion) and the alternating nasal cycle

The Nasal Cycle: Normally, the nasal passages alternate dominance approximately every 90–120 minutes — one nostril is relatively open (the dominant), while the other is relatively congested. This cycle is coordinated with the autonomic nervous system and has yogic significance: the dominant nostril reflects the dominant brain hemisphere and ANS branch (right nostril — sympathetic/left brain; left nostril — parasympathetic/right brain).

IgE-Mediated Allergic Mechanism

  1. Sensitisation: On first allergen exposure, antigen-presenting cells activate T-helper 2 (Th2) lymphocytes → stimulate B-cells → produce allergen-specific IgE antibodies → IgE binds to mast cells and basophils in the nasal mucosa (sensitised state — no symptoms yet).

  2. Re-exposure (Early Phase — 0–1 hour): Allergen cross-links IgE on mast cells → mast cell degranulation → histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes released → sneezing, itch, rhinorrhoea (immediate response within minutes).

  3. Late Phase (4–8 hours): Eosinophils, T-cells infiltrate the mucosa → sustained nasal blockage; mucosal hyper-reactivity (nasal inflammation causes heightened response to subsequent irritants including cold air, pollution, perfume).

3. Causes & Symptoms

Common Allergens

Category Examples
Indoor House dust mites, cockroaches, cat/dog dander, indoor moulds
Outdoor seasonal Tree pollens (birch, oak), grass pollens, ragweed, outdoor moulds
Occupational Flour, latex, animal proteins, chemical agents

Symptoms

  • Sneezing: Often paroxysmal (in runs of 5–20 sneezes)
  • Rhinorrhoea: Clear, watery nasal discharge
  • Nasal obstruction/blockage: Congestion; reduced sense of smell
  • Nasal pruritus (itch): Characteristic "allergic salute" — rubbing the nose upward with the palm
  • Post-nasal drip: Mucus draining posteriorly → chronic throat clearing, cough
  • Ocular symptoms: Itching, redness, watering (allergic conjunctivitis — 50% of AR patients)
  • Ear symptoms: Eustachian tube dysfunction; hearing impairment; tinnitus (in children)
  • Sleep disturbance: Nasal congestion causes mouth breathing, poor sleep quality, daytime somnolence
  • Fatigue, reduced concentration, school/work performance impairment
  • "Allergic shiner": Infraorbital darkening from venous congestion

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

  • Clinical history (timing, triggers, seasonal pattern, allergic family history)
  • Skin prick testing (SPT): most common allergy test — positive wheal-and-flare reaction to specific allergens
  • Specific IgE blood tests (RAST/ImmunoCAP)
  • Nasal cytology or biopsy (specialist centres)

Medical Management

Category Examples Mechanism
Oral antihistamines (2nd generation) Cetirizine, Loratadine, Fexofenadine H1 receptor block; reduces itch, sneezing, rhinorrhoea
Intranasal corticosteroids (INCS) Fluticasone, Mometasone, Budesonide nasal spray Anti-inflammatory; most effective for all symptoms especially congestion
Intranasal antihistamines Azelastine nasal spray Fast onset; local H1 block
Decongestants Pseudoephedrine (oral); Oxymetazoline (nasal — short term only) Alpha-1 agonist → vasoconstriction → decongestion
Leukotriene receptor antagonists Montelukast Blocks LTD4; useful when asthma co-exists
Immunotherapy (Allergen-specific desensitisation) SCIT, SLIT Only disease-modifying treatment; reprogrammes immune response

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

AR is a disorder of the Annamaya Kosha (nasal mucosa structure) and Pranamaya Kosha (nasal breathing function), with a Manomaya Kosha dimension (hypersensitivity — the excessive immunological reactivity that parallels the excessive mental reactivity of the stressed modern lifestyle). Yoga therapy addresses all three with specific, targeted practices.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Cleanse the nasal passages; strengthen and normalise the nasal mucosal immune defence; reduce mucosal congestion.

Jala Neti (Nasal Saline Irrigation) — Primary Treatment: Jala Neti involves flowing isotonic or slightly hypertonic warm saline solution through the nasal cavity — in one nostril and out the other — using a neti pot. This is the primary and most evidence-supported yoga therapy for AR. Mechanism:

  • Physically removes allergen particles, mucus, and inflammatory mediators from the nasal mucosa
  • Dilutes and washes away histamine and inflammatory cytokines
  • Restores mucociliary clearance function (cilia work best in a moist, well-hydrated environment)
  • Reduces nasal mucosal oedema through osmotic effect (slightly hypertonic saline)
  • Prevents secondary bacterial infection (by removing the mucus that harbours bacteria)

Jala Neti is performed daily (morning) in AR patients, with additional sessions during acute symptom episodes.

Sutra Neti (Rubber Catheter Neti): A thin rubber catheter is passed through the nose and retrieved through the mouth, then gently worked back and forth. This mechanically clears deeper nasal passages, stimulates mucosal blood flow, and trains the breathing through the nasopharyngeal passage. Sutra Neti strengthens the nasal mucosal tissues and is used weekly (not suitable for beginners — requires training).

Kapalabhati (as nasal cleansing): In the context of AR, Kapalabhati is applied primarily as a nasal cleansing technique — the forceful exhalations dry and clear the nasal passages, expelling mucus and reducing congestion. This is the direct Annamaya-level action — physically removing the environment that supports allergen exposure and mast cell sensitisation.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Restore nasal breathing dominance; correct the disrupted nasal cycle; reduce nasal hyperreactivity.

  • Nadi Suddhi: Systematic alternating nasal breathing retrains the nasal cycle, restores proper nasal mucosal circulation, and balances the ANS through the nasal-autonomic reflexes. Also gently clears each nostril alternately.
  • Kapalabhati (pranayama aspect): Trains diaphragmatic and abdominal muscle coordination; produces air velocities through the nose that clear secretions.
  • Cooling Pranayama — Sitali and Sitkari: Though apparently counter-intuitive (cooling for rhinitis), these pranayamas strengthen the mucosal adaptive response to temperature change. The initial coolness strengthens vascular reactivity in the nasal mucosa. Used carefully and intermittently.
  • Vibhagiya Svasana: Restores full nasal breathing — the therapeutic goal is to ensure the patient breathes through the nose (not the mouth), which normalises nasal airflow, humidity, and immune function.
  • Bhramari: The nasal resonance during Bhramari increases nitric oxide (NO) production in the paranasal sinuses — NO is a potent vasodilator and antimicrobial agent that improves nasal airflow and immune defence.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Reduce the immune hypersensitivity by reducing the overall Manomaya hypersensitivity that predisposes to allergic over-reaction.

In the yogic model, allergy is related to the same hypersensitivity (Bhavasamskaras — reactive impressions) that characterises the stressed, reactive Manomaya Kosha. Calming the mind reduces the immune system's inflammatory over-readiness:

  • Cyclic Meditation: Reduces cortisol; modulates Th2 immune polarisation (stress promotes Th2 → allergic phenotype; relaxation rebalances toward Th1)
  • DRT: Full-body relaxation reduces nasal mucosal congestion (sympathetic withdrawal → nasal vasodilation → decongestion)
  • OM Meditation: Sustained inner calm; reduces reactive sensory sensitivity

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Environmental management through understanding of allergen triggers.

Allergen avoidance is the first-line management of AR — the Vijnanamaya layer involves understanding:

  • How to reduce dust mite load (mattress covers, hot washing of bedding, reducing soft furnishings)
  • Pollen avoidance strategies during high-pollen days
  • Pet dander management
  • Diet: some evidence for anti-inflammatory dietary approaches (omega-3 rich foods, quercetin-containing foods)

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Develop equanimity toward environmental irritants — the immune system's over-reaction mirrors the mind's over-reaction.

The Anandamaya insight: the nose is the primary organ through which prana enters the body — each breath is an opportunity for conscious engagement with the life force. Jala Neti becomes not just a medical intervention but a daily ritual of purification (Shauca) — one of Patanjali's Niyamas.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Jala Neti: Use only isotonic or slightly hypertonic warm saline (not plain water — risk of amoebic sinusitis). Tilt head correctly. After neti, dry the nose with Kapalabhati (fast exhalations) to prevent retained moisture. Do not perform in acute sinusitis or nasal polyps (may push infection deeper).
  • Sutra Neti: Requires supervised instruction; not for self-teaching.
  • Kapalabhati: Avoid in moderate-severe hypertension, active middle ear infection, recent nasal surgery.
  • Hypertension patients: Jala Neti should be attempted with caution; the bending position increases intra-cranial pressure. Obtain medical clearance.
  • Nasal polyps: Jala Neti may be difficult; Sutra Neti is contraindicated. Medical management first.
  • Acute sinusitis: Defer Sutra Neti and vigorous Kapalabhati until infection resolved. Gentle Jala Neti with saline may help drainage in mild cases.
