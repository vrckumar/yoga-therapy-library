Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

1. Definition & Classification

OCD is a chronic psychiatric disorder characterised by recurrent, persistent, intrusive thoughts, images, or impulses (obsessions) that cause marked anxiety, and repetitive behavioural or mental acts (compulsions) performed to reduce that anxiety. The compulsive behaviours provide temporary relief but perpetuate the cycle by preventing habituation to the feared thoughts.

Common obsession themes: contamination, harm (to self or others), symmetry/order, sexual or blasphemous thoughts, and checking.

Common compulsion types: handwashing, checking (locks, appliances), ordering, counting, mental rituals (prayer, reviewing past actions), reassurance-seeking.

OCD is classified as an Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorder in DSM-5 (separate from anxiety disorders, reflecting its distinct neurobiology). Severity ranges from mild (occupies <1 hour/day) to severe (occupies most of the day, grossly impairs function).

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The Orbitofrontal-Striatal Circuit

The neurobiological substrate of OCD involves hyperactivity of a specific cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) loop:

Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) → Caudate nucleus (striatum) → Thalamus → back to OFC

This circuit normally governs habit formation and the detection of "something is wrong" signals. In OCD, the circuit is stuck in a loop: the OFC generates an error signal ("something is dirty/wrong/incomplete"), the caudate fails to filter it out, the thalamus amplifies it back to the OFC — creating a persistent, unresolvable "feeling of wrongness" that drives compulsive attempts to correct it.

Neuroimaging consistently shows:

  • Hyperactivity in the OFC, caudate, and thalamus at baseline and during symptom provocation
  • Successful treatment (CBT or SSRI) normalises this hyperactivity
  • The OFC hyperactivity correlates with subjective feelings of incompleteness and wrongness

Serotonin plays a key modulatory role: SSRIs (which increase serotonergic tone) are effective for OCD at doses higher than those needed for depression, suggesting that serotonergic modulation of the CSTC circuit is the mechanism of action. Yoga's effects on serotonin metabolism are relevant here.

The Role of Anxiety

Obsessions trigger anxiety through conditioned fear learning — the intrusive thought becomes associated with threat through a process identical to phobia acquisition. Compulsions are negative-reinforcement behaviours: they temporarily reduce anxiety, which reinforces their repetition. The absence of compulsions (response prevention) initially increases anxiety but, with sustained exposure, produces habituation — the basis of CBT's exposure and response prevention (ERP) approach.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes

  • Genetic: Concordance in identical twins 60–87%; first-degree relatives have 3–5× increased risk
  • Neurobiological: Serotonin and glutamate dysregulation; OFC-caudate-thalamic circuit hyperactivity
  • Psychological: Early learning experiences; religious or cultural environments emphasising sin, contamination, or perfectionism
  • PANDAS/PANS: Paediatric cases triggered by streptococcal infection (neuroinflammation mechanism)
  • Stress: OCD symptoms typically worsen markedly under psychological stress — a key entry point for yoga therapy

Symptoms

The Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale (Y-BOCS) assesses time spent, distress caused, interference, resistance, and degree of control for both obsessions and compulsions. The OCD cycle: triggering stimulus → intrusive thought → anxiety → compulsion → temporary relief → return of anxiety → reinforcement of cycle.

4. Conventional Treatment

  • First-line: Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) — the gold standard psychological treatment; graded confrontation with feared stimuli without performing compulsions
  • Medication: SSRIs (fluvoxamine, paroxetine, sertraline, fluoxetine) at high doses; clomipramine (TCA) — often more effective than SSRIs but less tolerated
  • Combined: ERP + SSRI most effective for moderate-severe OCD
  • Refractory cases: Augmentation strategies (antipsychotics), deep brain stimulation (DBS) targeting the subthalamic nucleus or nucleus accumbens in severe, treatment-resistant cases

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

OCD's core mechanism — the loop of intrusive thought → anxiety → compulsion → temporary relief — is directly addressable through yoga therapy. The primary yogic tools are those that train the mind to disengage from thought content (MEMT) and those that address the anxiety-generating OFC-thalamic circuit through relaxation and autonomic balancing.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Physical tension is both a symptom and a perpetuator of OCD: the compulsive activity cycle maintains a state of muscular vigilance and readiness. Gentle physical practice provides release:

  • Loosening exercises — simple, predictable joint mobilisation; establishes bodily calm as a foundation for mental work
  • Pavanamuktasana series — for patients with somatic anxiety manifestations (GI symptoms, tension headaches)
  • Avoid highly complex sequences — the perfectionism inherent in OCD can be triggered by performance demands in yoga; simplicity and non-judgement are therapeutic in themselves

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Nadi Suddhi (primary) — reduces the chronic sympathetic hyperarousal that amplifies OFC error signals; lengthens the "pause" between intrusive thought and compulsive response
  • Bhramari — sound-based grounding; the internal resonance interrupts the obsessive rumination loop by providing a compelling, pleasant sensory anchor that is incompatible with the negative thought spiral
  • Diaphragmatic breathing (extended exhalation) — 1:2 breath ratio activates the parasympathetic system, reducing anxiety at the physiological level simultaneously with the cognitive work at the Vijnanamaya level

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

MEMT (Mind Emptying Meditation Technique) — PRIMARY: The core meditation approach for OCD. Rather than attempting to suppress or analyse obsessive thoughts (which perpetuates engagement), MEMT trains the skill of allowing thoughts to arise without engagement:

  • Observe thoughts as events in awareness, not commands to be obeyed
  • Allow thoughts to pass without performing the compulsive response
  • Return attention gently to the breath/sound anchor

This is functionally equivalent to the "defusion" technique in ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) — but grounded in the yoga tradition of the Sakshi (witness) — cultivating the observing awareness that sees thoughts without becoming them.

Cyclic Meditation — the active-passive cycling reduces baseline OFC hyperactivity through deep rest states.

DRT — progressive body relaxation provides the experience of bodily ease that interrupts the physical tension component of the compulsion cycle.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

Vijnanamaya counselling is essential in OCD:

  • Understanding the neurobiology of the OCD loop — "This is my OFC stuck in a loop, not a real emergency" — provides the cognitive distance needed to resist compulsion
  • Viveka (discrimination) — the ability to distinguish the real self (the observing awareness) from the intrusive thoughts that feel so compelling and ego-syntonic in OCD
  • Challenging the perfectionism schema — many OCD patients equate "wrongness" or "incompleteness" with catastrophic consequences; yoga philosophy's Santosha (contentment with imperfection) directly challenges this

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • Karma Yoga — structured, purposeful activity without attachment to perfect outcome directly confronts the perfectionism that drives OCD. The yogi's instruction to act without obsessing over results is an Anandamaya intervention for the compulsive mind
  • Devotional community — the social acceptance of a yoga group provides the unconditional belonging that OCD patients often fear losing if others discover their "shameful" thoughts

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Perfectionism in practice: Explicitly instruct that there is no "perfect" way to do yoga; mistakes are welcomed as practice opportunities
  • Religious/spiritual OCD: If patient has religious-themed obsessions, devotional practices should be introduced carefully with awareness of potential triggers
  • Coordinate with ERP: Yoga therapy is adjunctive to ERP, not a replacement; relaxation should not become a compulsion itself (reassurance-seeking through DRT)
  • Monitoring: Symptoms can fluctuate significantly; establish baseline Y-BOCS and reassess monthly
