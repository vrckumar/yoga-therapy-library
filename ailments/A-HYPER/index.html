Hyperthyroidism

Hyperthyroidism

1. Definition & Classification

Hyperthyroidism is an endocrine disorder in which the thyroid gland produces and secretes excessive amounts of thyroid hormones (T3 and T4), causing the body's metabolic rate to accelerate beyond physiological norms. The result is a state of systemic hyperactivation — cardiovascular, neurological and metabolic systems are all overstimulated.

Classification:

Type Description
Graves' disease Most common cause; autoimmune production of TSH-receptor antibodies that continuously stimulate the thyroid
Toxic multinodular goitre Multiple autonomously functioning thyroid nodules secreting excess hormone
Toxic adenoma Single autonomous nodule (Plummer's disease)
Thyroiditis-related Transient hyperthyroid phase from subacute/postpartum thyroiditis as stored hormone leaks
Exogenous/iatrogenic Excessive levothyroxine supplementation
TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma Rare secondary hyperthyroidism

Graves' disease accounts for approximately 60–80% of all hyperthyroidism and is markedly more common in women, particularly those aged 20–40 years.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The thyroid gland sits anteriorly in the lower neck, overlying the trachea at C5–T1, just below the Adam's apple. It is highly vascular — blood supply from the superior and inferior thyroid arteries — and enlarges substantially when overstimulated.

Normal hormone regulation: The hypothalamic–pituitary–thyroid (HPT) axis operates via negative feedback:

  • Hypothalamus releases TRH → Pituitary releases TSH → Thyroid produces T3/T4 → rising T3/T4 suppresses further TRH and TSH release

Pathophysiology of Graves' disease: Thyrotropin receptor antibodies (TRAb) bind to and continuously activate TSH receptors on thyroid follicular cells, bypassing the normal negative feedback. The result is unregulated, excessive hormone synthesis and thyroid enlargement (diffuse goitre).

Systemic effects of excess T3/T4:

  • Markedly raised BMR — increased O₂ consumption, heat production, weight loss despite hyperphagia
  • Tachycardia, increased cardiac output, atrial fibrillation risk
  • Adrenergic sensitisation — tremor, anxiety, sweating, heat intolerance
  • Negative calcium balance — risk of osteoporosis with prolonged hyperthyroidism
  • Ophthalmopathy in Graves' (exophthalmos) — TSH receptor antibodies stimulate retroorbital connective tissue

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes

  • Autoimmune — Graves' disease (TRAb-mediated); familial predisposition
  • Nodular thyroid disease — toxic multinodular goitre, toxic adenoma
  • Inflammation — subacute de Quervain's thyroiditis; postpartum thyroiditis
  • Excess iodine — Jod-Basedow phenomenon (iodine load triggering hyperthyroidism in susceptible glands)
  • Medications — amiodarone (high iodine content), interferon
  • Stress — recognised trigger for autoimmune thyroid disease onset or relapse

Symptoms

System Manifestations
Metabolic Weight loss despite increased appetite, heat intolerance, excessive sweating
Cardiovascular Tachycardia, palpitations, hypertension, arrhythmias (AF risk in older patients)
Neurological Nervousness, irritability, anxiety, fine tremor, difficulty sleeping
Musculoskeletal Muscle weakness (proximal myopathy), reduced exercise tolerance
Dermatological Warm moist skin, fine hair, pretibial myxoedema (Graves')
Ophthalmological Lid lag, proptosis/exophthalmos (Graves' ophthalmopathy)
Reproductive Irregular or scanty menstrual periods, reduced fertility
Gastrointestinal Increased bowel frequency, diarrhoea
Thyroid Diffuse goitre, thyroid bruit (turbulent blood flow)

4. Conventional Treatment

Anti-thyroid medications:

  • Carbimazole / Methimazole — first-line; blocks thyroid peroxidase (TPO), reducing T3/T4 synthesis
  • Propylthiouracil (PTU) — alternative; also blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion; preferred in pregnancy (first trimester)
  • Treatment courses typically 12–18 months; ~50% remission rate with Graves'

Radioactive iodine (RAI, ¹³¹I):

  • Most common definitive treatment; selectively destroys thyroid tissue
  • Most patients develop hypothyroidism post-RAI and require lifelong levothyroxine

Surgery (thyroidectomy):

  • Indicated for large goitres causing compression, failed medical therapy, or patient preference
  • Post-operative hypothyroidism universal; levothyroxine required

Beta-blockers (propranolol, atenolol):

  • Adjunct for rapid symptom relief of adrenergic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor, anxiety) while awaiting anti-thyroid drug effect

Ophthalmopathy management:

  • Selenium supplementation, immunosuppression, orbital radiotherapy or decompression surgery in severe cases

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale (Pancha Kosha)

Hyperthyroidism is a disorder of hyperactivation — the metabolic engine is running far too fast. The IAYT approach is the mirror opposite of the hypothyroid protocol: the objective at every kosha level is to cool, calm and regulate an over-stimulated system. Strong, heating or stimulating practices are contraindicated; the emphasis is on parasympathetic activation, deep rest and cooling pranic practices.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Goal: Reduce systemic hyperactivation through gentle, calming physical practice; avoid any practice that further raises sympathetic tone or stimulates the thyroid gland.

  • AVOID Sarvangasana — the cervical compression-release mechanism that benefits hypothyroidism would over-stimulate an already hyperactive thyroid. Contraindicated until euthyroid state is restored with medical treatment.
  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) / Savasana — prolonged, conscious physical relaxation is the primary annamaya tool; allows the hyperactive system to down-regulate. 20–30 minutes.
  • Pavanamuktasana series (gentle) — slow, deliberate joint-loosening exercises calm the nervous system without raising metabolic rate; address musculoskeletal weakness.
  • Gentle forward bends (Paschimottanasana, Shashankasana) — forward-folding postures activate the parasympathetic response, reduce heart rate, promote internalization.
  • Avoid strong backbends that further open the throat region (Ustrasana, Dhanurasana, full Matsyasana with chin raised) — these may additionally stimulate the thyroid area.
  • Avoid vigorous dynamic practices — Surya Namaskara at fast pace, jumping sequences, or any practice that significantly raises heart rate and body temperature.
  • Sattvic cooling diet — hydrating, light foods; avoid stimulants (coffee, tea, alcohol, spicy foods) that further accelerate metabolic rate.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Goal: Activate the left-nostril (parasympathetic/lunar) pranic channel; use cooling pranayama to reduce the internal heat and overstimulation; avoid any practice that raises pranic activation further.

  • Chandra Anuloma Viloma (Left-Nostril Breathing) — left-nostril dominance activates the parasympathetic (Ida nadi/Chandra/moon) channel, reducing sympathetic tone, lowering heart rate and metabolic rate. 9 rounds. Primary indicated pranayama.
  • Sitali Pranayama (Cooling Breath) — inhale through rolled tongue (or lips if unable to roll), exhale through the nose. Direct cooling effect on the body; reduces internal fire (pitta). 9 rounds.
  • Sitkari Pranayama — alternative to Sitali for those unable to roll the tongue; inhale through teeth. Similar cooling mechanism.
  • Nadi Suddhi / Nadi Shodhana (without retention) — alternate-nostril breathing without kumbhaka (breath retention); balances pranic flow, calms agitation, reduces anxiety. 9 rounds.
  • Bhramari Pranayama (Humming Bee Breath) — humming on exhalation activates the vagus nerve, reduces heart rate and anxiety; prolonged exhalation activates parasympathetic response. 9 rounds.
  • AVOID:
    • Surya Anuloma Viloma (right-nostril) — would further raise metabolic rate
    • Kapalabhati — vigorous sympathetic stimulation contraindicated
    • Bhastrika — forceful bilateral stimulation contraindicated
    • Any pranayama with prolonged kumbhaka (breath retention) — raises internal pressure and stimulation

Manomaya Kosha (Mental-Emotional Body)

Goal: Address the anxiety, irritability, sleeplessness and emotional volatility that are both symptoms and perpetuating factors of hyperthyroidism; cultivate equanimity and calm.

  • MSRT (Mind Sound Resonance Technique) — extended progressive relaxation with OM chanting; deeply calming to a racing mind. 30–45 minutes.
  • Yoga Nidra — systematic body-scan relaxation in Savasana; activates parasympathetic response, reduces cortisol, improves sleep quality. Particularly valuable for the insomnia component of hyperthyroidism.
  • Cyclic Meditation — alternating activation and deep rest phases recalibrate the autonomic nervous system toward parasympathetic dominance.
  • Trataka (gentle) — concentration practice that stills mental chatter without raising arousal; short duration (5 min) to prevent eye strain given potential ophthalmopathy in Graves'.
  • Dhyana (OM Meditation) — sustained meditative absorption calms the manomaya layer; reduces the speed-of-mind quality that perpetuates adrenergic activation.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Intellect/Discriminative Body)

Goal: Help the individual understand the relationship between stress, autoimmune trigger and hyperthyroid relapse; develop discriminative awareness of over-activation patterns.

  • Yogic counselling — the connection between chronic stress, Graves' disease onset and relapse is well-documented; identifying stress patterns is therapeutically essential.
  • Notional correction — many hyperthyroid patients identify with high achievement, urgency and doing; learning to value rest and stillness as equally productive is a key insight.
  • Svadhyaya — self-observation of body signals (palpitations, heat, tremor) as signals from the pranamaya layer rather than threats; building self-regulatory awareness.
  • Happiness Analysis — distinguishing the hyperactive energy of early hyperthyroidism (which may be misperceived as wellbeing) from true equanimity.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Causal Body)

Goal: Address the causal layer of agitation and the deep-rooted tendency toward over-doing and hyperactivation; access the stillness that underlies all activity.

  • Extended meditation — as the medical treatment stabilises hormone levels and yoga therapy calms the pranamaya layer, deeper meditation becomes accessible; this is where the autoimmune tendency to attack the self (thyroid) is addressed at the causal level.
  • Karma Yoga — with detachment — action performed without anxious attachment to outcomes; the antidote to the driven, urgent quality that characterises many hyperthyroid individuals.
  • Satsang — immersion in a calm, spiritually supportive environment; the quality of community energy (sattvic company) provides a stable external reference for inner regulation.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

Situation Guidance
Sarvangasana Absolutely contraindicated — over-stimulates already hyperactive thyroid
Stimulating pranayama Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Surya Anuloma Viloma, Kumbhaka — all contraindicated
Vigorous dynamic asana Avoid fast Surya Namaskara, jumping sequences, hot yoga — raise metabolic rate further
Strong throat-opening backbends Ustrasana, full Dhanurasana — avoid while thyrotoxic
Cardiac involvement (tachycardia, AF) Medical clearance required; avoid inverted poses, strong pranayama and breath retentions
Graves' ophthalmopathy Restrict Trataka; avoid facial congestion from inversions; protective eyewear in sunlight
Osteoporosis risk (prolonged hyperthyroidism) Avoid weight-bearing impact postures if bone density is compromised
Pregnancy PTU preferred medically in first trimester; yoga adapted for obstetric context
Thyroid storm Acute medical emergency; yoga therapy not applicable until medically stabilised
Post-RAI / post-thyroidectomy Likely transitioning to hypothyroidism; shift gradually to hypothyroid protocol as TSH rises

Key research directions:

  • Cooling pranayama (Sitali/Chandra Anuloma) and autonomic modulation in hyperthyroidism
  • Yoga Nidra and sleep architecture in thyrotoxic insomnia
  • Stress reduction as adjunct to anti-thyroid drug therapy for Graves' disease remission rates
  • HRV as biomarker for yoga therapy response in hyperthyroidism
