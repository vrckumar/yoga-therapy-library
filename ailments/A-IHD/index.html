Ischemic Heart Disease (IHD/CHD)

1. Definition & Classification

Ischemic Heart Disease (IHD), also called Coronary Heart Disease (CHD) or Coronary Artery Disease (CAD), is a condition in which the coronary arteries — the blood vessels supplying the heart muscle itself — become narrowed or blocked by atherosclerosis (fatty plaque deposits), reducing or completely interrupting blood flow to myocardial tissue. When the heart muscle is deprived of oxygen (ischaemia), it produces symptoms ranging from chest pain (angina) to permanent muscle death (myocardial infarction — heart attack).

IHD is the single largest cause of death in most developed countries and is rapidly increasing in South Asia. The yogic framework places it firmly in the category of Adhija Vyadhi — the erratic modern lifestyle of speed, greed, and over-stimulation percolating from Manomaya into Annamaya Kosha as arterial disease.

Classification

Stable Angina Pectoris: Chest pain on exertion, relieved within minutes by rest. Consistent, predictable pattern. Indicates fixed, flow-limiting coronary stenosis (typically >70% narrowing).

Unstable Angina: Angina at rest, or with minimal exertion, or with increasing frequency and severity. Medical emergency — indicates active plaque disruption.

NSTEMI (Non-ST Elevation Myocardial Infarction): Partial occlusion of a coronary artery; cardiac troponin elevated; no ST elevation on ECG.

STEMI (ST Elevation Myocardial Infarction — Full Heart Attack): Complete occlusion; full-thickness myocardial infarction; ST elevation on ECG. Requires emergency reperfusion (primary PCI or thrombolysis).

Silent Ischaemia: Objective evidence of ischaemia on testing (ECG, stress test) without symptoms — common in diabetics with neuropathy.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Coronary Circulation

The heart muscle (myocardium) requires continuous oxygen and glucose delivery. The coronary arteries — the first branches of the aorta — supply this demand: the left coronary artery (divides into left anterior descending [LAD] and circumflex), and the right coronary artery (RCA). Any interruption to these vessels has immediate consequences because the myocardium has almost no oxygen reserve (unlike skeletal muscle, which has significant myoglobin reserves).

Atherosclerosis — The Mechanism of IHD

Atherosclerosis is the gradual accumulation of fatty, fibrous, and calcified material (plaque) within the arterial wall, producing progressive narrowing (stenosis) of the lumen.

Stages:

  1. Endothelial injury — from hypertension, smoking, oxidised LDL, diabetes, chronic inflammation
  2. LDL cholesterol infiltration — into the sub-endothelial space; oxidised → triggers immune response
  3. Macrophage engorgement — macrophages ingest oxidised LDL → become foam cells → fatty streaks visible
  4. Fibrous cap formation — smooth muscle cell migration forms a fibrous cap over the lipid core
  5. Plaque growth — progressive lumen narrowing; plaques may calcify (stable) or remain lipid-rich (vulnerable)
  6. Plaque rupture — vulnerable plaques with thin fibrous caps rupture, exposing the lipid core to blood → acute thrombosis → acute coronary syndrome (ACS)

Stress and IHD

The autonomic nervous system directly mediates the stress-IHD connection:

  • Sympathetic activation → tachycardia + increased myocardial oxygen demand (supply-demand mismatch → angina)
  • Cortisol → hypertension, dyslipidaemia, insulin resistance, endothelial dysfunction (accelerates atherosclerosis)
  • Catecholamines → coronary artery spasm (Printzmetal's angina)
  • Chronic stress → sustained inflammation → plaque progression and vulnerability

3. Causes & Symptoms

Risk Factors

Modifiable: Hypertension, dyslipidaemia, smoking, diabetes, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, chronic stress, excessive alcohol.

Non-modifiable: Age (men >45; women >55), male sex, family history of premature IHD (<55 years in male first-degree relative, <65 in female), South Asian ethnic origin.

Symptoms

  • Angina pectoris: Central chest pain/pressure, typically radiating to left arm, jaw, or back; precipitated by exertion, emotion, cold, or meals; relieved within 5 minutes by rest or GTN (glyceryl trinitrate)
  • Breathlessness on exertion: When left ventricular function is impaired
  • Palpitations: Ischaemia-induced arrhythmia
  • Syncope: Ventricular arrhythmia or severe outflow obstruction
  • MI: Crushing chest pain at rest, lasting >20 minutes; not relieved by GTN; associated with sweating, pallor, nausea, vomiting, collapse

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

  • ECG (resting, exercise stress test)
  • Echocardiography (wall motion abnormalities)
  • Cardiac troponin (myocardial injury marker)
  • Coronary angiography (gold standard — visualises coronary stenoses)
  • CT coronary angiography

Medical Management

Secondary prevention (post-MI or confirmed IHD):

  • Antiplatelet therapy: Aspirin + Clopidogrel (dual antiplatelet — post-ACS)
  • Statins: LDL reduction; plaque stabilisation; anti-inflammatory
  • Beta-blockers: Reduce heart rate and myocardial oxygen demand; anti-arrhythmic
  • ACE inhibitors: Reduce afterload; cardioprotective remodelling
  • GTN (short-acting) and nitrates (long-acting): Vasodilate coronary arteries

Revascularisation:

  • Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) with stent — for acute STEMI and significant stable disease
  • Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) — for multi-vessel disease

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

IHD is the cardiovascular expression of chronic psychosomatic imbalance. The yoga therapy approach must respect the physical limitations imposed by compromised coronary perfusion while addressing the Manomaya root.

Critical principle: All yoga therapy for IHD is GENTLE. No practices that significantly increase myocardial oxygen demand. No strong pranayama. No inversions.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Gentle physical activity to improve cardiovascular fitness without precipitating ischaemia; provide the cardiac rehabilitation equivalent.

  • Gentle loosening exercises: Slow, rhythmic movements of all joints; walking in place. These improve peripheral circulation and venous return without significantly increasing heart rate.
  • Gentle asanas (cardiac-safe): Slow, smooth postures with resting between each: Ardhakati Cakrasana, Baddha Konasana, Vajrasana, Setu Bandhasana (mild, supported). All performed with complete breath freedom — no breath retention.
  • Pavanamuktasana series Part I: Wind-relieving exercises — gentle, rhythmic; safe for cardiac patients; improves peripheral circulation.
  • No vigorous asanas: Avoid strong standing postures, arm balances, or postures requiring sustained effort.
  • Satvik diet: Low-fat, low-salt, high-fibre, fresh food. Direct cardiac dietary benefit — reduces LDL, cholesterol, and inflammatory load.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Activate the parasympathetic nervous system to reduce heart rate and sympathetic vascular tone; reduce myocardial oxygen demand through ANS balance.

  • Nadi Suddhi (without Kumbhaka): Primary pranayama for IHD. Balances ANS; reduces heart rate variability imbalance; activates vagal protective mechanisms for the heart.
  • Vibhagiya Svasana (sectional breathing): Slow, complete breathing — particularly emphasising the extended, relaxed exhalation — maximally activates vagal tone.
  • OM chanting (prolonged exhalation): The extended "Mmmm" phase of OM chanting produces the same physiological vagal activation as extended exhalation — safe and accessible.
  • Bhramari: Gentle vagal resonance; reduces sympathetic vascular tone; safe in IHD.
  • AVOID: Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Surya Bhedana (right nostril stimulating pranayama), any pranayama with Kumbhaka (breath retention) — all increase sympathetic tone or intra-thoracic pressure, both of which are contraindicated in IHD.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Reduce the chronic stress and type-A personality patterns that are the primary Manomaya drivers of IHD.

  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique): Core intervention — extended, monitored relaxation directly reduces myocardial oxygen demand via parasympathetic dominance. All cardiac yoga sessions end with DRT.
  • Cyclic Meditation: Documented (S-VYASA) to reduce blood pressure, cortisol, and sympathetic activity. Appropriate for stable IHD.
  • OM Meditation: Sustained inner silence; counteracts the "type-A" urgency, hostility, and time-pressure that characterise IHD-prone personalities.
  • Yoga Nidra: Deep rest equivalent; reduces sympathetic arousal; improves sleep quality.
  • Devotional sessions: Social support is a well-established protective factor against cardiac mortality. Group practice addresses this dimension.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Address the personality patterns — perfectionism, excessive work drive, hostility, time urgency — that are the Vijnanamaya expression of IHD risk.

Notional correction targets the false belief that value, security, and happiness depend on ceaseless productive effort and achievement. Cardiac rehabilitation programmes include psychological counselling — the yoga therapy equivalent provides philosophical depth to this work.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Discover the inner contentment that reduces the compulsive drive for external achievement — the deepest anti-IHD medicine.

Karma Yoga — acting without anxious attachment to results — removes the sustained effort-anxiety that keeps the sympathetic nervous system in overdrive. This is not passivity; it is the discovery that effective action can occur without internal tension.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Unstable angina or recent MI (within 4–6 weeks): No active yoga therapy; only DRT and Yoga Nidra in monitored setting.
  • No vigorous asanas, no inversions, no arm balances.
  • No Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Kumbhaka, or strong pranayama — all increase sympathetic drive or intrathoracic pressure.
  • No Surya Namaskara at pace — rhythmic dynamic sequences increase myocardial oxygen demand.
  • Monitor heart rate: Maintain below 60–70% of maximum heart rate (220 − age). Stop if chest pain, breathlessness, or palpitations develop.
  • Hypertension co-morbidity: Avoid Sarvangasana, Sirsasana, and strong exhalatory pranayama.
  • Post-PCI or CABG patients: Obtain cardiologist clearance before commencing. Restrict arm postures after CABG until sternal wound fully healed.
  • Cardiac medications: Never advise discontinuation. Yoga therapy is adjunctive to medications.
  • Always in supervision initially: IHD yoga therapy should begin in a supervised clinical yoga programme, not home-based.
