Hypothyroidism

1. Definition & Classification

Hypothyroidism is an endocrine disorder characterised by insufficient production of thyroid hormones (thyroxine T4 and triiodothyronine T3) by the thyroid gland. Because thyroid hormones regulate the rate at which cells convert nutrients into energy, a deficiency slows metabolic activity throughout the body.

Classification:

Type Description
Primary hypothyroidism Dysfunction originates in the thyroid gland itself (most common)
Secondary hypothyroidism Failure of the pituitary to secrete adequate TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone)
Tertiary hypothyroidism Hypothalamic dysfunction reducing TRH output
Subclinical hypothyroidism Elevated TSH with normal T3/T4; often asymptomatic
Congenital hypothyroidism Present from birth; requires early hormone replacement to prevent developmental delay

The most common cause worldwide is iodine deficiency; in iodine-sufficient regions, Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune destruction of thyroid tissue) predominates.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The thyroid gland is a butterfly-shaped structure situated at the anterior aspect of the lower neck, overlying the trachea at the C5–T1 vertebral level, just inferior to the laryngeal prominence (Adam's apple). It consists of two lobes connected by the isthmus.

Hormone synthesis: Thyroid follicular cells absorb iodine from the bloodstream and incorporate it into thyroglobulin to produce T4 and the more biologically active T3. Release is governed by the hypothalamic–pituitary–thyroid (HPT) axis:

  • Hypothalamus → TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone)
  • Anterior pituitary → TSH (thyrotropin)
  • Thyroid → T3 / T4 → negative feedback to hypothalamus and pituitary

Physiological roles of thyroid hormones:

  • Regulate basal metabolic rate (BMR)
  • Support cardiovascular function (heart rate, contractility)
  • Influence growth, bone development and CNS maturation
  • Modulate mood, cognition and neuromuscular function
  • Interact with reproductive hormones — deficiency causes menstrual irregularities

In hypothyroidism the reduced hormone level causes TSH to rise (elevated TSH is the primary diagnostic marker in primary hypothyroidism). Persistent low metabolic drive leads to accumulation of glycosaminoglycans in tissues (myxoedema), pericardial effusion, and diastolic dysfunction if untreated.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes

  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis — autoimmune lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of thyroid parenchyma
  • Iodine deficiency — most common global cause; goitre may develop as the gland enlarges compensatorily
  • Post-thyroidectomy / post-radioactive iodine therapy — iatrogenic hypothyroidism
  • Medications — lithium, amiodarone, interferon-alpha suppress thyroid function
  • Pituitary or hypothalamic disease — secondary/tertiary forms
  • Postpartum thyroiditis — transient hypothyroid phase following delivery

Symptoms

System Manifestations
Metabolic Weight gain despite normal or reduced appetite, cold intolerance, low BMR
Neurological Fatigue, sluggishness, poor concentration, impaired memory, depression
Cardiovascular Bradycardia, diastolic hypertension, pericardial effusion
Musculoskeletal Muscle weakness, cramps, myalgia, joint stiffness
Dermatological Dry coarse skin, hair thinning/loss, brittle nails, periorbital puffiness
Reproductive Irregular/heavy menstrual periods, reduced fertility
Gastrointestinal Constipation, slow gut motility
Endocrine Goitre (enlarged thyroid), hyperlipidaemia

4. Conventional Treatment

Pharmacological:

  • Levothyroxine (synthetic T4) — first-line treatment; oral daily dose titrated to normalise serum TSH
  • Combination T3/T4 therapy considered in select cases of residual symptoms on T4 alone

Monitoring:

  • TSH measured 6–8 weeks after dose change; annual monitoring once stable
  • Special attention during pregnancy — requirements increase by ~30%

Nutritional considerations:

  • Adequate dietary iodine (150 µg/day; 200 µg/day in pregnancy)
  • Selenium supports T4-to-T3 conversion
  • Goitrogens (raw cruciferous vegetables in large amounts) may mildly suppress synthesis

Surgical — rarely required; indicated for large compressive goitre unresponsive to medical therapy.

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale (Pancha Kosha)

Hypothyroidism is primarily a disorder of hypometabolism — the system is sluggish, the energy (prana) is low, and the metabolic "fire" (agni) is dampened. The IAYT approach works across all five koshas to stimulate thyroid function, rekindle metabolic energy, and address the emotional-cognitive dampening that accompanies the condition.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Goal: Directly stimulate the thyroid gland through physical compression-release mechanisms; increase peripheral circulation and metabolic activity.

  • Sarvangasana (Shoulder Stand) — the most important asana for hypothyroidism. Venous compression at the base of the neck during the posture followed by release upon coming down creates a "milking" effect that increases blood flow to the thyroid gland, stimulating hormone secretion. Held for 1–3 minutes with normal breathing.
  • Matsyasana (Fish Pose) — practised as a counter-pose to Sarvangasana. The cervical hyperextension stretches the anterior neck, further stimulating the thyroid region. Benefits include improved respiratory function and endocrine gland activation.
  • Surya Namaskara (Sun Salutation) — dynamic 12-step sequence that raises core body temperature, improves circulation to endocrine glands, and counters the physical lethargy characteristic of hypothyroidism. 3–6 rounds progressively.
  • Ustrasana, Matsyasana, Setu Bandhasana — throat-opening backbends that extend and stimulate the thyroid area.
  • Pavanamuktasana series — loosening exercises to counter musculoskeletal stiffness; also aid gut motility (addressing constipation).
  • Shatkarmas — Jala Neti maintains nasal and upper respiratory hygiene; Kapalabhati (vigorous abdominal pumping) serves as both kriya and energising pranayama, increasing oxygen delivery and metabolic stimulation.
  • Sattvic diet — adequate iodine from whole foods; avoidance of excessive raw goitrogens; warm, easily digestible foods to support low agni.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Goal: Activate the right-nostril (sympathetic) pranic channel to counteract the low-energy, hypothyroid state; use throat-vibrating pranayama to directly stimulate the thyroid.

  • Surya Anuloma Viloma (Right-Nostril Breathing) — right-nostril dominance activates the sympathetic nervous system (Pingala nadi), raising metabolic rate and body temperature. Stimulates the sun (surya) energy channel — directly indicated for low-metabolic conditions. 9 rounds.
  • Ujjayi Pranayama (Victorious Breath) — the glottal constriction that produces the characteristic oceanic sound creates vibration in the laryngeal/tracheal area, directly stimulating the thyroid gland. Also generates internal heat (tapas). 9–12 rounds.
  • Kapalabhati — vigorous abdominal exhalations stimulate sympathetic tone, raise prana, increase O₂ delivery and cellular metabolism. 25–30 rounds.
  • Vibhagiya Pranayama (Sectional Breathing) — thoracic section particularly improves upper thoracic and cervical circulation.
  • Avoid: prolonged Chandra Anuloma Viloma (left-nostril / cooling) practices which further suppress metabolic fire.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental-Emotional Body)

Goal: Address the cognitive slowing, depressive affect and low motivation that accompany hypothyroidism; cultivate alertness and positive engagement.

  • Trataka (Candle Gazing) — sustained visual focus counters cognitive fog, improves concentration, and has a mild sympathetic-activating effect. 5–10 minutes.
  • MSRT (Mind Sound Resonance Technique) — OM chanting and Nada practices create vibration in the pharyngeal-cervical region, providing resonant stimulation. Manomaya purification through sound.
  • Energising/motivating visualisations — during relaxation practices, visualise warmth, vitality, bright light spreading through the body — countering the cold, heavy quality of hypothyroid states.
  • Svadhyaya (self-study) — observing mood patterns and energy cycles; building awareness of the link between stress, emotional suppression and thyroid function.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Intellect/Discriminative Body)

Goal: Correct cognitive misconceptions about the disease; develop understanding of the HPT axis and lifestyle factors; build adherence and self-regulation.

  • Yogic counselling — understanding that hypothyroidism has both physical and psychosomatic components; medication compliance plus lifestyle interventions yield optimal outcomes.
  • Notional correction — addressing the tendency toward passivity, withdrawal and "learned helplessness" that can develop with chronic fatigue.
  • Happiness Analysis (HA) — identifying sources of low mood versus physiological fatigue; distinguishing medication-responsive symptoms from lifestyle-responsive ones.
  • Diet and lifestyle education — iodine sources, goitrogen awareness, sleep hygiene, adequate physical activity.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Causal Body)

Goal: Address the deepest layer of energy suppression; reconnect with inherent vitality and the joy of living that chronic illness may obscure.

  • Karma Yoga — engaging in purposeful action; counteracting withdrawal and lethargy by finding meaning in service and activity.
  • Devotional practices (bhajans, satsang) — group energy and positive emotional resonance counteract the isolation and low affect of hypothyroid states.
  • Deepening meditation — as treatment progresses and cognitive clarity returns, deepening samadhi-oriented practice allows the individual to access the bliss body where the disease has no foothold.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

Situation Guidance
Active Hashimoto's flare Gentle practice only; avoid strong inversions during acute inflammation
Cervical spondylosis Avoid Sarvangasana and strong neck flexion/extension; substitute Viparita Karani with wall support
Hypertension Sarvangasana requires caution if BP is elevated; check with treating physician
Severe myxoedema Medical stabilisation with levothyroxine must precede vigorous yoga; begin with gentle practices
Cardiovascular involvement (pericardial effusion, bradycardia) Cardiologist clearance required; avoid strong inversions and breath retentions
Pregnancy Levothyroxine requirements increase significantly; yoga to be adapted for obstetric context; no strong inversions in second/third trimester
Cooling pranayama Sitali, Sitkari, Chandra Anuloma Viloma contraindicated — would further suppress metabolism
Post-thyroidectomy Neck region is sensitive; avoid direct pressure or extreme neck movement; Sarvangasana may be contraindicated until full healing
