Menstrual Disorders

1. Definition & Classification

Menstrual disorders are physical or emotional conditions that interfere with the normal menstrual cycle, causing pain, abnormal bleeding patterns, delayed or absent menstruation, or significant premenstrual disturbance. Nearly every woman of reproductive age will experience one or more menstrual irregularities at some point in her life.

Classification:

Type Description
Dysmenorrhoea Painful menstruation — the most common menstrual disorder
↳ Primary dysmenorrhoea No underlying pathology; related to prostaglandin-mediated uterine contractions; typically onset 1–3 years after menarche
↳ Secondary dysmenorrhoea Due to an underlying cause (endometriosis, fibroids, PID, adenomyosis)
Amenorrhoea Absence of menstruation
↳ Primary amenorrhoea No menstruation by age 16
↳ Secondary amenorrhoea Cessation of previously established periods for ≥3 months
Menorrhagia Heavy or prolonged menstrual bleeding
Oligomenorrhoea Infrequent periods (cycle >35 days)
Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS) / PMDD Cyclical emotional and physical symptoms in the luteal phase; PMDD involves severe mood disruption

This page focuses primarily on dysmenorrhoea and amenorrhoea as the two most commonly addressed menstrual disorders in the PGDYT 104 curriculum, with general principles applicable to related conditions.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The female reproductive cycle:

The uterus is a hollow, pear-shaped organ in the pelvis. Its innermost layer, the endometrium, undergoes cyclical changes regulated by ovarian hormones. The normal menstrual cycle averages 28 days and is divided into three phases:

  1. Menstrual phase (Days 1–5): Endometrial lining sheds as oestrogen and progesterone levels fall; uterine contractions assist expulsion of menstrual blood.
  2. Proliferative/postmenstrual phase (Days 6–14): Rising oestrogen from developing ovarian follicles stimulates endometrial regrowth. Culminates in ovulation (LH surge ~Day 14).
  3. Secretory/premenstrual phase (Days 15–28): Corpus luteum produces progesterone; endometrium thickens in preparation for potential implantation. If no fertilisation occurs, corpus luteum regresses, hormone levels fall, and the cycle repeats.

Hormonal axis: Hypothalamus → GnRH → Anterior Pituitary → FSH & LH → Ovaries → Oestrogen & Progesterone. Disruption at any level can cause menstrual irregularities. The pituitary gland controls both ovulation and the menstrual cycle.

Prostaglandins and dysmenorrhoea: In primary dysmenorrhoea, elevated levels of prostaglandins (particularly PGF2α and PGE2) cause intense uterine muscle contractions, vasoconstriction and ischaemia, generating pain. Women with severe dysmenorrhoea have significantly higher prostaglandin levels in menstrual blood.

Amenorrhoea and the stress-HPA axis: Psychological and physical stress activates the HPA (hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal) axis, raising cortisol and CRH levels. Elevated CRH suppresses GnRH pulsatility, inhibiting LH/FSH secretion and thereby suppressing ovulation and menstruation. This is the mechanism underlying stress-related secondary amenorrhoea — of central relevance to yoga therapy.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Dysmenorrhoea

Causes:

  • Primary: Prostaglandin overproduction; narrow cervical os (particularly in nulliparous women); uterine hypercontractility
  • Secondary: Endometriosis, uterine fibroids, pelvic inflammatory disease, adenomyosis, IUD use, uterine polyps, ovarian cysts

Symptoms:

  • Crampy lower abdominal pain, typically beginning 1–2 days before or at onset of menstruation
  • Radiation to the lower back and inner thighs
  • Associated nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, headache, dizziness, fatigue
  • Secondary: pain may persist throughout the cycle and be unilateral

Amenorrhoea

Causes:

  • Primary: Turner's syndrome, genetic/anatomical abnormalities, low body weight, intense exercise
  • Secondary: Pregnancy, stress, rapid weight loss, excessive exercise, thyroid disorders, hyperprolactinaemia, PCOS, premature ovarian insufficiency, hypothalamic suppression
  • The "female athlete triad": energy deficiency + menstrual dysfunction + low bone density

Symptoms:

  • Absence of menstruation; may be accompanied by symptoms of the underlying cause
  • Infertility, hot flushes (in premature ovarian insufficiency), galactorrhoea (hyperprolactinaemia), symptoms of low oestrogen

Menorrhagia

  • Heavy, prolonged bleeding (soaking a pad/tampon hourly for several hours, or periods lasting >7 days)
  • Fatigue and iron-deficiency anaemia from blood loss
  • Hormonal imbalance, fibroids, polyps, coagulopathy, thyroid dysfunction as common causes

PMS / PMDD

  • Bloating, breast tenderness, headaches, mood swings, irritability, depression, anxiety in the luteal phase
  • Symptoms resolve within days of menstrual onset
  • PMDD: severe mood disruption meeting DSM criteria; significantly impairs functioning

4. Conventional Treatment

Dysmenorrhoea:

  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) — reduce prostaglandin synthesis; highly effective (64–100%)
  • Combined oral contraceptive pills — suppress ovulation, reduce endometrial prostaglandins; 90% effective
  • Heat therapy — topical heat relieves uterine muscle spasm
  • Secondary: treat underlying cause (laparoscopic excision of endometriosis, myomectomy for fibroids)

Amenorrhoea:

  • Treat underlying cause; weight restoration for hypothalamic amenorrhoea
  • Hormone replacement (combined OCP) for oestrogen deficiency and cycle regulation
  • Pulsatile GnRH therapy or gonadotrophins for fertility

Menorrhagia:

  • NSAIDs, tranexamic acid (anti-fibrinolytic), levonorgestrel IUS
  • Combined OCP; progestins
  • Surgery: endometrial ablation or hysterectomy for severe refractory cases

PMDD:

  • SSRIs (particularly continuous or luteal-phase dosing)
  • OCP (particularly drospirenone-containing)
  • CBT, aerobic exercise, calcium/magnesium/vitamin B6 supplementation

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale (Pancha Kosha)

Menstrual disorders, particularly dysmenorrhoea, amenorrhoea and PMS/PMDD, are significantly influenced by the psychoneuroendocrine axis — the interplay between psychological stress, HPA activation and HPG (hypothalamic–pituitary–gonadal) axis suppression. Yoga therapy addresses this connection comprehensively across all five koshas. The S-VYASA IAYT framework recognises menstrual disorders as examples of Adhija Vyadhi (psychosomatic disorders) in which mental agitation and pranic imbalance disturb the physical reproductive cycle.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Goal: Improve pelvic circulation, reduce uterine congestion, release pelvic floor and lower back tension, and address musculoskeletal components of menstrual pain.

  • Mariichyasana (Sage MarichI's Pose) — seated spinal twist that compresses and then releases the abdominal and pelvic viscera, improving circulation to the uterus and ovaries; aids liver and adrenal function relevant to hormonal metabolism. 1 minute each side.
  • Baddha Konasana (Bound Angle / Butterfly Pose) — opens the inner groin, hip adductors and pelvic floor; improves blood flow to reproductive organs; particularly beneficial for dysmenorrhoea. With butterfly flapping (20 counts) as a dynamic loosening component.
  • Malasana (Garland / Squat Pose) — deep squat that opens the pelvis, stretches the pelvic floor and lower back; relieves uterine congestion; supports the downward apana vayu movement that governs menstruation.
  • Pavanamuktasana series — systematic joint loosening including hip rotation, knee-ankle movements; counters lower back and pelvic stiffness; forward-backward bending, side bending, and pelvic twisting rounds as listed in the PGDYT 104 protocol.
  • Surya Namaskara — 3–5 rounds as warm-up; full-body activation improving pelvic circulation and hormonal balance; avoid during active heavy menstrual flow.
  • Sarvangasana and Viparitakarni — inversions that regulate pelvic blood flow; stimulate reproductive organs; beneficial in the inter-menstrual phase. Avoid during active menstruation — inverted practices conflict with the natural downward apana flow.
  • Naukasana / Navasana (Boat Pose) — strengthens core and pelvic floor musculature; improves abdominal tone relevant to uterine support.
  • Rocking and Rolling — as listed in PGDYT 104 protocol; releases lumbar spine tension, massages sacral nerves supplying the uterus.
  • Lumbar stretch exercises — 5 rounds; address the lower back component of dysmenorrhoea.
  • Straight leg raises, side leg raises, cycling — strengthen lower abdominal and hip musculature; improve pelvic circulation.
  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) — complete physical relaxation; reduces uterine muscle hypertonicity during pain phases.
  • Sattvic diet — iron-rich foods to address menorrhagia-related anaemia; avoid cold, heavy, processed foods that increase kapha and worsen pelvic congestion; warm, easily digestible foods during menstruation.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Goal: Regulate the apana vayu (the downward-moving pranic current that governs menstruation and elimination); balance the ida-pingala polarity that influences the HPG axis.

  • Nadi Suddhi / Nadi Shodhana (Alternate Nostril Breathing) — the primary pranayama for hormonal balance in menstrual disorders. Balances the left (Ida/lunar/Chandra) and right (Pingala/solar/Surya) pranic channels, directly correlating with oestrogen-progesterone balance in the yogic framework. 9 rounds. Particularly important for amenorrhoea and PMS/PMDD.
  • Surya Anuloma Viloma (Right-Nostril Breathing) — stimulates sympathetic tone and metabolic fire; useful in amenorrhoea with hypothalamic suppression to re-energise the HPG axis. 9 rounds.
  • Kapalabhati — abdominal pumping stimulates pelvic viscera and improves circulation to reproductive organs; 25–30 rounds. Avoid during active heavy menstruation.
  • Vibhagiya Pranayama (Sectional Breathing) — abdominal section (diaphragmatic breathing) directly massages and stimulates pelvic organs; thoracic section improves intercostal flexibility; 5 minutes.
  • PET (Pranayama with Extension and Traction) — specific S-VYASA breathing technique; supports thoraco-pelvic coordination.
  • AVOID during active menstruation: Kapalabhati, Bhastrika and Kumbhaka (breath retention) — these create abdominal pressure that may intensify menstrual cramping or disrupt natural menstrual flow.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental-Emotional Body)

Goal: Address the emotional and psychological components of menstrual disorders — anxiety, mood fluctuations (PMS/PMDD), the stress-HPA axis suppression of GnRH (amenorrhoea), and pain catastrophising in dysmenorrhoea.

  • Nadanusandhana — chanting and sound practices that calm the hypothalamic stress response; pelvic-abdominal resonance supports reproductive organ health.
  • Dhyana / OM Meditation — sustained meditative stillness reduces cortisol, calms HPA hyperactivation, and allows the HPG axis to normalise; 20 minutes.
  • MSRT (Mind Sound Resonance Technique) — extended relaxation with OM; reduces PMS-related irritability and mood disturbance; calms the speed-of-mind quality.
  • MIRT, MEMT — movement and emotional movement practices support cathartic release of suppressed emotions that may contribute to menstrual pathology.
  • Cyclic Meditation — structured alternation of activity and rest; particularly valuable for PMDD management.
  • Awareness of menstrual cycle phases — cultivating conscious attunement to the body's natural rhythms (menstrual, follicular, ovulatory, luteal) as a svadhyaya practice.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Intellect/Discriminative Body)

Goal: Develop understanding of the psychosomatic nature of menstrual disorders; correct cultural and personal misconceptions about menstruation; build self-regulation capacity.

  • Yogic counselling — the connection between stress, emotional suppression and menstrual cycle irregularity is a central teaching; helping women understand their cycles as communicating systems rather than problems.
  • Notional correction — addressing menstrual shame, fear, or negative cultural conditioning that compounds physical symptoms.
  • Satsang — positive community of women who share understanding; normalises the menstrual experience within a holistic framework.
  • VISAK — Values Identification and Self-Assessment through Knowledge; develops clarity about the relationship between lifestyle choices and hormonal health.
  • Happiness Analysis — identifying sources of chronic stress that are suppressing the reproductive axis.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Causal Body)

Goal: Access the deepest layer of Being where natural rhythms are undisturbed; reconnect with the inherent intelligence of the body's hormonal cycles.

  • Enjoying the silence — post-meditation stillness allows access to the anandamaya layer; the natural state beneath all agitation and pain.
  • Karma Yoga — engaging purposefully with life without the anxiety that drives HPA activation; the antidote to the overwork and chronic stress that suppress menstrual function.
  • Always being in Awareness — living in present-moment awareness naturally reduces the anticipatory anxiety (about pain, bleeding, premenstrual symptoms) that amplifies the manomaya and pranamaya agitation driving the cycle.
  • Realising the Self — at the deepest level, the yogic aspiration is to identify with the witness rather than the fluctuating phenomenology of the body-mind complex.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

Situation Guidance
Active menstrual flow (heavy) AVOID: strong inversions (Sarvangasana, Sirsasana), Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Kumbhaka; AVOID strong abdominal compression postures; Prefer gentle forward bends, DRT, Bhramari, Nadi Suddhi
Secondary dysmenorrhoea Identify and treat underlying cause (endometriosis, fibroids); yoga is adjunct, not definitive treatment
Primary amenorrhoea with anatomical cause Surgical evaluation required; yoga is supportive only
Hypothalamic amenorrhoea with very low BMI Weight restoration is primary; yoga should be non-vigorous and non-depleting; avoid dynamic practices
PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) Common cause of secondary amenorrhoea; yoga beneficial but requires specific PCOS protocol considerations
Endometriosis Avoid deep spinal twists and inversions during heavy pain phases; DRT and gentle forward folds preferred
Pregnancy Menstrual disorder context does not apply; transfer to dedicated pregnancy yoga protocol
Severe anaemia (from menorrhagia) Rest-dominant practice; no vigorous asana; medical iron replacement essential first
Acute pelvic inflammatory disease Rest completely; no abdominal/pelvic pressure; refer for antibiotic treatment
PMDD with severe depression Mental health co-management required; yoga as adjunct; no isolated yoga-only approach

Key research directions:

  • Yoga and prostaglandin reduction in primary dysmenorrhoea
  • Nadi Shodhana and HPG axis regulation in functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea
  • Mind-body intervention for PMS/PMDD — controlled trials with cortisol and LH/FSH measurement
  • Baddha Konasana and pelvic floor effects on menstrual pain
  • Cyclic Meditation and luteal-phase mood regulation
