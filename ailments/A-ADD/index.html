Addison's Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)

1. Definition & Classification

Addison's disease (primary adrenal insufficiency) is a rare but life-threatening endocrine disorder caused by destruction or dysfunction of the adrenal cortex, resulting in deficient production of all adrenal cortical hormones: cortisol (glucocorticoid), aldosterone (mineralocorticoid), and adrenal androgens. It was first described by Thomas Addison in 1855.

Distinction from secondary/tertiary adrenal insufficiency:

  • Primary (Addison's): Adrenal gland itself is damaged — ACTH is elevated (no negative feedback from cortisol)
  • Secondary: Pituitary ACTH deficiency (e.g., after long-term corticosteroid therapy, pituitary tumour) — no hyperpigmentation
  • Tertiary: Hypothalamic CRH deficiency

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Adrenal Gland Structure

The adrenal (suprarenal) glands sit atop each kidney. Each gland has two functionally distinct zones:

  • Adrenal cortex (80% of gland): Produces steroid hormones from cholesterol
    • Zona glomerulosa → Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid) — sodium retention, potassium excretion
    • Zona fasciculata → Cortisol (glucocorticoid) — stress response, glucose regulation, anti-inflammatory
    • Zona reticularis → Adrenal androgens (DHEA, androstenedione) — precursors to sex hormones
  • Adrenal medulla (20%): Produces adrenaline and noradrenaline (catecholamines)

Cortisol Physiology

Cortisol is essential for:

  • Glucose homeostasis — stimulates gluconeogenesis in the liver; counteracts insulin
  • Stress adaptation — the primary "stress hormone"; enables the body to respond to physiological and psychological stressors
  • Anti-inflammatory regulation — suppresses immune and inflammatory responses; excess impairs immunity; deficiency leaves inflammation unregulated
  • Cardiovascular function — maintains vascular tone and cardiac contractility; cortisol deficiency causes hypotension

HPA axis regulation: CRH (hypothalamus) → ACTH (pituitary) → cortisol (adrenal cortex). Cortisol normally provides negative feedback. In Addison's, the cortex cannot respond → ACTH rises dramatically.

Aldosterone Physiology

Aldosterone acts on the distal nephron to reabsorb sodium (retaining water and volume) and excrete potassium. Deficiency → sodium and volume loss → hypotension, hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes

  • Autoimmune adrenalitis (70% in developed countries) — autoreactive lymphocytes destroy the adrenal cortex; often associated with autoimmune polyglandular syndrome (type 1: with hypoparathyroidism, candidiasis; type 2: with thyroid disease, type 1 diabetes)
  • Tuberculosis (most common globally) — TB was the leading cause historically; remains common in high-prevalence countries
  • Bilateral adrenal haemorrhage — Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (meningococcal sepsis), anticoagulation
  • Adrenal metastases — bilateral adrenal involvement in lung, breast, colon, and renal carcinomas
  • Infections — histoplasmosis, CMV (AIDS), fungal infections

Symptoms

Chronic (insidious onset):

  • Fatigue and weakness (often profound — "tired all the time")
  • Weight loss, anorexia, nausea, abdominal pain
  • Hyperpigmentation — ACTH elevation cross-reacts with melanocortin receptors; skin darkens in sun-exposed areas, pressure points, mucous membranes, palmar creases (pathognomonic sign of primary adrenal insufficiency)
  • Postural hypotension (dizziness on standing)
  • Hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, mild hypoglycaemia
  • Salt craving (physiological drive to replace sodium loss)
  • Depression, irritability, fatigue

Adrenal crisis (Addisonian crisis — medical emergency):

  • Precipitated by any physiological stressor (infection, surgery, emotional trauma, vomiting, missed medication)
  • Profound hypotension, vomiting, abdominal pain, confusion, coma
  • Requires immediate IV hydrocortisone and fluid replacement — life-threatening without treatment

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Glucocorticoid replacement: Hydrocortisone 15–25 mg/day in divided doses (mimicking diurnal cortisol rhythm — larger dose in morning); or prednisolone
  • Mineralocorticoid replacement: Fludrocortisone (aldosterone analogue) for sodium/potassium balance and blood pressure
  • Adrenal androgen replacement: DHEA in women with fatigue and sexual dysfunction
  • Sick-day rules: Patients must double or triple their hydrocortisone dose during intercurrent illness, surgery, or significant stress — failure to do so risks adrenal crisis
  • Emergency hydrocortisone injections: Patients carry IM hydrocortisone for self-injection if unable to take oral medication during illness
  • Medical alert bracelet: Essential; enables emergency personnel to administer hydrocortisone

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for Addison's disease is gentle, supportive, and energy-conserving. The primary challenge is that the very mechanisms yoga therapy normally harnesses (stress reduction, autonomic regulation, cortisol modulation) are disrupted by the fundamental absence of cortisol production. The approach must be carefully calibrated to avoid both physiological stress (which cannot be adequately cortisol-mediated) and immobility-related deconditioning.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

The key principle: no vigorous exertion. Addison's patients cannot mount an adequate cortisol response to exercise stress; vigorous practice risks precipitating hypoglycaemia, hypotension, and fatigue disproportionate to the effort.

  • Gentle loosening exercises (Sithilikarana Vyayama) — low-intensity joint mobilisation; seated or slow standing; maintains mobility without metabolic demand
  • Gentle floor asanas — Pavanamuktasana series, Balasana, Savasana variations; no standing sequences requiring significant muscular effort initially
  • Graduated progression: As hormone replacement stabilises, and with physician clearance, gentle standing asanas can be introduced; Surya Namaskara at slowest pace only when well-managed on replacement therapy
  • Timing: Practise after morning hydrocortisone dose has had time to act (30–60 minutes after taking medication); not in fasting states (hypoglycaemia risk)

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Diaphragmatic breathing (primary) — calming, accessible, requires no physical effort; activates the parasympathetic system without inducing the physiological stress response
  • Nadi Suddhi (without retention) — gentle autonomic balancing; reduces anxiety and postural hypotension-induced hyperarousal without sympathetic stimulation
  • Bhramari — vagal activation; gentle calming for the fatigue-related anxiety common in Addison's

Avoid: Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, or any vigorous/forceful breathing that constitutes physiological stress; Kumbhaka (breath retention) — adds to physiological load.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

Addison's disease carries significant psychological burden: the constant vigilance required to prevent crisis (monitoring symptoms, adjusting medication, carrying emergency injection kits, explaining the condition to others) creates chronic background anxiety. The diagnosis itself is life-altering.

  • DRT (primary) — provides the experience of profound rest within a safe, low-stimulation environment; directly reduces the anxiety burden
  • Yoga Nidra — the deepest accessible rest state; helps address the exhaustion that is the defining symptom of adrenal insufficiency
  • Cyclic Meditation (gentle version) — provides structured rest with minimal physical demand

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Sick-day management education — understanding when and how to increase hydrocortisone dose is literally lifesaving; yoga therapy sessions provide an opportunity to reinforce this knowledge in a calm, supportive context
  • Energy management — pacing strategies; identifying energy patterns within the day and matching activity to cortisol peaks (morning dose)
  • Stress identification and reduction — yoga philosophy's tools for reducing unnecessary psychological stress (Vairagya, Santosha) directly reduce the physiological stress burden that the adrenal-insufficient patient is poorly equipped to handle

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Addison's disease, like many chronic conditions requiring daily medication for survival, confronts patients with existential questions of vulnerability, mortality, and dependence. The yogic framework of equanimity — accepting what is, within what is — provides genuine spiritual support.

  • Karma Yoga — finding meaning in contributing within physical limitations; gentle service appropriate to current capacity
  • Devotional practices — passive reception of devotional music; participation in group settings when health permits, providing community and belonging

Frequency: Daily gentle practice recommended; adjust intensity to energy levels on any given day; never push through unusual fatigue (may signal early adrenal crisis).

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Vigorous practice of any kind — contraindicated without medical clearance and stable hormone replacement
  • Fasting practice — contraindicated; hypoglycaemia risk
  • Hot yoga / hot environments — increase fluid and electrolyte loss; particularly dangerous given aldosterone deficiency
  • Breath retention (Kumbhaka) — adds physiological stress without compensatory cortisol
  • Any intercurrent illness — pause vigorous yoga practice; follow sick-day rules; contact physician
  • Emergency awareness: All yoga therapists working with Addison's patients must know the signs of adrenal crisis and the patient's emergency medication location
