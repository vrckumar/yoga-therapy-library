Cerebrovascular Accident (Stroke)

1. Definition & Classification

A cerebrovascular accident (CVA), or stroke, is a neurological emergency characterised by sudden onset of focal neurological deficit resulting from interruption of cerebral blood supply. Neurons without blood supply die within minutes; rapid recognition and treatment is essential to salvage penumbral tissue.

FAST acronym: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency services.

Classification:

Ischaemic stroke (80% of cases):

  • Thrombotic: Atherosclerotic plaque in a cerebral or carotid artery ruptures, triggering in-situ thrombosis
  • Embolic: Thrombus (usually from atrial fibrillation — left atrial appendage; or from carotid atherosclerosis) travels to cerebral arteries and lodges distally
  • Lacunar: Small vessel disease in perforating arteries → small deep infarcts in basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule, pons

Haemorrhagic stroke (20% of cases):

  • Intracerebral haemorrhage: Spontaneous rupture of a deep perforating artery, most commonly from uncontrolled hypertension; into basal ganglia, thalamus, cerebellum, pons
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage: Rupture of a saccular (berry) aneurysm at an arterial bifurcation → "thunderclap headache" (worst headache of life)

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Cerebrovascular Anatomy

The brain's blood supply arrives via two paired systems:

  • Internal carotid arteries → Middle cerebral arteries (MCA — supplies motor cortex, sensory cortex, Broca's area, temporal lobe) and Anterior cerebral arteries (ACA — supplies frontal lobes, leg motor/sensory cortex)
  • Vertebrobasilar system → Posterior cerebral arteries (PCA — occipital lobe, visual cortex, thalamus), basilar artery, cerebellar arteries

These systems anastomose at the Circle of Willis at the base of the brain — providing collateral supply if one vessel is compromised.

Ischaemic Cascade

Within seconds of arterial occlusion:

  1. ATP depletion → failure of Na+/K+ pump → cellular oedema
  2. Release of glutamate (excitotoxicity) → excessive Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial damage → apoptosis
  3. Penumbra: Surrounding the infarcted core, a larger zone of ischaemic but potentially salvageable neurons exists — these cells are electrically silent but structurally intact; reperfusion within hours (thrombolysis or thrombectomy) can rescue them

Neurological Deficits by Vessel Territory

Vessel Territory Deficit
MCA (dominant hemisphere, left) Motor cortex, Broca's/Wernicke's area Contralateral hemiplegia (arm > leg), aphasia
MCA (non-dominant hemisphere, right) Motor cortex, parietal lobe Contralateral hemiplegia, hemispatial neglect
ACA Frontal lobe, leg motor cortex Leg > arm weakness, personality/behaviour change
PCA Occipital cortex, thalamus Contralateral hemianopia, thalamic pain, alexia
PICA (posterior inferior cerebellar) Lateral medulla, cerebellum Wallenberg syndrome (vertigo, dysphagia, ataxia, Horner's)

Neuroplasticity and Recovery

Recovery after stroke is driven by:

  • Peri-infarct plasticity: Surviving neurons adjacent to the infarct zone sprout new synaptic connections; cortical representations reorganise
  • Diaschisis recovery: Remote brain areas temporarily suppressed by the stroke (due to deafferentation) gradually recover function
  • Compensatory strategies: Contralateral hemisphere assumes some functions of the damaged hemisphere (limited by the degree of inter-hemispheric connectivity)

The degree of recovery correlates with the intensity and specificity of rehabilitation practice — yoga therapy contributes directly to this neuroplastic recovery.

Symptoms by Stage

Acute: Sudden onset focal deficit (face, arm, leg weakness; speech difficulty; vision loss; ataxia; severe headache)

Subacute (days to weeks): Post-stroke depression (30% prevalence, doubles mortality risk); spasticity; shoulder subluxation; dysphagia; urinary incontinence; fatigue; emotional lability

Chronic: Variable residual deficit; recurrence risk (10%/year without secondary prevention); post-stroke dementia; falls; social isolation

4. Conventional Treatment

Acute (first hours):

  • IV thrombolysis with alteplase (within 4.5 hours of symptom onset, ischaemic stroke)
  • Mechanical thrombectomy (within 24 hours for large vessel occlusion)
  • ICU monitoring, BP management, glucose control, temperature management

Secondary prevention:

  • Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin, clopidogrel) — ischaemic stroke
  • Anticoagulation (warfarin, DOAC) — cardioembolic (AF-related) stroke
  • BP control, statins, diabetes management
  • Atrial fibrillation management

Rehabilitation:

  • Physiotherapy: Motor retraining (Bobath approach, task-specific practice, constraint-induced movement therapy)
  • Speech and language therapy: Aphasia, dysphagia
  • Occupational therapy: ADL retraining
  • Neuropsychology: Cognitive and emotional sequelae
  • Yoga therapy: Complementary across all domains

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for stroke rehabilitation addresses the motor, cognitive, emotional, and existential dimensions of recovery, using neuroplasticity principles to guide the intensity and specificity of practice.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Chair yoga — foundation of all stroke yoga therapy: Post-stroke patients frequently cannot safely practise floor-based or standing postures independently. All practices initially conducted from a chair, with adaptations for the affected limb(s).

Affected side ROM (Range of Motion) practice:

  • Passive and active-assisted movements of the affected arm and leg — maintains joint integrity, prevents subluxation and contracture, stimulates sensory-motor cortex even in severely paretic limbs
  • Caregiver-assisted passive mobilisation of the affected shoulder (subluxation prevention)
  • Slow, careful weight-shifting in standing (with chair support) — begins to re-load the affected leg

Bilateral movement practice — mirror neuron activation: Bilateral symmetrical movements (both arms moving simultaneously and identically) activate mirror neuron circuits and the unaffected motor cortex's projections to the affected side — a key neuroplasticity mechanism in motor recovery. Specific practices:

  • Bilateral Sakti Vikasaka arm movements (both arms together)
  • Modified Garudasana arm movements (both arms crossing midline simultaneously)
  • Mirrored hand and wrist exercises: the patient watches the unaffected hand and imagines/attempts to replicate with the affected hand

Progressive balance training: Supported Vrikshasana → unsupported → tandem standing → slow walking — graded precisely to current balance capacity.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Vibhagiya Svasana — restoration of full breathing pattern; post-stroke patients often develop restricted, upper-chest breathing; restoration of abdominal breathing reduces secondary pulmonary complications (particularly important in dysphagic patients with aspiration risk)
  • Bhramari and OM chanting — SPEECH REHABILITATION:
    • Bhramari produces sustained exhalation with voicing — directly exercises the vocal apparatus; accessible even in moderate aphasia (non-speech sound production is often preserved when propositional speech is impaired); the vibration in the facial structures provides sensory feedback relevant to articulatory rehabilitation
    • OM chanting engages preserved right-hemisphere melodic-prosodic processing even when left-hemisphere language areas are damaged — chanting uses different neural pathways than speech (melodic intonation therapy principle)
    • Group chanting provides graduated, socially reinforced vocal practice

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

Post-stroke depression is not merely a psychological reaction — it has a neurobiological component (damage to monoaminergic pathways particularly in frontal lobe strokes). Untreated, it significantly impairs rehabilitation engagement and outcomes.

  • DRT — provides the deeply rest state that overloaded, fatigued stroke survivors urgently need; reduces catastrophising and the fear of recurrence; gentle progressive body relaxation accessible in chair
  • MIRT (Mind-body Integration and Relaxation Training) — specifically addresses the disrupted body schema (the internal neurological map of the body that is disrupted by stroke — body neglect, body image changes, phantom sensations); systematically rebuilds an integrated, accepting body awareness
  • Yoga Nidra — deep rest for the tired brain; BDNF production during rest supports neuroplasticity; emotional regulation for lability and post-stroke anxiety

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Understanding neuroplasticity — explaining the brain's capacity for recovery gives hope and provides the intellectual rationale for intensive, consistent rehabilitation practice
  • Secondary prevention education — stroke survivors have a 10%/year recurrence risk; understanding and consistently managing risk factors (BP monitoring, medication adherence, diet, smoking cessation) is life-saving
  • Accepting partial recovery — the yoga philosophical framework of equanimity and adaptation supports the difficult work of building a good life with permanent neurological deficit

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Stroke is often experienced as a catastrophic disruption of identity — the person who could play tennis, drive a car, articulate complex thoughts, now cannot. The Anandamaya dimension addresses the deepest level of this loss:

  • Devotional music and group practice reconnects the stroke survivor to the community and to a sense of belonging that does not depend on physical or cognitive performance
  • The yogic framework of the Atman (pure awareness, undisturbed by physical or neurological change) provides a genuine spiritual resource for patients confronting permanent disability
  • Karma Yoga — finding small, meaningful acts of contribution within current capacity — rebuilds the sense of purpose and efficacy that stroke can destroy

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Acute phase (first 2 weeks): No active yoga; rest is primary; passive nursing positioning to prevent contracture and pressure injury
  • Haemorrhagic stroke: Later return to yoga therapy than ischaemic (typically 4–6 weeks minimum post-event, with BP well controlled)
  • Epilepsy (post-stroke seizures): See epilepsy protocol; Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, and retention contraindicated
  • Dysphagia: Careful posture management; head position during Bhramari/chanting; aspiration risk with head dropped forward
  • Cardiac monitoring: Atrial fibrillation (common cause of stroke); monitor heart rate; avoid vigorous exertion in uncontrolled AF
  • Falls risk: Non-negotiable support during all balance practice; non-slip mat; appropriate footwear
