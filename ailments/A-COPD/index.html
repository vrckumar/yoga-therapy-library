COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease)

1. Definition & Classification

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a common, preventable, and treatable disease characterised by persistent airflow limitation that is usually progressive and associated with chronic inflammation of the airways and lung tissue. COPD encompasses two pathological entities that frequently coexist:

  • Chronic Bronchitis: Defined clinically as cough with productive sputum for at least 3 months per year for 2 consecutive years. Caused by chronic airway inflammation → goblet cell hyperplasia → excess mucus production → airway narrowing and plugging.
  • Emphysema: Defined pathologically as permanent, abnormal enlargement of the air spaces distal to the terminal bronchioles, with destruction of alveolar walls. Loss of alveoli → reduced gas exchange surface → reduced elastic recoil → air trapping.

Both cause airflow obstruction — measured by spirometry as reduced FEV₁/FVC ratio (<0.70) — that is not fully reversible with bronchodilators (distinguishing COPD from asthma).

In the yogic framework, COPD sits at the Annamaya-Pranamaya Kosha interface: decades of lifestyle insult (primarily tobacco smoke) destroying the lung's structural integrity, compounded by chronic Pranamaya disruption (disordered breathing) and Manomaya burden (anxiety, depression, social isolation — which are extremely common in COPD).

IV (Very Severe) <30% Chronic respiratory failure; oxygen dependent

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Normal Respiratory Mechanics

Air enters through the nose/mouth → larynx → trachea → bronchi (right and left) → bronchioles → alveoli. The bronchial tree divides 23 times before reaching the alveoli — the functional gas exchange units. Each alveolus is surrounded by a network of pulmonary capillaries; oxygen diffuses from alveolus to blood, CO₂ in the reverse direction.

Elastic recoil is the key physical property of the lung: alveolar walls contain elastin, which stores energy during inhalation and releases it during exhalation — so normal exhalation is passive and effortless. This property is destroyed in emphysema.

The respiratory muscles:

  • Diaphragm: Primary inspiratory muscle — accounts for 60–70% of normal breathing effort. A dome-shaped muscle at the base of the chest; flattens on contraction, drawing air in.
  • Intercostal muscles: Expand the rib cage (intercostal, 12 pairs)
  • Accessory muscles (scalenes, sternomastoid, trapezii): Recruited only in distress

In normal breathing: diaphragm works on inhalation; passive recoil provides exhalation. In COPD: loss of elastic recoil → air trapping → hyperinflation → diaphragm is pushed down and flattened (mechanical disadvantage) → breathing becomes inefficient → accessory muscles recruited even at rest.

Pathophysiology of COPD

In chronic bronchitis:

  • Chronic irritant exposure (tobacco smoke, dust, pollution) → mucosal inflammation → goblet cell hyperplasia → excess mucus → airway narrowing + bacterial colonisation → recurrent exacerbations → progressive fibrosis

In emphysema:

  • Tobacco carcinogens activate macrophages and neutrophils → release proteases (elastase) → destroy alveolar walls → alveolar coalescence into enlarged, ineffective air spaces → loss of gas exchange surface + loss of elastic recoil → air trapping → barrel chest (hyperinflation)

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes

Factor Contribution
Cigarette smoking Primary cause (90%+ of COPD in developed countries)
Indoor air pollution Biomass fuel burning (primary cause in India and developing world)
Occupational dust/fumes Coal dust, silica, cadmium
Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency Genetic — emphysema in non-smokers
Recurrent childhood respiratory infections Impairs lung development

Symptoms

  • Dyspnoea (breathlessness): Progressive; initially on exertion, eventually at rest
  • Chronic cough: Productive; worse mornings; "smoker's cough"
  • Purulent sputum: Increased in volume during exacerbations
  • Wheeze: Expiratory (from turbulent flow through narrowed airways)
  • Reduced exercise tolerance: Progressive restriction of daily activities
  • Barrel chest: Hyperinflation — visible chest wall change
  • Pursed-lip breathing: Instinctively adopted by COPD patients to prevent airway collapse during exhalation (creates back-pressure PEEP)
  • Cyanosis: Bluish discoloration (severe hypoxaemia)
  • Cor pulmonale: Right heart failure from chronic pulmonary hypertension

Exacerbations: Acute worsening of symptoms (increased breathlessness, sputum, and cough) often triggered by respiratory infections. Each exacerbation accelerates disease progression and increases mortality risk.

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

  • Spirometry: FEV₁/FVC <0.70 post-bronchodilator (confirms obstruction); FEV₁ % predicted (severity)
  • Chest X-ray: Hyperinflation; flattened diaphragm; bullae
  • CT thorax: Extent of emphysema; airway changes
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG): Hypoxaemia; hypercapnia (Type 2 respiratory failure)

Medical Management

Category Drugs Mechanism
Short-acting bronchodilators (SABA/SAMA) Salbutamol, Ipratropium Immediate symptom relief during exacerbations
Long-acting bronchodilators (LABA/LAMA) Salmeterol, Tiotropium Maintain bronchodilation; reduce exacerbations
Inhaled corticosteroids Fluticasone, Budesonide Reduce airway inflammation (in frequent exacerbators)
Methylxanthines Theophylline Bronchodilator + respiratory muscle stimulant
Antibiotics Amoxicillin, Azithromycin Treat infective exacerbations
Oxygen therapy Long-term oxygen (LTOT) For Stage IV with PaO₂ <55 mmHg — only treatment to extend survival
Pulmonary rehabilitation Exercise, education, psychology Established as reducing exacerbations and improving QoL

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

COPD presents a unique challenge: the structural damage at the Annamaya Kosha is largely irreversible (destroyed alveoli do not regenerate). Yoga therapy therefore focuses on:

  1. Maximising the function of remaining healthy lung tissue
  2. Training respiratory muscles (diaphragm, intercostals) to work more efficiently
  3. Reducing the anxiety cycle (dyspnoea → anxiety → increased respiratory rate → worsened dyspnoea) through Manomaya intervention
  4. Improving exercise tolerance and quality of life

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Train respiratory muscles; improve chest wall mobility; reduce hyperinflation; enhance mucus clearance.

  • Vibhagiya Svasana (Sectional Breathing — all three sections): The primary physical intervention for COPD.

    • Abdominal (diaphragmatic) section: Retrains the diaphragm — the muscle most compromised by COPD's hyperinflation. Teaching conscious diaphragmatic excursion is equivalent to diaphragmatic re-education physiotherapy.
    • Thoracic (intercostal) section: Improves intercostal muscle function and chest wall mobility.
    • Clavicular section: Mobilises the upper thorax and engages accessory muscles when needed.
    • Full-section integration: Complete breathing that sequentially engages all three sections — the most efficient breathing pattern for COPD patients.

  • Modified Kapalabhati: In mild COPD (GOLD I–II), gentle Kapalabhati — emphasising the active exhalation rather than forceful inhalation — can strengthen the expiratory muscles, promote mucus clearance, and prevent alveolar collapse. Must be gentle; avoid vigorous practice in moderate-severe COPD.

  • Pursed-lip breathing equivalent (Ujjayi Pranayama): Creating slight upper airway resistance on exhalation — as COPD patients instinctively do with pursed lips — prevents premature airway collapse (dynamic hyperinflation) and slows respiratory rate. Ujjayi provides the same physiological benefit (expiratory PEEP) within a yogic framework.

  • Chest opening postures:

    • Ustrasana, Bhujangasana, Matsyasana — open the anterior chest wall, counteracting the forward-flexed, barrel-chested posture of hyperinflation
    • Supported Setu Bandhasana — opens the chest while the spine is extended

  • General loosening exercises: Shoulder and thoracic mobility; reduce the tight, rigid chest wall of hyperinflation.

  • Exercise rehabilitation (within dyspnoea limits): Pavanamuktasana series provides graduated exercise that trains peripheral muscle efficiency — reducing oxygen demand per unit of physical work.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Develop conscious mastery of the breath; slow the respiratory rate; retrain diaphragmatic breathing; reduce the work of breathing.

  • Diaphragmatic breathing (primary focus): The flattened diaphragm of COPD needs systematic rehabilitation. Supine diaphragmatic breathing with a light hand on the abdomen for biofeedback is the starting point.
  • Extended exhalation (1:2 ratio): Slowing and extending exhalation reduces dynamic hyperinflation (air trapping), prevents premature airway collapse, and activates the vagal brake — reducing the anxiety that compounds COPD breathlessness.
  • Nadi Suddhi (without Kumbhaka): Balances ANS; reduces anxiety; slows respiratory rate over time.
  • Ujjayi (Victorious Breath): Gentle partial glottic closure on exhalation creates expiratory positive airway pressure — directly replaces the pursed-lip breathing COPD patients intuitively adopt.
  • Bhramari (gentle): Slows respiratory rate; vagal stimulation; reduces breathlessness anxiety.
  • AVOID: Kapalabhati in moderate-severe COPD; Bhastrika; Suryabhedana; any pranayama with Kumbhaka (breath retention) — air trapping risk.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Break the dyspnoea-anxiety-worsened dyspnoea cycle; address depression and isolation (70%+ of COPD patients have clinically significant anxiety or depression).

  • DRT: Full-body relaxation; reduces accessory muscle tension that contributes to the work of breathing.
  • Cyclic Meditation (modified for COPD): Adapted for patients who cannot lie flat for extended periods — semi-recumbent position acceptable.
  • OM Meditation: Breathing awareness and mental calming; the exhalatory OM chant trains extended, conscious exhalation.
  • Yoga Nidra (semi-recumbent): Deep rest; anxiety reduction; improved sleep quality.
  • Devotional sessions: Social engagement reduces the profound isolation of advanced COPD; bhajans provide breath training in disguise (singing requires controlled exhalation).

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Smoking cessation (primary); understanding of COPD self-management.

Notional correction around smoking: the yogic understanding that the body is the temple of consciousness (Annamaya Kosha as the vehicle of the divine journey) provides philosophical motivation for cessation that transcends the typical fear-based approach. Self-management education — recognising exacerbation warning signs, correct inhaler technique, when to seek help — is the Vijnanamaya layer of COPD management.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Sustain motivation for daily breathing practice through connection with the deeper significance of breath.

Prana is not merely air — it is the vital force. The Upanishadic teaching that prana is the link between the body and the higher self gives the act of conscious breathing profound spiritual significance. The daily Vibhagiya Svasana practice becomes not just respiratory rehabilitation but a ritual of reconnection with life force itself.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Kapalabhati and Bhastrika: Avoid in moderate-severe COPD (GOLD III–IV) — risk of dynamic hyperinflation and hypoxic episodes.
  • Kumbhaka (breath retention): Absolutely contraindicated — worsens air trapping.
  • Acute COPD exacerbation: Yoga therapy deferred until patient is medically stable. Only DRT and breathing awareness are acceptable.
  • Hypoxaemia (SpO₂ < 88%): Monitor oxygen saturation during practice with pulse oximetry. Stop and administer supplemental oxygen if SpO₂ falls.
  • Pursed-lip breathing: If a patient instinctively uses pursed-lip breathing — do not correct it; this is a physiologically beneficial compensatory mechanism.
  • Supine lying in severe COPD: Dyspnoea worsens supine — use semi-recumbent (30–45°) position for all supine practices.
  • Smoking: Active smoking significantly impairs yoga therapy benefits and accelerates disease progression. Smoking cessation counselling is an essential component.
