Multiple Sclerosis

1. Definition & Classification

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic, immune-mediated, demyelinating disease of the central nervous system characterised by episodes of inflammation causing demyelination and axonal damage in the brain and spinal cord, resulting in a wide spectrum of neurological symptoms.

Clinical phenotypes:

  • Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS — 85% at onset): Episodes of acute neurological dysfunction (relapses) lasting days to weeks, followed by partial or complete recovery (remission); the most common form; between relapses, no disease progression
  • Secondary Progressive MS (SPMS): After a period of RRMS, most patients develop gradual disability progression between relapses; approximately 65% of RRMS patients convert to SPMS within 15 years
  • Primary Progressive MS (PPMS — 15%): Gradual disability accumulation from onset, without distinct relapses; treated with ocrelizumab (anti-CD20)
  • Progressive Relapsing MS (PRMS): Rare; progressive from onset with occasional relapses

EDSS (Expanded Disability Status Scale): 0–10; measures disability; scores >6.5 require bilateral ambulation aids.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Demyelination — The Pathological Mechanism

Myelin, produced by oligodendrocytes in the CNS, wraps axons in insulating layers that enable rapid saltatory (node-to-node) conduction of electrical impulses at speeds of 50–100 m/sec. In MS:

  1. Immune-mediated attack: Autoreactive T lymphocytes (CD4+ Th1 and Th17 cells) breach the blood-brain barrier, triggered by molecular mimicry (viral antigens resembling myelin basic protein, proteolipid protein, or myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein)
  2. Localised inflammation: B cells produce demyelinating antibodies; macrophages and activated microglia phagocytose myelin sheaths
  3. Demyelination: Loss of myelin sheath slows or blocks axonal conduction → neurological symptoms appear
  4. Lesions (plaques): Areas of demyelination are visible on MRI as hyperintense lesions (T2/FLAIR) — in white matter of periventricular regions, corpus callosum, brainstem, cerebellum, spinal cord, and optic nerves
  5. Repair: Partial remyelination by surviving oligodendrocyte precursors produces "shadow plaques" and clinical remission; incomplete repair leaves residual deficits
  6. Axonal degeneration: Over time, repeated demyelination-remyelination cycles fail to protect axons → irreversible axonal loss → progressive disability

Uhthoff's Phenomenon — Heat Sensitivity

A characteristic feature of MS: heat worsens neurological symptoms. The mechanism: even partially remyelinated axons conduct adequately at normal temperature but fail when temperature rises slightly (as little as 0.5°C):

  • Elevated temperature → reduced safety factor of conduction → temporary conduction block in already-compromised axons → symptoms worsen
  • Recovery occurs as temperature normalises (pseudoexacerbation)
  • This is the single most important practical consideration for yoga therapy in MS: heat must be avoided

MS Fatigue

MS-related fatigue is the most common and disabling symptom (80–95% of MS patients). It is qualitatively different from normal tiredness:

  • Comes on unpredictably, disproportionate to activity
  • Not relieved by rest alone
  • Worsened by heat, infection, and overexertion
  • Mechanism: combination of axonal conduction inefficiency (requiring more energy for same neural output), inflammatory cytokine effects on the CNS, and secondary fatigue from disrupted sleep and depression

Common Symptoms

  • Optic neuritis — common first presentation; unilateral painful vision loss; recovers partially; MRI shows optic nerve lesion
  • Motor: Weakness, spasticity (corticospinal tract involvement)
  • Sensory: Paraesthesia, numbness, Lhermitte's sign (electrical sensation down spine on neck flexion — posterior column involvement)
  • Cerebellum: Ataxia, tremor (cerebellar tremor — action tremor, worsens with movement), dysarthria
  • Bladder/bowel: Urgency, frequency, incontinence, hesitancy
  • Fatigue — most prevalent; most disabling
  • Cognitive: Slowed processing speed, memory, attention; prevalent even in early RRMS
  • Pain: Trigeminal neuralgia, central neuropathic pain, musculoskeletal pain (from spasticity, gait abnormalities)
  • Depression — 50% lifetime prevalence

4. Conventional Treatment

Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs):

  • First-line (moderate efficacy): Interferon-beta (IFN-β), glatiramer acetate, dimethyl fumarate, teriflunomide
  • High-efficacy: Natalizumab (anti-VLA-4, prevents lymphocyte trafficking), ocrelizumab (anti-CD20, B cell depletion), alemtuzumab, cladribine; greater efficacy but more serious side effects
  • Oral: Siponimod (SPMS)
  • Relapse management: IV methylprednisolone (speeds recovery, does not affect long-term disability)

Symptomatic management:

  • Spasticity: Baclofen, tizanidine; physiotherapy
  • Fatigue: Amantadine, modafinil; pacing strategies; exercise
  • Bladder: Oxybutynin (overactive), self-catheterisation (retention)
  • Pain: Gabapentin, pregabalin, amitriptyline
  • Depression: SSRIs; psychological support

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for MS is significantly shaped by two overriding considerations: heat avoidance (Uhthoff's phenomenon) and fatigue management (session length and intensity must be carefully calibrated). Within these constraints, yoga therapy offers valuable contributions across all five koshas.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Short sessions — fatigue management: Sessions should be 20–30 minutes initially; extended only as tolerance is established. The MS patient must be taught to stop before reaching the point of fatigue — "resting while you can still rest" rather than exercising to exhaustion.

  • Chair yoga (primary) — essential for patients with ataxia, spasticity, or significant lower limb weakness; eliminates fall risk; preserves therapeutic benefit
  • Gentle mobility exercises — joints range-of-motion to counteract spasticity and disuse contracture; gentle slow stretches rather than deep sustained stretches
  • Balance practice (supported) — Vrikshasana with wall; proprioceptive training addresses the cerebellar ataxia of MS; extremely gradual progression
  • Pacing principle — teach patients to alternate activity with brief rest periods within the session (active 3 minutes → rest 1 minute → repeat); mirrors the boom-bust cycle avoidance central to MS fatigue management

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Cooling pranayama — HIGHEST PRIORITY:

  • Sitali pranayama (primary): Inhalation through curled tongue (or through clenched teeth — Sitkari, for those unable to curl tongue); the incoming air is cooled by evaporation on the moist tongue surface; slight internal cooling effect; psychological cooling effect significant; specifically recommended for MS due to Uhthoff's concern
  • Sitkari: As above — tongue between teeth; produces similar cooling and calming effect
  • Nadi Suddhi — ANS balance; spasticity has an autonomic component (sympathetic hyperactivity increases spasticity); parasympathetic activation through Nadi Suddhi helps manage spasticity
  • Bhramari — calming; addresses MS depression and anxiety; gentle exhalation is cooling in character

Avoid: Hot, stimulating pranayama; Kapalabhati and Bhastrika generate heat internally; in MS patients sensitive to Uhthoff's, these practices could transiently worsen symptoms.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

MS depression (50% lifetime prevalence) is multifactorial: direct neurobiological effect of MS lesions on mood-regulating circuits, psychosocial response to chronic progressive disability, and medication effects (interferon-beta is particularly associated with depression).

  • DRT — regular daily practice; most accessible mental-layer intervention; brief (10 minutes is sufficient for initial stages)
  • Yoga Nidra — addresses the non-restorative sleep common in MS; the experience of deep rest demonstrates to the fatigued patient that genuine restoration is possible without activity
  • Cyclic Meditation (modified) — shorter, cooler, lower-intensity active phases; the rest-restoration principle is preserved

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Energy management (pacing): The most important lifestyle skill for MS fatigue management; understanding that energy is finite and variable, that boom-bust cycles worsen fatigue, and that consistent pacing produces better outcomes than either overexertion or excessive rest
  • Heat management: Practical strategies — cool shower before practice, cool room, cooling vest, cold drink during session, practice in early morning (before daily heat accumulation)
  • Disease literacy: Understanding MS phenotype, DMT mechanism, relapse recognition and reporting — yoga therapy sessions reinforce this knowledge in a supportive environment
  • Accepting uncertainty: MS is characterised by fundamental unpredictability (unknown when relapses will occur, unknown rate of progression); yoga philosophy's tools for working with uncertainty (Vairagya, Santosha) are directly applicable

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • Group practice — MS patients who practise in groups report significantly better quality of life than those practising alone; the community dimension addresses the social withdrawal that MS fatigue and disability often produce
  • Devotional music (passive) — bhajan listening or very gentle vocal participation provides positive affect without the exertion risk of active asana practice; appropriate even during relapses
  • Karma Yoga — contributing to the MS yoga group (helping set up chairs, welcoming newcomers, mentoring newly diagnosed patients) provides purpose within physical limitation

Nadi Suddhi (no retention) 5 min ANS, spasticity, mood
Bhramari 3–5 min Calming, depression
DRT 10–15 min Fatigue, depression
Yoga Nidra 15–20 min Sleep, restorative

Environment: Cool room (below 22°C ideal); fan available; cool water; morning schedule preferred.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Hot environment: Absolute contraindication to practice in hot, humid conditions or hot yoga; even mild heat can trigger Uhthoff's phenomenon
  • Kapalabhati, Bhastrika: Generates internal heat; avoid in heat-sensitive MS patients
  • Vigorous practice: Triggers fatigue; monitor carefully; stop before symptom exacerbation
  • Falls risk: Non-negotiable support for all balance practices; cerebellar ataxia produces unpredictable balance loss
  • Spasticity: Do not forcefully stretch spastic muscles — risk of muscle tear; slow, gentle stretch; warmth (not heat) may help if not Uhthoff-sensitive
  • Bladder urgency: Ensure bathroom access; avoid practices that increase intra-abdominal pressure when bladder is full
  • Relapse period: Rest; no yoga practice during acute relapse (weeks 1–2); gentle return as relapse resolves
