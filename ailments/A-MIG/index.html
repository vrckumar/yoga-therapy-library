Migraine

Migraine

1. Definition & Classification

Migraine is a recurrent neurovascular headache disorder characterised by moderate-to-severe, typically unilateral, throbbing headache lasting 4–72 hours, often accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and hypersensitivity to light (photophobia), sound (phonophobia), and smell (osmophobia). It is one of the most prevalent and disabling neurological conditions globally — ranking in the top ten causes of disability worldwide.

Migraine is classified in the yogic framework as an Adhija Vyadhi — the excessive speed of the mind, hypersensitivity, and emotional reactivity that characterise the stressed modern lifestyle express themselves in the brain's neurovascular system as migraine.

Classification (ICHD-3)

Migraine without aura (Common Migraine): Recurrent headache without preceding neurological symptoms. Most common type (~75% of migraineurs).

Migraine with aura (Classic Migraine): Recurrent reversible focal neurological symptoms (aura) precede or accompany the headache by 20–60 minutes. Most common aura: visual (zigzag lines, scotoma, flickering lights). Less common: sensory (tingling), motor, speech disturbances.

Chronic Migraine: Headache on ≥15 days/month for >3 months, with migraine features on ≥8 days/month.

Status Migrainosus: Migraine attack lasting more than 72 hours (medical emergency).

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Neurovascular Mechanism of Migraine

Migraine arises from dysfunctional trigemino-vascular mechanisms:

Cortical Spreading Depression (CSD): Migraine begins with a wave of neuronal excitation followed by sustained suppression spreading across the cortex at ~3mm/min — this is the correlate of the aura. CSD triggers trigeminal nerve activation.

Trigemino-vascular activation: The trigeminal nerve (cranial nerve V) innervates the cerebral blood vessels and meningeal vessels. Trigeminal activation → release of vasoactive neuropeptides (CGRP — calcitonin gene-related peptide, substance P) → meningeal vasodilation + neurogenic inflammation → pain signal transmitted via trigeminal nucleus caudalis → thalamus → cortical pain perception.

Central sensitisation: Repeated migraine attacks sensitise the trigeminal nucleus — threshold for activating the pain pathway falls progressively, explaining why migraineurs develop increased headache frequency over time.

HPA axis and serotonin: Stress → HPA activation → cortisol elevation → serotonin metabolism disruption (serotonin transiently rises then falls during a migraine attack) → cerebrovascular dysregulation. Serotonin plays a critical role in regulating cerebrovascular tone.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes

  • Genetic predisposition: Strong family history; specific gene variants affect calcium channels (ion channelopathy basis of familial hemiplegic migraine)
  • Hypothalamic and limbic dysregulation: The prodrome (premonitory) phase — occurring 24–48 hours before headache — includes yawning, food craving, mood changes, and fatigue, suggesting hypothalamic involvement
  • Female predominance: 3:1 female to male ratio; strongly linked to oestrogen fluctuation
  • Chronic stress and hypersensitivity: The Manomaya Kosha root — excessive reactive mind, poor stress regulation

Migraine Phases

  1. Prodrome (Premonitory): 24–48 hours before — yawning, food craving, neck stiffness, mood change, fatigue
  2. Aura (if present): 20–60 minutes — visual, sensory, or motor neurological symptoms; fully reversible
  3. Headache phase: 4–72 hours — unilateral, throbbing, moderate-severe, worsened by physical activity; nausea, vomiting, photophobia, phonophobia
  4. Postdrome: 24 hours after — fatigue, "migraine hangover"; cognitive slowness

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

Clinical history (ICHD-3 criteria — no diagnostic test required). MRI/CT brain if atypical features, first or worst headache, neurological deficit.

Medical Management

Acute/Abortive treatment:

  • Simple analgesics: NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen), Paracetamol (early in attack)
  • Triptans (Sumatriptan, Rizatriptan): Serotonin (5-HT1B/1D) agonists — vasoconstrict dilated meningeal vessels; block neuropeptide release. Most effective acute treatment.
  • Anti-emetics: Metoclopramide, Domperidone (also improve gastric motility for better drug absorption)
  • Ergot alkaloids: Ergotamine, Dihydroergotamine (older; less used now)

Preventive treatment (≥4 attacks/month):

  • Beta-blockers: Propranolol, Metoprolol
  • Topiramate (anticonvulsant)
  • Amitriptyline (tricyclic)
  • Valproate
  • CGRP monoclonal antibodies (Erenumab, Fremanezumab — newer; highly effective)

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

Migraine is a quintessential Manomaya-Pranamaya-Annamaya Kosha cascade. The root is in the hypersensitive, reactive Manomaya Kosha (stress sensitivity, emotional reactivity, perfectionism). This creates Pranamaya disruption (altered nostril dominance, irregular breathing, disrupted prana flow to the cerebrovascular territory). The Annamaya expression is the throbbing headache of meningeal vasodilation and neurogenic inflammation.

Critical principle: During an acute migraine attack — NO yoga practices. Rest in darkness and silence is the appropriate intervention. Yoga therapy is for prevention and inter-ictal management.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Gentle physical practices that do not increase intracranial pressure or cerebrovascular load during the inter-ictal period; neck and shoulder tension release (which can trigger migraine).

  • Gentle neck loosening exercises: Neck half-circles, chin-to-chest, ear-to-shoulder — releasing the trapezius and sub-occipital muscles that are common trigger points for migraine. Performed slowly, without jerky movements.
  • Shoulder rolling and shrugging: Releases the upper trapezius tension that predisposes to tension-type headache that can trigger migraines.
  • Paschimottanasana (seated forward bend): Gentle traction on the posterior cervical muscles; calming.
  • Sashankasana (Rabbit/Child's pose): Head below heart; gentle inversion effect without cervical loading — allows blood to pool slightly in the cranium, which paradoxically reduces cerebrovascular reactivity in some migraineurs.
  • Avoid: Vigorous Surya Namaskara, strong inversions (Sirsasana — increases intracranial pressure; contraindicated), or any posture that requires holding the neck in unusual positions.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Balance cerebrovascular tone through nostril-ANS regulation; reduce the sympathetic hyperreactivity that precedes migraine attacks.

  • Chandra Anuloma Viloma (Left Nostril Breathing — primary for migraine): Left nostril breathing activates right hemisphere parasympathetic tone, reducing cerebrovascular reactivity. Particularly beneficial in the prodrome and in stress-triggered migraine. The left nostril is the cooling, calming Chandra Nadi.
  • Nadi Suddhi: Balances nostril dominance; the migraine aura and attack are often preceded by disrupted nostril dominance patterns. Regular Nadi Suddhi practice reduces migraine frequency by stabilising the nostril cycle.
  • Inversions (carefully, not during attack): Mild inversions (Viparita Karani — legs up wall, not Sirsasana) in the inter-ictal period may improve cerebrovascular regulation through baroreceptor training. Do not attempt during or immediately before an attack.
  • Bhramari (between attacks): Sound resonance that calms the trigeminal nucleus region; reduces central sensitisation over time. Do not use during an acute attack (sound sensitivity is heightened).
  • Extended exhalation: Reduces sympathetic vascular tone; slows cerebral blood flow velocity.
  • AVOID during or near attack: Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Surya Bhedana — all stimulate cerebrovascular activity. Strong pranayama during prodrome may trigger an attack.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Reduce the chronic stress and hypersensitivity that lower the migraine threshold; develop identification of prodromal warning signs.

  • Cyclic Meditation (primary preventive practice): Reduces cortisol, reduces HPA axis hyperreactivity, and lengthens the threshold before migraine triggers take effect. Regular Cyclic Meditation has been documented to reduce headache frequency in stress-related headache disorders.
  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique): Performed in a dark, quiet room — mimics the conditions migraineurs naturally seek. Systematic muscle relaxation releases the cervicogenic tension that is a common migraine trigger.
  • OM Meditation: Sustained inner stillness; reduces the "excessive speed of mind" that is the Manomaya root of migraine.
  • Yoga Nidra: Deep rest equivalent to several hours of sleep — particularly valuable in migraineurs whose attacks are triggered by sleep deprivation.
  • VISAK (if migraine is phobia-associated): Some migraineurs have developed significant anticipatory anxiety about having attacks — this secondary anxiety lowers threshold further. VISAK addresses phobic reactivity.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Identify and modify personal migraine triggers; develop a headache diary discipline; understand the let-down migraine pattern.

Trigger identification through keeping a headache diary (recording onset, duration, severity, associated triggers, menstrual cycle) is a Vijnanamaya Kosha practice — systematic self-knowledge. Understanding the "let-down" pattern (migraines frequently occur after acute stress resolves — weekends, holidays) helps manage the timing of relaxation practices.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Develop equanimity and inner steadiness that is not perturbed by environmental triggers.

The Anandamaya insight: the brain's pain response is attempting to signal a need for deep rest that the Manomaya Kosha has been ignoring. Migraine can be understood as the body's enforced retreat into darkness and silence — the stillness that was not chosen voluntarily. Regular meditation creates voluntary inner silence, gradually reducing the brain's need to enforce it through migraine.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • During acute migraine attack: No yoga practice. Rest only.
  • Sirsasana (Headstand): Contraindicated in migraine — increases intracranial pressure significantly; major risk of triggering or worsening attack.
  • Strong pranayama during prodrome or attack: Kapalabhati, Bhastrika — contraindicated; stimulate cerebrovascular activity.
  • Strong inversions: Any inversion that loads the cervical spine or dramatically increases cranial blood pressure is contraindicated.
  • Photophobia and phonophobia during attack: All sensory inputs should be minimised.
  • Ergot alkaloids + strong pranayama: Combination may cause excessive cerebrovascular constriction — caution.
  • Women: menstrual migraine: Track relationship to menstrual cycle; adjust practice intensity around menstrual period.
