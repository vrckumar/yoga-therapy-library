Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

1. Definition & Classification

Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) is a chronic functional gastrointestinal disorder characterised by recurring abdominal pain or discomfort associated with altered bowel habits (diarrhoea, constipation, or alternating), in the absence of any identifiable organic cause. The word "functional" is key — there is no structural or biochemical abnormality detectable by conventional investigation; IBS is a disorder of gut function, not gut structure.

IBS is the most common GI disorder worldwide, affecting 10–15% of the population, causing significant personal suffering, occupational disability, and health care utilisation. The yoga therapy model places IBS squarely within Adhija Vyadhi — disorders originating in the Manomaya Kosha (mind) and manifesting through the gut-brain axis as disordered bowel function.

Classification (Rome IV Criteria)

IBS-D (Diarrhoea-predominant) — more than 25% of stools are loose/watery; autonomic pattern of excess parasympathetic drive to the colon (colon contracts, sphincter relaxes → urgency, frequency).

IBS-C (Constipation-predominant) — more than 25% of stools are hard or lumpy; autonomic pattern of excess sympathetic drive (sphincter contracts, colon slows → bloating, incomplete evacuation).

IBS-M (Mixed) — alternating loose and hard stools; variable autonomic dominance.

IBS-U (Unclassified) — does not fit criteria for IBS-D, C, or M.

Yogic Classification of GID

In the yogic understanding, the two autonomic subtypes of IBS correspond to the two branches of bowel motility:

  • Type 1 (holding nerve — sympathetic): Sphincter contracts, colon relaxes → constipation
  • Type 2 (parasympathetic emptying nerve): Colon contracts, sphincter relaxes → diarrhoea

The IAYT approach balances both through ANS regulation rather than treating either type with opposing drugs.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The Gut-Brain Axis

IBS is fundamentally a disorder of the gut-brain axis — the bidirectional communication network between the central nervous system (CNS) and the enteric nervous system (ENS) of the gut. The ENS contains 200–600 million neurons (comparable to the spinal cord) and communicates via:

  • Vagus nerve: The primary parasympathetic pathway; regulates gut motility, secretion, and immune function. Vagal dysfunction is central to IBS pathophysiology.
  • Sympathetic splanchnic nerves: Reduce gut motility; increase sphincter tone.
  • Serotonin (5-HT) signalling: The GI tract contains 95% of the body's serotonin. Enterochromaffin cells release 5-HT in response to gut distension. In IBS, serotonin receptor activity is abnormal — people with IBS have diminished receptor uptake capacity, causing abnormal levels of serotonin to persist in the GI tract, leading to disordered motility and sensation.
  • HPA axis: Stress activates the HPA axis, releasing cortisol, which directly affects gut motility, secretion, and immune function. Women with IBS may have more symptoms during menstrual periods, suggesting reproductive hormones also modulate gut sensitivity.

Why IBS Patients Have Heightened Gut Sensitivity

The colon of an IBS patient is particularly sensitive and reactive. This visceral hypersensitivity means that normal gut movements — which most people do not perceive — produce pain or urgency in IBS patients. The gut's sensory threshold is lowered, so stimuli (a meal, mild stress, certain foods) that would be sub-threshold for most people trigger pain and altered motility in IBS.

How Stress Disrupts Bowel Function

When emotional stress (anger, anxiety, fear, urgency) activates the limbic-hypothalamic axis, the signal modulates gut function via the vagus and sympathetic nerves simultaneously. Depending on the individual's autonomic pattern, the result is either spasmodic peristalsis (IBS-D) or inhibited peristalsis (IBS-C). Chronic stress sustains this dysregulation, converting episodic functional disturbance into a chronic condition.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes & Triggers

Factor Mechanism
Psychological stress Primary driver — activates gut-brain axis; triggers spasm or inhibition of colon
Abnormal serotonin signalling Dimished receptor uptake → dysregulated motility and sensation
Post-infectious IBS Previous gastroenteritis alters ENS; changes mucosal immune response
Food sensitivities Specific foods trigger immune-mediated or motility-mediated response
Reproductive hormones Worsening during menstrual periods in women
Depression and anxiety Frequently co-morbid; bidirectional relationship with IBS severity

Dietary triggers (most common):

  • Large meals; fatty foods
  • Wheat, rye, barley (gluten)
  • Dairy (lactose)
  • Chocolate, alcohol, caffeine (coffee, tea, colas)
  • Carbonated drinks (gas bloating)

Symptoms (Rome IV)

Core diagnostic criteria:

  • Abdominal pain or discomfort for at least 12 weeks (not necessarily consecutive) in the previous 12 months
  • The abdominal pain has two of three features:
    1. Relieved by defecation
    2. Onset associated with change in frequency of stool
    3. Onset associated with change in form (appearance) of stool

Associated symptoms:

  • Change in stool frequency (more than 3/day or fewer than 3/week)
  • Change in stool form (hard, lumpy, loose, watery)
  • Defecation urgency or straining
  • Mucus in stool
  • Bloating (abdominal distension)
  • Sensation of incomplete evacuation
  • Depression and anxiety (frequently co-morbid)

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical (Rome IV criteria) after excluding organic causes:

  • Full blood count (anaemia?)
  • ESR, CRP (inflammation?)
  • Thyroid function (hypothyroidism causes constipation)
  • Coeliac serology (anti-TTG IgA)
  • Stool microscopy and culture
  • Colonoscopy if alarm features present (blood in stool, age >50, weight loss, family history of bowel cancer)

Medical Management

No single medication cures IBS. Treatment is symptom-directed:

Symptom Approach
IBS-D (diarrhoea) Loperamide; Alosetron hydrochloride (Lotronex — women with severe IBS-D)
IBS-C (constipation) Fibre supplements; Tegaserod maleate (Zelnorm — women with IBS-C)
Pain Antispasmodics (Hyoscine, Mebeverine); low-dose tricyclics
Bloating Simethicone; dietary FODMAPs restriction
Comorbid anxiety/depression SSRIs, CBT (cognitive-behavioural therapy)

The Rome IV consensus acknowledges that lifestyle modification, dietary change, stress management, and psychological therapies are as important as pharmacological treatment — creating an explicit parallel to yoga therapy's multidimensional approach.

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

IBS is one of the clearest demonstrations of the gut-brain axis and thus of the Pancha Kosha model. The disease begins at the Manomaya Kosha (reactive mind and suppressed emotions), manifests as Pranamaya Kosha disruption (disordered autonomic regulation of gut motility), and expresses at the Annamaya Kosha (cramping, diarrhoea, constipation, pain). Healing must address all three simultaneously.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Directly regulate GI motility; reduce visceral hypersensitivity; cleanse the gut.

  • Pavanamuktasana series (Part I): The primary physical intervention for IBS. Systematic progressive exercise series beginning with toe-finger movements and proceeding through all joints — performed with breath coordination and awareness. These practices improve peripheral circulation, reduce abdominal gas, and gently massage the GI tract without direct abdominal compression.
  • Laghu Shankhaprakshalana: Gentle saline intestinal cleanse using warm salt water with the five classical postures (Tadasana, Tiryaka Tadasana, Kati Cakrasana, Tiryaka Bhujangasana, Udarak Sakti Vikasaka). Resets GI motility pattern; clears fermentative residues that cause gas and bloating.
  • GID-specific asanas: Postures that compress, stretch, or twist the abdomen: Paschimottanasana, Ardha Matsyendrasana, Sashankasana, Marichyasana. Each is held with internal awareness of the abdomen — the practice of "focusing and defocusing" on the abdominal organs that is specifically mentioned in PGDYT 104 for digestive disorders.
  • Vajrasana: Post-meal sitting promotes efficient gastric emptying and reduces excessive fermentation in the large intestine.
  • Satvik diet: Small, frequent meals; well-cooked; low-fibre during flares (paradoxically); avoidance of known personal triggers.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Balance the two branches of the enteric autonomic system; heal the vagal dysfunction at the root of gut-brain axis dysregulation.

  • Abdominal breathing in lying position (Shavasana): The most fundamental Pranamaya intervention for IBS. Deep, conscious abdominal breathing directly stimulates the Samana Vayu — the prana of digestion. Performed with AAA chanting, it diffuses prana blocks at the solar plexus and navel centre, normalising motility.
  • Nadi Suddhi (alternate nostril breathing): Balances sympathetic and parasympathetic activity; directly addresses the autonomic imbalance at the root of both IBS-D and IBS-C subtypes.
  • Bhramari in Viparita Karani (legs elevated): Specifically recommended in PGDYT 104 for IBS and inflammatory bowel disorders. The combination of vagal resonance (Bhramari) and gravity-assisted venous drainage (Viparita Karani) produces a powerful parasympathetic normalisation of gut function.
  • QRT (Quick Relaxation Technique) immediately after bowel movement: 3 minutes of abdominal breath awareness immediately after passing stool. This specific instruction from PGDYT 104 addresses the most distressing IBS symptom — the feeling of incomplete evacuation. QRT allows the ANS to settle, reducing the urge to strain further.
  • Chandra Anuloma Viloma (left nostril breathing): Cooling and calming; particularly useful for IBS-D to reduce the excess parasympathetic drive to the colon.
  • Sectional breathing with abdominal emphasis: Reasserts conscious control over abdominal musculature, which in IBS patients is often held in chronic protective spasm.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Reduce the anxiety-gut cycle that perpetuates IBS; develop mastery over emotional reactivity that triggers bowel episodes.

  • Cyclic Meditation: Reduces cortisol and sympathetic arousal; documented to reduce stress-related GI symptoms by interrupting the HPA-axis → gut pathway.
  • OM Meditation with navel focus: PGDYT 104 specifically instructs: "apply this process of focussing and defocusing of the energies in navel region, to diffuse the prana and achieve balance." The navel (Manipura Chakra) is the energetic centre of digestion — conscious meditation on this centre has direct regulatory influence on the ENS.
  • Yoga Nidra: Accesses the deep emotional substrate of IBS — the suppressed fears, unresolved anxiety, and stored tension that create chronic gut hypersensitivity.
  • DRT: Every practice session closes with complete physical and mental release — giving the GI tract sustained periods of genuine relaxation.
  • Devotional sessions: Bhakti yoga as emotion management — developing acceptance, contentment, and sharing rather than urgency, competition, and anxiety.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Correct the underlying cognitive patterns — urgency, perfectionism, excessive anticipatory anxiety — that sensitise the gut.

Notional correction through counselling and yoga philosophy challenges the "target-meeting" mentality that keeps the IBS sufferer in chronic sympathetic activation. Understanding the gut-brain axis — that what happens in the mind is immediately registered in the gut — transforms the relationship with IBS symptoms from helplessness to agency. Satsang and lectures provide cognitive reframing within a supportive philosophical framework.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Dissolve the existential urgency and inner dissatisfaction that maintains the chronic stress driving IBS.

Karma Yoga — working with contentment, present-moment focus, and equanimity toward outcomes — naturally reduces the chronic low-level anxiety state that creates colonic hypersensitivity. The discovery that inner bliss is available without achieving external targets removes the root of urgency that characterises the IBS personality pattern.

IBS Subtype Adjustments

IBS-D (diarrhoea-predominant): Emphasise Chandra Anuloma Viloma; reduce Kapalabhati; Viparita Karani after bowel episodes.

IBS-C (constipation-predominant): Add Kapalabhati (gentle, not forceful); Surya Namaskara (mild stimulating); Uddiyana Bandha (when proficient — compresses the ascending and descending colon).

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Active colitis or bowel inflammation: Distinguish IBS from inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis) before commencing yoga therapy. Vigorous abdominal practices are contraindicated in active IBD.
  • Laghu Shankhaprakshalana: Contraindicated in active diarrhoea phase, hypertension, pregnancy, hernia, or recent abdominal surgery.
  • Kapalabhati: Use gently in IBS — avoid in IBS-D where increased abdominal pressure may stimulate colon.
  • Bhramari with Kumbhaka: Avoid breath retention (Kumbhaka) in IBS as it increases intra-abdominal pressure. Bhramari without retention is beneficial.
  • Dietary changes: Any major dietary modification should be introduced gradually — sudden high-fibre introduction may worsen bloating in IBS.
  • Alarm symptoms requiring medical evaluation before yoga therapy: Rectal bleeding, nocturnal symptoms awakening from sleep, unintentional weight loss, fever — these suggest organic disease, not IBS.
  • Women during menstruation: Avoid strong abdominal compression practices during menstruation — use gentler alternatives.

Research limitations: IBS studies are methodologically challenging because the condition is self-reported and highly responsive to placebo and attention effects. Yoga therapy's multi-component nature makes isolating individual practice effects difficult. General yoga therapy research and S-VYASA Arogyadhama data demonstrate consistent symptomatic improvement. The gut-brain axis provides a strong physiological rationale for yoga's mechanism: vagal activation → reduced visceral hypersensitivity → improved bowel regularity.
