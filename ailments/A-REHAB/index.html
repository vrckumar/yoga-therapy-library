Rehabilitation (Neurological / Cardiac / Musculoskeletal)

1. Definition & Classification

Rehabilitation medicine is the branch of medicine concerned with restoring functional ability, reducing disability, and improving quality of life in individuals with acute or chronic physical, cognitive, or psychosocial impairment resulting from illness, injury, or surgery. Yoga therapy's graded, whole-person approach makes it uniquely suited to rehabilitation settings.

Three primary rehabilitation domains in this context:

Neurological rehabilitation: Restoring function after stroke (CVA), traumatic brain injury (TBI), spinal cord injury, Guillain-Barré syndrome, multiple sclerosis relapse, or post-neurosurgical recovery.

Cardiac rehabilitation: Structured exercise and lifestyle programme after myocardial infarction (MI), coronary artery bypass graft (CABG), percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), or heart failure diagnosis. Evidence-based phases: Phase I (in-hospital), Phase II (outpatient), Phase III (maintenance).

Musculoskeletal rehabilitation: Recovery of joint function, muscular strength, and movement after orthopaedic surgery (joint replacement, fracture fixation), sports injury, disc surgery, spinal injury, or major soft tissue injury.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Neuroplasticity — The Science Behind Neurological Rehabilitation

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to reorganise its neural circuits in response to experience, learning, injury, or disease. It is the fundamental biological mechanism enabling neurological recovery:

  • Synaptic plasticity: Use-dependent strengthening (long-term potentiation, LTP) and weakening (long-term depression, LTD) of individual synapses — the cellular basis of learning and rehabilitation
  • Axonal sprouting: Surviving neurons grow new axonal branches to reinnervate territories previously served by damaged neurons
  • Cortical remapping: Adjacent cortical areas can assume functions of damaged regions; intensive, task-specific practice drives this remapping (use-dependent plasticity)
  • Neurogenesis: New neurons can be generated in the hippocampus and olfactory bulb in adults; BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) is the principal promoter; exercise, meditation, and cognitive challenge all upregulate BDNF

Key principle: Neuroplasticity is experience-dependent — the amount and specificity of functional recovery correlates directly with the intensity and task-specificity of rehabilitation practice. This is the rationale for the yoga therapy rehabilitation principle of intensive, progressive, meaningful practice.

Cardiac Rehabilitation Physiology

After myocardial infarction, the damaged myocardium is replaced by non-contractile scar tissue. The remaining viable myocardium must compensate — initially through cardiac remodelling (hypertrophy, dilatation) which, if unchecked, progresses to heart failure. Cardiac rehabilitation:

  • Aerobic training — promotes peripheral adaptations (improved skeletal muscle oxygen extraction efficiency), reduces resting heart rate (vagal training), improves endothelial function (shear-stress-mediated NO production), reduces sympathetic dominance
  • Resistance training — maintains skeletal muscle mass; reduces work of daily activities relative to maximal capacity
  • Psychological component — addresses the depression and anxiety prevalent post-MI (major depression occurs in 15–20% of MI patients and doubles mortality risk)
  • Risk factor modification — smoking cessation, dietary change, BP and lipid management, diabetic control

Musculoskeletal Rehabilitation Physiology

Post-surgical or post-injury rehabilitation must respect the biological timeline of tissue healing:

  • Inflammatory phase (0–5 days): Vascular response; phagocytosis of debris; avoid anything that exacerbates inflammation
  • Proliferative phase (5 days – 3 weeks): Fibroblast activity; collagen deposition; early gentle movement maintains tissue quality, prevents adhesion formation
  • Remodelling phase (3 weeks – 2 years): Collagen cross-linking and alignment; tissue strength progressively improves; graded loading promotes optimal remodelling; appropriate stress is essential — immobilisation produces inferior repair

3. Causes & Symptoms

Neurological Rehabilitation Presentations

  • Post-stroke: Motor impairment (hemiplegia/hemiparesis), sensory impairment, aphasia, dysphagia, visuospatial neglect, cognitive impairment, emotional lability, depression
  • Traumatic brain injury: Cognitive impairment, behavioural changes, fatigue, headache, balance disorders, post-traumatic stress
  • Spinal cord injury: Motor paralysis/paresis (level-dependent), sensory loss, autonomic dysfunction, bladder/bowel dysfunction, chronic pain, pressure injury risk

Cardiac Rehabilitation Presentations

  • Post-MI: Fatigue, exercise intolerance, dyspnoea, anxiety (cardiac phobia — excessive fear of physical activity), depression, chest tightness (musculoskeletal, post-surgical)
  • Post-CABG: Sternal wound pain, shoulder restriction (from chest retraction during surgery), post-operative depression

Musculoskeletal Rehabilitation Presentations

  • Post-arthroplasty: Wound healing, oedema, stiffness, progressive strength and ROM recovery
  • Post-fracture: Disuse osteopenia, muscle wasting (disuse atrophy), proprioceptive deficit at repaired joint
  • Post-disc surgery: Core weakness, scar tissue formation, radicular pain resolution (variable)

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Neurological: Physiotherapy (task-specific motor retraining, gait rehabilitation), occupational therapy (ADL retraining), speech and language therapy, neuropsychology, constraint-induced movement therapy (CIMT), robotic assistance
  • Cardiac: Graded aerobic exercise (target 60–80% HRR); resistance training; dietary counselling; smoking cessation; psychological support; secondary prevention medications
  • Musculoskeletal: Physiotherapy (ROM exercises, strengthening, hydrotherapy), occupational therapy, orthotics, pain management, surgical review if complications

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for rehabilitation is a progressive protocol that follows a graded pathway from complete rest through to independent, active practice — matching the patient's evolving capacity and using all five koshas to address the multi-dimensional impact of illness or injury.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body) — PROGRESSIVE PROTOCOL

The structured rehabilitation pathway:

Phase 1 — Chair Yoga: For patients who cannot stand safely or independently; post-acute phase; early cardiac and neurological rehabilitation. All physical practices performed seated or with full support.

  • Seated Sakti Vikasaka for all joints above and below the impaired region
  • Seated Utkatasana progressions (sit-to-stand training — core functional movement)
  • Supported standing with chair/wall: brief weight-bearing practice

Phase 2 — Loosening Exercises (Standing with support): When patient can safely stand with minimal support. Introduces upright posture and gravitational challenges.

  • Standing Sakti Vikasaka (Sithilikarana Vyayama)
  • Supported Vrikshasana (balance training)
  • Gentle weight shifts, tandem standing

Phase 3 — Gentle Asanas: When independent standing is achieved and basic strength is restored.

  • Standing postures (Tadasana, Ardhakati Chakrasana, Trikonasana)
  • Supine postures (Pavanamuktasana series, Setu Bandhasana)
  • Avoid asanas involving the specifically injured region until cleared

Phase 4 — Pranayama and Full Programme: As physical capacity approaches normal range; incorporates the full pranayama programme and meditation.

Sakti Vikasaka for joint rehabilitation: The cornerstone Annamaya practice for all three rehabilitation domains — systematically restores range of motion, proprioception, and neuromuscular coordination in a safe, progressive, pain-limited manner.

MIRT (Mind-body Integration and Relaxation Training): For neurological rehabilitation specifically — rebuilds the cortical body map that has been disrupted by stroke, brain injury, or spinal cord injury through systematic, attention-guided body awareness.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Vibhagiya Svasana (sectional breathing) — re-establishes full respiratory function, particularly important in cardiac rehabilitation (post-CABG sternal pain inhibits deep breathing → pooling of secretions) and neurological rehabilitation (autonomic dysregulation)
  • Nadi Suddhi — central practice for all rehabilitation phases; reduces the depression and anxiety that consistently impair rehabilitation engagement and outcomes; improves HRV (cardiac rehabilitation)
  • Bhramari — rapid anxiety and pain management; useful during painful physiotherapy sessions as a complementary self-management tool

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

Post-illness/injury depression is both a clinical problem and a rehabilitation barrier — depressed patients attend fewer sessions, perform fewer repetitions, and achieve poorer functional outcomes. Addressing the mental-emotional layer is not optional.

  • DRT — provides the rest and recovery state essential to tissue repair (physiological rest promotes healing); reduces fear of movement (cardiac phobia, kinesiophobia in musculoskeletal rehabilitation)
  • Yoga Nidra — manages post-surgical pain; reduces opioid requirements; improves sleep quality which is essential for neuroplasticity and tissue repair
  • MIRT — specifically designed for rehabilitation settings to address the psychological dimension of disability and re-establish positive relationship with the recovering body

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Education about rehabilitation science: Understanding neuroplasticity, the dose-response relationship in recovery, and the importance of consistent practice — provides motivation for repetitive, sometimes tedious rehabilitation exercises
  • Graded return to activity planning: Setting achievable functional goals (returning to cooking, gardening, walking to the shop), celebrating incremental progress
  • Secondary prevention: For cardiac rehabilitation — understanding and managing cardiovascular risk factors; for musculoskeletal — body mechanics, ergonomics, injury prevention

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Rehabilitation requires sustained motivation over months or years, often through plateau periods with minimal apparent progress. The Anandamaya dimension provides what purely technical rehabilitation cannot:

  • Karma Yoga — reframing rehabilitation practice as a form of service (to family, community, self, the body's inherent healing intelligence)
  • Acceptance and adaptation — equanimity toward permanent disability where full recovery is not possible; finding meaning and purpose within changed capacity
  • Group rehabilitation yoga — community, shared struggle, peer encouragement, collective celebration of small victories

6. Recommended Protocol (Phased)

Phase Trigger Practices Duration
Phase 1: Chair Yoga Patient can sit safely Seated Sakti Vikasaka, seated breath training, DRT, MSRT 2–4 weeks
Phase 2: Loosening + Supported Standing Can stand with support Add supported standing asanas, Nadi Suddhi, Bhramari 2–4 weeks
Phase 3: Gentle Asanas Independent standing Add standing + supine asanas, Yoga Nidra 4–8 weeks
Phase 4: Full Programme Near-functional recovery Full pranayama, meditation, advance asanas as cleared Ongoing

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Neurological:
    • Active haemorrhage or oedema: no physical practice until cleared
    • Seizure history: see epilepsy page; avoid breath retention and stimulating pranayama
    • Severe spasticity: avoid passive stretching (increases spasm); use relaxation first
  • Cardiac:
    • Post-MI: no vigorous practice for 6–8 weeks; ECG monitoring during initial sessions
    • Post-CABG: sternal precautions (no lifting arms above head, pushing/pulling) for 6–8 weeks
    • Target HR limits: generally 60–80% HRR; monitor symptoms
  • Musculoskeletal:
    • Respect surgical healing timelines — coordinate with surgeon and physiotherapist
    • Weight-bearing restrictions must be adhered to (e.g., non-weight-bearing post-hip fracture fixation)
    • Prosthetic joint precautions (e.g., post-hip arthroplasty: no hip flexion >90°, no adduction crossing midline)
