Osteoarthritis

Osteoarthritis

1. Definition & Classification

Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common form of arthritis worldwide — a chronic, progressive degenerative joint disease characterised by cartilage breakdown, subchondral bone remodelling, osteophyte (bone spur) formation, and secondary synovial inflammation. Unlike Rheumatoid Arthritis, OA is not an autoimmune disease — it is a degenerative condition related primarily to ageing, mechanical loading, and metabolic changes within the cartilage itself. The immune system does not attack the joint; rather, the joint simply wears out, accelerated by obesity, previous injury, malalignment, and chronic metabolic stress.

OA is classified in the yogic framework as an Adhija Vyadhi in the sense that chronic lifestyle factors — sedentary habits, obesity, postural imbalances, and the metabolic consequences of chronic stress — accelerate the natural degenerative process beyond what would occur with healthy ageing and movement.

Types

Primary OA: Idiopathic — occurring as part of the normal ageing process, without a specific preceding cause. Most common form.

Secondary OA: Develops in a joint previously damaged by injury, gout, chronic inflammatory disease, or septic arthritis. Can affect any joint and any age group.

By joint distribution:

  • Knee OA (Gonarthrosis) — most common; both medial and lateral compartments
  • Hip OA (Coxarthrosis) — weight-bearing; major cause of disability
  • Hand OA — DIP joints (Heberden's nodes), PIP joints (Bouchard's nodes), first CMC joint
  • Spine (Spondylosis) — cervical and lumbar facet joints; associated with disc degeneration
  • Generalised OA — multiple joints involved

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Structure of the Normal Synovial Joint

Each synovial joint consists of: articular cartilage (smooth, lubricated), synovial membrane (secretes synovial fluid), joint capsule, ligaments, and supporting musculature. Articular cartilage is avascular (has no blood supply) and is nourished entirely by diffusion through the synovial fluid — making it dependent on joint movement for nutrition. Cartilage consists of chondrocytes embedded in a matrix of collagen, proteoglycans (chondroitin, keratan), and water.

How OA Develops

Articular cartilage maintains itself through a dynamic balance between:

  • Synthesis — chondrocytes continuously produce new matrix components
  • Degradation — enzymes (matrix metalloproteinases, aggrecanases) continuously degrade old matrix

In OA, this balance is disrupted:

  1. Biochemical changes: The ratio of chondroitin to keratan decreases; glycosaminoglycan concentration falls; proteoglycan aggregation is disrupted. The cartilage loses its water-binding capacity and shock-absorbing function.

  2. Physical changes: The smooth cartilage surface develops pitting and fibrillation (surface cracking). These changes progress from the articular margins inward.

  3. Bone exposure and osteophyte formation: As cartilage thins, the underlying bone (subchondral bone) is exposed at the margins. To compensate, the body deposits extra bone — osteophytes. These bony spurs impede movement and irritate adjacent soft tissues.

  4. Joint space narrowing: X-ray demonstrates progressive loss of joint space as cartilage is lost.

  5. Reactive synovitis: Unlike RA's primary synovitis, OA synovitis is reactive — cartilage fragments shed into the joint fluid trigger mild inflammatory responses. This explains OA's pain and swelling despite being primarily degenerative.

  6. Pain sensitisation: Bone exposed at margins stimulates pain-sensitive nerve endings in the subchondral bone, periosteum, and joint capsule — producing OA pain.

Risk Factors

Factor Mechanism
Age Reduced chondrocyte repair capacity; cumulative mechanical wear
Obesity Increased joint loading (each kg of body weight = 4–6 kg force on the knee)
Gender Women > men; accelerated post-menopause (oestrogen protects cartilage)
Previous joint injury Disrupts cartilage architecture; alters load distribution

Sedentary lifestyle Reduces cartilage nutrition (which depends on movement-driven diffusion)
Occupational loading Repeated impact forces, kneeling, climbing

3. Causes & Symptoms

Symptoms

Pain: The primary complaint. OA pain is characteristically:

  • Mechanical — worse with activity, better with rest (opposite of RA morning stiffness pattern)
  • Weight-bearing — particularly with stair climbing, prolonged walking
  • Deep, aching quality
  • May be associated with sudden sharp pain on specific movements (loose bodies, osteophyte impingement)

Stiffness: Typically brief morning stiffness (less than 30 minutes — distinguishes from RA's more than 60 minutes) and inactivity stiffness (gelling after sitting prolonged periods — also called "start-up pain").

Crepitus: Audible or palpable creaking/grating in the joint during movement (bone roughness against roughness).

Bony enlargement: Palpable bony outgrowths around joint margins (osteophytes).

Reduced range of motion: Progressive loss of joint mobility as osteophytes and scarring develop.

Joint deformity: Varus (bow-leg) or valgus deformity in advanced knee OA.

Acute exacerbations: Episodes of acute swelling and pain (may represent reactive synovitis, chondrocalcinosis, or crystal shedding).

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

  • Clinical examination
  • X-ray: joint space narrowing, subchondral sclerosis, osteophytes, cyst formation
  • MRI: early cartilage changes, meniscal pathology, synovitis
  • Blood tests: ESR, CRP normal (unlike RA); RF negative

Medical Management

Category Approach Limitations
Analgesics Paracetamol; Tramadol (moderate pain) Symptomatic only
NSAIDs Ibuprofen, Diclofenac, Naproxen Symptomatic; GI and cardiovascular risk
Topical NSAIDs Diclofenac gel Fewer systemic effects
Intra-articular steroids Corticosteroid injection Short-term pain relief; risk of cartilage damage with repeated use
Intra-articular hyaluronate Viscosupplementation Variable benefit
Chondroprotective Glucosamine, Chondroitin Modest evidence for symptom modification
Surgery Arthroscopy (controversial), osteotomy, joint replacement For end-stage OA

Physical therapy — exercise, weight management, physiotherapy, orthotics — is consistently rated as highly effective in OA management and is a direct interface with yoga therapy's physical component.

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

OA management differs from RA in an important way: the physical degeneration at the Annamaya Kosha is the primary pathology, whereas in RA it is secondary to immune dysfunction rooted in the Manomaya Kosha. Nevertheless, the Pancha Kosha model still applies — chronic pain creates its own Manomaya (fear, depression, limitation of movement, withdrawal) and Pranamaya (disrupted breathing, guarded posture) consequences that perpetuate the disease cycle and must be addressed.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Improve joint mobility through safe ROM exercises; strengthen periarticular musculature to reduce mechanical loading on damaged joint surfaces; improve cartilage nutrition through movement; manage body weight.

Sakti Vikasaka series (joint-specific exercises): The primary physical intervention for OA. Gentle, consciousness-imbued full range of motion (ROM) exercises for each affected joint:

  • Knee OA: slow knee flexion-extension in sitting, Pavanamuktasana Kriya (supine knee movements), gentle Virasana preparation
  • Hip OA: hip circles, Supta Baddha Konasana, gentle Ardha Matsyendrasana
  • Hand OA: finger and wrist movements, Namaste with pressure

The key principle: cartilage nutrition depends on movement. Immobility causes cartilage to dry out; gentle movement cycles synovial fluid through cartilage, nourishing chondrocytes. The movement must be slow, smooth, and within a comfortable range — never forceful through pain.

Strengthening the supporting musculature:

  • Quadriceps strengthening for knee OA — even small increases in quadriceps strength (10–20%) significantly reduce knee pain
  • Straight-leg raising in supine without active knee flexion
  • Setu Bandhasana (Bridge Pose) — strengthens gluteals and hamstrings, reducing hip loading
  • Pratikriyasana (gentle extension exercises) for spine OA

Weight management: Every 1 kg of weight loss removes 4–6 kg of loading force from the knee. Yoga's metabolic effects (improved insulin sensitivity, reduced cortisol, increased physical activity, satvik dietary guidance) contribute to weight normalisation — a significant structural benefit in OA.

Hydrotherapy equivalents: Water reduces gravitational joint loading — the yogic equivalent is Shavasana or supine aquatic practices for acute phases. Warm water immersion relaxes periarticular muscles and reduces pain perception.

Kriyas (where appropriate): Laghu Shankhaprakshalana and Kunjal Kriya for systemic cleansing and metabolic support.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Improve circulation to joints; activate healing Prana; reduce pain-mediated breathing restriction.

  • Nadi Suddhi: Primary pranayama — balances ANS, reduces cortisol-driven metabolic burden on joints, improves peripheral circulation.
  • Sectional breathing: Re-establishes full respiratory movement; relieves the thoracic and abdominal rigidity that accompanies guarded movement in chronic pain.
  • Pranic Energisation Technique (PET): Directs conscious breath-prana to affected joints. With internal awareness on the joint, slow inhalation expands awareness into the joint space; slow exhalation releases tension. This appears simple but is one of the most powerful pain-relief techniques in yoga therapy for chronic joint conditions.
  • Vibhagiya Svasana: Full respiratory engagement normalises the diaphragm-abdominal rhythm disrupted by chronic pain posture.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Address the chronic pain psychology — fear of movement, depression, loss of function, identity erosion — that often accompanies OA.

  • Cyclic Meditation: Reduces pain sensitivity through cortisol reduction and endorphin release. Provides a deep rest state that counteracts the fatigue and sleep disturbance associated with chronic pain.
  • DRT: Systematic muscle relaxation releases the chronic periarticular muscle spasm that aggravates joint pain.
  • OM Meditation: Sustained inner focus develops pain equanimity — the ability to observe pain without adding the Manomaya layer of fear and suffering.
  • Yoga Nidra: Addresses the deep-seated emotional contraction around chronic pain — the guarding, the anticipatory dread, the grief of lost mobility.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Understand OA as a manageable, partially reversible condition — counteracting the learned helplessness common in degenerative joint disease.

Notional correction: OA is not an inevitable downhill trajectory. Research consistently shows that exercise and weight management produce significant functional improvement even in advanced OA. Understanding the cartilage nutrition mechanism (movement = nourishment) motivates regular practice. Counselling on the role of obesity and inactivity in OA progression empowers constructive lifestyle change.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Find the inner stillness that exists independently of the physical body's limitations.

The deep bliss available in meditation is not conditional on physical freedom of movement. As physical limitation increases with advanced OA, the Anandamaya Kosha becomes increasingly relevant — the inner resource that transcends the body's ageing process.

Phase-Specific Modifications

Acute pain flare:

  • PET, DRT, Yoga Nidra — rest-oriented practices
  • No weight-bearing on acutely painful joint
  • Cold application if acute swelling

Subacute/maintenance:

  • Progressive Sakti Vikasaka; introduce strengthening
  • Begin low-impact movement gradually

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Active joint effusion (swelling): Avoid loading and active ROM in the acutely swollen joint. Isometric exercises (muscle contraction without joint movement) are permissible.
  • Severe structural deformity (bone-on-bone OA): ROM may be severely limited. Gentle passive movement only; consider aquatic therapy equivalent.
  • Post-joint replacement surgery: Specific restrictions apply (no deep flexion, no crossing legs for hip replacement). Confirm with orthopaedic surgeon before commencing yoga therapy.
  • Strong forward bends in lumbar OA: Avoid extreme lumbar flexion in lumbar spondylosis — may increase disc loading.
  • Cervical spondylosis: No extreme neck rotation or extension; neck-loading inversions (Sarvangasana, Sirsasana) contraindicated.
  • Knee OA: Avoid Vajrasana (deep knee flexion in sitting on heels) in moderate to severe knee OA — substitute with sitting on a folded blanket.
  • Vigorous practices: OA joints do not tolerate high-impact, repetitive loading. All yoga therapy is slow, smooth, and gentle.

The mechanism is multifactorial: quadriceps strengthening, proprioception improvement, weight management, pain psychology, and systemic ANS regulation all contribute.
