Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR)

1. Definition & Classification

Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR) is defined as failure of a fetus to reach its genetically determined growth potential, operationally defined as estimated fetal weight below the 10th centile for gestational age on ultrasound. IUGR must be distinguished from small-for-gestational-age (SGA) — a constitutionally small but otherwise healthy fetus — based on evidence of pathological restriction of growth (abnormal Doppler flow, oligohydramnios, declining growth velocity).

Classification by timing:

  • Symmetric IUGR (early onset, <28 weeks): All fetal parameters proportionately small; suggests early insult (chromosomal abnormality, congenital infection); poorer prognosis
  • Asymmetric IUGR (late onset, >28 weeks): Abdominal circumference most affected (liver glycogen depletion); head relatively spared ("brain-sparing"); suggests uteroplacental insufficiency; better prognosis with appropriate management

Severity classification by Doppler:

  • Normal Doppler → moderate restriction
  • Absent end-diastolic flow (AEDF) → severe restriction
  • Reversed end-diastolic flow (REDF) → critical restriction; delivery indicated

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Placenta and Uteroplacental Circulation

The placenta is the fetal lifeline — a specialised organ of exchange that develops from the trophoblastic invasion of the decidualised maternal endometrium. Spiral arteries of the myometrium undergo physiological conversion (trophoblast-mediated remodelling) that transforms them from high-resistance, muscular vessels into wide, low-resistance conduits capable of delivering the 500–600 mL/min of blood required in the third trimester.

In IUGR, this trophoblastic invasion is defective — spiral arteries remain partially muscular and high-resistance, reducing uteroplacental blood flow and thus the delivery of oxygen and nutrients to the fetus. The placenta responds by optimising extraction (increasing oxygen extraction fraction) and prioritising flow to vital organs (brain, heart, adrenal glands — the "brain-sparing" response), but these compensatory mechanisms have limits.

Fetal Response to Nutrient Restriction

The growth-restricted fetus activates a cascade of compensatory mechanisms:

  • Redistribution of cardiac output to vital organs (brain, heart)
  • Reduction of growth factor signalling (IGF-1 reduction → reduced hepatic and adipose growth)
  • Hepatic glycogen mobilisation (reducing abdominal circumference)
  • Polycythaemia (increased red blood cell production in response to hypoxia)
  • Reduced fetal movements (conserving energy)

These adaptations preserve neurological integrity at the cost of somatic growth, but prolonged restriction ultimately exhausts even these mechanisms, risking stillbirth.

Maternal Physiological Contributions

  • Stress and cortisol: Maternal cortisol excess (from psychological stress, or conditions like Cushing's) reduces uterine blood flow and crosses the placenta, directly suppressing fetal growth
  • Hypertension (PIH/pre-eclampsia): The most common associated condition; vasoconstriction reduces uteroplacental perfusion
  • Anaemia: Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of maternal blood limits oxygen delivery to the placenta
  • Nutritional status: Maternal undernutrition or malabsorption limits substrate availability for placental transfer

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes

Category Specific Causes
Maternal PIH/pre-eclampsia, hypertension, malnutrition, anaemia, smoking, alcohol, diabetes, chronic renal disease, autoimmune disease (antiphospholipid syndrome), thrombophilias, high altitude
Placental Placental infarction, partial abruption, circumvallate placenta, velamentous cord insertion, twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome
Fetal Chromosomal abnormalities (trisomy 13, 18, 21), congenital anomalies, congenital infection (TORCH), multiple pregnancy

Diagnosis and Monitoring

  • Ultrasound biometry: Serial measurements of abdominal circumference, head circumference, femur length, estimated fetal weight — declining growth velocity more important than a single measurement
  • Doppler velocimetry: Umbilical artery (uteroplacental resistance), middle cerebral artery (brain-sparing), ductus venosus (fetal cardiac reserve)
  • Biophysical profile (BPP): Combines fetal movements, tone, breathing movements, and liquor volume — assesses fetal wellbeing
  • Cardiotocography (CTG): Fetal heart rate monitoring — late decelerations and reduced variability signal fetal compromise

Fetal/Neonatal Consequences

  • Perinatal asphyxia (hypoxia during labour)
  • Necrotising enterocolitis, hypoglycaemia (depleted glycogen stores), hypothermia (reduced subcutaneous fat)
  • Long-term: Barker hypothesis — IUGR increases adult risk of hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease (fetal programming)

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Regular fetal monitoring — Doppler, BPP, growth scans every 2 weeks
  • Maternal optimisation — treat underlying PIH (antihypertensives), correct anaemia (iron, folate), optimise nutrition, bed rest in left lateral position, smoking cessation
  • Aspirin (low-dose) — begun in first trimester for high-risk patients; reduces placental insufficiency
  • Corticosteroids — if delivery before 34 weeks anticipated; promotes fetal lung maturity
  • Delivery — timing depends on Doppler findings, gestational age, and BPP; premature delivery must be balanced against risks of prematurity

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for IUGR is supportive of maternal physiology — it does not directly treat the placental pathology but can improve the maternal conditions that contribute to it: reducing psychological stress (cortisol → improved uterine blood flow), promoting optimal blood pressure management, ensuring adequate sleep and rest, and supporting the mother emotionally through a high-anxiety clinical situation.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Lateral Savasana (primary physical practice): Left lateral decubitus (LLD) position is the most important single physical recommendation for IUGR management. When a pregnant woman lies supine, the gravid uterus compresses the inferior vena cava and aorta, significantly reducing venous return and uteroplacental perfusion. The left lateral position eliminates this compression, maximising venous return and thus cardiac output, and restoring optimal uteroplacental blood flow.

All Annamaya practices are conducted in the lateral position or seated:

  • Gentle upper limb loosening exercises (seated) — maintains circulation, reduces oedema
  • Gentle lower limb exercises (seated or lying) — ankle pumping, knee flexion-extension; reduces DVT risk and improves venous return
  • No compression of the abdomen — all practices that compress the abdominal region (prone positions, strong twists, forward bends that compress the uterus) are absolutely contraindicated
  • No inversions — no Sarvangasana, Halasana, or any fully inverted position during pregnancy

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Nadi Suddhi (primary) — reduces maternal sympathetic tone; cortisol reduction directly improves uterine and uteroplacental blood flow; the most important pranayama for maternal-fetal wellbeing in IUGR
  • Diaphragmatic breathing — slow, deep diaphragmatic breath increases maternal oxygen saturation and reduces the respiratory alkalosis of anxiety; enhances oxygen delivery to the placenta
  • Bhramari — immediate vagal calming; reduces the acute anxiety spikes triggered by clinical monitoring appointments, which transiently increase maternal BP and reduce uterine blood flow

Avoid: Kapalabhati, Bhastrika — both increase intra-abdominal pressure, are contraindicated in pregnancy especially with IUGR; Kumbhaka (breath retention) — reduces maternal oxygen saturation transiently.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

IUGR is an extremely anxiety-provoking diagnosis for pregnant women, involving frequent hospital monitoring, fear of stillbirth, and uncertainty about fetal outcomes. Chronic maternal anxiety itself worsens uteroplacental blood flow through sustained sympathetic vasoconstriction.

  • DRT in lateral Savasana — the most accessible and beneficial mental practice; profound relaxation in the optimal position for uteroplacental perfusion; ideally practised twice daily
  • Yoga Nidra (left lateral) — if the mother can sustain the position comfortably; deep rest reduces cortisol substantially; improved sleep quality as a direct benefit
  • Guided visualisation of healthy fetal growth — positive imagery of the baby growing and thriving; not a medical claim but a legitimate use of positive affect to reduce anxiety and its physiological consequences

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Understanding the condition: Knowing what IUGR means (and does not mean), why lateral positioning helps, how stress affects fetal blood flow — empowers the mother to participate actively in fetal wellbeing
  • Nutrition counselling: Adequate caloric intake, iron and folate, omega-3 fatty acids, hydration — all within yoga therapy's lifestyle education scope
  • Managing information anxiety: Teaching discernment about which information sources to trust (clinical team vs. internet) and how to use monitoring information constructively rather than catastrophically

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • Connection with the unborn child: Gentle attention practices (feeling fetal movements, speaking to the baby, devotional music) build maternal-fetal bonding and provide Anandamaya grounding in the present relationship rather than anxiety about future outcomes
  • Karma Yoga of motherhood: The perspective that caring for oneself is an act of love and service to the unborn child — motivating optimal self-care without guilt

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • No supine lying beyond 16–20 weeks (earlier in multiple pregnancy) — aortocaval compression
  • No abdominal compression — all postures pressing on the uterus are absolutely contraindicated
  • No breath retention — reduces maternal oxygen saturation
  • No vigorous exertion — conserve maternal energy; oxygen demand increase reduces placental supply
  • No hot environments — maternal hyperthermia reduces uteroplacental blood flow and is teratogenic in early pregnancy
  • Medical team coordination: Yoga therapy is adjunctive; all decisions about fetal monitoring timing and delivery remain with the obstetric team
