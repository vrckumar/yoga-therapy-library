Diabetes Mellitus

1. Definition & Classification

Diabetes mellitus (from the Greek diabaino — to pass through; mellitus — sweet) is a chronic metabolic disorder characterised by persistently elevated blood glucose levels resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. It was known in the ancient world as a disease of "sweet urine and excessive muscle loss."

Normal fasting blood glucose: 80–100 mg/dL. Diabetes is diagnosed when fasting blood glucose exceeds 126 mg/dL on two occasions, or post-meal glucose exceeds 200 mg/dL.

Types

Type I — Insulin Dependent Diabetes Mellitus (IDDM) The pancreas produces little or no insulin. Typically diagnosed in childhood or early adulthood. The immune system mistakenly destroys pancreatic beta cells (autoimmune mechanism). Daily insulin injections are required for survival.

Type II — Non-Insulin Dependent Diabetes Mellitus (NIDDM) The most common form (90%+ of all cases). The pancreas produces insulin but either insufficient amounts or the body's cells cannot use it effectively (insulin resistance). Usually develops in adults over 40, though increasingly seen in younger people. Closely linked to obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and chronic stress.

Gestational diabetes — elevated blood glucose during pregnancy in a previously non-diabetic person.

Secondary diabetes — elevated blood glucose due to another condition (pancreatitis, Cushing's syndrome, acromegaly) that disrupts beta cell function or insulin action.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The Pancreas and Glucose Regulation

The pancreas — a gland located behind and below the stomach, weighing 80–100 g in adults — performs both digestive and endocrine functions. Its endocrine function is carried out by approximately one million Islets of Langerhans, each containing:

  • Beta cells — produce, store, and release insulin in response to rising blood glucose
  • Alpha cells — produce glucagon, which raises blood glucose by releasing stored glycogen from the liver

How Blood Glucose Balance Works

When carbohydrates are digested, glucose is absorbed into the bloodstream. Beta cells detect the rising glucose and release insulin. Insulin acts as a key — it binds to receptors on muscle, fat, and liver cells, enabling glucose to enter and be used for energy or stored as glycogen.

When blood glucose falls (between meals or during exercise), alpha cells release glucagon, which signals the liver to release stored glycogen as glucose.

During stress, additional hormones — adrenaline, noradrenaline, cortisol, growth hormone, and thyroxine — all act to raise blood glucose to meet the increased energy demand. This is adaptive in short-term stress but destructive when chronic.

How Diabetes Develops (Type II)

Chronic psychosocial stress → sustained cortisol elevation → persistent blood glucose elevation → beta cells must produce more insulin → over years, beta cells become fatigued → progressive decline in insulin production. Simultaneously, cortisol and adrenaline make cells less responsive to insulin (insulin resistance). Both mechanisms compound each other, creating a vicious cycle.

Yoga therapy's understanding: diabetes is classified as an Adhija Vyadhi — a psychosomatic disease rooted in disturbance at the Manomaya Kosha (excessive speed of mind, chronic stress) that percolates downward through the Pranamaya Kosha (disrupted nostril dominance, altered autonomic function) to the Annamaya Kosha (impaired beta cell function, glucose dysregulation).

Indians, both in India and as migrants, have a genetic predisposition to higher prevalence of Type II Diabetes compared to many European populations.

Classic Symptoms

  • Polyuria — frequent, excessive urination (glucose spilling into urine above renal threshold of 180 mg/dL)
  • Polydipsia — excessive thirst
  • Polyphagia — increased appetite
  • Unexplained weight loss (Type I — cells cannot use glucose, muscle is broken down)
  • Fatigue, blurred vision
  • Slow-healing infections, impotence (Type II)

Long-term Complications (if uncontrolled)

Retinopathy (eyes), Neuropathy (nerves), Nephropathy (kidneys), Atherosclerosis (arteries), Gastroparesis, Dermopathy, Diabetic Ketoacidosis (Type I emergency)

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

  • Fasting blood glucose > 126 mg/dL (on two occasions)
  • Post-meal (2-hour) glucose > 200 mg/dL
  • Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)
  • HbA1c (glycated haemoglobin — reflects average glucose over 3 months)

Medical Management

Type I: Daily insulin injections (mandatory). Insulin cannot be taken orally.

Type II: Lifestyle modification first (diet + exercise). Oral hypoglycaemic agents:

  • Insulin secretagogues (stimulate beta cells — Glipizide, Glibenclamide)
  • Insulin sensitisers (reduce resistance — Metformin, Pioglitazone)
  • Alpha-glucosidase inhibitors (slow carbohydrate absorption — Acarbose)
  • Insulin injections if oral agents fail

Diet: Low glycaemic index, high fibre, reduced refined carbohydrates and saturated fats. Satvik diet (fresh, simple, vegetarian) is particularly recommended in the yogic framework.

Exercise: Regular physical activity reduces blood glucose by promoting glucose uptake in skeletal muscle independent of insulin — a key rationale for the active physical component of yoga therapy.

Foot care: Critical due to peripheral neuropathy and vascular compromise — daily inspection, proper footwear, smoking cessation.

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

How Yoga Addresses Diabetes — Layer by Layer

Diabetes is not simply a pancreatic problem. The yoga therapy model treats it as a whole-system psychosomatic imbalance that must be addressed at every level of the Pancha Kosha.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Stimulate pancreatic function, improve peripheral glucose uptake, reverse sedentary metabolic patterns.

The physical layer of yoga therapy for diabetes focuses on three mechanisms:

  1. Abdominal visceral stimulation — Spinal twisting postures (Vakrasana, Ardha Matsyendrasana, Parivrtta Trikonasana) apply lateral compression and release to the abdominal viscera. The mechanical pressure on the pancreatic tissue stimulates beta cell activity and improves hepatic glucose regulation. This is the most direct physical mechanism yoga therapy offers for diabetes.

  2. Skeletal muscle glucose uptake — Dynamic asana practice increases muscular demand for glucose, driving uptake via insulin-independent pathways (GLUT-4 transporter activation). This is equivalent to the benefit of aerobic exercise — lowering post-meal blood glucose and improving insulin sensitivity over time.

  3. Abdominal organ massage via kriyas — Agnisara (rapid abdominal pumping) and Kapalabhati applied as a kriya directly massage the pancreas, liver, and intestines, improving circulation and metabolic function in these organs. Sankhaprakshalana (full GI cleansing) can help reset hypoglycaemic states.

Loosening exercises (Sithilikarana Vyayama) at the start of every session mobilise joints, improve circulation, and prepare the nervous system — replacing the "physical exercise" role that most diabetics avoid due to fatigue and sedentary conditioning.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Correct the disrupted prana flow; activate parasympathetic nervous system; reduce cortisol-driven blood glucose elevation.

The stress-blood glucose connection operates through the autonomic nervous system. Chronic stress maintains sympathetic dominance, which sustains cortisol and adrenaline levels, which keep blood glucose elevated. Pranayama directly intervenes in this cycle:

  • Kapalabhati (abdominal emphasis) — rapid rhythmic exhalations activate diaphragmatic and abdominal muscles, massaging the pancreas while clearing respiratory passages. The effort-recovery rhythm trains the breath-glucose relationship.
  • Sectional breathing (Vibhagiya Svasana) — full engagement of abdominal, thoracic, and clavicular sections normalises respiratory patterns disrupted by chronic shallow stress-breathing.
  • Nadi Suddhi (alternate nostril breathing) — the most important pranayama for diabetes. Alternating stimulation of left and right nasal passages balances sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous system activity, corrects disrupted nostril dominance (an early sign of autonomic imbalance in yogic physiology), and activates the vagus nerve, reducing cortisol and improving insulin receptor sensitivity.
  • Bhramari (humming bee breath) — vagal stimulation through sound resonance, immediately calming for the nervous system. Reduces stress reactivity.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Remove the deep psychological patterns — speed of mind, perfectionism, anxiety — that sustain chronic stress and HPA dysregulation.

Diabetes is, in yogic understanding, fundamentally an Adhija Vyadhi — a disease of the mind's chronic agitation percolating into the body. The mental layer is therefore the root, not a secondary consideration.

  • Cyclic Meditation — clinically documented to reduce blood glucose levels. Combines active and passive phases, producing a deep rest state equivalent (by EEG measures) to several hours of sleep. Directly reduces cortisol.
  • Yoga Nidra — deep systematic relaxation at the threshold of sleep. Reduces sympathetic arousal and cortisol burden.
  • Deep Relaxation Technique (DRT) — guided progressive relaxation used to close each practice session.
  • Devotional sessions (bhajans, chants) — bring about emotional stabilisation by calming hypothalamic reactivity. The group experience provides social regulation.
  • Nadanusandhana / OM Meditation — sustained meditative practice cultivates the single-pointed, slow mind that is the antithesis of the hypersensitive, high-speed mental state that triggers stress-cortisol-glucose dysregulation.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Correct the notional errors — wrong beliefs about food, lifestyle, body, happiness — that sustain unhealthy behaviour patterns.

Counselling, lifestyle education, and notional correction through yoga philosophy provide the intellectual framework for sustained change. Understanding why chronic stress causes blood glucose elevation — not just being told to "avoid stress" — enables a person to engage their intelligence as an ally in managing their condition.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Develop a relationship with inner silence and equanimity that does not depend on external circumstances.

The deepest cause of diabetes-aggravating lifestyle — overeating, indulgence, sedentary behaviour — is the pursuit of momentary pleasure to compensate for inner emptiness. Karma Yoga (learning to act without compulsive craving for outcome) and meditation that reveals an inner bliss independent of circumstances gradually remove the root of compulsive behaviour.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Hypoglycaemia risk: Monitor blood glucose before and after sessions. Carry glucose tablets. Do not practice on empty stomach in insulin-dependent patients.
  • Kapalabhati: Avoid in moderate to severe hypertension, pregnancy, hernia, recent abdominal surgery. Do not practice immediately after meals.
  • Sarvangasana: Avoid in hypertension, cervical spondylosis, glaucoma, menstruation.
  • Dynamic and fast practices (Bhastrika, Surya Namaskara at speed): Monitor cardiovascular response in patients with co-existing hypertension or IHD.
  • Foot care: Do not allow barefoot practice on rough surfaces — neuropathy may prevent pain detection of injury.
  • Sankhaprakshalana: Perform only under guidance. Contraindicated in active hypoglycaemia.
