Pulmonary Tuberculosis

Pulmonary Tuberculosis

1. Definition & Classification

Pulmonary tuberculosis (TB) is a chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis — an acid-fast, aerobic bacillus that primarily infects the lung parenchyma. TB remains one of the top ten causes of death worldwide and the leading cause of death from a single infectious agent. India carries approximately 26% of the global TB burden.

Classification:

  • Primary TB — first infection; usually subclinical or mild; may progress to primary progressive TB in immunocompromised individuals (children, HIV)
  • Post-primary (reactivation) TB — most common form in adults; reactivation of dormant bacilli in the lung apices due to immunosuppression (HIV, diabetes, malnutrition, ageing, corticosteroid use)
  • Latent TB infection (LTBI) — infected but not infectious; positive tuberculin skin test or IGRA without active disease; 10% lifetime risk of reactivation
  • Extrapulmonary TB — pleural, lymph nodal, bone (Pott's disease — spinal TB), meningeal, renal, pericardial, and disseminated (miliary TB)
  • Drug-resistant TB: MDR-TB (resistant to isoniazid and rifampicin); XDR-TB (additionally resistant to fluoroquinolones and injectable agents)

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Mycobacterium tuberculosis — Pathogen Biology

M. tuberculosis is a uniquely adapted intracellular pathogen:

  • Transmitted via aerosol droplets (1–5 μm) produced by coughing, sneezing, or speaking; a single infective droplet nucleus can contain 1–3 bacilli
  • Inhaled bacilli reach the alveoli; alveolar macrophages phagocytose them but cannot kill them (mycobacterial cell wall components, including mycolic acids, inhibit phagosome-lysosome fusion)
  • The infected macrophage migrates to the regional lymph node — the primary complex (Ghon focus + hilar lymph node = Ghon complex in primary TB)

Immunopathology: Granuloma Formation

T lymphocytes (CD4+ Th1 cells) are recruited to the site of infection and produce IFN-γ, which activates macrophages and enhances their killing capacity. The immune response contains but rarely eliminates the bacilli:

Granuloma formation: Activated macrophages fuse into epithelioid cells and giant cells (Langhans giant cells), surrounded by lymphocytes — forming the characteristic granuloma (tubercle). At the centre of the granuloma, tissue necrosis produces caseous necrosis (cheese-like material) due to the combination of direct bacterial toxicity and hypersensitivity reactions.

Cavitation: When caseous material liquefies and drains into a bronchus, an air-filled cavity forms in the lung parenchyma. Cavitary lesions:

  • Are highly infectious (contain large numbers of bacilli)
  • Create fibrotic scarring and lung volume loss on healing
  • Provide the anatomical substrate for haemoptysis (erosion of pulmonary blood vessels)

Post-TB Lung Damage

Even after successful treatment, TB leaves behind structural lung damage:

  • Fibrosis — reduces lung compliance and volumes
  • Bronchiectasis — dilated, damaged bronchi with impaired mucociliary clearance
  • Cavitary residua — predispose to aspergilloma (Aspergillus superinfection)
  • Emphysematous changes — air trapping
  • Pleural thickening — if pleurisy occurred

This post-TB lung disease produces persistent respiratory symptoms despite bacteriological cure — the context in which yoga therapy's respiratory rehabilitation is most valuable.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Risk Factors for Active TB

Risk Factor Mechanism
HIV infection CD4+ T cell depletion → loss of granuloma integrity
Diabetes mellitus Impaired macrophage function; immune suppression
Malnutrition Global immune deficit
Overcrowding / poor ventilation High transmission probability
Tobacco smoking Mucociliary damage, impaired macrophage function
Silicosis Impaired alveolar macrophage function
Corticosteroid therapy Immunosuppression — reactivation risk
Poverty Combination of malnutrition, overcrowding, delayed diagnosis

Symptoms

Cardinal symptoms:

  • Cough > 2 weeks (most common presenting symptom in India: anyone with cough > 2 weeks should be screened for TB)
  • Haemoptysis (blood in sputum — from cavitary erosion of blood vessels)
  • Night sweats
  • Low-grade evening fever
  • Weight loss and anorexia (the original name "consumption" reflected the wasting)
  • Chest pain (from pleurisy or extensive parenchymal disease)

Constitutional: Fatigue, malaise, dyspnoea (in extensive disease)

Diagnosis

  • Sputum AFB smear (rapid; low sensitivity)
  • Sputum culture on Lowenstein-Jensen medium (gold standard; takes 6–8 weeks)
  • GeneXpert MTB/RIF (molecular; results in 2 hours; detects rifampicin resistance)
  • CXR: Apical shadowing, cavitation, fibrosis, lymphadenopathy

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Standard first-line regimen (DOTS/RNTCP): 6 months total
    • Intensive phase (2 months): Isoniazid (H) + Rifampicin (R) + Pyrazinamide (Z) + Ethambutol (E)
    • Continuation phase (4 months): Isoniazid + Rifampicin
  • Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course (DOTS) — patient takes medication under observation to ensure adherence (critical for preventing resistance)
  • MDR-TB: 18–24 months of second-line agents (bedaquiline, delamanid, linezolid, fluoroquinolones)
  • Nutritional support: Essential component — malnutrition both predisposes to TB and is worsened by it; adequate protein, caloric, and micronutrient intake accelerates recovery

Medical clearance before yoga therapy: Active infectious TB (smear-positive pulmonary TB) requires isolation until sputum smear converts to negative (typically 2–4 weeks of treatment). Yoga therapy begins after the infectious period has passed and the treating physician has cleared the patient for group activities.

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for TB targets two distinct phases:

  1. During treatment (post-infectious): Nutritional support, immune enhancement through stress reduction, and gentle respiratory rehabilitation
  2. Post-TB lung disease: Respiratory rehabilitation to maximise functional recovery despite structural lung damage

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Post-infectious phase (when cleared for activity):

  • Bhujangasana — prone backbend; expands the anterior chest and mobilises the chest wall restricted by pleurisy or cough; the prone position facilitates drainage of apical secretions
  • Sarvangasana — inversion alters regional ventilation-perfusion relationships; promotes drainage of dependent lung zones; must be introduced cautiously — begin only when general strength allows
  • Chest-opening asanas (Matsyasana, Ustrasana) — expand anterior thorax, counteracting the protective postural flexion adopted in response to cough and chest pain
  • Jala Neti — nasal irrigation; clears the upper respiratory tract; reduces the secondary sinusitis that commonly accompanies prolonged cough

Avoid: Any practice requiring vigorous exertion during active TB treatment; practices that cause haemoptysis or significant dyspnoea; prone position in large pleural effusion

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Pranayama for respiratory rehabilitation (gentle) — re-establishes efficient breathing patterns disrupted by months of chronic cough, fatigue, and respiratory pain
  • Vibhagiya Svasana (sectional breathing) — restores full three-section breathing (abdominal, thoracic, clavicular); patients with TB frequently develop restricted upper chest breathing and need systematic re-expansion of all zones
  • Nadi Suddhi — reduces the autonomic burden of chronic illness; supports sleep quality which is critical for immune recovery (growth hormone released during slow-wave sleep supports immune function)
  • Kapalabhati (gentle, when cleared) — improves mucociliary clearance in post-TB bronchiectasis; strengthens abdominal and respiratory muscles weakened by prolonged illness

Important: Kapalabhati and all pranayama are introduced only after the treating physician confirms bacteriological cure (negative sputum culture/GeneXpert); during active pulmonary TB with haemoptysis, all vigorous breathing practices are contraindicated.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

TB carries significant stigma in India and globally, often leading to social isolation, job loss, and relationship disruption. The psychological burden of a 6-month treatment course — with medication side effects, social isolation, and financial stress — is substantial.

  • DRT — reduces the stress and depression associated with TB diagnosis and prolonged treatment
  • Yoga Nidra — addresses the fatigue and sleep disruption that characterise active TB and its treatment
  • Cyclic Meditation — supports immune function through cortisol regulation; stress impairs TB immunity specifically (cortisol impairs macrophage activation and granuloma integrity)

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • DOTS adherence education — understanding why completing the full course is essential for cure and preventing resistance; yoga therapy sessions reinforce this message in a supportive, non-judgmental context
  • Nutrition counselling — protein requirements are elevated during active TB; dietary education about high-protein, calorie-dense, vitamin-rich foods
  • Stigma reduction — factual education about TB transmission (requires prolonged close contact; not contagious after 2 weeks of treatment) reduces self-isolation and family avoidance

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • Karma Yoga — participation in TB awareness or peer-support roles provides purpose and meaning within the illness experience; former TB patients who support others during treatment demonstrate improved own treatment adherence and outcomes
  • Devotional practices — group singing and chanting engage the respiratory muscles in therapeutic exhalation while providing the community connection that counters TB-associated social isolation

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Active infectious TB (smear-positive): No group yoga; individual supervised practice at home only; isolation precautions
  • Haemoptysis: No pranayama, no Kapalabhati, no inversions; rest and medical evaluation
  • Large pleural effusion: No inversions; avoid lateral positioning toward affected side
  • Pneumothorax (TB complication): No practice until medically cleared
  • Severe anaemia (Hgb < 8 g/dL): Restrict to very gentle, seated practices
  • Kanamycin/streptomycin (aminoglycosides for MDR-TB): Monitor for vestibular toxicity (dizziness, balance problems — affects safe practice)
