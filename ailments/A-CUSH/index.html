Cushing's Syndrome

Cushing's Syndrome

1. Definition & Classification

Cushing's syndrome is a clinical disorder caused by prolonged, excessive exposure to glucocorticoids (cortisol), either from endogenous overproduction or from exogenous administration (iatrogenic Cushing's — the most common cause).

Causes:

  • Iatrogenic (exogenous) — most common overall: Long-term corticosteroid therapy (for asthma, RA, IBD, organ transplantation); identical syndrome from pharmaceutical glucocorticoids
  • Cushing's disease (pituitary-dependent — most common endogenous cause, 70%): ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma (usually microadenoma) → bilateral adrenal hyperplasia → cortisol excess
  • Ectopic ACTH syndrome: ACTH secretion from non-pituitary tumours (small cell lung cancer, carcinoid tumours)
  • Adrenal adenoma/carcinoma: Autonomous cortisol production independent of ACTH

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Cortisol Excess Effects — System by System

Cortisol, in sustained excess, produces pathological effects in virtually every organ system through its widespread receptor expression:

Metabolic effects:

  • Promotes gluconeogenesis → hyperglycaemia → secondary diabetes (steroid-induced diabetes)
  • Promotes visceral fat deposition (central adiposity — "apple shape")
  • Inhibits peripheral glucose uptake → insulin resistance
  • Stimulates protein catabolism → muscle wasting, thin skin, striae (stretch marks), poor wound healing

Skeletal effects:

  • Inhibits osteoblast activity and promotes osteoclast activity → osteoporosis → vertebral compression fractures (most common fracture site)
  • Inhibits calcium absorption from the gut and increases renal calcium excretion → hypocalcaemia → secondary hyperparathyroidism

Cardiovascular effects:

  • Mineralocorticoid effects (sodium retention, potassium wasting at high doses) → hypertension
  • Endothelial dysfunction → accelerated atherosclerosis
  • Hypercoagulability → DVT risk

Immune effects:

  • Suppresses cellular and humoral immunity → increased infection risk
  • Suppresses inflammatory mediators → can mask infection signs

Neuropsychiatric effects:

  • Depression (most common), anxiety, emotional lability, psychosis (high-dose)
  • Cognitive impairment (hippocampal damage from cortisol excess)

Dermatological features:

  • Moon face (facial fat redistribution)
  • Buffalo hump (posterior cervical fat deposition)
  • Plethoric face (facial ruddiness)
  • Purple striae on abdomen, thighs, buttocks
  • Easy bruising
  • Hirsutism (androgen excess component in Cushing's disease)

3. Causes & Symptoms

Characteristic Features

The classic Cushing's triad: centripetal obesity + proximal muscle weakness + psychiatric disturbance. Additional features:

  • Hypertension (85% of cases)
  • Glucose intolerance / diabetes (40–50%)
  • Osteoporosis with vertebral fractures (40–60%)
  • Proximal myopathy (difficulty climbing stairs, rising from chair)
  • Oligo/amenorrhoea in women
  • Reduced libido in men
  • Growth failure in children

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Pituitary Cushing's (Cushing's disease): Transsphenoidal surgery (pituitary adenomectomy) — first-line; radiotherapy if surgery fails; steroidogenesis inhibitors (metyrapone, ketoconazole) as medical management or bridge to surgery
  • Adrenal adenoma: Unilateral adrenalectomy
  • Ectopic ACTH: Treat underlying tumour; bilateral adrenalectomy if tumour cannot be found/resected
  • Iatrogenic: Gradual steroid taper if clinically feasible; never abrupt cessation (risk of adrenal crisis — HPA axis is suppressed)

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for Cushing's syndrome is primarily targeted at the Manomaya Kosha — reducing the psychological and autonomic drivers of HPA axis overactivation (in endogenous Cushing's) and addressing the psychiatric sequelae (depression, anxiety, cognitive impairment) that are prominent features of the syndrome.

The Annamaya approach must be adapted to the severe physical consequences of hypercortisolism: osteoporosis, proximal muscle weakness, and skin fragility all demand a careful, graduated physical approach.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

The key constraint: osteoporosis with vertebral fracture risk. Strong asanas, deep twists, forward bends with spinal flexion, and inversions may risk vertebral compression fractures in patients with significant cortisol-induced bone loss.

Safe Annamaya practices:

  • Chair yoga — all physical practice initially from a seated position or with wall support; eliminates axial loading risk
  • Gentle upper and lower limb Sakti Vikasaka — moves the limbs without axial spinal loading; maintains peripheral muscle function in the context of proximal myopathy
  • Gentle supine asanas — Pavanamuktasana series (without strong abdominal effort), Savasana variations
  • Progressive muscle strengthening (very gentle): Naukasana (boat pose, very gentle version); gentle Virabhadrasana I progression as bone density improves and medical clearance obtained

Avoid: All strong forward bends, deep twists, Halasana, Sarvangasana (neck and spinal loading in osteoporotic spine); vigorous Surya Namaskara; any impact

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Nadi Suddhi (primary) — the most important pranayama for Cushing's: reduces sympathetic hyperactivity and normalises the ANS state that perpetuates HPA activation; the bi-directional balance of alternate nostril breathing is directly relevant to cortisol regulation
  • Bhramari — vagal activation; rapid reduction in sympathetic arousal and anxiety
  • Diaphragmatic breathing — establishes a calm, regular baseline; addresses the hyperventilation pattern associated with anxiety and the abdominal obesity-related respiratory restriction
  • Extended exhalation ratio breathing (1:2) — prolongs parasympathetic activation

Avoid: Kapalabhati (increases intra-abdominal pressure — risk with visceral obesity and potential hernias); strong Bhastrika.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body) — PRIMARY DOMAIN

The neuropsychiatric effects of cortisol excess (depression, anxiety, cognitive impairment, emotional lability) are the most distressing symptoms for many Cushing's patients and directly impair quality of life and treatment adherence.

  • Cyclic Meditation (primary) — the most evidenced yoga practice for cortisol reduction; documented to reduce salivary cortisol significantly; the active-passive cycling structure is both accessible (no strong physical demands) and deeply effective
  • DRT — repeated, regular practice provides frequent cortisol-reducing rest states; particularly important for iatrogenic Cushing's patients who cannot rapidly taper steroids
  • Yoga Nidra — addresses the sleep disruption common in Cushing's (hypercortisolaemia disrupts the normal diurnal cortisol nadir at night → insomnia, non-restorative sleep); Yoga Nidra before bedtime facilitates the parasympathetic shift needed for sleep initiation

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Distinguishing reducible from non-reducible stress: Many Cushing's patients are on long-term corticosteroids for legitimate medical reasons; Vijnanamaya work focuses on reducing unnecessary psychological stressors while maintaining compliance with essential medical treatment
  • Body image counselling: The physical changes of Cushing's (moon face, weight gain, striae, hirsutism) are profoundly distressing; yoga philosophy's perspective on the body as a vehicle rather than an identity provides genuine support
  • Understanding the cortisol-mood connection: Knowing that depression and anxiety are partly direct effects of cortisol excess (not personal weakness) reduces shame and supports engagement with mental health care

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • Inner silence as the antidote to cortisol excess: The yogic goal of reducing the "speed of mind" (Chitta Vritti Nirodha) is a direct Anandamaya intervention in chronic cortisol overproduction, for which the underlying stimulus is perpetual mental reactivity
  • Karma Yoga — acting with equanimity and without compulsive stress responses; the practice of reducing urgency and pressure in daily engagement

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Osteoporosis assessment required before any weight-bearing or axial loading asana
  • Hypertension monitoring — blood pressure before and after sessions; avoid practices that raise BP acutely (Kapalabhati, inversions, vigorous sequences)
  • Skin fragility — avoid practices requiring gripping, friction, or pressure on thin, easily bruised skin
  • Infection risk — immune suppression means any skin break (mat friction) can become a serious infection; strict hygiene, individual mat use
  • Iatrogenic Cushing's taper: Yoga therapy reduces the psychological distress that often leads patients to resist steroid reduction — supportive role in taper process
  • Adrenal insufficiency risk during taper: Patients tapering steroids can develop relative adrenal insufficiency; exercise caution as with Addison's during this phase
