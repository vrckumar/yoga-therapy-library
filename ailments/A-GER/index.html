Common Geriatric Illnesses

Common Geriatric Illnesses

1. Definition & Classification

Geriatric medicine addresses the health needs of older adults — typically those aged 65 and above — whose presentation, management, and rehabilitation differ from younger adults due to: multiple coexisting conditions (multimorbidity), polypharmacy, reduced physiological reserve, social isolation, and the unique interplay of physical, cognitive, and psychosocial factors in determining functional outcome.

The Geriatric Giants (common presentations):

  • Falls and instability
  • Confusion / delirium
  • Immobility / functional decline
  • Incontinence
  • Polypharmacy complications

Common geriatric conditions relevant to yoga therapy:

  • Sarcopenia and frailty
  • Osteoporosis with fracture risk
  • Osteoarthritis (particularly hip and knee)
  • Cognitive decline and mild cognitive impairment (MCI)
  • Depression (40% prevalence in hospitalised older adults)
  • Chronic pain
  • Cardiovascular disease (hypertension, heart failure, atrial fibrillation)
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
  • Social isolation and loneliness

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Sarcopenia — Loss of Muscle Mass and Function

After age 30, muscle mass decreases at approximately 0.5–1% annually; after 60, this accelerates. By age 80, individuals may have lost 30–50% of their peak muscle mass. The underlying mechanisms:

  • Anabolic hormone decline: Testosterone (men), oestrogen (women), growth hormone, and IGF-1 all decline with age, reducing the anabolic drive for muscle protein synthesis
  • Chronic low-grade inflammation (inflammageing): TNF-α, IL-6, and CRP promote muscle protein catabolism; the same cytokines impair satellite cell function
  • Motor neuron loss: Progressive loss of lower motor neurons reduces the number of motor units; the remaining motor neurons innervate more muscle fibres each (motor unit remodelling) — reduced precision and strength
  • Nutritional factors: Reduced appetite, protein intake below requirements, vitamin D deficiency (affects muscle as well as bone)
  • Inactivity: The primary modifiable factor; muscle responds to mechanical loading throughout life

Functional consequences of sarcopenia: Falls (leading cause of injury-related death in older adults), functional dependence, slow gait speed, reduced exercise tolerance, metabolic changes (reduced glucose disposal → insulin resistance).

Osteoporosis

Bone mass peaks around age 30, then declines. Oestrogen deficiency (menopause) produces accelerated trabecular bone loss — particularly in the first 5 years post-menopause. Age-related cortical bone loss follows more slowly. The result: increased fracture risk at the hip (50,000 annual deaths in the UK), vertebral bodies (compression fractures — height loss, kyphosis, chronic pain), wrist, and shoulder.

Reduced Neuroplasticity and Cognitive Decline

  • BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) production declines with age, reducing synaptic plasticity and hippocampal neurogenesis
  • Cerebral blood flow decreases; white matter hyperintensities accumulate (small vessel disease)
  • The default mode network shows reduced deactivation efficiency → increased mind-wandering, rumination
  • Normal ageing: mild slowing of processing speed, reduced working memory capacity
  • Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI): greater-than-expected memory or other cognitive decline; 10–15% annual conversion to dementia
  • Dementia (Alzheimer's, vascular, Lewy body): progressive loss of multiple cognitive domains

Exercise and meditation are the most evidence-supported interventions for maintaining brain health in ageing — both promote BDNF production, improve cerebrovascular function, and reduce dementia risk.

Cardiovascular Deconditioning

  • Reduced maximal heart rate (220 − age)
  • Reduced cardiac compliance (diastolic dysfunction predominates in older adults)
  • Increased arterial stiffness (aortic PWV increases with age)
  • Reduced baroreceptor sensitivity → orthostatic hypotension on standing
  • Reduced VO₂max (peak oxygen consumption) — falls approximately 1%/year after age 30

3. Causes & Symptoms

The Frailty Phenotype (Fried Criteria)

Frailty — the state of increased vulnerability to stressors — is characterised by three or more of:

  1. Unintentional weight loss (>4.5 kg in past year)
  2. Exhaustion (self-reported)
  3. Weakness (grip strength below threshold)
  4. Slow gait speed
  5. Low physical activity level

Frailty strongly predicts: falls, hospitalisation, functional dependence, institutionalisation, and death.

Falls — The Primary Clinical Concern

Falls occur in 30% of community-dwelling adults over 65 annually; 10% cause fractures. Risk factors:

  • Muscle weakness (sarcopenia)
  • Balance impairment (vestibular, visual, proprioceptive decline)
  • Gait abnormalities (shortened step length, reduced step height, reduced gait speed)
  • Polypharmacy (sedatives, antihypertensives causing orthostatic hypotension)
  • Environmental hazards (loose mats, poor lighting)
  • Fear of falling (itself causing further balance impairment through hypervigilance and stiffness)

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Sarcopenia/frailty: Progressive resistance training (most evidenced intervention); protein supplementation (1.2–1.5 g/kg/day); vitamin D; testosterone (selected cases)
  • Osteoporosis: Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate); denosumab; vitamin D + calcium; romosozumab for high fracture risk; weight-bearing exercise
  • Falls prevention: Multifactorial: exercise programme (balance + strength training); medication review; home hazard assessment; vision correction
  • Cognitive decline: Cognitive stimulation, social engagement, physical exercise (no effective pharmacological prevention); acetylcholinesterase inhibitors for dementia symptoms (modest benefit)
  • Depression: Antidepressants (caution with falls and cardiovascular risk); psychotherapy (CBT, interpersonal therapy); social engagement

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Geriatric yoga therapy is one of the most rewarding clinical applications of the yogic approach — older adults often have the time, reflective capacity, and motivation to engage deeply with yoga practice, and the benefits (improved balance, strength, cognition, mood, and social connection) are immediately functionally relevant.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Chair yoga — the foundation of geriatric yoga therapy: All traditional yoga asanas can be adapted to be performed seated in a chair or with chair support, eliminating fall risk while preserving the therapeutic benefits of joint mobilisation, muscular engagement, and breath-body coordination.

Chair-based Sakti Vikasaka (dynamic joint exercises):

  • Neck: Gentle rotation, forward-back, side bend
  • Shoulders: Circumduction, abduction-adduction
  • Wrists: Circumduction, flexion-extension
  • Hips: Seated hip rotation, knee raises
  • Ankles: Circumduction, dorsiflexion-plantarflexion

Balance practices with progressive challenge:

  • Vrikshasana (tree pose) with wall or chair support — single-leg balance challenge; trains proprioceptive system; graduated: fingertips on chair → one finger → no support as confidence and ability improve
  • Garudasana (eagle pose) — arm component — seated bilateral arm crossing; improves shoulder mobility; adds cognitive challenge (crossing midline)
  • Tandem standing/walking — heel-to-toe walking alongside a support rail; challenges the vestibular system

Strengthening:

  • Utkatasana (chair pose) — modified — chair pose seated to standing repeatedly; functional strength for rising from seated position (most important daily movement)
  • Virabhadrasana I (seated, supported) — hip flexor stretch; counters the hip flexor shortening of prolonged sitting

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Nadi Suddhi — supports sleep quality (prevalent insomnia in older adults), reduces anxiety, balances ANS (older adults show reduced parasympathetic reserve)
  • Bhramari — calming; beneficial for age-related anxiety, tinnitus (the humming can mask and habituate tinnitus perception), and the social isolation-related dysphoria common in geriatric populations
  • Diaphragmatic breathing re-education — older adults commonly adopt accessory-muscle-dominant breathing patterns; re-establishing diaphragmatic primacy improves oxygenation and reduces respiratory fatigue
  • OM chanting (group) — vibration meditation accessible to all; provides language stimulation (beneficial for cognitive maintenance), social engagement, and spiritual practice

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

  • DRT — addresses the chronic pain, insomnia, and depression prevalent in older adults through repeated deep neuromuscular and autonomic relaxation
  • Yoga Nidra (shorter version, 15 minutes) — adjusted for attention span; provides deep rest; beneficial for cognitive function through improved sleep architecture
  • Group meditation — social dimension is as important as the meditative content; combating isolation is itself a therapeutic intervention

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Cognitive stimulation through yoga: Learning new movement sequences, coordinating breath and movement, sequential practice — all provide cognitive challenge (motor learning engaging prefrontal, parietal, and cerebellar circuits)
  • Medication review discussion: Yoga therapy session provides opportunity to review polypharmacy effects (falls risk, sedation, orthostatic hypotension) — liaison with prescribing physician
  • Nutrition: Adequate protein, vitamin D, calcium; hydration awareness (older adults have reduced thirst sensation)

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Social engagement through group practice is the most important Anandamaya intervention in geriatric yoga therapy. The epidemic of loneliness and social isolation among older adults has health impacts equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes per day. The yoga group provides:

  • A regular reason to leave the house
  • Structured social interaction
  • Shared purpose and meaning
  • A sense of belonging and community

Devotional practices (kirtan, bhajan), story-sharing from yoga philosophy, and group reflection on meaning and ageing are all Anandamaya-level geriatric yoga therapy tools.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Falls risk: Non-negotiable chair or wall support for all balance practices; no inversions; non-slip surfaces; appropriate footwear
  • Osteoporosis: No strong forward bends, deep twists, Sarvangasana, Halasana; vertebral fracture risk
  • Cognitive impairment: Simplified instructions; repetition; cue cards with pictures; familiar sequences; familiar facilitator
  • Cardiac conditions: Monitor for dyspnoea, angina, palpitations; individualise intensity; AT limit awareness
  • Polypharmacy/orthostatic hypotension: Rise from seated slowly; pause before standing; monitor for dizziness on standing
  • Hearing impairment: Face patients when giving instructions; large clear gestures; buddy system in group
