Pregnancy Induced Hypertension (PIH)

Pregnancy Induced Hypertension (PIH)

1. Definition & Classification

Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension (PIH) is a form of high blood pressure that develops during pregnancy in a previously normotensive woman. It is defined as a blood pressure reading persistently above 140/90 mmHg arising after the 20th week of gestation in a woman who was normotensive before pregnancy. PIH affects 5–8% of all pregnancies and remains a leading cause of maternal and foetal morbidity and mortality worldwide.

Also known as toxaemia or gestational hypertension, PIH encompasses a spectrum from mild hypertension alone to the severe life-threatening conditions of pre-eclampsia (hypertension + proteinuria ± oedema) and eclampsia (pre-eclampsia + seizures).

Classification

1. Gestational Hypertension (mild PIH):

  • Blood pressure ≥ 140/90 mmHg after 20 weeks gestation
  • No proteinuria; no systemic features
  • Normalises after delivery

2. Pre-eclampsia:

  • Blood pressure ≥ 140/90 mmHg after 20 weeks
  • Proteinuria (≥ 300 mg/24 hours or ≥ 1+ on dipstick)
  • May include oedema (though oedema alone is not diagnostic)

3. Severe Pre-eclampsia:

  • Blood pressure ≥ 160/110 mmHg
  • Significant proteinuria
  • Headache, visual disturbances, epigastric pain, oliguria, pulmonary oedema

4. Eclampsia:

  • Pre-eclampsia + tonic-clonic seizures
  • Obstetric emergency; requires immediate hospitalisation

5. HELLP Syndrome:

  • Haemolysis + Elevated Liver enzymes + Low Platelets
  • Complication of severe pre-eclampsia; hepatic involvement

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Cardiovascular Changes in Normal Pregnancy

Pregnancy itself is a state of profound haemodynamic adaptation:

  • Blood volume increases by 40–50% (to meet foetal-placental demand)
  • Cardiac output increases 30–50% (increased heart rate + stroke volume)
  • Systemic vascular resistance (SVR) decreases in normal pregnancy — peripheral vasodilation accommodates the increased blood volume without hypertension
  • Blood pressure typically falls in the first and second trimester

The placenta plays a central role in vascular regulation — it normally produces vasodilatory factors (prostacyclin, nitric oxide) that reduce SVR and ensure adequate uteroplacental blood flow.

Pathophysiology of PIH

The exact cause of PIH remains incompletely understood, but the central mechanism involves:

  1. Abnormal placentation: In PIH, trophoblastic invasion of the uterine spiral arteries is incomplete. These arteries fail to dilate appropriately, remaining narrow, high-resistance vessels. The result: reduced uteroplacental blood flow.

  2. Endothelial dysfunction: Ischaemic placenta releases factors (sFlt-1, anti-angiogenic factors) that damage maternal endothelial cells systemically. Damaged endothelium loses its vasodilatory function — systemic vascular resistance rises → hypertension.

  3. Imbalance of vasodilators and vasoconstrictors: Reduced prostacyclin and nitric oxide; increased thromboxane A2 and endothelin → vasoconstriction → hypertension.

  4. Consequences of increased SVR:

    • Reduced organ perfusion: liver (elevated enzymes, epigastric pain), kidneys (proteinuria, reduced GFR), brain (headache, visual disturbances, seizures), placenta (foetal growth restriction)
    • Platelet consumption → coagulopathy
    • Pulmonary oedema from fluid redistribution

Stress and PIH

Chronic psychosocial stress activates the HPA axis, raising circulating cortisol and catecholamines. These increase vascular tone and blood pressure by direct vasoconstriction (alpha-1 adrenoceptor stimulation) and renin-angiotensin system activation. In a pregnant woman with the underlying placental pathology of PIH, stress further compounds the haemodynamic burden — a clear Manomaya-to-Annamaya Kosha pathway.

4. Conventional Treatment

Monitoring

  • Blood pressure measurement (lying, sitting, and standing)
  • Urinalysis (proteinuria)
  • Full blood count (platelets — HELLP screening)
  • Liver and kidney function tests
  • Foetal monitoring: CTG (cardiotocography), biophysical profile, Doppler umbilical artery studies
  • Frequent weight measurements

Medical Management

Mild PIH (outpatient):

  • Bed rest (though evidence is mixed)
  • Regular BP monitoring
  • Avoid high-salt foods; avoid NSAIDs
  • Antihypertensives if BP persistently ≥ 150/100: Methyldopa (safest in pregnancy), Labetalol, Nifedipine

Severe pre-eclampsia / Eclampsia (inpatient):

  • Magnesium sulphate (IV/IM) — prevention and treatment of eclamptic seizures
  • Antihypertensives (IV Labetalol or oral Nifedipine for hypertensive urgency)
  • Fluid management (avoid over-hydration)
  • Foetal monitoring
  • Delivery of baby — the definitive treatment; timing depends on gestational age and severity

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

PIH sits at the intersection of the physiological (Annamaya — placental vascular dysfunction), the autonomic (Pranamaya — ANS-driven vasoconstriction), and the psychological (Manomaya — stress-driven cortisol and catecholamine elevation worsening vascular tone). The yoga therapy protocol for PIH is highly specific: it must be:

  • Gentle enough for pregnancy (no inversions, no abdominal compression)
  • Focused on parasympathetic activation (ANS calming → vasodilation → BP reduction)
  • Non-stimulating (avoid Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Kumbhaka, strong pranayama)

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Gentle mobilisation to reduce peripheral oedema; improve venous return without increasing cardiac demand.

  • Sithilikarana Vyayama (specific PIH set — from PGDYT 104 Block 7):

    • Wrist loosening (each side, ½ min)
    • Pada Sancalana — ankle rotation (5 × each, ½ min) — critical for reducing ankle oedema and improving peripheral venous return
    • Finger loosening (½ min)
    • Shoulder rotation (½ min)
    • Drill walking in place (5 × 10 steps, 1 min)

  • Breathing practices as physical preparation:

    • Hands in and out breathing (5 rounds, 1 min) — coordinated arm movements with breath
    • Horizontal hand stretch (5 rounds, 1 min)
    • Ankle stretch breathing (5 rounds, ½ min)

  • Yogasanas (PIH-specific, from PGDYT 104 Block 7):

    • Ardhakati Cakrasana — lateral side bend; opens intercostal spaces; improves lymphatic flow
    • Baddha Konasana — butterfly pose; opens pelvic girdle; reduces lower limb oedema; safe throughout pregnancy
    • Gomukhasana (upper body only) — shoulder opening
    • Vajrasana — seated upright; promotes abdominal organ circulation without compression

  • Setu Bandhasana (with caution in late pregnancy): Bridge pose — mild inversion of legs above pelvis reduces ankle oedema, improves venous return. In late pregnancy, use supported version (bolster under sacrum).

Critical physical contraindications in PIH:

  • No inversions (Sarvangasana, Sirsasana, Halasana)
  • No supine lying flat after 16 weeks (compresses inferior vena cava — use left lateral or semi-recumbent position)
  • No abdominal compression (Nauli, Uddiyana, Kapalabhati)
  • No strong postures requiring breath retention

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Reduce sympathetic vascular tone; achieve sustained parasympathetic dominance; normalise blood pressure through vagal activation.

  • Nadi Suddhi (9 rounds): The primary pranayama for PIH. Balances sympathetic and parasympathetic activity; reduces peripheral vascular resistance through parasympathetic-mediated vasodilation. Safe in pregnancy when performed without Kumbhaka.
  • Sitali (9 rounds): Cooling pranayama — reduces vascular heat/tone; mildly lowers blood pressure. Safe in pregnancy.
  • Bhramari (5 rounds): Vagal resonance — immediate blood pressure reduction through parasympathetic shift. Safe in pregnancy.
  • PET (Pranic Energisation Technique): Directs healing prana to the uterine-placental circulation, promoting relaxation of vascular tone in the uteroplacental bed.
  • All pranayamas are performed without Kumbhaka (breath retention) — this is essential in pregnancy.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Reduce the anxiety, fear, and stress that compound physiological hypertension in PIH.

  • IRT (Instant Relaxation Technique) — 2 min: rapid neuromuscular release between asana sections.
  • QRT (Quick Relaxation Technique) — 3 min: brief autonomic reset used between sections.
  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) — 30 min (PGDYT 104 Block 7 specifically): extended in PIH protocol to achieve sustained parasympathetic dominance and blood pressure reduction.
  • Cyclic Meditation (CM) — 30 min: documented blood pressure reduction; safe in pregnancy when performed in semi-recumbent position (not supine flat).
  • Nadanusandhana — 9 min: sound-mediated relaxation.
  • OM Meditation (Dhyana) — 9 min: sustained meditative rest; reduces hypothalamic stress-hormone release.
  • MSRT (Mind Sound Resonance Technique) — 30 min: systematic mantra-based relaxation reaching deep ANS calming.
  • MIRT (Mind Imagery Relaxation Technique) — 30 min: guided imagery of safe, calm environments; reduces fear and anticipatory anxiety about labour and delivery.
  • MEMT (Mind Emotions Management Technique) — 30 min: systematic emotional processing; addresses the deeper emotional causes of sympathetic arousal.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Correct fear-based thinking about the pregnancy; develop trust in the body's capacity to sustain the pregnancy when supported by appropriate practices.

  • Notional correction: Understanding that most cases of PIH can be managed with appropriate care; that the baby is being monitored and protected; that the practices directly help vasodilation and pressure normalisation.
  • Happiness Analysis: Examining the sources of worry and replacing them with conscious appreciation of the present moment and the growing life.
  • Satsang: Community support for pregnant women — group practice provides normalisation and reduces isolation-based anxiety.
  • VISAK technique: For pregnancy-specific phobias (fear of delivery, fear of foetal harm).

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Connect with the natural bliss of pregnancy and creation; reduce the existential anxiety that increases vascular tone.

Karma Yoga applied to pregnancy: working (caring for the growing child) with complete present-moment attention and without excessive anxiety about outcomes. Enjoying the silence of deep rest — found in DRT and meditation — provides the most direct antidote to the chronically elevated sympathetic tone of PIH. Realising the self is the goal: the meditative discovery of inner stillness as independent of physical circumstances.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Severe pre-eclampsia or eclampsia: Medical management is the priority; yoga therapy is strictly adjunctive and must not delay medical treatment.
  • No inversions of any kind: Sarvangasana, Sirsasana, Halasana, Viparita Karani (in advanced pregnancy) — all contraindicated.
  • No supine lying flat after 16 weeks: Inferior vena cava compression reduces venous return to the heart. Substitute with left-lateral lying or semi-recumbent positions.
  • No Kumbhaka (breath retention): Increases intrathoracic pressure; may compromise foetal circulation.
  • No Kapalabhati or Bhastrika: Vigorous abdominal pumping is contraindicated in pregnancy.
  • No Nauli, Uddiyana, or abdominal locks.
  • Monitor blood pressure before, during, and after each session.
  • If BP > 160/100 during session: Stop all practice; rest in left-lateral position; seek immediate medical review.
  • Oedema monitoring: Daily weight measurement; sudden weight gain > 1 kg/week warrants urgent medical review.
  • Foetal monitoring: Any decrease in foetal movements during yoga practice sessions warrants immediate medical evaluation.
