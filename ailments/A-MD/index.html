Muscular Dystrophy

Muscular Dystrophy

1. Definition & Classification

Muscular dystrophies are a heterogeneous group of inherited neuromuscular disorders characterised by progressive skeletal muscle weakness and degeneration, resulting from mutations in genes encoding structural proteins of the muscle cell membrane (sarcolemma), the cytoskeleton, or the nuclear envelope.

Major types:

  • Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) — most common and severe; X-linked recessive; mutations in the dystrophin gene (Xp21); absent dystrophin protein; onset in early childhood (2–5 years); loss of ambulation by age 12; cardiomyopathy; death in 3rd–4th decade from respiratory failure or cardiomyopathy
  • Becker Muscular Dystrophy (BMD) — same gene as DMD but partial dystrophin function preserved; milder course; longer survival
  • Myotonic Dystrophy (DM1 — Steinert's disease) — most common adult MD; CTG repeat expansion in DMPK gene; myotonia (impaired muscle relaxation) + multi-system involvement (cardiac conduction, cataracts, endocrine, cognitive)
  • Facioscapulohumeral MD (FSHMD) — autosomal dominant; face, scapular, humeral muscles affected; very slow progression; normal lifespan
  • Limb-Girdle MD (LGMD) — multiple subtypes; proximal limb-girdle weakness; variable severity and genetics
  • Emery-Dreifuss MD — X-linked or AD; joint contractures + cardiac conduction defects (life-threatening arrhythmias)

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Muscle Cell Biology and Dystrophin

The skeletal muscle fibre is a specialised multinucleated cell capable of forceful contraction. The force generated by myosin-actin cross-bridging is transmitted from the sarcomere through the Z-disc and costamere to the sarcolemma and extracellular matrix, and ultimately to tendon and bone.

Dystrophin is a large (427 kDa) rod-shaped protein that links the intracellular actin cytoskeleton to the dystrophin-associated protein complex (DAPC) on the sarcolemma, which in turn connects to laminin in the extracellular matrix. This mechanical linkage:

  • Stabilises the sarcolemma during the mechanical stress of muscle contraction
  • Prevents membrane microruptures during forceful contraction-relaxation cycles
  • Mediates signalling between the extracellular matrix and the intracellular cytoskeleton

Without dystrophin (DMD):

  • Each contraction-relaxation cycle creates micro-tears in the sarcolemma
  • Calcium floods in through the membrane defects → calcium overload → mitochondrial dysfunction → muscle fibre death
  • Satellite cells (muscle stem cells) initially regenerate damaged fibres (explains the apparent normalcy in early childhood)
  • Progressive exhaustion of satellite cell pool → fibres cannot regenerate → replacement by fat and fibrous connective tissue → muscle wasting and weakness

Cardiomyopathy in MD

Dystrophin is also expressed in cardiac muscle. Dilated cardiomyopathy is universal in DMD (often precedes skeletal muscle symptoms), and is increasingly the primary cause of death as respiratory management improves. Myotonic dystrophy uniquely involves cardiac conduction tissue → life-threatening heart block and ventricular arrhythmias.

Respiratory Muscle Involvement

As thoracic and diaphragmatic muscle weakness progresses (particularly in DMD/BMD), respiratory reserve falls:

  • Vital capacity (VC) declines progressively; when VC < 1 L, nocturnal hypoventilation begins
  • NIV (non-invasive ventilation) is initiated when VC < 50% predicted or symptoms develop
  • Cough becomes ineffective (peak cough flow < 270 L/min) → inability to clear secretions → recurrent respiratory infections

3. Causes & Symptoms

Genetics

All muscular dystrophies are genetic:

  • DMD/BMD: X-linked; females are carriers (rarely mildly affected); new mutations account for 30%
  • Myotonic dystrophy: Autosomal dominant; anticipation (CTG repeat expands in successive generations → earlier onset, more severe disease)
  • FSHMD: AD; deletion of D4Z4 repeats on chromosome 4
  • LGMD: Multiple genes; both AD and AR subtypes

Progressive Course

The universal feature: muscle weakness that is progressive, irreversible, and follows each disease's specific pattern. Weakness typically begins proximally (difficulty climbing stairs, rising from floor, lifting arms over head).

DMD progression:

  • 2–5 years: Delay in walking, Gowers' sign (child climbs up own legs to rise from floor — proximal hip extensor weakness)
  • 5–12 years: Calf pseudohypertrophy (fat and fibrous infiltration, not true hypertrophy); joint contractures; scoliosis
  • 12–15 years: Loss of ambulation
  • Adulthood: Wheelchair-dependent; respiratory and cardiac management primary

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Corticosteroids (DMD): Deflazacort, prednisone — slow disease progression by anti-inflammatory and membrane-stabilising mechanisms; extend ambulation by 2–3 years; significant side effects (obesity, osteoporosis, behaviour)
  • Gene therapy (emerging): Exon-skipping (eteplirsen, golodirsen — restores partial dystrophin reading frame); gene replacement (for smaller dystrophies); dystrophin restoration therapy
  • Cardiac management: ACE inhibitors/ARBs, beta-blockers; ICD for lethal arrhythmias (myotonic dystrophy)
  • Respiratory: NIV (BiPAP), cough-assist devices, physiotherapy
  • Physiotherapy: Stretching to prevent contractures; hydrotherapy; standing programmes (weight-bearing slows bone demineralisation)
  • Orthotics: Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs); spinal supports for scoliosis
  • Surgical: Tendon releases for contractures; spinal fusion for scoliosis

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for muscular dystrophy is one of the most medically complex and clinically sensitive applications. The progressive, irreversible nature of the disease means that yoga therapy does not aim to reverse weakness but to:

  1. Preserve existing function for as long as possible
  2. Prevent secondary complications (contractures, respiratory complications, disuse atrophy superimposed on primary weakness)
  3. Support psychological wellbeing and quality of life
  4. Address the pranamaya (energy/breath) dimension — respiratory health is life-prolonging

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Extremely gentle; passive mobilisation primary in advanced stages:

The absolute constraint: any muscle overwork in muscular dystrophy can cause additional muscle fibre damage (eccentric contractions are particularly damaging). The principle is to work within capacity, not to push against it.

  • Passive mobilisation: In advanced DMD, the yoga therapist (or carer) gently moves each limb through its range of motion — hips, knees, ankles (plantar flexion and dorsiflexion are critical — prevents equinus contracture), shoulders, elbows, wrists. This maintains joint mobility without any active muscular effort from the patient
  • Gentle active-assisted range of motion: In milder cases (early DMD, BMD, FSHMD) — patient moves limbs with the therapist providing support through part of the range; prevents the atrophy that complete inactivity produces
  • Hydrotherapy principle (warm pool): If available, buoyancy-supported movement allows fuller range of motion with minimal gravitational load on weak muscles; the yoga-in-water approach is optimal for moderate–advanced DMD
  • Supported standing (when ambulatory): Maintaining upright weight-bearing slows bone demineralisation; prevents the postural deformities of prolonged sitting; can be done at a standing frame or parallel bars

Avoid: Any overexertion; eccentric loading (lowering heavy weights); prolonged unassisted effort that produces muscle soreness (indicates damage); deep stretching of already weak, potentially fibrotic muscles.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body) — CRITICAL DOMAIN

Respiratory health is literally life-prolonging in muscular dystrophy. Yoga therapy's pranayama contribution:

  • Diaphragmatic breathing (primary) — re-establishes and maintains diaphragmatic breathing pattern as long as diaphragmatic function remains; every diaphragmatic breath strengthens the remaining diaphragmatic motor units and maintains alveolar recruitment
  • PET (Pranayama Energy Technique) — adapted gentle breathing practice that does not challenge weakened respiratory muscles but maintains breath awareness and lung expansion
  • Incentive spirometry equivalent (inspiratory muscle training): Can be incorporated as a yoga-based breathing exercise — maximal sustained inhalation (within comfortable range) trains inspiratory intercostals and accessory muscles
  • Cough augmentation: Supported cough (caregiver-assisted abdominal compression) can be taught as a yoga-adjacent skill — the exhalation-cough sequence; improves secretion clearance

For patients on NIV: Yoga pranayama continues alongside NIV; breathing practices scheduled in NIV-free periods; coordination with respiratory team.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

Muscular dystrophy — particularly DMD from childhood — carries a profound psychological burden: the progressive loss of motor milestones achieved, the knowledge of the disease course, family grief, and the existential challenges of living in a progressively dependent body.

  • DRT — accessible at all stages; provides rest and relaxation that is meaningful regardless of physical capacity; the "rest in stillness" of DRT is particularly valuable for patients whose disease forces physical stillness anyway
  • Yoga Nidra — particularly accessible for non-ambulatory patients; provides deep inner rest and a positive relationship with the inner life that does not depend on physical function
  • PET as energy management — breath practices as active tools for managing the fatigue and low-energy states characteristic of respiratory-compromised MD

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Understanding the disease: Realistic, compassionate education about MD progression; working with the medical team to set achievable, meaningful functional goals
  • Pacing and energy conservation: Understanding activity tolerance; avoiding the boom-bust cycle of overexertion and extended recovery
  • Advance care planning: Yoga therapists may play a supportive role as patients and families navigate decisions about ventilation, gastrostomy, and end-of-life care — Vijnanamaya work that is practically informed by yoga philosophy's approach to death and suffering

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

  • PET as connection to the inner energy body: Even when the physical body is severely weakened, the breath connects the patient to the pranamaya dimension — the sense of being a living, breathing being with inner life and presence is an Anandamaya gift
  • Devotional music: Listening and gentle participation in devotional music engages the Anandamaya dimension without physical demand; can be practised from a wheelchair or bed
  • Karma Yoga of acceptance: The deep yoga philosophical work of finding meaning, peace, and even grace within a profoundly limited body is the ultimate Anandamaya offering to MD patients and their families

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Medical supervision mandatory — all yoga therapy must be coordinated with the neuromuscular specialist, physiotherapist, and respiratory team
  • No overexertion — must be strictly enforced; rhabdomyolysis (muscle fibre breakdown with myoglobinuria) can be precipitated by unaccustomed exercise
  • Cardiac monitoring — cardiomyopathy (DMD) and arrhythmia risk (myotonic dystrophy); cardiac clearance before any active practice
  • Respiratory monitoring — if VC < 50% predicted: no practices that significantly increase respiratory demand; coordinate with respiratory team
  • Corticosteroid effects — bone fragility (avoid strong weight-bearing postures in patients on long-term steroids); obesity (modified positional awareness); mood effects
  • Scoliosis — avoid lateral bending asanas in patients with established or progressing scoliosis; spinal support may be needed
