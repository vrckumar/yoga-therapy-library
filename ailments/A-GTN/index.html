Goiters / Thyroid Nodules

1. Definition & Classification

A goitre is an enlargement of the thyroid gland, visible or palpable as a swelling at the front of the neck. Goitre does not specify thyroid function — the gland may be enlarged with normal, reduced, or increased hormone production. A thyroid nodule is a discrete lump within an otherwise normal or enlarged thyroid gland; most are benign, but approximately 5–10% may be malignant.

Classification of goitre by aetiology:

  • Simple (non-toxic) goitre — diffuse enlargement without over- or underfunction; most commonly iodine deficiency
  • Endemic goitre — goitre in >10% of a population due to iodine deficiency in soil/water
  • Toxic multinodular goitre — multiple nodules, with autonomous hormone production → hyperthyroidism
  • Colloid goitre — accumulation of thyroid colloid in dilated follicles; typically benign, asymptomatic
  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis — autoimmune goitre with hypothyroidism
  • Graves' disease — autoimmune goitre with hyperthyroidism
  • Thyroid adenoma / carcinoma — solitary nodule requiring investigation

Grading (WHO):

  • Grade 0: No goitre
  • Grade 1: Goitre palpable but not visible with neck in normal position
  • Grade 2: Goitre visible with neck in normal position

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Thyroid Gland Structure

The thyroid gland is a bilobar gland lying anterior to the trachea in the lower neck, connected by the isthmus. It weighs 20–30 g in adults and has a rich blood supply (the superior and inferior thyroid arteries). Microscopically, it consists of spherical follicles lined by follicular epithelium and filled with colloid (thyroglobulin — the storage form of thyroid hormone). A second cell population, parafollicular (C) cells, produces calcitonin.

Thyroid Hormone Synthesis

  1. Iodide trapping: the thyroid actively concentrates iodine from blood (sodium-iodide symporter)
  2. Oxidation: iodide → iodine (thyroid peroxidase)
  3. Organification: iodine + tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin → MIT (monoiodotyrosine) and DIT (diiodotyrosine)
  4. Coupling: MIT + DIT → T3; DIT + DIT → T4 (stored as thyroglobulin in follicular colloid)
  5. Secretion: TSH stimulates thyroglobulin proteolysis → T3 and T4 released into blood
  6. Peripheral conversion: T4 (prohormone) → T3 (active form, 3–5× more potent) in peripheral tissues

Regulation: Hypothalamus (TRH) → pituitary (TSH) → thyroid → T3/T4. Negative feedback: rising T3/T4 suppresses TSH and TRH.

Iodine Deficiency Mechanism

Insufficient iodine → inadequate T4 synthesis → reduced T4 blood levels → TSH rises (loss of negative feedback) → TSH stimulates thyroid follicular cell proliferation → gland enlarges in compensation → diffuse goitre. Over time, areas of autonomous growth develop → nodular goitre.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes of Goitre

Cause Mechanism
Iodine deficiency Reduced T4 → TSH rise → glandular hyperplasia
Hashimoto's thyroiditis Autoimmune lymphocytic infiltration
Graves' disease TSH-receptor stimulating antibodies
Puberty / pregnancy Increased iodine demand; oestrogen effects
Goitrogens (dietary) Raw brassicas (Brussels sprouts, cabbage) contain thiocyanates that block iodine uptake
Medications Lithium, amiodarone, propylthiouracil
Thyroid carcinoma Malignant growth (usually solitary cold nodule)

Symptoms

  • From gland enlargement: Cosmetic concern; compressive symptoms — difficulty swallowing (dysphagia), difficulty breathing (dyspnoea), voice change (recurrent laryngeal nerve compression in large goitres)
  • Associated hypothyroid symptoms (if goitre + hypothyroidism): fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin
  • Associated hyperthyroid symptoms (toxic nodular goitre): weight loss, palpitations, heat intolerance, tremor, anxiety

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Iodine deficiency goitre: Dietary iodine supplementation (iodised salt); iodine supplementation in endemic areas
  • Hypothyroid goitre: Levothyroxine replacement (suppresses TSH → reduces glandular stimulation)
  • Toxic nodular goitre: Anti-thyroid drugs; radioiodine ablation; surgery
  • Thyroid nodules: Fine-needle aspiration cytology (FNAC) to exclude malignancy; monitoring benign nodules; surgical excision for malignant or compressive nodules

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for goitre and thyroid nodules is supportive — it addresses the functional dimensions of thyroid health (autonomic regulation, stress, circulation) without directly modifying structural enlargement. It is particularly valuable alongside treatment of iodine-deficiency goitre and Hashimoto's-related goitre, where stress and immune dysregulation play contributing roles.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Asanas targeting the neck and thyroid region:

  • Sarvangasana (Shoulderstand) — the foremost asana for thyroid health in the yogic tradition. The chin lock (Jalandhara Bandha — chin to sternum) created in Sarvangasana is thought to increase thyroid blood flow and stimulate glandular activity through mechanical compression-release. Particularly indicated for hypothyroid-associated goitre
  • Matsyasana (Fish Pose) — counterpose to Sarvangasana; the neck extends in the opposite direction, stretching the anterior neck structures and stimulating the thyroid region from the anterior aspect
  • Ustrasana (Camel Pose) — full anterior neck extension; broad thyroid stimulation
  • Halasana (Plough) — deepens the chin lock begun in Sarvangasana
  • Neck Sakti Vikasaka — slow circumduction, forward/back, side bend; improves cervical circulation

Simha Mudra (Lion's gesture): Maximum mouth opening with tongue protrusion, combined with breath, stimulates the submandibular and thyroid regions.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Ujjayi pranayama — the partial glottic closure and audible breath creates vibration in the larynx and anterior neck; believed to stimulate the thyroid gland through local vibration and improved regional vascularity
  • Bhramari — humming creates sustained vibration in the head, neck, and chest; pharyngeal and thyroidal vibration may support regional circulation
  • Nadi Suddhi — fundamental ANS balancing; relevant for both hypothyroid patients (who often have autonomic sluggishness) and hyperthyroid patients (who show autonomic hyperactivity)

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

Autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto's, Graves') and stress are strongly linked — cortisol dysregulation affects T4→T3 conversion, and sustained psychological stress may trigger or exacerbate autoimmune activity.

  • DRT — reduces the cortisol burden and normalises T4→T3 conversion
  • Cyclic Meditation — documented to reduce cortisol; relevant for autoimmune thyroid management
  • Devotional practices — emotional stabilisation; reduces hypothalamic reactivity that disrupts TRH-TSH feedback

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Dietary counselling: Iodine-adequate nutrition (seafood, iodised salt, dairy); avoidance of excessive raw goitrogens if iodine deficiency is suspected
  • Medication compliance for goitre requiring treatment (levothyroxine, anti-thyroid drugs)
  • Understanding stress-thyroid connection empowers lifestyle modification

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

For patients with cosmetically distressing goitre or those facing surgery for thyroid cancer, the Anandamaya dimension — equanimity, Santosha (contentment), and the yogic perspective on impermanence of the physical body — provides genuine psychological support beyond pharmacological and surgical management.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Large compressive goitre: Sarvangasana and Halasana are contraindicated if the goitre is large enough to cause tracheal compression — posture-related worsening of dyspnoea is a risk
  • Thyroid carcinoma: Medical treatment is primary; avoid strong neck pressure before surgical clearance
  • Graves' disease / hyperthyroidism: Avoid vigorous, heating practices; Kapalabhati and Bhastrika; monitor heart rate (atrial fibrillation risk)
  • Hashimoto's with hypothyroidism: May coexist with thyroid cancer (lymphoma risk); any rapid goitre growth requires urgent medical evaluation regardless of ongoing yoga therapy
