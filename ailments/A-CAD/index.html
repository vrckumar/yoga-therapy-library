Chemical Abuse / Substance Dependence

1. Definition & Classification

Substance dependence (addiction) is a chronic, relapsing neurobiological disorder characterised by compulsive substance use despite harmful consequences, loss of control over use, and persistence of use in the face of strong motivation to stop. The ICD-11 classifies substance use disorders along a continuum from harmful use to dependence.

Substance classes commonly involved:

  • Alcohol (most prevalent; legal status makes normalisation of harmful use common)
  • Tobacco/nicotine (highest global prevalence of dependence)
  • Cannabis (most widely used illicit drug)
  • Opioids (heroin, prescription opioids — highest mortality risk)
  • Stimulants (cocaine, amphetamines, methamphetamine)
  • Sedatives and benzodiazepines
  • Inhalants (common in adolescent abuse in low-income settings)

Diagnostic criteria (DSM-5/ICD-11): Impaired control, social impairment, risky use, pharmacological criteria (tolerance, withdrawal).

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The Reward Pathway — Mesolimbic Dopamine System

Addiction hijacks the brain's natural reward circuit — a system evolved to reinforce adaptive behaviours (eating, reproduction, social bonding) by releasing dopamine in the nucleus accumbens when those behaviours occur.

Key structures:

  • Ventral tegmental area (VTA) — dopaminergic neuron cell bodies
  • Nucleus accumbens (NAc) — primary reward centre; receives dopaminergic projections from VTA; activity here generates the subjective "high" and motivational drive toward reward
  • Prefrontal cortex (PFC) — executive control over the NAc; when functional, inhibits impulsive reward-seeking; addiction weakens this inhibitory control
  • Amygdala — encodes emotional memories associated with drug use; drives craving in response to environmental cues (conditioned stimuli)
  • Hippocampus — contextual memory of drug use; triggers cravings when encountering previously drug-associated environments

What substances do: All addictive substances increase dopamine in the nucleus accumbens, either directly (cocaine, amphetamines block dopamine reuptake; nicotine stimulates nicotinic acetylcholine receptors on VTA neurons) or indirectly (opioids inhibit GABAergic interneurons that normally restrain VTA dopamine neurons, releasing the "brake" and causing dopamine to flood the NAc; alcohol similarly disinhibits dopamine release).

Neuroadaptation: With repeated use, the brain compensates for the dopamine flood by downregulating dopamine receptors and reducing natural dopamine production. The result: the same dose produces less effect (tolerance); without the substance, baseline dopamine activity falls below normal (anhedonia, dysphoria — the negative reinforcement driving continued use to feel "normal"). The PFC's inhibitory capacity deteriorates, reducing voluntary control over drug-seeking.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Risk Factors

Category Risk Factors
Genetic Family history (heritability 40–70% for alcohol); specific receptor variants
Psychological PTSD, depression, anxiety, trauma history, adverse childhood experiences
Social Peer influence, availability, family dysfunction, poverty, social exclusion
Pharmacological Early initiation, route of administration (IV > inhaled > oral), substance potency

Signs of Dependence

  • Increasing doses required for same effect (tolerance)
  • Physical or psychological withdrawal symptoms on cessation
  • Persistent desire to reduce use without success
  • Continued use despite knowing the harm
  • Preoccupation with obtaining and using the substance
  • Neglect of roles, relationships, and health

Withdrawal Syndromes

  • Alcohol: Autonomic hyperactivity (tremor, sweating, tachycardia), anxiety, insomnia; severe: seizures, delirium tremens (potentially fatal)
  • Opioids: Flu-like syndrome, pain, anxiety, insomnia, diarrhoea — intensely unpleasant but rarely fatal
  • Stimulants: Crash (fatigue, hypersomnia, depression, anhedonia) — no acute medical emergency but severe psychological distress
  • Nicotine: Irritability, anxiety, increased appetite, cravings

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Detoxification: Medical management of withdrawal (alcohol/benzodiazepines: chlordiazepoxide taper; opioids: methadone or buprenorphine substitution or clonidine for symptom control)
  • Medication-assisted treatment (MAT): Naltrexone (opioid and alcohol dependence), buprenorphine-naloxone (opioid dependence), varenicline/NRT (nicotine), acamprosate (alcohol)
  • Psychosocial interventions: Motivational interviewing (MI), cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), 12-step programmes (AA, NA), contingency management, residential rehabilitation
  • Harm reduction: Needle exchange, opioid agonist therapy, naloxone distribution
  • Relapse is common and expected — average 3–4 treatment episodes before sustained recovery

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy addresses addiction across all five koshas, with particular strength at the Manomaya level (craving reduction through ANS modulation) and the Anandamaya level (providing an alternative source of inner satisfaction that reduces the existential emptiness driving substance use).

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

The physical body in substance dependence typically shows: deconditioning (from inactivity during active addiction), nutritional deficiency (alcohol displaces nutrition; stimulant use suppresses appetite), and the sequelae of substance-specific organ damage (hepatic, pulmonary, cardiovascular).

  • VISAK (Visualisation and Imagination with Self-Awareness and Kinaesthetics) — begins with gentle, awareness-based movement that rebuilds the somatic self-schema disrupted by chronic substance use
  • Loosening exercises and Surya Namaskara — restore physical condition, provide the endorphin-mediated "natural high" that begins to offer an alternative reward pathway
  • Pavanamuktasana series — addresses constipation common in opioid dependence and poor nutrition

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Nadi Suddhi (primary practice) — documented reduction in craving intensity through ANS normalisation; the rhythmic alternating breath pattern calms the hyperactivated limbic system and brings the nervous system into a state that contrasts with craving-driven agitation
  • Bhramari — rapid calming via vagal stimulation; useful during acute craving episodes
  • Kapalabhati (when stable) — provides energisation and mood lift through respiratory stimulation as an alternative to stimulant-induced arousal

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) — provides the experience of profound relaxation without substances; each DRT session demonstrates to the patient that deep rest states are accessible naturally
  • Yoga Nidra — the hypnagogic deep rest state offers an analogue to the sedation sought by alcohol and opioid users; regularly practiced, it significantly reduces the appeal of substance-induced sedation
  • Cyclic Meditation — reduces the cortisol dysregulation underlying the anxiety that drives self-medication

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

  • Psychoeducation about the neuroscience of addiction — understanding how the reward pathway has been hijacked, and how yoga reverses this, activates intellectual engagement with recovery
  • Notional correction — examining and challenging the belief systems that sustain substance use: "I need it to cope," "I can't feel normal without it," "Recovery is impossible"
  • Yoga philosophy's concept of Viveka (discrimination) — training the intellect to distinguish short-term sensory gratification from lasting wellbeing

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body) — PRIMARY ANANDA FOCUS

The root of addiction, in the yogic framework, is the search for bliss (Ananda) in the wrong places — through chemical alteration of consciousness rather than through the natural Ananda accessible through meditative stillness.

  • Karma Yoga — purposeful, selfless action provides the sense of meaning and contribution that substances temporarily provide; recovering addicts who engage in service demonstrate markedly better outcomes
  • Devotional practices — bhajan, kirtan, and group meditation provide the community, positive affect, and sense of connection to something larger than oneself that the drinking/using culture had previously supplied
  • Sustained meditation — as practice deepens, the glimpses of inner stillness and natural wellbeing (Sahaja Ananda) progressively demonstrate that the substance-induced state was a pale imitation of what is available through yoga

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Medical clearance required before yoga during active withdrawal — particularly for alcohol (seizure risk), opioids (autonomic instability)
  • Kapalabhati and Bhastrika: Avoid in active alcohol/benzo withdrawal (lowers seizure threshold); introduce cautiously once stable
  • Dehydration: Common in active substance use; ensure hydration before practice
  • Liver damage (alcoholic hepatitis/cirrhosis): Avoid strong abdominal compression and inversions
  • Cardiac complications (stimulant abuse): Monitor heart rate; avoid vigorous practice until cardiac assessment
  • Concurrent psychiatric illness (dual diagnosis): Very common; coordinate yoga therapy with psychiatric management
  • Relapse sensitivity: Do not use shame-based language; build resilience for setbacks without catastrophising
