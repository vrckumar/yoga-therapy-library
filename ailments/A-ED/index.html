Eating Disorders (Anorexia Nervosa / Bulimia Nervosa)

1. Definition & Classification

Eating disorders are severe, life-threatening psychiatric conditions characterised by persistent disturbances in eating behaviour and related thoughts and emotions, significantly impairing physical health and psychosocial functioning.

Anorexia Nervosa (AN):

  • Restriction of energy intake relative to requirements, leading to significantly low body weight (BMI < 17.5 kg/m² in adults)
  • Intense fear of gaining weight or persistent behaviour that prevents weight gain
  • Disturbance in perception of body weight or shape (body image distortion)
  • Subtypes: Restricting type (dieting, fasting, excessive exercise) and Binge-eating/purging type
  • Has the highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder (5–10% per decade)

Bulimia Nervosa (BN):

  • Recurrent episodes of binge eating (eating large amounts rapidly, feeling out of control)
  • Recurrent compensatory behaviours to prevent weight gain: self-induced vomiting, laxative misuse, diuretic use, excessive exercise, fasting
  • Episodes occur at least once per week for 3 months
  • Self-evaluation unduly influenced by body shape and weight
  • BMI is typically normal or above normal

Other types: Binge Eating Disorder (BED — binges without purging), ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder — not driven by body image), OSFED.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Neurobiological Mechanisms

Body image distortion — Interoceptive dysfunction: The parietal cortex, insular cortex, and somatosensory cortex together generate the brain's representation of the body. In anorexia, these circuits systematically overestimate body size — patients genuinely perceive themselves as larger than they are, not merely claim to. This is a neurobiological distortion, not simply a cognitive error, which explains why rational argument alone rarely resolves it.

Reward dysregulation:

  • In AN, reward circuitry shows an inverted response to food: eating produces anxiety (via amygdala) rather than pleasure (via nucleus accumbens). Food restriction paradoxically reduces anxiety (negative reinforcement).
  • In BN, the binge episode produces brief relief from tension or dysphoria (through dopamine release and the disinhibition associated with rapid eating), followed by guilt and compensatory behaviours.

Serotonin dysregulation:

  • Serotonin affects both appetite and anxiety. In AN, elevated cerebrospinal fluid 5-HIAA (serotonin metabolite) suggests increased serotonergic tone — which may contribute to the hyperanxiety, perfectionism, and obsessionality characteristic of AN
  • Binge eating temporarily depletes serotonin → mood improvement during binge, then crash

HPA axis and malnutrition: In severe AN, starvation activates the HPA axis — elevated cortisol, elevated growth hormone (paradoxically unable to promote IGF-1 production due to low nutritional substrate). The combination produces the cachexia, osteoporosis, and immune impairment of chronic starvation.

Physical Consequences of Eating Disorders

Anorexia:

  • Cardiovascular: Bradycardia, hypotension, QTc prolongation (arrhythmia risk — primary cause of sudden death)
  • Bone: Osteoporosis (oestrogen deficiency + malnutrition; potentially irreversible)
  • Endocrine: Hypothalamic amenorrhoea; low T3 (euthyroid sick syndrome); elevated cortisol
  • Renal: Hypokalaemia (purging subtype or diuretic misuse); acute kidney injury
  • Haematological: Pancytopenia (bone marrow suppression)

Bulimia:

  • Parotid gland enlargement (sialadenosis from repeated vomiting)
  • Dental erosion (gastric acid)
  • Hypokalaemia (from vomiting) → arrhythmias, muscle weakness
  • Oesophageal Mallory-Weiss tears

3. Causes & Symptoms

Risk Factors

Category Factors
Psychological Perfectionism, obsessionality, high negative affect, history of trauma/abuse, low self-esteem
Sociocultural Thin-ideal internalisation, social comparison, media exposure, dieting culture
Biological Female sex (10:1 F:M for AN), family history (heritability 50–80% for AN), adolescent onset
Developmental Adverse childhood experiences, emotional dysregulation, insecure attachment
Precipitating Puberty, critical comments about weight, life transitions, trauma

Clinical Presentation

AN: Weight loss, amenorrhoea, lanugo hair, dry skin, cold extremities, bradycardia, hypotension, fatigue, food rituals, social isolation, denial of illness severity.

BN: Often normal weight; secret binge-purge cycles; dental erosion; Russell's sign (calluses on knuckles from self-induced vomiting); electrolyte abnormalities; shame and secrecy.

4. Conventional Treatment

  • Medical stabilisation (AN): Nutritional rehabilitation — may require hospital admission for refeeding syndrome monitoring; cardiac monitoring; bone density management
  • Psychotherapy: Family-based therapy (FBT) — evidence-based first-line for adolescent AN; Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Eating Disorders (CBT-E) for adults; Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT) for emotional dysregulation; Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)
  • Medication: SSRIs for BN (fluoxetine, FDA-approved); limited medication evidence for AN; olanzapine for weight restoration in severe AN (weight gain, anxiety reduction)
  • Multidisciplinary team: Psychiatrist, psychologist/therapist, dietitian, physician, yoga therapist

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Yoga therapy for eating disorders requires exceptional clinical sensitivity and must operate within a comprehensive multidisciplinary treatment framework. The primary domain is Manomaya (body image, emotional regulation), with Vijnanamaya (cognitive reframing) and Anandamaya (inner nourishment) providing the distinctive yogic contributions.

Core principle: Yoga therapy for eating disorders is NOT primarily about physical exercise, weight, or calories. It is about restoring a compassionate, non-judgmental relationship with the body — working from the inside out rather than the outside in.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

  • Gentle, non-vigorous asanas — specifically chosen not to be weight-reducing in character; focus on flexibility, balance, and body awareness rather than exertion
  • Yin yoga or restorative yoga — held, supported postures that develop the capacity to be still with the body, accepting sensations without acting to change them
  • Body scan awareness (not DRT in isolation) — practised with explicit attention to neutral or positive physical sensations (warmth, breath, heartbeat) to counter the catastrophically negative interoceptive experience of body image distortion
  • Avoid all vigorous sequences (Bhastrika, rapid Surya Namaskara) — these can be used compulsively for calorie burning in AN patients; the therapeutic approach is non-compulsive, body-compassionate movement

Medical monitoring: In AN with BMI < 15, even gentle physical practice requires cardiac clearance; exercise of any kind is contraindicated in severe medical compromise (bradycardia HR < 40, hypotension).

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

  • Gentle diaphragmatic breathing — re-establishing a relaxed relationship with the abdomen (which AN patients often consciously hold rigid); the breath naturally moves the belly — acceptance of this movement is therapeutic
  • MIRT (Mind-body Integration and Relaxation Training) — specifically targets interoceptive dysregulation by systematically building a neutral, accepting awareness of body sensations
  • Bhramari — calming; provides internal sound resonance that draws attention to the interior of the body in a non-threatening way

Avoid: Kapalabhati (strong abdominal awareness in eating disorder context may be uncomfortable; also stimulating in a way that could increase food-related anxiety); any practices that feel like calorie-burning.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body) — PRIMARY DOMAIN

  • Yoga Nidra with body compassion focus — systematic body awareness practice; the body-rotation sequence in Yoga Nidra builds a complete, non-judgmental internal body image; as the therapist's voice moves through body parts, the explicit intention is neutral awareness, not evaluation
  • DRT — releases the chronic muscular tension associated with body vigilance (constant checking, measuring, comparing); demonstrates that the body can be a source of rest rather than only a source of anxiety
  • Meditation on inner qualities — deliberately shifting the locus of self-worth from body shape to inner qualities: patience, kindness, courage, intelligence — Manomaya work that begins to loosen the equation of self-worth with weight

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body) — REFRAMING BODY IMAGE

  • Notional correction: The yogic framework challenges the cultural thin-ideal at its root: the body (Annamaya Kosha) is the least important of the five sheaths — not a measure of worth, not the self
  • Viveka (discrimination): Developing the capacity to distinguish the conditioned social narrative ("thin = worthy") from the deeper truth of one's own value and identity
  • Counselling on the treatment-recovery process: Understanding refeeding syndrome (why careful medical monitoring is essential during weight restoration in AN); understanding that BN binges are neurobiologically driven (not moral failures)
  • Vijnanamaya work must be concurrent with, not instead of, formal psychotherapy — yoga therapy does not replace CBT-E, FBT, or DBT

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body) — INNER NOURISHMENT

The deepest yogic insight into eating disorders: the restriction or purging behaviour is, at its root, a misguided search for control, purity, or transcendence of the body's needs. The Anandamaya approach:

  • Inner nourishment as a spiritual concept — introducing the idea that genuine nourishment (Ananda) comes from stillness, connection, creativity, love, and meaning — not from achieving a particular body weight
  • Karma Yoga — purposeful engagement with the world beyond the body; contributing to others; finding meaning that is independent of physical appearance
  • Community and belonging — the yoga group provides the social connection and non-judgmental acceptance that counters the shame-driven isolation of eating disorders

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Medical clearance mandatory: BMI < 15, HR < 40, BP < 90/60 — no physical practice without cardiac/medical clearance
  • No vigorous practice — compulsive exercise is a maintaining factor in AN; yoga therapist must recognise and not reinforce exercise behaviour
  • No BMI or weight discussion during sessions — all references to weight are handled by the dietitian/physician
  • Extreme sensitivity required in body awareness work — never comment on physical appearance; frame all body-awareness practices as neutral observation
  • Refeeding monitoring — yoga therapy may continue during nutritional rehabilitation but must be coordinated with medical team monitoring for refeeding syndrome (hypophosphataemia, cardiac changes)
  • Compulsive practice monitoring — some patients use yoga itself as compulsive exercise; monitor for excessive home practice and set appropriate limits
