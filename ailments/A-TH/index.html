Tension Headache

Tension Headache

1. Definition & Classification

Tension-Type Headache (TTH) is the most common primary headache disorder, affecting an estimated 40% of the global population at some point. It is characterised by mild-to-moderate, bilateral, pressing or tightening (non-throbbing) head pain, typically described as a "band-like" or "vice-like" pressure around the head. Unlike migraine, TTH is generally not worsened by physical activity, not associated with significant nausea or vomiting, and does not usually include photophobia or phonophobia (though mild sensitivity to one may occur).

TTH is the primary headache most directly caused by muscular tension, psychological stress, and posture — making it the most accessible to yoga therapy intervention. In the yogic model, TTH is a direct Manomaya-to-Annamaya Kosha translation: the excessive mental tension of modern life solidifies into pericranial muscular contraction, which restricts circulation and creates the characteristic head pressure.

Classification (ICHD-3)

Infrequent Episodic TTH: Less than 1 day/month (fewer than 12 days/year). Minimal impact on functioning.

Frequent Episodic TTH: 1–14 days/month (≥12 and <180 days/year). Significant impact. The most important category for yoga therapy.

Chronic TTH: ≥15 days/month for >3 months. Often associated with medication overuse; significant disability.

2. Anatomy & Physiology

Pericranial Muscles and the Headache Mechanism

The "tension" in tension headache refers primarily to increased tenderness and sustained contraction of the pericranial muscles — those surrounding and attached to the skull:

  • Temporalis: Fan-shaped muscle over the temple; temporal headache
  • Masseter and pterygoids: Jaw muscles; contributes to temporal and jaw pain; worsened by bruxism (teeth grinding, common in stress)
  • Sternocleidomastoid (SCM): Large neck muscle; referred pain to the occiput and forehead
  • Trapezius (upper fibres): Most commonly implicated — the classic "stiff neck/shoulders" of chronic stress. Chronic contraction → local ischaemia → accumulation of metabolites (bradykinin, serotonin, potassium) → sensitisation of local nociceptors → referred head pain.
  • Sub-occipital muscles: Small muscles at the base of the skull; compress the greater occipital nerve when chronically contracted → occipital neuralgia/TTH overlap.

Central Sensitisation (Chronic TTH)

In frequent and chronic TTH, central sensitisation develops — the trigeminal nucleus and thalamus become sensitised to ongoing muscle tension signals, lowering the pain threshold and causing pain that persists even when muscle tension is not acutely elevated. This is the same mechanism as chronic migraine — distinguishing acute episodic TTH from the chronic form requires attention to this difference.

Stress → Muscle Contraction → Headache Pathway

Psychological stress → sustained sympathetic activation → increased motor neurone firing → persistent pericranial muscle contraction (often below conscious awareness) → local ischaemia → metabolite accumulation → nociceptor sensitisation → headache. Poor posture (forward head position — chin-forward during screen use) directly loads the posterior cervical muscles and compresses the greater occipital nerve.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Causes & Triggers

Factor Mechanism
Psychological stress Primary cause — sustained muscle motor neurone activation
Poor posture Prolonged neck extension or forward head posture; screen use
Sleep deprivation Reduced central pain modulation
Eye strain Sustained frontal and temporal muscle contraction
Skipped meals Fasting → hypoglycaemia → pain threshold reduction
Dehydration Reduced cerebral perfusion; pain sensitisation
Bruxism (teeth grinding) Masseter and temporalis overuse; common in anxiety
Caffeine overuse or withdrawal Altered cerebrovascular tone

Symptoms

  • Location: Bilateral; forehead, temples, occipital region, and/or "band" around the whole head
  • Quality: Pressing, tightening, "vice-like" — NOT throbbing (throbbing suggests migraine)
  • Severity: Mild to moderate (rarely severe — distinguish from migraine)
  • Duration: 30 minutes to 7 days
  • Not aggravated by physical activity (walking does not worsen it — may even improve it)
  • No significant nausea or vomiting
  • Pericranial muscle tenderness on manual palpation — pathognomonic; tenderness of temporal, masseteric, SCM, or trapezius muscles on palpation

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis based on ICHD-3 criteria. No investigations required if headache is typical and there are no alarm features (no sudden onset, no neurological deficit, no fever, no papilloedema).

Medical Management

Episodic TTH:

  • Simple analgesics: Paracetamol, Ibuprofen, Aspirin (effective for acute episodes)
  • Avoid frequent (>10 days/month) analgesic use — leads to medication overuse headache (MOH)

Chronic TTH (prevention):

  • Low-dose Amitriptyline (tricyclic antidepressant — most evidence-based preventive)
  • Physiotherapy: Trigger point therapy, massage, posture correction
  • Stress management and psychological therapy (CBT) — yoga therapy directly fills this role

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis

TTH is perhaps the most directly accessible of all headache disorders to yoga therapy, because the causal chain — mental stress → muscle tension → headache — is addressed at every level by yoga practices.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Goal: Release pericranial and cervical muscle tension; correct the postural patterns that perpetuate muscle loading; restore normal circulation to the scalp and neck.

  • Neck loosening exercises (Sithilikarana for the cervical region): The primary physical intervention for TTH.

    • Chin to chest (3×): stretches the sub-occipital and upper cervical muscles
    • Ear to shoulder (3× each side): stretches SCM and trapezius
    • Neck half-circles (slow, 3×): releases the entire posterior cervical chain
    • Neck rotation (3× each side): releases SCM and deep cervical rotators All performed slowly, without force, with breath coordination.

  • Shoulder rolling and shrugging: Releases the upper trapezius — the muscle most commonly responsible for TTH. Shrug to ears on inhalation; drop on exhalation.

  • Shithilikarana Vyayama (general loosening): Full body loosening at the start of each session reduces global muscle tension, lowering the pericranial tension that drives TTH.

  • Matsyasana (Fish Pose) / Supported Setu Bandhasana: Neck extension in these postures counteracts the chronic chin-forward head posture of screen work; improves sub-occipital circulation.

  • Paschimottanasana: Posterior cervical stretching in a controlled, supported way; reduces sub-occipital compression.

  • Hot water shower or warm compress to the neck/shoulders: While not a yoga practice per se, it is recommended as part of the Annamaya approach — local heat relaxes the pericranial muscles and improves circulation.

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Goal: Reduce the sympathetic tone that maintains pericranial muscle contraction; achieve immediate and sustained parasympathetic dominance.

  • Bhramari (Humming Bee Breath) — Primary pranayama for TTH: The most immediately effective pranayama for tension headache. The humming vibration resonates through the cranium, directly soothing the pericranial tissues and the brainstem. Specific note from PGDYT 104 Block 3: Bhramari is particularly indicated for headache disorders. Produces:

    • Vagal activation → immediate sympathetic withdrawal → muscle relaxation
    • Nitric oxide release in the sinuses and cranium → vasodilation → improved circulation to pericranial tissues
    • The sound vibration creates a gentle "massage" of the cranial cavity

  • Nadi Suddhi: Balances ANS; reduces the sympathetic drive that maintains muscle contraction.

  • Extended exhalation (1:2 ratio): Immediate vagal brake activation; rapid relaxation of pericranial muscles.

  • Vibhagiya Svasana: Full breathing engagement; releases the thoracic restriction that compounds upper trapezius tension.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Address the chronic stress and mental tension that is the root cause of TTH; develop genuine mental relaxation.

  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) — Core intervention: Systematic progressive muscular relaxation directly targets the pericranial muscles. When a DRT specifically includes guidance to release the muscles of the face (forehead, around the eyes, jaw), temples, neck, and shoulders — the headache dissolves during practice.
  • OM Meditation: Sustained inner stillness; the sustained gentle exhalation of OM chanting relaxes the jaw and face — major contributors to TTH.
  • Cyclic Meditation: Reduces HPA hyperreactivity; provides the deep rest that interrupts the chronic stress → chronic muscle tension cycle.
  • Yoga Nidra: Accesses and releases the deep body-memory of muscular tension that has become habitual.
  • Self-massage guidance: Teaching patients to apply gentle circular pressure at the temples, along the nuchal line, and over the upper trapezius — while breathing slowly — provides immediate relief and empowers self-management.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Identify and correct the postural and lifestyle patterns maintaining TTH; develop tension awareness before it becomes headache.

  • Ergonomic awareness: Screen height, neck position during work, pillow height during sleep — all direct Vijnanamaya inputs.
  • Jaw tension awareness: Many patients are unaware of chronic jaw clenching or teeth grinding. Drawing attention to this pattern enables voluntary release.
  • Regular break habits: Teaching patients to take 5-minute breaks every 45–60 minutes of screen work — with neck loosening and Bhramari — prevents cumulative muscle tension.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Discover that the underlying state of the mind is peaceful — the tension is added, not inherent.

OM Meditation and Cyclic Meditation progressively reveal the natural stillness beneath mental agitation. When this stillness is experienced directly, the habit of mental tension — which drives muscular tension — begins to dissolve at its root.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Neck exercises: No forceful or extreme range movements in the presence of cervical spondylosis or disc prolapse. Obtain medical clearance.
  • Sirsasana (Headstand): Contraindicated in TTH — loads the cervical spine; worsens occipital compression.
  • Vigorous inversions: Avoid during acute headache phase.
  • Medication overuse headache (MOH): If the patient uses analgesics >10 days/month, this itself is causing daily headache. Yoga therapy supports medication withdrawal — but must be done gradually and with medical guidance.
  • Cervical spondylosis coexistence: Neck loosening must be modified — no extreme flexion-extension, no active rotation into resistance.
  • Bruxism: Night-guard (occlusal splint) should be recommended alongside yoga therapy; dental consultation.
