Hepatitis

Hepatitis

1. Definition & Classification

Hepatitis means inflammation of the liver (hepar — liver; itis — inflammation). It may be caused by viral infection (most common globally), alcohol, drugs and toxins, autoimmune processes, or metabolic disorders. When the liver is inflamed, it cannot perform its essential functions — purifying blood, synthesising proteins, storing metabolic substrates, and producing bile — leading to a characteristic clinical syndrome of jaundice, fatigue, right upper quadrant pain, and in severe cases, liver failure.

The yoga therapy approach to hepatitis is explicitly staged: a rest-dominant approach during the acute phase (asana and pranayama are contraindicated initially), transitioning to a graduated restoration programme during recovery. The primary yoga intervention in the acute phase is QRT with abdominal breath awareness and Japa (mantra repetition). Yoga therapy plays its most active role in the post-acute recovery phase to regenerate liver function and prevent chronic progression.

Classification by Cause

Viral Hepatitis:

  • Hepatitis A (HAV): Fecal-oral transmission; acute only; self-limiting; never becomes chronic. Excellent prognosis.
  • Hepatitis B (HBV): Blood, sexual contact, vertical (mother to child) transmission. 6–10% develop chronic HBV; risk of cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma.
  • Hepatitis C (HCV): Blood-borne (shared needles, transfusions). 50–70% develop chronic HCV; major cause of cirrhosis and liver cancer worldwide.
  • Hepatitis D (HDV): Only occurs with Hepatitis B (requires HBV surface antigen to survive). Rapidly progressive.
  • Hepatitis E (HEV): Fecal-oral; self-limiting in immunocompetent; severe in pregnancy.

By duration:

  • Acute: Duration <6 months; most HAV and HEV; many HBV
  • Chronic: Duration ≥6 months; HBV and HCV; leads to cirrhosis and liver failure

2. Anatomy & Physiology

The Liver and Its Functions

The liver is the largest gland in the body, weighing 1.2–1.5 kg in adults, located in the right upper quadrant of the abdomen beneath the rib cage. It receives blood from two sources: the portal vein (nutrient-rich blood from the intestines) and the hepatic artery (oxygenated blood from the aorta).

Vital hepatic functions:

  1. Detoxification: Converts harmful substances (drugs, alcohol, ammonia, bilirubin) into water-soluble forms for excretion in urine or bile
  2. Protein synthesis: Albumin (plasma osmotic pressure), clotting factors (I, II, V, VII, IX, X), complement proteins, carrier proteins
  3. Glycogen storage and gluconeogenesis: Buffers blood glucose by storing excess glucose as glycogen (after meals) and releasing glucose when blood sugar falls
  4. Lipid metabolism: Cholesterol synthesis; bile acid production; fatty acid processing
  5. Bile production: ~500–1000 mL/day; essential for fat digestion and absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)
  6. Immune function: Kupffer cells (liver macrophages) clear bacteria, antigens, and cellular debris from portal blood

When the liver is inflamed, hepatocytes are destroyed. The immune response to hepatitis viruses itself causes much of the hepatocyte damage — the body's white cells attacking virally infected liver cells. This reduces all hepatic functions proportionally to the severity of involvement.

3. Causes & Symptoms

Transmission Routes

Virus Transmission Chronic potential
HAV Contaminated food/water; fecal-oral No
HBV Blood, sexual contact, vertical Yes (6–10%)
HCV Blood-borne (injecting drug use, transfusion) Yes (50–70%)
HEV Fecal-oral (contaminated water — common in South Asia) No (except in immunocompromised)

Symptoms

Prodromal phase (1–2 weeks before jaundice):

  • Flu-like illness: fatigue, malaise, anorexia, nausea, vomiting
  • Right upper quadrant dull ache (liver capsule distension)
  • Fever (especially HAV)
  • Arthralgia, rash (especially HBV)
  • Dark urine (bilirubin in urine — conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia)

Icteric (jaundice) phase:

  • Yellow colouring of skin and sclerae (jaundice — bilirubin >2–3 mg/dL)
  • Pale/clay-coloured stools (reduced bile reaching intestine)
  • Pruritus (itch from bile salt deposition)
  • Continued fatigue, anorexia

Laboratory findings:

  • Elevated transaminases: ALT and AST (hepatocellular damage markers)
  • Elevated bilirubin (conjugated and unconjugated)
  • Elevated alkaline phosphatase and GGT (cholestatic component)
  • Prolonged prothrombin time (severe disease — clotting factor synthesis impaired)

Complications:

  • Fulminant hepatic failure (rare, <1%): encephalopathy, coagulopathy, renal failure — life-threatening
  • Chronic hepatitis → cirrhosis → portal hypertension, oesophageal varices, ascites, hepatocellular carcinoma

4. Conventional Treatment

Diagnosis

  • Liver function tests (LFT): ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, bilirubin, albumin, PT
  • Viral serology: HAV IgM (acute), HBsAg (chronic HBV), HCV antibody + RNA
  • Liver ultrasound
  • Liver biopsy (for chronic hepatitis staging — fibrosis assessment)

Medical Management

Acute viral hepatitis: Primarily supportive:

  • Rest, adequate nutrition, hydration
  • Avoid hepatotoxic substances (alcohol, NSAIDs, paracetamol at high doses)
  • No specific antiviral for HAV or HEV

Chronic HBV: Antivirals — Tenofovir, Entecavir. Suppress viral replication; reduce cirrhosis progression.

Chronic HCV: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — Sofosbuvir/Velpatasvir, Glecaprevir/Pibrentasvir. Cure rates >95% in 8–12 weeks. Major advance in HCV treatment.

Alcoholic hepatitis: Complete alcohol abstinence. Prednisolone in severe cases. Nutritional support.

5. Yoga Therapy Rationale

Pancha Kosha Analysis — Phase-Dependent Approach

The PGDYT 104 Block 4 protocol for hepatitis is explicitly divided into two phases. This is one of the clearest examples in the S-VYASA curriculum of phase-dependent yoga therapy.

Phase 1 (Acute/Active Hepatitis): Rest dominant. Yoga has a limited and specific role — essentially just QRT, abdominal breath awareness, and Japa.

Phase 2 (Recovery/Remission): Active yoga therapy commences, with a graduated return to full practice.

Annamaya Kosha — Physical Body

Acute phase:

  • Rest is the primary prescription — liver regeneration requires energy; physical activity diverts blood flow away from the liver and increases metabolic demand.
  • Satvik diet: Small, frequent, easily digestible meals. In the initial days: citrus fruits or juices, ripe papaya (nausea relief). Astringent foods (radish) — regularly. Avoid: meat, eggs, spices, oils, butter, ghee for at least 6 weeks; alcohol for 6 months to 1 year.
  • No asanas or pranayama for at least 6 weeks.

Recovery phase — specific asanas (from PGDYT 104 Block 4):

  • Pavanamuktasana Part I (2+ weeks of recovery): Wind-relieving exercises — gentle, progressive; first physical practice. Followed by abdominal breath awareness in Shavasana and gentle Nadishuddhi.
  • Surya Namaskara (3–7 rounds at sunrise): When healing is underway; provides light cardiovascular activation, improves liver circulation, promotes mild sweating for toxin elimination.
  • Paschimottanasana: Specifically recommended for optimal recovery of liver tissues — the seated forward fold applies gentle compression to the liver region on forward flexion and releases it on return.
  • Viparita Karani Mudra: Specifically recommended for liver recovery — inversion improves hepatic venous drainage; reduces hepatic congestion.
  • Sashankasana: Forward rest pose; gentle hepatic compression-release.
  • Other abdominal practices: Ardha Padma Padmottanasana, Yoga Mudra, Halasana, Merudandasana — "when healing process is underway" (PGDYT 104 Block 4).

Pranamaya Kosha — Energy & Breath Body

Acute phase:

  • QRT with abdominal breath awareness: The only breathing practice during acute hepatitis. Promotes healing by activating the parasympathetic nervous system — "rest and digest" mode — which is optimal for liver cell regeneration.

Recovery phase:

  • Nadi Suddhi (Nadishuddhi pranayama): "For two weeks or more: followed by abdominal breath awareness in Shavasana and gentle Nadishuddhi" — directly from PGDYT 104 Block 4.
  • Surya Bhedana (Right Nostril Breathing): During recovery phase; stimulates the hepatic prana; useful for the low-energy convalescent state.
  • No pranayama with Kumbhaka: Increased intra-abdominal pressure is contraindicated.

Manomaya Kosha — Mental & Emotional Body

Goal: Provide psychological support during the enforced rest of acute hepatitis; reduce the anxiety and depression common in prolonged hepatic illness.

Acute phase:

  • Japa (silent mantra repetition): The most accessible Manomaya Kosha practice during acute illness when physical practices are contraindicated. Provides a meditative anchor; reduces anxiety; maintains spiritual connection during enforced bed rest.
  • Yoga Nidra (gentle): Can be practised from early in the acute phase — pure mental practice requiring no physical effort. Provides deep rest equivalent; supports recovery.

Recovery phase:

  • DRT: Returns to the practice schedule.
  • Cyclic Meditation: Appropriate once the patient is ambulatory.
  • OM Meditation: Sustained meditative rest.
  • Devotional sessions: Social and spiritual support during recovery.

Vijnanamaya Kosha — Wisdom & Intellect Body

Goal: Understand the specific dietary and lifestyle requirements for liver recovery; alcohol and hepatotoxin avoidance as a Vijnanamaya prescription.

Education about the transmission routes of hepatitis viruses (for prevention of spread and re-infection), understanding the regenerative capacity of the liver (which can restore up to 70% function from as little as 30% healthy tissue), and the critical importance of alcohol abstinence are all Vijnanamaya layer interventions.

Anandamaya Kosha — Bliss & Spiritual Body

Goal: Find acceptance and meaning in the illness; use the enforced retreat of acute hepatitis as an opportunity for inner renewal.

The yogic perspective on illness as an opportunity for purification (Shodhana) — hepatitis literally forces the liver to purify itself — provides a philosophical framework for the patient to engage with their recovery.

7. Contraindications & Cautions

  • Active acute hepatitis: All asana and pranayama are contraindicated until LFTs stabilise. Rest is the primary prescription.
  • Oesophageal varices (portal hypertension in chronic liver disease): Kunjal Kriya (Vamana Dhauti) is absolutely contraindicated — vomiting may rupture oesophageal varices → life-threatening haemorrhage.
  • Kumbhaka (breath retention): Increases intra-abdominal pressure; avoid in all phases.
  • Laghu Shankhaprakshalana: Only after confirmed recovery; not during active hepatitis.
  • NSAIDs and paracetamol (high dose): Hepatotoxic — avoid throughout recovery.
  • Alcohol: Abstinence for 6 months to 1 year minimum; may be permanent in alcoholic hepatitis.
  • Vigorous practice: Do not progress too rapidly — hepatocyte regeneration takes weeks to months; premature heavy physical practice may cause a setback (transaminase flare).
  • Contact sports / physically demanding work: Avoid until liver function fully normalised.
