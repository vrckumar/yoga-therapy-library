Gomukhasana

Gomukhasana (Cow Face Pose)

Go (Sanskrit) — cow; Mukha — face. The crossed legs in the final posture are said to resemble the wide face and tapering snout of a cow. Gomukhasana is unique among the classical sitting postures in that it simultaneously addresses two of the most clinically restricted joint complexes in the human body: the shoulder girdle (requiring extreme combined internal/external rotation) and the hip joint (requiring deep adductor and external rotator stretching). This dual action makes it the primary posture for Arthritis protocols involving both the shoulder and hip joints.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Daṇḍāsana — sit on the floor with legs stretched in front.

Practice (lower body):

  1. Place the palms on the floor and raise the seat slightly.
  2. Bend the right knee back and sit on the right foot so the right heel is under the right buttock. Bend the left knee and place the left thigh over the right thigh, bringing both knees in line. Using the hands, draw the ankles close together so the back of the heels touch each other. Rest the toes pointing backward.
  3. Both buttocks should rest evenly on the heels/floor. The sitting bones bear equal weight.

Practice (upper body): 4. Raise the left arm overhead, bend it at the elbow, and lower the left hand down behind the neck between the shoulder blades (palm facing the spine). 5. Lower the right arm down beside the body, then bend the right elbow and raise the right forearm up behind the back from below until the right hand is level with and between the shoulder blades. 6. Clasp the left and right hands together behind the back, between the shoulders. 7. Keep the neck and head erect; look straight ahead with a relaxed face. 8. Hold from 30 to 60 seconds with normal breathing. 9. Unclasp the hands. Extend the legs. Repeat the pose on the opposite side — right knee over left knee; right arm overhead/left arm from below.

Breathing: Normal throughout. Inhale to set up; exhale to deepen the clasp; normal breathing during hold.

Duration/rounds: 30–60 seconds each side; 1 round per session.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged — upper body

  • Triceps brachii (upper arm): the arm raised overhead is stretched across the posterior aspect of the elbow; the triceps long head is placed under significant stretch with the arm in full overhead adduction
  • Deltoid (posterior fibres) and rotator cuff (infraspinatus, teres minor): the overhead arm is internally rotated behind the neck — these external rotators are stretched maximally
  • Deltoid (anterior fibres) and subscapularis (internal rotator): the lower arm is behind the back in internal rotation — these internal rotators are stretched in this arm
  • Latissimus dorsi and teres major: stretched as the upper arm moves into full overhead elevation and internal rotation
  • Rhomboids and middle trapezius: the retracted position of the clasped hands draws the shoulder blades toward the spine, isometrically contracting these scapular retractors

Primary muscles engaged — lower body

  • Hip adductors (adductor magnus, longus, brevis, pectineus): the crossed-knee position places the hip in deep adduction; these muscles are under strong stretch
  • Hip external rotators (piriformis, obturator, gemellus, quadratus femoris): with the hip in flexion and adduction, the external rotators are maximally stretched — this is the signature hip stretch of the pose
  • Gluteus medius and minimus: actively engaged to maintain the level pelvis against the adduction stress

Organs and structures affected

  • Shoulder joint capsule: both the posterior (upper arm) and anterior (lower arm) aspects of the shoulder capsule are stretched simultaneously — the only yoga posture that addresses the entire circumference of the glenohumeral capsule
  • Brachial plexus: the overhead arm position gently stretches the brachial plexus from the cervical roots; relevant for cervicogenic shoulder/arm pain
  • Hip joint capsule: the combined flexion-adduction position stretches the posterior hip capsule and the dense inferior glenohumeral-equivalent (the acetabular labrum surrounding tissue) of the hip

3. Physiological Effects

Shoulder joint rehabilitation — the full capsular stretch mechanism

Most shoulder pathologies — including frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis), rotator cuff tendinopathy, impingement syndrome, and osteoarthritis — are associated with adaptive shortening of specific sectors of the shoulder capsule. The capsule is a continuous sleeve; conventional physiotherapy stretches usually address only one or two planes of restriction.

Gomukhasana creates a unique multidirectional capsular challenge:

  • Upper arm (overhead, internally rotated): stretches the posterior capsule, posterior rotator cuff tendons (infraspinatus, teres minor), and the triceps origin. This is the most commonly restricted sector in frozen shoulder.
  • Lower arm (behind back, internally rotated): stretches the anterior capsule, subscapularis, and the pectoralis minor. This is restricted in impingement and the second phase of frozen shoulder.

When both arms are clasped simultaneously, the two stretches reinforce each other: the overhead arm's pull draws the lower arm's hand higher, and the lower arm's upward reach draws the upper arm's hand lower. The result is a progressive, symmetrical mobilisation of the entire shoulder capsule — the reason SVYASA includes Gomukhasana specifically in the Arthritis protocol for shoulder involvement.

Over repeated practice:

  1. The posterior capsule thickening characteristic of impingement syndrome gradually resolves, restoring internal rotation range
  2. The anterior capsular contracture of frozen shoulder is progressively released, restoring abduction and external rotation
  3. The glenohumeral cartilage receives cyclic compression-distraction loading as the joint is taken through its combined end-range, stimulating synovial fluid circulation and cartilage nutrition

Hip joint rehabilitation — the adductor and external rotator mechanism

The hip joint is the largest ball-and-socket joint in the body and the one most commonly affected by degenerative arthritis in the lower limb. In the crossed-knee position of Gomukhasana:

The hip adductors — the most powerful muscles crossing the medial thigh — undergo a sustained static stretch. In most people these muscles are chronically shortened due to sedentary sitting and habitual hip-neutral postures. Their shortening progressively reduces hip abduction range, alters gait mechanics, and concentrates compressive load on the antero-superior hip joint where degenerative change typically begins.

The hip external rotators (primarily piriformis) are placed in maximum stretch with the hip simultaneously flexed and adducted. These muscles, when hypertonic, externally rotate the femur and compress the piriformis tendon against the sciatic nerve — the cause of piriformis syndrome (pseudo-sciatica). Sustained stretching in Gomukhasana normalises piriformis length and sciatic nerve mobility.

Circulatory effect

The fully crossed legs create a moderate compression of the femoral vessels in the groin. Upon releasing the posture, reactive arterial vasodilation sends a surge of blood into the previously compressed limb — a mechanism used in IAYT cardiovascular protocols. This compression-release cycling has been observed to reduce chronic lower limb oedema and improve peripheral circulation, relevant in cardiac rehabilitation.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — multidirectional shoulder capsule stretch; hip adductor and external rotator lengthening; femoral vascular compression-release.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the chest expansion produced by the clasped-hands-behind-back position (drawing the shoulder blades together) opens the anahata (heart chakra) region; the SVYASA classification of the posture in the cardiac rehabilitation protocol reflects this thoracic-energetic dimension.

6. Contraindications

  • Active arthritis flare: avoid strong stretching during acute inflammatory episodes; wait for the sub-acute phase
  • Severe shoulder impingement with pain arc: the overhead arm position may provoke impingement symptoms; begin with the lower arm only until range improves
  • Knee ligament injury or severe knee pain: the deep knee flexion and crossed-knee position places stress on the medial knee; avoid or prop the crossed knee with a blanket
  • Acute hip bursitis or labral tear: the deep hip adduction-flexion position stresses the acetabular rim; use with medical guidance

7. Variations & Progressions

Beginner (arms): If the hands cannot clasp, hold a strap between the two hands and gradually work the hands toward each other over weeks of practice. This is the standard modification in IAYT settings.

Beginner (legs): If the full crossed-knee position is uncomfortable, simply sit cross-legged with the top knee drawn as close to the midline as possible. The hip stretch is less intense but still beneficial.

Standard (as described): Full crossed-knee position, heels touching, hands clasped behind back. 30–60 seconds each side.

Forward bend variation: From the full position, exhale and fold forward until the forehead rests on the crossed knees. This dramatically intensifies the hip external rotator stretch and adds a lumbar flexion component.

Progression: Ardha Matsyendrasana — the half spinal twist performed with a similar leg crossing — adds rotational loading to the already-active hip adductor stretch, progressing both the hip and spinal dimensions.
