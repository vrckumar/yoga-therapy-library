Neck Forward and Backward Movement

Neck Forward and Backward Movement (Greeva Sanchalana — Poorva Pashchima)

Cervical flexion and extension — the sagittal-plane mobilisation of the cervical spine. Mobilises the anterior and posterior cervical muscles and intervertebral joints through the primary axis of neck movement. Requires care in cervical spondylosis.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana (standing) or seated in Dandasana. Spine erect.

Practice:

  1. Stand or sit erect. Keep the shoulders relaxed and stationary.
  2. While exhaling, slowly lower the chin toward the chest — full cervical flexion. Feel the stretch in the back of the neck (posterior cervical muscles).
  3. While inhaling, slowly raise the chin and tilt the head backward — cervical extension. Feel the stretch in the front of the neck.
  4. This constitutes one round. Perform 10 rounds.
  5. Relax in Sithila Tadasana (or seated relaxation).

Note: The backward tilt should be gentle and within comfortable range — do not force the head backward; stop at any point of dizziness or discomfort.

Breathing: Exhale on forward (chin to chest); inhale on backward tilt.

Duration: 10 rounds.

2. Anatomical Activation

Cervical flexion (chin to chest)

  • Sternocleidomastoid (bilateral): primary cervical flexors — bilateral contraction brings chin to chest
  • Longus colli and longus capitis: deep anterior cervical muscles — maintain cervical lordosis while flexing
  • Anterior scalenes: assist cervical flexion
  • Posterior cervical muscles (semispinalis, splenius): eccentrically lengthened during flexion

Cervical extension (head tilting back)

  • Semispinalis capitis and cervicis: primary extensors of the cervical spine
  • Splenius capitis and cervicis: cervical extension and slight rotation
  • Trapezius (upper fibres): cervical extension component
  • Anterior cervical muscles: eccentrically controlled during extension

3. Physiological Effects

Cervical intervertebral joint mobilisation

The cervical spine has seven vertebrae with six intervertebral discs and 14 facet joints. Flexion opens the posterior facet joints and stretches the posterior longitudinal ligament and intervertebral discs. Extension closes the posterior facets and opens the anterior disc space. The rhythmic cycling between flexion and extension distributes synovial fluid across the facet joint articular surfaces and maintains the viscoelastic properties of the intervertebral discs.

Anterior cervical muscle mobilisation

The deep anterior cervical muscles (longus colli, longus capitis) are frequently inhibited in individuals with forward head posture — they become lengthened and neurologically suppressed. Cervical flexion activates these muscles concentrically, providing a gentle reactivation of the deep neck flexors that are the primary stabilisers of the cervical spine.

Posterior cervical muscle elongation

The posterior cervical muscles — particularly the suboccipital muscles (rectus capitis posterior, obliquus capitis) — are chronically shortened in forward head posture and cervicogenic headache. Chin-to-chest flexion provides a daily elongation stretch for these muscles.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — cervical facet joint mobilisation; anterior-posterior cervical muscle conditioning; intervertebral disc hydration through cyclic compression-decompression.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the neck is the vishuddha (throat) energy junction between the body and the head in yogic anatomy; cervical mobility maintains the flow of prana between the trunk and the higher centres.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Cervical Spine / Spondylosis (A003) Facet joint mobilisation; disc hydration; cervical ROM maintenance
Cervicogenic Headache Suboccipital and posterior cervical muscle elongation
Forward Head Posture Deep anterior cervical flexor reactivation; posterior muscle elongation
Back Pain (A004) — with cervical component Cervical mobility affects the entire spinal kinetic chain
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) Cervical preparation before pranayama and meditation postures

6. Contraindications

  • Cervical spondylosis with myelopathy — avoid extremes of flexion (posterior disc bulge) and extension (cord compression at stenotic levels); practise gentle mid-range movement only
  • Cervical disc herniation (acute) — flexion may aggravate posterior disc protrusion; extension may aggravate anterior disc
  • Vertebral artery insufficiency — backward tilt may compress the vertebral arteries; any dizziness on extension = stop immediately
  • Atlanto-axial instability (e.g., Down syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis) — avoid flexion; specialist assessment required

7. Variations & Progressions

Gentle mid-range (spondylosis modification): Reduce range to 50% — chin toward (not to) chest; backward tilt only to neutral (no extension beyond neutral).

Supported (variation): Seated with back support; reduces the proprioceptive demand on the neck muscles.

Sequencing context: First neck exercise. Begins the cervical group (P0023–P0029). All seven cervical exercises together form a comprehensive cervical mobilisation system.
