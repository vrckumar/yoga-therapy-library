Lip Stretch

Lip Stretch (Oshtha Sakti Vikasaka / Lip Exercises)

Oshtha = lips. A series of deliberate lip exercises — stretching, puckering, spreading, pressing, and blowing movements — targeting the orbicularis oris (lip sphincter muscle) and the surrounding perioral facial muscles. Used in facial rehabilitation after stroke, Bell's palsy, and other conditions affecting the facial nerve (Cranial Nerve VII). Also used as preparatory exercises before pranayama practices involving lip-controlled exhalation (such as Sitali and Sitkari) and as part of the vocal warm-up before mantra chanting.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrasana or seated in a chair, spine erect, face relaxed.

Practice:

Stage I — Wide smile stretch:

  1. Spread the lips into the widest possible smile — draw the corners of the mouth laterally as far as possible without opening the mouth.
  2. Hold 5 seconds. Relax.
  3. Repeat 5–10 times.

Stage II — Pucker (kiss shape):

  1. Press the lips together and project them forward into a firm pucker (as in making a kissing shape or pronouncing "O").
  2. Hold 5 seconds. Relax.
  3. Alternate with Stage I (wide smile ↔ pucker) for 10 rounds.

Stage III — Lip press:

  1. Press both lips together firmly, as if determined not to open the mouth.
  2. Use the orbicularis oris to create maximum compression.
  3. Hold 10 seconds. Release.
  4. Repeat 5 times.

Stage IV — Air inflation:

  1. Close the lips firmly and puff the cheeks with air (as in holding a mouthful of air against resistance).
  2. Use the buccinator and orbicularis oris to resist the air pressure.
  3. Hold 10 seconds. Release.
  4. Repeat 5 times.

Stage V — Vibration (lip trill):

  1. Exhale through loosely closed lips, allowing them to vibrate (lip trill or "brrrr" sound).
  2. The vibration massages the perioral muscles and warms up the vocal tract.
  3. Repeat for 3–5 breath cycles.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles

  • Orbicularis oris: the circular sphincter muscle of the lips; the primary muscle trained in all stages; responsible for lip closure, puckering, and resistance to pressure
  • Buccinator: the cheek muscle; active in the air inflation stage; forms the oral wall
  • Zygomaticus major and minor: lift the corners of the mouth in the wide smile (Stage I)
  • Depressor anguli oris and mentalis: the downward lip corner muscles; trained eccentrically in the wide smile
  • Levator labii superioris: elevates the upper lip in the smile phase
  • Risorius: draws the corner of the mouth laterally

Facial nerve (CN VII) branches activated

  • Buccal branch of CN VII: activates the orbicularis oris, buccinator, and zygomaticus
  • Marginal mandibular branch of CN VII: activates the lower lip depressors and mentalis
  • Zygomatic branch of CN VII: activates the muscles around the eye and upper cheek

3. Physiological Effects

Facial nerve rehabilitation

Bell's palsy and stroke both impair the facial nerve's motor function to the perioral muscles. Lip exercises are among the most effective early rehabilitation interventions because:

  1. The lip muscles are under direct voluntary control (unlike the deep face muscles)
  2. The motor cortex representation of the lips is the largest facial region — the highest density of motor neurons in the face
  3. Daily active lip movement practice stimulates cortical reorganisation (neuroplasticity) in the motor cortex representation of the face

Articulation improvement

Clear pronunciation of bilabial consonants (B, P, M) and labiodental consonants (F, V) requires precise lip muscle strength and control. For patients with dysarthria (slurred speech) — whether from stroke, Parkinson's disease, or other neurological conditions — lip exercise is a foundational speech therapy technique.

Perioral anti-ageing

Chronic lip exercises increase the tone of the orbicularis oris and prevent the perioral wrinkling associated with muscle atrophy and sun damage. While this is not the primary IAYT indication, it is a noted secondary benefit in the Yoga Therapy literature.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — perioral and facial muscle rehabilitation; facial nerve activation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — lip exercises directly influence the breath exit pathway — the lips and buccinator determine exhalation resistance and shape; vyana vayu (peripheral vital energy) in the facial region.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Stroke rehabilitation Facial nerve rehabilitation; perioral muscle reactivation; articulation improvement
Bell's palsy CN VII motor rehabilitation
Dysarthria (speech difficulties) Lip strength and precision for bilabial/labiodental sounds
Before pranayama (Sitali, Sitkari) Warm-up for lip-controlled breath practices
Before mantra chanting Perioral warm-up for clear articulation

6. Contraindications

  • Acute facial trauma: avoid movement until healing is complete
  • Dental/oral surgery (recent): limit the stretch range until surgical sites are healed
  • No significant contraindications for the basic stages in healthy individuals
