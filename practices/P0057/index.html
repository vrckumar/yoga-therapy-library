Hands In and Out Breathing

A foundational breath-movement coordination exercise performed in Tadasana (standing). Arms spread wide on inhalation and cross the chest on exhalation, expanding the ribcage laterally and training thoracic breathing awareness. Used at the opening of most IAYT sessions to establish breath-body rhythm before the main asana sequence.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana — stand erect with feet together (heels together, toes 4–6 inches apart), arms relaxed at the sides.

Practice:

  1. Stretch both arms out in front at shoulder level with palms pressed together.
  2. Inhaling: spread the arms wide sideways in the horizontal plane until they are fully extended to either side. The chest opens, the ribcage expands laterally.
  3. Exhaling: bring the arms forward again with palms pressing together in front of the sternum. The chest returns to a neutral position.
  4. This is one round. Perform 5 rounds continuously, keeping the arm movement flowing and unbroken so that breath and movement are perfectly synchronised.
  5. Return to Sthila Tadasana (relaxed standing). Close the eyes briefly and feel the changes in the breath, arms, shoulders, and back of the neck.

Breathing: Inhale on the outward spread; exhale on the return to midline.

Duration/rounds: 5 rounds per session.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Pectoralis major and anterior deltoid: eccentrically controlled on inhalation as arms spread; concentrically active on exhalation as arms return
  • Middle and lower trapezius, rhomboids: actively retract the scapulae during the outward arm spread, supporting thoracic opening
  • External intercostals: the lateral expansion of the arms facilitates lift of the ribs, activating the intercostal muscles associated with thoracic breathing
  • Serratus anterior: stabilises the scapula against the thoracic wall throughout the movement

Respiratory mechanics

The outward arm spread physically lifts and expands the lateral chest wall. This:

  • Increases the transverse diameter of the thorax on inhalation
  • Stretches the intercostal muscles and pleura, facilitating thoracic (mid-lobe) breathing
  • Encourages recruitment of the lateral ribcage during inspiration — the most commonly underused breathing zone in anxious or sedentary individuals

3. Physiological Effects

Thoracic breathing training

Most dysfunctional breathing patterns in IAYT protocols (asthma, anxiety, COPD) feature an over-reliance on either purely abdominal or purely apical (clavicular) breathing with the mid-thoracic zone locked. This exercise directly addresses mid-thoracic restriction by pairing arm abduction with inhalation — the movement mechanically forces rib cage expansion in the lateral plane.

Breath-body coordination

The exercise trains the neuromuscular synchronisation between limb movement and respiratory effort. For patients with respiratory conditions who have habitually dissociated movement from breathing (breath-holding during activity being a common pattern), this re-establishes the coordination reflex.

Calming and rhythm restoration

Synchronising slow, continuous arm movement with steady inhalation and exhalation activates proprioceptive flow and reduces sympathetic arousal. The bilateral symmetry of the movement and the crossing of the arms at the midline on exhalation also have a mild regulatory effect on interhemispheric balance (cross-lateral midline pattern).

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — direct mechanical action on the thoracic wall, intercostals, and shoulder girdle.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — lateral chest expansion promotes vyana vayu (expansive vital energy associated with peripheral circulation and the arms).

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Asthma / Respiratory Disorders (A007) Thoracic rib-cage expansion; trains lateral breathing zone; opens compressed chest posture
Anxiety / Depression (A006) Rhythmic bilateral movement restores breath regularity; chest opening counters the contracted, withdrawn posture of anxiety
Hypertension (A005) Slow rhythmic movement lowers sympathetic tone; used as warm-up in hypertension protocol
All IAYT protocols (general) Used as session opener to establish breath awareness before therapeutic asanas

6. Contraindications

  • Severe shoulder injury or rotator cuff tear — limit range of lateral arm spread
  • Acute cardiac event — avoid vigorous arm movements until stabilised
  • No significant contraindications for the basic form; highly accessible practice

7. Variations

Seated variation: When the patient cannot stand, the same practice is performed seated in a chair with identical arm movement and breath synchronisation. The thoracic benefit is equivalent.

Progression: Increase the number of rounds to 10 as the breath coordination improves; slow the arm movement to extend the inhalation duration.
