Vibhagiya Svasana (Sectional Breathing)

Vibhagiya (Sanskrit) — sectional, divided; Svasana — breathing. Vibhagiya Svasana systematically isolates and trains the three anatomical sections of the respiratory apparatus — abdominal, thoracic, and clavicular — before integrating them into Full Yogic Breathing. It is the foundational corrective pranayama of the IAYT system, designed to diagnose and reverse faulty breathing patterns that are the root of reduced vital capacity in most adult populations.

In the IAYT model, Vibhagiya Svasana is placed between kriyas and formal pranayama in every protocol. Its function is both corrective (retraining habituated shallow breathing) and preparatory (opening the respiratory apparatus before Nadi Suddhi and other pranayamas).

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Sit in Vajrasana (thunderbolt pose) — kneeling with the spine erect and natural. All four sections are traditionally practised in this position, as Vajrasana provides a stable base and keeps the lumbar spine in a slight natural lordosis that facilitates diaphragmatic movement.

Section A — Abdominal / Diaphragmatic Breathing (Adhama Pranayama):

  • Mudra: Cin Mudra — touch tip of index finger to tip of thumb; other three fingers extended, palms resting on knees facing upward.
  • Inhale slowly through both nostrils. Feel the abdomen bulge forward and outward — the diaphragm descends, creating space for lung expansion. No chest movement.
  • Exhale slowly. Feel the abdomen draw inward and upward as the diaphragm ascends. Exhalation is passive.
  • 5 rounds. Attention rests entirely on the abdominal movement.

Section B — Thoracic / Intercostal Breathing (Madhyama Pranayama):

  • Mudra: Cinmaya Mudra — Cin Mudra with the three extended fingers curled inward (fist with thumb-index-tip contact). Palms face downward on the knees.
  • Abdomen remains still (gently held). Inhale — expand the chest cage forwards, outwards, and upwards. Feel the ribs splay laterally with each inhalation. The sternum lifts.
  • Exhale — ribs descend and the chest cage contracts inward. No abdominal movement.
  • 5 rounds. Attention on the lateral and anterior chest wall.

Section C — Clavicular / Apical Breathing (Adya Pranayama):

  • Mudra: Adi Mudra — thumb folded into the palm, other four fingers curl over it (closed fist). Hands rest on knees, palms downward.
  • Abdomen and chest remain still. Inhale — raise the collar bones upward and the shoulders slightly upward and backward. The apex of the lungs fills — a small but distinct movement.
  • Exhale — collar bones and shoulders descend gently.
  • 5 rounds. Attention on the clavicular and shoulder region.

Section D — Full Yogic Breathing (Mahat Yoga Pranayama):

  • Combine all three sections in one continuous inhalation: abdomen expands first → chest expands → collar bones rise. All three phases flow without interruption.
  • Exhalation reverses: collar bones descend → chest contracts → abdomen draws inward.
  • 5 rounds. The complete breath fills the lungs from the base to the apex in a single smooth wave.

Dosage in IAYT protocols: 5 rounds per section, performed daily. "10 minutes of daily practice works wonders."

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary structures engaged

Section A — Abdominal:

  • Diaphragm — the primary respiratory muscle. In most adults habituated to shallow chest breathing, the diaphragm is underused. Section A directly retrains its full descent range.
  • Abdominal wall — the rectus abdominis, external obliques, and transverse abdominis relax on inhalation (allowing the abdomen to bulge) and gently recoil on exhalation.
  • Pelvic floor — descends slightly on inhalation in concert with the diaphragm; the two form the floor and ceiling of the abdominal pressure chamber.

Section B — Thoracic:

  • External intercostal muscles — pull adjacent ribs toward each other and upward (bucket-handle movement), expanding the lateral diameter of the thorax.
  • Pectoral minor and serratus anterior — assist anterior chest elevation.
  • Costovertebral joints — the rib articulations at the spine undergo full rotational range of motion, often reduced in habitual chest-breathers and in elderly or sedentary individuals.

Section C — Clavicular:

  • Scalene muscles (anterior, medial, posterior) — elevate the first and second ribs, lifting the apex of the lung.
  • Sternocleidomastoid — assists in lifting the sternum.
  • Trapezius (upper fibres) and levator scapulae — elevate and retract the clavicle.
  • The apex of the lung — the portion most prone to reduced ventilation, stagnation of secretions, and, historically, tubercular infection — is specifically aerated in Section C.

Neural structures

Each mudra position subtly alters the proprioceptive input from the hand, directing nervous system attention to the corresponding body region. Cin Mudra (open palm) facilitates receptive awareness of abdominal expansion; Cinmaya Mudra (partial fist) encourages chest engagement; Adi Mudra (closed fist) directs attention upward to the clavicular region.

3. Physiological Effects

Correcting habituated shallow breathing — the primary mechanism

The adult breathing pattern is predominantly clavicular or upper-chest in times of stress and shallow diaphragmatic at rest. The diaphragm, when chronically underused, stiffens and loses its full excursion range. This produces a pattern of rapid, shallow breathing that keeps the body in a low-grade sympathetic state — higher respiratory rate, lower tidal volume, reduced oxygenation efficiency, and higher baseline cortisol.

Vibhagiya Svasana systematically retrains all three sections through isolation training — the same principle used in physiotherapy rehabilitation. By practising each section in isolation with conscious attention, the practitioner:

  1. Identifies their habitual dominant pattern — most people discover they use primarily one section. This awareness is itself corrective.
  2. Reactivates underused sections — particularly diaphragmatic breathing, which is typically the most suppressed in anxious or sedentary individuals.
  3. Develops neuromuscular coordination — the ability to selectively activate different respiratory muscles, which is then integrated in Full Yogic Breathing (Section D).

Vital capacity improvement

Full Yogic Breathing utilises a significantly greater tidal volume than habitual shallow breathing. Research on yogic breathing consistently shows measurable improvements in forced vital capacity (FVC), forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV₁), and peak expiratory flow rate (PEFR) with sustained practice. The mechanism is increased alveolar recruitment — more alveoli are ventilated per breath cycle, improving gas exchange efficiency and reducing physiological dead space.

Autonomic nervous system effects

The shift from upper-chest to diaphragmatic breathing has direct autonomic consequences:

  • Slow diaphragmatic breathing activates vagal afferents in the diaphragmatic pleura, increasing parasympathetic tone (the same mechanism as Nadi Suddhi).
  • Reduced respiratory rate (from the habitual 14–18 to 6–10 breaths/min during Full Yogic Breathing) increases heart rate variability (HRV), a reliable marker of parasympathetic health.
  • The calming effect reported by practitioners — reduced anger, anxiety, and fatigue — is physiologically mediated through this vagal activation and the resulting reduction in sympathetic arousal.

More power, vitality, and improved thinking

Increased tidal volume raises alveolar oxygen partial pressure and improves CO₂ clearance, delivering better-oxygenated blood to the brain and skeletal muscles. The traditional teaching that "10 minutes daily works wonders" reflects these compounding effects: improved oxygenation, lower cortisol, improved sleep quality (through parasympathetic dominance), and better mental clarity.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — direct mechanical training of the respiratory muscles at the physical body level. The sectional isolation of diaphragm, intercostals, and apical muscles is entirely somatic work.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — in yogic physiology, the full yogic breath activates and clears all three major pranic regions: the apana field (lower abdomen and pelvis) in Section A, the prana field (heart and lungs) in Section B, and the udana field (throat, head, and upper chest) in Section C. Full Yogic Breathing harmonises all pranic flows before pranayama commences.

The mudra sequence (Cin → Cinmaya → Adi) maps to progressively higher pranic centres, and the practice creates an upward movement of awareness that prepares the mind for meditation.

6. Contraindications

  • No absolute contraindications. Vibhagiya Svasana is the most universally safe pranayama in the IAYT repertoire. The PGDYTP 102 notes: "No limitations."
  • Recent thoracic or abdominal surgery — Sections A–C involve active respiratory muscle engagement. Modify by reducing effort and avoiding forced movements until surgical clearance (typically 6–8 weeks post-operatively).
  • Acute respiratory infection with high fever — rest rather than active respiratory training during acute illness; resume when fever resolves.
  • Acute disc prolapse with referred pain — ensure Vajrasana position is pain-free; modify to seated on a chair if kneeling is contraindicated.

7. Variations & Progressions

Supine version: Sections A and D can be practised lying in Savasana — useful for patients who cannot maintain Vajrasana, or for intensive diaphragmatic retraining in cardiac rehabilitation.

Chair variation: For elderly or mobility-impaired practitioners, all four sections can be performed seated on a chair with feet flat on the floor and spine supported but erect.

Integration with breath ratio: Once the mechanics of Full Yogic Breathing are established (typically 2–4 weeks of daily practice), a gentle Puraka:Recaka ratio of 1:2 (inhale 4 counts, exhale 8 counts) is introduced to deepen the parasympathetic effect.

As awareness meditation: Advanced practitioners practise all four sections with eyes closed, directing awareness through the body regions without mudra assistance. The movement of attention itself becomes the practice — a bridge to Pratyahara (sense withdrawal).

Combination with Ujjayi: The gentle glottal constriction of Ujjayi pranayama is added to Full Yogic Breathing once the basic pattern is established, producing audible breath and further slowing the respiratory rate.
