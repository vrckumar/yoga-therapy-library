Parvatasana

Parvatasana (Mountain Pose / Inverted-V)

Parvata (Sanskrit) = mountain. The pose in which the body forms an inverted-V (or upward-pointing triangle), with both hands and both feet on the floor, hips raised to the highest point. Equivalent to Adho Mukha Svanasana (Downward-Facing Dog) in the modern vinyasa tradition. Position 7 (and symmetrically position 5 in some versions) in the Surya Namaskar sequence — entered on exhalation after Bhujangasana. Provides a full posterior chain stretch (calves, hamstrings, gluteals, thoracolumbar fascia, shoulder extensors) and is a mild inversion, bringing the head below the heart.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Within the Surya Namaskar flow — entered from Bhujangasana (prone cobra).

Practice (Step 7 of Surya Namaskar):

  1. From Bhujangasana (prone position with trunk raised), exhale and without shifting the position of the hands or feet:
  2. Raise the buttocks toward the ceiling.
  3. Push the head downward so the neck is in line with the arms (ears between the upper arms).
  4. Press the heels toward the floor — attempt to have the heels touch the ground. (They may not in beginners with tight hamstrings, but this is the direction of effort.)
  5. The body forms a clear inverted-V: straight arms, raised hips, legs as straight as possible, head hanging down.
  6. Hold briefly in the Surya Namaskar flow; or maintain for up to 1 minute in the static version.

For the static Parvatasana (outside Surya Namaskar):

  1. From a prone position, press up to the inverted-V as above.
  2. Maintain for 30–60 seconds with normal breathing.
  3. Return to the prone position or transition to the next pose.

Notes:

  • Hands should be shoulder-width apart, feet hip-width or together.
  • The arms and the back ideally form one long diagonal line from wrists to hips.
  • Allow gravity to work on the hamstrings — do not force the heels to the floor.

Duration: Briefly (2–5 seconds) in Surya Namaskar flow; up to 1 minute in static practice.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged and stretched

Upper body:

  • Triceps brachii: fully contracted (elbows straight) under the body's weight
  • Serratus anterior: protracts and upwardly rotates the scapula — critical for proper shoulder mechanics in this position
  • Deltoids (anterior and middle): active in shoulder flexion
  • Latissimus dorsi and teres major: strongly stretched as the arms are overhead and the spine elongates
  • Thoracolumbar fascia: under strong longitudinal stretch as the hips rise away from the hands

Posterior chain (stretched):

  • Hamstrings: the primary limiting factor in most practitioners; stretched as the hips rise and the legs straighten
  • Gastrocnemius and soleus: powerfully stretched as the heels press toward the floor
  • Gluteus maximus: stretched in the hip-flexed position of the inverted-V

Spinal:

  • The spine is in a neutral to mildly flexed position under traction — the body weight hangs from the hip apex, creating longitudinal traction on all spinal segments simultaneously

3. Physiological Effects

Full posterior chain stretch — the defining benefit

Parvatasana applies simultaneous longitudinal stretch to the entire posterior kinetic chain from the plantar fascia through the Achilles, gastrocnemius, hamstrings, thoracolumbar fascia, and thoracic extensors. This global posterior stretch:

  1. Reduces tension in the myofascial posterior line — one of the most commonly dysfunctional fascial lines in sedentary adults
  2. Decompresses all vertebral segments simultaneously via the hanging traction effect
  3. Prepares the posterior chain for the full extension demanded in the subsequent Bhujangasana (cobra) in Surya Namaskar

Mild inversion effects

With the head below the heart, Parvatasana produces mild intracranial venous drainage and a modest cardiac preload increase. The inversion duration is brief in the Surya Namaskar context (a few seconds), so no significant hemodynamic change occurs. In the static version held for 30+ seconds, a mild calming/clarifying effect is noted — consistent with the improved cerebral venous drainage.

Spinal decompression

The inverted-V geometry creates axial traction on the spine — the body weight hangs between the two triangular bases (hands and feet), with the apex (hips) at the highest point. This is one of the few yoga positions in which gravity directly assists spinal decompression rather than compression.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — posterior chain stretch, spinal decompression, upper body strengthening.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the mild inversion position and elongated spine promote unrestricted prana flow along the sushumna (central energy channel).

7. Contraindications

  • Hypertension (severe) or glaucoma: mild inversion; assess suitability in severe cases
  • Wrist injury: full body weight through the wrists; use fist support or avoid
  • Hamstring injury (acute): reduce the depth of the heel-press and maintain slight knee bend
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome: wrist dorsiflexion under load is contraindicated

8. Relationship to Adho Mukha Svanasana

Parvatasana and Adho Mukha Svanasana (Downward-Facing Dog) describe the same geometric position. The SVYASA tradition uses the name Parvatasana in the context of Surya Namaskar and Alternate Bhujangasana-Parvatasana; the modern Iyengar/vinyasa tradition uses Adho Mukha Svanasana with slightly more refined shoulder alignment cues. The technique and benefits are equivalent.
