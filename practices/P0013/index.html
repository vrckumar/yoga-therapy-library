Feet Movement Inward and Outward

Feet Movement Inward and Outward (Paadha Anguli Naman — Antar Bahir)

Isolated inversion (supination) and eversion (pronation) of the foot — moving the sole of the foot inward (toward the midline of the body) and outward (away from midline). This exercise specifically targets the subtalar joint and the peroneal/invertor muscle balance critical for ankle stability.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Dandasana (seated with legs extended) or Supine Posture. Legs together or slightly apart.

Practice:

  1. Keep both legs straight on the floor, heels resting on the ground.
  2. Slowly turn both feet inward simultaneously — soles facing each other (inversion/supination). Hold briefly.
  3. Slowly turn both feet outward simultaneously — soles facing away from each other (eversion/pronation). Hold briefly.
  4. This constitutes one round. Perform 10 rounds.
  5. Relax in Sithila Dandasana.

Breathing: Exhale on each movement; inhale returning to neutral.

Duration/rounds: 10 rounds.

2. Anatomical Activation

Inward movement (inversion)

  • Tibialis anterior: primary invertor, also dorsiflexes the foot
  • Tibialis posterior: powerful invertor from behind the medial malleolus
  • Flexor digitorum longus: assists inversion
  • Flexor hallucis longus: assists inversion

Outward movement (eversion)

  • Peroneus longus: primary evertor; runs behind the lateral malleolus, crosses the foot plantarly to attach to the first metatarsal — also supports the transverse arch
  • Peroneus brevis: primary evertor; attaches to fifth metatarsal base
  • Extensor digitorum longus: assists eversion

Joint primarily mobilised

  • Subtalar (talocalcaneal) joint: the exclusive anatomical axis for foot inversion/eversion in the weight-bearing context; the dominant joint mobilised by this exercise

3. Physiological Effects

Subtalar joint mobilisation — the ankle stability joint

The subtalar joint is the critical joint for frontal plane foot motion and for dynamic ankle stability. Unlike the talocrural joint (which functions as a hinge), the subtalar joint's oblique axis allows the complex inversion-eversion motion that adapts the foot to uneven surfaces. Restricted subtalar motion (subtalar stiffness) forces compensatory movements at the knee and hip — contributing to upstream joint problems including knee valgus stress and iliotibial band tightening.

Peroneal muscle retraining — lateral ankle stability

The peroneal muscles (longus and brevis) are the primary dynamic stabilisers of the lateral ankle — the structures that prevent the ankle from inverting during a lateral step or an uneven surface landing. In chronic ankle instability following repeated sprains, the peroneal reflex latency increases — the muscle fires too slowly to prevent the sprain. Foot eversion exercises directly retrain the peroneal muscles' activation speed and strength.

Medial longitudinal arch support

Tibialis posterior — the primary invertor trained by the inward movement — is also the primary dynamic supporter of the medial longitudinal arch. Weakness of tibialis posterior is the leading cause of acquired flat foot (adult-onset pes planus). The inversion component of this exercise strengthens the tibialis posterior, providing a conservative intervention for early tibialis posterior dysfunction.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — subtalar joint mobilisation; peroneal and tibialis posterior retraining; arch support mechanism.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the grounding quality of the foot is directly related to inversion-eversion balance; balanced feet support stable prana flow through the lower limbs.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Chronic Ankle Instability (A003) Peroneal strengthening and proprioceptive retraining — direct mechanism
Flat Foot / Acquired Pes Planus Tibialis posterior reactivation — supports medial longitudinal arch
Arthritis — Subtalar Joint Subtalar joint mobilisation; synovial fluid distribution in the subtalar articulation
Back Pain (A004) Corrects foot pronation/supination imbalance that affects lumbar mechanics
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) Complements ankle bending and rotation with isolated frontal-plane ankle mobilisation

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lateral ankle ligament sprain — inversion movement loads the torn lateral ligaments; avoid inversion; eversion strengthening is acceptable
  • Peroneal tendon subluxation (acute) — eversion movement may worsen the subluxation; avoid in acute phase
  • Subtalar arthritis with acute effusion — reduce range

7. Variations & Progressions

Bilateral simultaneous (standard): Both feet together.

Alternating unilateral: One foot at a time — allows assessment and treatment of side-specific weakness.

Standing progression (advanced): Single-leg standing with controlled inversion-eversion — maximum peroneal proprioceptive challenge, requiring balance training.

Sequencing context: This exercise is typically placed in the foot/ankle group alongside P0003–P0005 and is the final foot-specific exercise before progressing to the kneecap/knee group (P0006 onward).
