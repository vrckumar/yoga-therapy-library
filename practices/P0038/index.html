Crow Walking

Crow Walking (Kakasana Gati)

Walking in a deep squat position — moving forward in malasana (deep squat/garland pose) by alternately stepping each foot while remaining in the squatted position. A unique combination of hip joint mobilisation, inner thigh strengthening, and coordination training.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Malasana (deep squat). Feet hip-width or wider; heels on the floor (or raised if ankle mobility is limited).

Practice:

  1. Come into a deep squat — knees wide, feet approximately hip-width, thighs as close to parallel with the floor as comfortable.
  2. Place the hands forward for balance if needed (or keep them on the knees or in namaskara).
  3. Walk forward: step the right foot forward slightly, then the left — maintaining the squatted position throughout. The body remains low.
  4. Walk forward 10 steps, then backward 10 steps. Repeat 2–3 times.
  5. Return to standing. Shake out the legs.

Breathing: Normal throughout.

Duration: 10 steps forward + 10 steps backward = 1 round; 2–3 rounds.

2. Anatomical Activation

  • Hip adductors (adductor longus, magnus, gracilis): maximally lengthened in the deep squat position while walking
  • Gluteus maximus and medius: hip extension and abduction during the step
  • Quadriceps: isometric support of the deep flexed knee
  • Gastrocnemius and soleus: ankle dorsiflexion demand in the deep squat position — the ankle dorsiflexion requirement of the squat stretches the gastrocnemius
  • Tensor fasciae latae and IT band: stretched in deep squat
  • Hip capsule: the deep squat position opens the posterior hip capsule

3. Physiological Effects

Deep hip joint mobilisation in weight-bearing

The deep squat position achieves maximum hip joint flexion under weight-bearing load — a position rarely achieved in daily life but essential for full hip joint ROM. The femoral head contacts the posterior and inferior acetabulum in this position — areas not loaded in normal gait. Walking in this position mobilises the hip joint through this deep flexion range, distributing synovial fluid to the posterior acetabular articular surfaces.

Inner thigh and adductor strengthening

The adductors must actively maintain the wide stance while walking in squat — providing dynamic inner thigh strengthening in a functional, low-load context. This is the primary therapeutic exercise for adductor weakness and hip instability.

Ankle dorsiflexion training

The deep squat position requires substantial ankle dorsiflexion. Those who cannot achieve the position without heel raising have ankle dorsiflexion restriction — crow walking progressively trains this range.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — deep hip flexion mobilisation; adductor strengthening; ankle dorsiflexion training; coordination of lower limb movement in a non-standard position.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the deep squat activates the mula bandha region (pelvic floor) and apana vayu.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Hip (A003) Deep hip flexion mobilisation in weight-bearing
Hip Tightness / Restricted ROM Adductor and posterior capsule elongation
Pelvic Floor Dysfunction Deep squat position activates and stretches pelvic floor muscles
Back Pain (A004) Hip flexion mobility reduces lumbar compensation
All IAYT protocols Hip joint deep mobilisation — functional complement to recumbent hip exercises

6. Contraindications

  • Severe knee osteoarthritis — deep squat position creates very high patellofemoral and tibiofemoral compression; avoid if deep flexion is painful
  • Recent knee surgery — deep flexion ROM restrictions apply
  • Severe ankle stiffness — may cause excessive stress at the ankle if heels are forced down

7. Variations & Progressions

Heels raised (modified): Place a rolled mat under heels — reduces ankle dorsiflexion requirement; makes the practice accessible.

Stationary deep squat (before walking): Hold the deep squat position for 10–20 breaths before attempting to walk.

Sequencing context: Follows P0037 (Jumping). Progresses to P0039 (Padasanchalana — supine cycling).
