Hip Twist

Hip Twist (Shroni Chakra)

Lateral hip rotation — alternately rotating the hip outward (external rotation) and inward (internal rotation) from a standing or supine position. Specifically targets the hip joint rotational range and the deep hip rotator muscles (external: piriformis; internal: gluteus medius/minimus in internal rotation role).

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Standing on the right leg; left knee bent to 90° (hip and knee at right angles), foot off the floor.

Practice:

  1. Stand on the right leg. Raise the left knee to 90°.
  2. Rotate the left hip outward (external rotation) — the foot swings outward. Then rotate inward (internal rotation) — the foot swings across the midline.
  3. Perform 10 rotations (outward-inward = 1 round) on the left.
  4. Repeat on the right leg (standing on the left).

Alternative — supine form:

  1. Lie supine. Bend both knees to 90°, feet flat on the floor.
  2. Let both knees fall to the right (external rotation of left hip, internal rotation of right hip). Return to centre. Let knees fall to the left.
  3. 10 rounds.

Breathing: Normal.

2. Anatomical Activation

External rotation (outward swing)

  • Piriformis: primary external hip rotator; also abducts the hip in flexion
  • Obturator internus and externus: external rotators
  • Gemelli superior and inferior: external rotators
  • Quadratus femoris: external rotator and adductor

Internal rotation (inward swing)

  • Gluteus medius (anterior fibres): internal rotation component
  • Gluteus minimus: internal rotation
  • Tensor fasciae latae: hip flexion and internal rotation

3. Physiological Effects

Hip rotational mobility — the neglected plane

The hip joint has three degrees of freedom — flexion-extension, abduction-adduction, and rotation. Most exercises and activities address the first two planes. Hip rotation — especially internal rotation — is the movement plane most restricted in hip arthritis and in patients with piriformis syndrome. Regular hip twist exercises maintain the rotational range and prevent the progressive loss of internal rotation that is a reliable early sign of hip osteoarthritis.

Piriformis conditioning

The piriformis is the primary external hip rotator and the muscle most commonly involved in piriformis syndrome (a cause of sciatic nerve compression at the greater sciatic notch). The alternating rotation — external then internal — cyclically activates and then elongates the piriformis, maintaining its flexibility and preventing the shortening and hypertonicity that causes piriformis syndrome.

Sciatic nerve mobilisation

The sciatic nerve passes either through or adjacent to the piriformis. Hip internal rotation (with the knee bent) provides a neural mobilisation component — gently tensioning and releasing the sciatic nerve within its fascial sleeve.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — hip rotational range mobilisation; piriformis and deep external rotator conditioning; sciatic nerve mobilisation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — activation of the svadhisthana region (sacral-pelvic energy centre) through hip movement.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Hip (A003) Internal rotation range maintenance — the earliest movement lost in hip OA
Piriformis Syndrome Direct piriformis conditioning; sciatic nerve mobilisation
Sciatica Piriformis flexibility; neural mobilisation (use gentle range only)
Back Pain (A004) Hip external rotator flexibility reduces compensatory lumbar rotation
All IAYT protocols Hip rotation preparation for seated and supine practices

6. Contraindications

  • Acute hip labral tear — rotation may engage the labrum; avoid in acute phase
  • Acute piriformis syndrome — gentle movement only; avoid end-range external rotation that maximally loads the piriformis
  • Hip replacement — posterior approach replacements have dislocation risk with combined hip flexion + internal rotation; follow surgeon's protocol

7. Variations & Progressions

Standing (standard): As described — balance challenge adds proprioceptive training.

Supine (modified): Bilateral knee drop as described — easier, no balance demand.

Sequencing context: Part of the trunk/hip group (P0030–P0035). Progresses to P0033 (Forward and Backward Bending).
