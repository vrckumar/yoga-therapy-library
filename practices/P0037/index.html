Jumping

Jumping (Plavana)

On-the-spot jumping — a higher-intensity dynamic exercise that adds vertical impact loading to the cardiovascular stimulation of jogging. Provides significant bone density stimulation through Wolff's Law and develops lower limb power alongside the mobility benefits of the loosening series.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana. Make loose fists and hold on the chest.

Practice:

  1. Begin with slow jogging (as in P0036, Stage I) as a transition into jumping.
  2. Progress from jogging to jumping on both feet — small, controlled vertical jumps on the toes.
  3. Perform 20–30 jumps at comfortable pace.
  4. Option: add the heel-to-buttock kick (as in P0036 Stage II) during the jump phase.
  5. Slow back to jogging, then walking. Stop.
  6. Perform Mukha Dhauti (as in P0036) to recover the breath.

Breathing: Natural rapid breathing during jumping; recovery breath through Mukha Dhauti.

Duration: 20–30 jumps; approximately 1–2 minutes.

2. Anatomical Activation

During the jump phase

  • Gastrocnemius and soleus: explosive plantarflexion driving the jump
  • Quadriceps: knee extension driving the jump
  • Gluteus maximus: hip extension power
  • Tibialis anterior and peroneals: landing stabilisation
  • Abdominal core (transversus, obliques): spinal stabilisation during each landing impact

Ground reaction forces

Jumping generates vertical ground reaction forces of approximately 4–6× body weight at landing (vs 2–3× for jogging). These forces are the primary bone-loading stimulus.

3. Physiological Effects

Wolff's Law — bone density stimulation

Bone tissue responds to mechanical loading by increasing bone mineral density in the direction of load (Wolff's Law). Vertical jumping provides the highest vertical ground reaction forces of any standard exercise, making it the most potent bone-density-stimulating exercise in the loosening series. High-impact loading is the most effective stimulus for osteoblastic activity in the weight-bearing skeleton (tibia, femoral neck, vertebral bodies).

Cardiovascular peak load

Jumping reaches higher peak heart rates than jogging, providing a brief high-intensity cardiovascular stimulus within the IAYT protocol. This brief peak load trains the heart's capacity for rapid rate increase and recovery — important in cardiac conditioning.

Lymphatic and calf venous pump — maximum activation

The repeated dorsiflexion-plantarflexion of each jump generates the maximum amplitude calf pump — superior to jogging for venous return and lymphatic flow.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — impact bone loading; cardiovascular peak stimulation; lower limb power development; maximum calf venous pump activation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the exhilaration and energy of jumping is a classical expression of rajas guna — dynamic vital energy.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Osteoporosis Highest impact bone loading — primary prevention
Obesity (A002) High caloric expenditure; lymphatic activation
Diabetes (A001) Explosive glycolytic exercise — rapid glucose utilisation
Depression / Anxiety Vigorous aerobic exercise and endorphin release

6. Contraindications

  • Osteoporosis (severe/established) — paradoxically: jumping is contraindicated for established osteoporosis (fracture risk); for prevention only in pre-osteoporotic individuals
  • Knee or ankle injury (acute) — impact loading on healing structures
  • Uncontrolled hypertension — peak cardiac loading; medical clearance required
  • Pregnancy — pelvic floor loading and impact; avoid from second trimester
  • Post-abdominal or joint surgery — impact loading during healing period

7. Variations & Progressions

Gentle toe rises (modified): For those who cannot jump; rises on tiptoe and lower — provides some loading without full impact.

Sequencing context: Follows P0036 (Jogging). Progresses to P0038 (Crow Walking).
