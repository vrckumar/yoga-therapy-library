Pranic Energisation Technique (PET)

PET is an advanced SVYASA practice that systematically trains the practitioner to recognise and direct prana through progressively finer levels of awareness — from breath observation to nerve impulse awareness, and finally to recognition and rotation of Vyana, the all-pervading dimension of prana. The technique bridges Pranamaya Kosha and Manomaya Kosha, using breath-balancing (Nadi Suddhi), body resonance (Bhramari), mudra-based sensitisation, and pranic rotation to revitalise every organ and system. It was developed specifically for immune strengthening and has been applied in the management of cancer, AIDS, and chronic fatigue syndrome.

1. Technique

Starting position: Sit in any comfortable meditative posture with spine erect. Eyes closed. Face relaxed and smiling.

Phase 1 — Watching and balancing the breath:

  • Bring attention to the tip of the nose. Watch the breath without controlling it. Observe the natural rhythm, pace, and any imbalance between the two nostrils.
  • Proceed to Nadi Suddhi (alternate nostril breathing) using Nasika Mudra:
    • Inhale through the left nostril (right closed with thumb). Enjoy the natural pause (Kevala Kumbhaka). Exhale slowly through the right.
    • Enjoy the pause. Inhale through the right. Pause. Exhale slowly through the left.
    • Repeat 9 rounds. Allow breath to become slow, rhythmic, and balanced between nostrils.
  • If nostrils are blocked, precede with alternate nostril Kapalabhati (60 cycles) to cleanse the respiratory tract.

Phase 2 — Awareness and relaxation (expanding from point to volume):

  • Trace the movement of air from the tip of the nose all the way to the lungs and back (linear awareness).
  • Feel the cool air entering, the warm air leaving (surface awareness).
  • Practise Bhramari (9 rounds): on exhalation produce a soft bee-hum resonance; feel the vibration throughout the body. Enjoy the Kevala Kumbhaka after each round.
  • Expand to 3-dimensional (3D) body awareness: feel heartbeat, pulse, and their synchronisation throughout the body.

Phase 3 — Sensitisation and recognition of Vyana (mudra sequence): Sequentially adopt four mudras, pressing and releasing each 9 times, tracing nerve impulses upward to the brain:

  • Cin Mudra (tip of index finger touches thumb tip): Feel nerve impulses travelling from fingertips to brain. Abdominal breathing region.
  • Cinmaya Mudra (as above; middle, ring, small fingers folded into palm): Feel thoracic breathing region, nerve impulses intensify.
  • Adi Mudra (thumb folded into palm, fingers closing over it): Feel clavicular region; an avalanche of nerve impulses.
  • Namaskara Mudra (palms joined): Feel heartbeat, pulse, and combined nerve impulse surge.
  • After mudra sequence, move palms slowly in and out (2 cm gap), churning clockwise and anticlockwise. Sense the space between the palms becoming energised — this is the recognition of Vyana, which unlike nerve impulses, can flow in any direction and at any speed.

Phase 4 — Rotation and energisation (pranic rotation):

  • With palms on the knees, begin rotating prana awareness:
    • Right side: From right heel, up the calf, thigh, buttock, low back, right spinal canal, shoulder, neck, right side of skull, across the crown, down the right forehead, eye, nostril, chin, thorax, abdomen, thigh, knee, foot and right fingers.
    • Left side: Mirror rotation.
    • Both sides simultaneously: From both heels and both hands, sweeping up the back, over the crown, and down the front. Feel each organ being vitalised with prana.

Phase 5 — Silence: Allow prana to expand around and beyond the body into the infinite blue sky. Feel expansive silence and associated bliss. Stay as long as comfortable.

Phase 6 — Resolution: Plant a clear, brief, positive resolve nine times in silence (e.g., "My immune system is very powerful", "I am full of energy").

2. Anatomical Activation

  • Respiratory system: Nadi Suddhi and Bhramari actively engage the diaphragm, intercostals, and accessory muscles with slow, controlled patterning.
  • Autonomic nervous system: Alternating nostril dominance relates to alternating sympathetic-parasympathetic tonal shifts via nasal-hypothalamic connections.
  • Peripheral nervous system — hands: The mudra sequence is concentrated at the hand's dense sensory representation in the somatosensory cortex (the cortical homunculus), making fine tactile and nerve-impulse awareness highly accessible.
  • Cardiovascular: Slow, deep breathing with Kevala Kumbhaka increases baroreflex sensitivity and heart rate variability (HRV), reflecting improved ANS regulation.
  • Thoracic and cranial resonators: Bhramari creates resonant vibration engaging the same cavities as MSRT's M-kara.

3. Physiological Effects

From gross breath to subtle prana — a bridge of awareness: PET is structured as a hierarchical expansion of awareness: from the tip of the nose (point/pointed awareness) → along the breath path (linear) → across the inner respiratory surface (surface/2D) → through the entire body via Bhramari resonance (3D). This progression is not metaphor; each step corresponds to recruiting progressively wider cortical and subcortical networks. The insula, anterior cingulate cortex, and interoceptive centres are specifically activated by this kind of layered body-awareness training.

Nadi Suddhi and hemispheric balance: Alternating nostril breathing is known to synchronise activity between the cerebral hemispheres, reducing cognitive reactivity and creating a state of calm alertness — the optimal internal environment for pranic perception. EEG studies have shown increased alpha coherence and reduced sympathetic markers after Nadi Suddhi.

Mudra-based sensitisation — from nerve impulse to Vyana: The mudra sequence works on a principle recognised in modern neuroscience: focused tactile attention amplifies the cortical representation of the stimulated area (cortical plasticity). By repeatedly pressing and releasing the fingertips while attending to the ascending signal, the practitioner trains the brain to resolve finer and finer gradients of somatosensory experience. The transition from "nerve impulse" (directional, speed-limited afferent signal) to "Vyana" (omnidirectional, instantaneous pranic flow) represents a shift from proprioceptive-tactile awareness to a more holistic interoceptive-energetic field perception. This is phenomenologically consistent with what neurophysiology terms "allostatic interoception" — the global monitoring of bodily resources.

Pranic rotation and immune energisation: The deliberate rotation of awareness around the body in Phase 4 constitutes a form of systematic body scan with an energising, not merely relaxing, intent. Directing attention to each organ in sequence while in a parasympathetic state is associated with increased local blood flow (through neurovascular coupling), reduced visceral tension, and — over time — improved organ-specific immune surveillance. The emphasis on Vyana in this phase is consistent with the concept of a distributed pranic matrix that corresponds functionally to the autonomic innervation and microvasculature of each body region.

Silence and immune modulation: The progression into silence in Phase 5 parallels the mechanism described in MSRT: dissolution of the DMN, reduction of cortisol, and entry into a state of alert stillness associated with enhanced natural killer cell activity, increased IL-2 production, and improved immune competence. The resolve planted in this state (Phase 6) leverages the heightened neuroplastic receptivity of the hypnagogic-like silence phase.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Pranamaya Kosha — PET is the dedicated Pranamaya practice in the SVYASA advanced technique suite. It directly cultivates awareness of Prana, Apana, Samana, Udana, and Vyana, with Vyana as the central vehicle for body-wide energisation.

Secondary: Manomaya Kosha — the expansion of awareness required to perceive Vyana trains the mind in defocused, receptive attention; Annamaya Kosha — the physical body benefits from improved respiratory mechanics, circulation, and ANS tone.

5. Indicated For

  • Cancer, AIDS, chronic fatigue syndrome (immune strengthening)
  • Hypertension, IHD (ANS rebalancing, HRV improvement)
  • Chronic anxiety, panic disorder (vagal activation, parasympathetic restoration)
  • Asthma and other respiratory disorders (Nadi Suddhi component)
  • Chronic pain conditions (interoceptive re-education)
  • Diabetes and metabolic syndrome (ANS normalisation, cortisol reduction)
  • Post-surgical or post-illness recovery (revitalisation and prana distribution)
  • Advanced yoga therapy practitioners deepening Pranamaya awareness

6. Contraindications

  • Acute respiratory infection with fever or severe dyspnoea (defer Kapalabhati component; Nadi Suddhi and Bhramari may continue gently)
  • Severe cardiovascular instability (practise Phase 1 only with medical supervision)
  • High blood pressure, IHD, slipped disc, spondylosis: avoid Kapalabhati; rest of practice is generally safe
  • Active psychosis: the deep interoceptive focus in Phase 4 may be destabilising; use with clinical supervision
  • Pregnancy: avoid Kapalabhati; Nadi Suddhi, Bhramari, and mudra phases are safe
  • Menstruation: avoid Kapalabhati; remainder is safe

7. Variations & Progressions

  • Beginner: Start with Phase 1 (breath watching + Nadi Suddhi, 5–7 minutes) only for the first two weeks.
  • Intermediate: Add Bhramari and mudra sequence (Phases 2–3), 15–20 minutes.
  • Advanced: Full six-phase practice, 30–45 minutes. Deepen the duration of Phase 5 (silence).
  • Clinical integration: In IAYT protocols, PET is placed after asana and IRT/QRT, with DRT following. It forms the Pranamaya level of the five-kosha module for cancer and immune-related conditions.
  • Paired with MSRT: MSRT (Manomaya level) and PET (Pranamaya level) are complementary. Many SVYASA programmes run them sequentially, PET first to open the pranic body, then MSRT to deepen the mental-sound continuum.
  • Kapalabhati alternative: If alternate nostril Kapalabhati is contraindicated, substitute with slow Sukha Pranayama (both nostril breathing) as the cleansing step.
