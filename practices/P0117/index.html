Vamana Dhauti (Kunjal Kriya)

Vamana Dhauti / Kunjal Kriya (Gastric Cleansing)

Vamana (Sanskrit) — to vomit, to expel; Dhauti — to wash, to cleanse; Kunjal — an elephant drawing water with its trunk (metaphor for the practice). This Shatkarma involves drinking large volumes of warm saline water and then expelling them through induced emesis, thoroughly cleansing the stomach, oesophagus, and pharynx of their contents.

The practice is described in the Hatha Yoga Pradipika and Gheranda Samhita as one of the primary Dhauti (cleansing) practices. In Ayurveda, it corresponds to Vamana — therapeutic emesis — one of the five Panchakarma procedures, specifically indicated for Kapha disorders.

In the IAYT system, Kunjal Kriya holds a specific and important role in protocols for acid peptic disease, diabetes, obesity, and general GI cleansing. The key distinction from pathological vomiting is that Kunjal is entirely voluntary, controlled, and non-traumatic — the stomach contents are the warm saline water that was just consumed, not acidic stomach contents under distress.

1. Technique

Timing: Early morning, on a completely empty stomach — before any food, tea, or other liquids. The stomach must be empty for the practice to work correctly and safely.

Preparation: Warm 4–6 glasses (1–1.5 litres) of warm saline water. Temperature: comfortably warm, not hot. Salt concentration: 1 teaspoon per 500 ml (isotonic or slightly hypertonic to prevent absorption).

Process:

  1. Drink rapidly: Consume all 4–6 glasses of warm saline water quickly — within 2–3 minutes. Drink without pausing; the large volume and speed of ingestion fill the stomach to near-capacity, which is both necessary for the practice to work and a strong trigger for the emetic reflex.
  2. Position: Stand leaning forward over a toilet, basin, or outdoor area. The forward lean facilitates the upward flow of stomach contents. Feet may be placed on a slightly elevated surface if available.
  3. Induce emesis: Place the middle and index fingers of the right hand into the back of the throat, touching the posterior pharyngeal wall (the area just above the epiglottis). This stimulates the gag reflex. Alternatively, press gently on the root of the tongue.
  4. Expulsion: All the water should come out in one or two bouts of vomiting. The expelled fluid should be clear or very slightly tinged with mucus — this is the normal appearance, as the saline has washed through the stomach.
  5. Completion: Continue until the stomach feels empty and no more fluid can be expelled. The total volume expelled should approximately equal the volume consumed.
  6. Post-practice rest: Lie down in Savasana or Deep Relaxation Technique (DRT) for 15–20 minutes. The stomach and oesophagus need time to recover and rehydrate. Do not eat immediately.
  7. First food: After the rest period, a light breakfast (fruit or herbal tea) is appropriate. A regular breakfast follows 30 minutes later.

Frequency:

  • In the IAYT Diabetes protocol: weekly (typically Sunday morning)
  • In the IAYT acid peptic disease protocol: as directed by the yoga therapist, based on acidity symptoms
  • General GI maintenance: weekly or bi-weekly
  • In obesity: weekly alongside dietary modification

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary structures engaged

Stomach (gaster): The primary organ targeted. The large volume of saline fills the stomach fully, expanding the gastric wall and triggering stretch receptors throughout the fundus, body, and antrum. The saline then physically contacts and washes the entire gastric mucosa — removing accumulated mucus, excess acid, food residue, and inflammatory mediators from the mucosal surface.

Oesophagus: As the water is expelled, it passes back through the entire length of the oesophagus, washing the oesophageal mucosa, the lower oesophageal sphincter (LES), and the upper oesophageal sphincter. This reverse flow, in the context of Kunjal (where the fluid being expelled is non-acidic saline), is not damaging — unlike pathological acid reflux, which exposes the oesophageal mucosa to corrosive gastric acid.

Pharynx and soft palate: The posterior pharyngeal wall is mechanically stimulated by the fingers to trigger the gag reflex. This area is also washed by the outgoing fluid.

Gastric cardiac sphincter (lower oesophageal sphincter / LES): The large volume of fluid and the distension of the gastric fundus stretches the LES. In patients with LES incompetence (hiatal hernia, GERD), this stretch can temporarily worsen reflux — hence the contraindication in these conditions.

Autonomic nervous system — vagal pathways: The stretch of the stomach wall and the gag reflex both strongly activate vagal afferents:

  • Gastric wall stretch → vagal afferents → nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) → emetic centre (area postrema) → motor efferents → emesis
  • Post-emetic state → strong parasympathetic rebound → the post-Kunjal rest period is characterised by deep parasympathetic dominance, explaining the profound relaxation reported

Diaphragm (during emesis): The diaphragm contracts forcefully as part of the emetic reflex, creating a strong positive intrathoracic pressure that assists in expelling the stomach contents upward. This vigorous diaphragmatic engagement is a secondary muscular benefit of the practice.

3. Physiological Effects

Gastric mucosal cleansing and acid neutralisation — the primary mechanism

1. Physical removal of excess acid In hyperacidity (acid peptic disease — elevated gastric acid secretion), the stomach contains a pool of concentrated hydrochloric acid (HCl), excess pepsin, and inflammatory prostaglandins in the gastric fluid. The large volume of saline:

  • Dilutes and mechanically displaces this acidic pool
  • Washes the gastric mucosa free of the excess acid that is causing mucosal irritation
  • The expelled fluid carries away the diluted acid, prostaglandins, and inflammatory mediators

The net result is an acute reduction in gastric acidity (a significant pH rise in the stomach) and a thorough washing of the mucosal surface — providing immediate relief from hyperacidity and gastric irritation.

2. Removal of excess Kapha (gastric mucus accumulation) In Ayurvedic physiology, the stomach is the primary site of Kapha accumulation. When digestion is sluggish or incomplete, undigested material (ama) accumulates in the stomach as a thick, heavy, mucus-like substance. This produces the characteristic morning heaviness, nausea, and sluggishness experienced by many people (particularly diabetics and those with slow metabolic function). The large volume of saline physically dislodges and removes this accumulated gastric mucus, explaining the remarkable improvement in energy and lightness reported after Kunjal.

3. Morning heaviness and sluggishness in diabetes Diabetics commonly experience morning heaviness, early satiety, and GI sluggishness — partly from autonomic neuropathy affecting the stomach (diabetic gastroparesis), partly from hyperglycaemia-induced osmotic fluid shifts, and partly from the ama accumulation of Ayurvedic description. Weekly Kunjal Kriya in the diabetes IAYT protocol addresses this directly: the complete emptying and washing of the stomach resets gastric function, improves gastric motility (through the vagal rebound), and provides the subjective experience of lightness and clarity that improves dietary compliance throughout the week.

4. The vagal rebound — post-Kunjal parasympathetic dominance The act of vomiting is a powerful sympathetic and vagal event. In the immediate aftermath of Kunjal, the body undergoes a parasympathetic rebound: heart rate slows, blood pressure normalises, gastric secretory activity is temporarily suppressed, and the practitioner experiences profound rest. This explains why DRT (15–20 minutes of Deep Relaxation Technique) is prescribed after Kunjal — the nervous system is in an optimal state for deep relaxation.

The parasympathetic rebound also produces a temporary suppression of gastric acid secretion, providing a window of time during which the washed mucosa can begin healing.

5. Weekly reset of GI health in diabetes The weekly Kunjal protocol for diabetics serves a specific function: resetting the GI environment once per week. Diabetics who practice Kunjal weekly consistently report:

  • Reduced morning hyperglycaemia (the stomach emptying removes the nocturnal gastric residue that impairs insulin sensitivity through the incretin mechanism)
  • Reduced morning nausea and heaviness
  • Improved appetite regulation throughout the week
  • Reduced frequency of acid peptic symptoms

The mechanism involves the combination of gastric mucosal cleansing, vagal rebound, and the parasympathetic reactivation of normal gastric motility (overcoming the gastroparetic tendency).

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — direct physical cleansing of the stomach, oesophagus, and pharynx. The removal of excess acid, mucus, and inflammatory mediators operates entirely at the physical body level.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — in yogic physiology, the stomach and upper GI tract are the primary site of Samana Vayu (the balancing pranic current responsible for digestion). Kunjal Kriya clears the obstructions to Samana Vayu function — the accumulated ama (undigested matter) and excess Kapha that impede pranic flow through the abdominal region.

Tertiary: Manomaya Kosha — the removal of accumulated morning heaviness and GI sluggishness has a direct effect on mental clarity. The traditional teaching that Kunjal "removes morning sluggishness" (tandra) is a Manomaya Kosha effect — the clearing of the physical GI burden produces a corresponding clearing of mental dullness.

6. Contraindications

  • Hernia (hiatal, inguinal, abdominal) — the increased intra-abdominal and intrathoracic pressure during emesis can worsen any type of hernia. Hiatal hernia specifically: the forceful expulsion can push more stomach through the diaphragmatic hiatus. Absolute contraindication.
  • Severe hypertension — the Valsalva-like effort during emesis can produce brief but significant spikes in blood pressure. Avoid if BP > 160/100.
  • Ischaemic Heart Disease (active) — the cardiovascular demands of emesis are contraindicated in active IHD.
  • Peptic ulcer (active, with or without bleeding) — the gastric washing and acid mobilisation can irritate or rupture an active ulcer. Absolute contraindication. May be carefully reintroduced in remission under guidance.
  • After meals — only on an absolutely empty stomach. If any food is present, emesis will produce acidic, partially digested material — causing oesophageal irritation and defeating the cleansing purpose.
  • Haematemesis (bleeding from the upper GI tract) — absolute contraindication.
  • Pregnancy — absolute contraindication. The emesis and diaphragmatic pressure are dangerous.
  • Severe reflux oesophagitis — if the oesophageal mucosa is already damaged by acid reflux, the reverse flow of even isotonic saline will further irritate it. Treat the oesophagitis first.
  • Epilepsy — the hyperventilation during vigorous vomiting may trigger a seizure.

7. Variations & Progressions

Volume adjustment: Beginners may start with 3 glasses (750 ml) and gradually work up to 4–6 glasses (1–1.5 litres). The minimum volume to trigger complete gastric emptying varies individually.

Temperature: Water should be warm but comfortable. Water that is too hot can injure the gastric mucosa; too cool is less effective at stimulating the emetic reflex.

Without finger induction: Some experienced practitioners develop the ability to initiate vomiting without finger induction — the full stomach and forward lean are sufficient. This is a skill that develops over months of practice and should not be forced initially.

Combination with Jala Neti: In protocols for sinusitis or allergic rhinitis where GI health is also compromised, Jala Neti and Kunjal Kriya may be performed in the same morning session — Jala Neti first (upper respiratory), then Kunjal (gastric). The DRT rest period follows both.

Laghu Shankhaprakshalana (P0118): The complete intestinal cleansing — Laghu Shankhaprakshalana — extends Kunjal's gastric cleansing through the entire GI tract. Kunjal alone addresses the stomach and upper GI; Laghu Shankhaprakshalana continues from stomach to rectum.

Post-practice diet: After Kunjal, the first meal is always light and easily digestible — fruit, yogurt, or a light porridge. A full IAYT diet resumes from the next meal.
