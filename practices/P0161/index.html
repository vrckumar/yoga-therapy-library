Straight Leg Raise Breathing

Straight Leg Raise Breathing

A supine breath-movement coordination exercise in which both legs are raised simultaneously on inhalation and lowered on exhalation, with the knees perfectly straight throughout. Engages the abdominal musculature and hip flexors strongly on the raise; demands slow controlled eccentric lowering on the exhalation. A more demanding supine exercise than the single-leg version, training abdominal and hip flexor coordination with breath. Used in back pain, obesity, and general strengthening protocols.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine posture — lie flat on the back with legs together. Stretch both arms overhead with the biceps touching the ears and palms facing the ceiling.

Stage I — Alternate legs (preparatory):

  1. Inhaling slowly: raise the right leg without bending the knee, as far as comfortable (up to 90° if possible).
  2. Exhaling: return the right leg to the floor as slowly as possible.
  3. Repeat with the left leg.
  4. This is one round. Perform 10 rounds (alternating legs).

Stage II — Both legs (main practice):

  1. Inhaling slowly: raise both legs together without bending at the knees, as far as comfortable (up to 90°).
  2. Exhaling: return both legs to the floor as slowly as possible.
  3. Perform 5 rounds.

Notes:

  • Arms remain stretched overhead throughout, biceps beside the ears.
  • Do not bend the knees at any point during the practice.
  • On the descent, as the legs approach the floor, resist the tendency to let them fall rapidly — use strength and control throughout the exhalation.
  • Do not raise the legs beyond 90°.
  • Perfectly synchronise the breathing with the leg movements — do not hold the breath.
  • Maintain perfect breath awareness during the practice.
  • Avoid Stage II (both legs) if there is low back pain — the bilateral version places significant strain on the lumbar spine, particularly in patients with weak abdominals.

Duration/rounds: Stage I: 10 rounds. Stage II: 5 rounds.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Iliopsoas (psoas major and iliacus): primary hip flexors; concentrically raise the legs; eccentrically control the descent
  • Rectus femoris: assists hip flexion and maintains knee extension throughout
  • Rectus abdominis and transversus abdominis: must contract isometrically to prevent the lumbar spine from hyper-extending during the bilateral leg raise — this is the primary abdominal training mechanism
  • Hip flexors (bilateral): in Stage II, both sides work simultaneously, increasing the demand on the lumbar stabilisers
  • Erector spinae: isometrically stabilise the lumbar spine against the large hip flexor pull

Critical biomechanical note

During bilateral straight leg raise, the combined pull of the psoas and rectus femoris on the lumbar vertebrae is very strong. If the abdominal core is insufficiently strong to stabilise the pelvis, the lumbar spine will hyperextend and arch off the floor — producing compression on the lumbar facets and potential aggravation of disc problems. This is why the exercise is contraindicated in active low back pain until core strength is established.

3. Physiological Effects

Abdominal core strengthening

The straight leg raise is one of the classic core-strengthening movements in physiotherapy rehabilitation. In the yoga context, synchronising the leg raise with slow inhalation:

  1. Requires the abdominal muscles to counteract the large hip flexor torque while simultaneously accommodating the inhalation breath
  2. Trains intra-abdominal pressure regulation under load — a key functional skill for spinal protection during daily activities

Hip flexor strength

Eccentric loading of the hip flexors on the slow descent provides progressive strength training. Weak hip flexors are associated with reduced walking speed, balance difficulties, and postural problems — particularly in the elderly. The controlled eccentric phase (slow lowering on exhalation) is more therapeutically valuable than the concentric phase.

Breath coordination under load

Synchronising breathing with leg movement trains the patient to maintain breath awareness during effortful activity — the functional skill that translates to avoiding breath-holding during exertion in daily life.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — abdominal and hip flexor training; lumbar stabilisation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the supine inhalation-with-leg-raise engages prana vayu (upward-lifting vital energy) in the lower abdomen and pelvis.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Obesity (A002) Strong abdominal and hip flexor training; tones lower abdominal wall
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Abdominal toning; lower body activation
Back Pain (A004) Only Stage I (single leg) during acute phases; bilateral version only when core strength is established
General reconditioning Progressive lower body and core strength building

6. Contraindications

  • Active low back pain: avoid Stage II (bilateral); use only Stage I with careful monitoring
  • Hip flexor strain or iliopsoas tendinopathy: reduce range and avoid end-range hip flexion
  • Pregnancy: avoid supine leg-raise exercises from second trimester
  • Severe osteoporosis of the lumbar spine: the mechanical demand of bilateral leg raise may be excessive

7. Variations & Progressions

Progression path:

  1. Single-leg alternating (Stage I) with slow breath coordination
  2. Bilateral (Stage II) when Stage I is well-established and pain-free
  3. Add arm overhead stretch (overhead reach with inhalation adds spinal traction)

Simplified version: Arms can be placed beside the body with palms down for added stability instead of overhead, reducing the demand on the abdominal musculature.
