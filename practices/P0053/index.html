Folded Leg Lumbar Stretch

Folded Leg Lumbar Stretch (Vakra Janu Prishtha Naman)

Supine spinal rotation with the knees folded to the side — a diagonal rotation stretch where both knees drop to one side while the shoulders remain flat on the floor. A key practice for lumbar rotation mobility and the most therapeutic lumbar rotational stretch in the series.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine Posture. Both knees bent, feet flat on floor.

Practice:

  1. Lie flat with both knees bent to 90°, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart.
  2. While exhaling, let both knees fall to the right — lower both knees toward the floor on the right side. Keep the shoulders and upper back flat on the floor.
  3. Look to the left (or forward) as the knees drop right — creating a diagonal rotation through the lumbar spine.
  4. Hold for 5 breaths.
  5. While inhaling, bring both knees back to centre.
  6. Exhale; let both knees fall to the left. Hold 5 breaths.
  7. Repeat 5 rounds alternating.

Breathing: Exhale on rotation; breathe normally during hold; inhale returning to centre.

Duration: 5 rounds each side.

2. Anatomical Activation

Structures stretched/mobilised

  • Lumbar rotator cuff (multifidus, rotatores lumborum): rotational stretch at the lumbar level — the lumbar spine is the structure connecting the stationary shoulders and the rotating knees
  • Thoracolumbar fascia: rotational shear force stretches the fascial layers
  • Lumbar facet joints: opened on the contralateral side; the rotation gaps the facet joints on one side while closing the other
  • Quadratus lumborum (contralateral): elongated during the lateral drop
  • Piriformis (upper leg side): external rotation of the upper hip in the drop position — piriformis stretch

3. Physiological Effects

Lumbar rotation — therapeutic distinction

The lumbar spine has very limited rotation (approximately 5° per level) and the facet joints of the lumbar spine are oriented to resist rotation. Paradoxically, lumbar rotation in the supine folded-knee position is among the most therapeutic movements available:

  1. Supine position eliminates axial load: without the weight of the trunk on the lumbar spine, the rotation occurs with minimal compression on the disc annulus — much safer than standing rotation
  2. Bilateral knees provide a controlled rotation moment: the combined weight of both legs in the dropped position provides a consistent, reproducible rotation torque that the practitioner cannot exceed (gravity-limited)
  3. Facet joint gapping on the contralateral side: the rotation opens the posterior facet joints on the contralateral side — direct decompressive mobilisation of the facets most compressed in lumbar lordosis

This practice is used in osteopathic and physiotherapy manual therapy as the "supine rotation test" — it is also the most common self-treatment exercise prescribed for lumbar facet joint pain.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — lumbar rotational mobilisation; thoracolumbar fascia rotational shear; facet joint decompressive gapping; piriformis elongation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the spinal twist is the classic prana channel activation movement in yogic anatomy; the supine version allows the twist to occur with complete muscular release.

Sacroiliac Joint Dysfunction Rotational loading on the SI joint in a gravity-limited position

All IAYT protocols Critical back pain practice; used in every IAYT lumbar protocol

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar disc herniation — rotation loads the annular fibres; use with caution in subacute phase (gentle range only)
  • Severe lumbar spondylolisthesis — rotation at the listhesis level may be destabilising; medical assessment required

7. Variations & Progressions

Without holding — dynamic (5 rounds): Drop right, return, drop left, return — continuous rhythmic rotation.

With sustained hold (therapeutic): Hold 1–2 minutes each side for deep fascial release.

Feet wide (increased rotation): Place feet wider than hips — the knee drop creates greater lumbar rotation.

Sequencing context: Most therapeutically significant exercise in the lumbar stretching sub-series. Follows P0051–P0052. Progresses to P0054–P0056.
