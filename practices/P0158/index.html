Chakrasana

Chakrasana / Urdhva Dhanurasana (Wheel Pose / Upward Bow)

Chakra (Sanskrit) — wheel, circle; Urdhva — upward; Dhanura — bow. From the supine position, the body arches upward into a full back-arch supported on the palms and soles, the spine forming a complete circle when viewed laterally. Chakrasana is the most intense spinal extension posture in the standard IAYT asana repertoire — it simultaneously demands maximum thoracic and lumbar spinal extension, shoulder external rotation and elevation, hip extension, and quadriceps strength. The source material (PGDYTP 102) calls it "vigorous" and emphasises that only practitioners who have mastered preparatory backbends (Bhujangasana, Dhanurasana, Ustrasana) should attempt it.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine — lie flat on the back on the floor.

Practice:

  1. Lie flat. Bend the knees and raise them; bring the feet toward the buttocks until they are near touching. Keep the feet parallel and approximately hip-width apart.
  2. Bend the elbows and place the palms on the floor beside the ears, fingers pointing toward the feet, wrists shoulder-width apart.
  3. Inhale, press the palms and raise the trunk; rest the crown of the head on the floor. Take two breaths in this intermediate position.
  4. Exhale fully, then press firmly through both palms and soles simultaneously; lift the head and entire trunk off the floor; arch the back upward.
  5. Straighten the arms from the shoulders until the elbows are fully extended. Simultaneously pull the thigh muscles up, pressing the feet firmly into the floor and extending the hips.
  6. To deepen: exhale, pull the thigh muscles further by momentarily lifting the heels off the floor, extending the chest, stretching the sacral region upward — then slowly lower the heels back to the floor while maintaining the stretched spine.
  7. Remain in the full position from 30 seconds to 1 minute with normal breathing.
  8. To release: exhale, bend the knees and elbows, carefully lower the body to the floor. Rest flat on the back and breathe deeply.

Breathing: Inhale to set up; exhale into the full lift; normal breathing during hold; exhale to lower.

Duration/rounds: 30 seconds to 1 minute; 1–3 rounds for experienced practitioners.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Erector spinae (thoracic and lumbar): concentrically active throughout; producing and sustaining the maximum spinal extension arc — the highest sustained erector spinae loading in the asana repertoire
  • Triceps brachii: fully extended under load as they push the body away from the floor — significant upper-body strength demand
  • Anterior deltoids: load the shoulder in extension under the full body weight; the shoulder must sustain an unusual combination of elevation, external rotation, and extension
  • Shoulder external rotators (infraspinatus, teres minor): active throughout to maintain the shoulder in external rotation required for safe full overhead loading
  • Wrist extensors: the palms bear a significant fraction of body weight in full wrist extension — a high loading moment for the dorsal wrist structures
  • Quadriceps: active to maintain the knee near-extension and prevent the hips from dropping
  • Gluteus maximus: concentrically active as the primary hip extensor driving the arch upward
  • Psoas and anterior hip: under maximum eccentric stretch as the hips are driven into extension

Organs and structures affected

  • Anterior thoracic contents: the thoracic cage is maximally expanded; the lungs, pericardium, and anterior mediastinum receive the most complete passive opening available in yoga
  • Thyroid and parathyroid glands: the cervical extension in the arch stretches the anterior cervical structures; the thyroid bed receives increased blood flow
  • Abdominal aorta and abdominal viscera: the anterior abdominal wall is maximally stretched; the abdominal contents are spread and decompressed
  • Adrenal glands: the lumbar extension with strong erector spinae activity stimulates the region of the adrenal glands

3. Physiological Effects

Full spinal extension — the rehabilitation and toning mechanism

Chakrasana is the maximum expression of spinal extension in the seated and supine posture repertoire. Unlike the partial extensions of Bhujangasana or Ustrasana, which open only the thoracic or lumbar spine respectively, Chakrasana extends the entire spinal column simultaneously from the cervical curve to the sacrum in a single, balanced arch.

The physiological consequences of this full-arc extension are profound:

1. Anterior disc decompression: habitual spinal flexion (sitting, forward bending, sedentary postures) creates chronic anterior disc compression. The full spinal extension of Chakrasana reverses this — the anterior aspect of each intervertebral disc is maximally opened, and the nucleus pulposus is encouraged toward its posterior-neutral position. In chronic low back pain with anterior disc degeneration, this is strongly rehabilitative.

2. Posterior facet joint loading and articular cartilage nutrition: the extension movement loads the posterior facet joints, compressing the articular cartilage and forcing synovial fluid into the joint under pressure. When the posture is released and the joints decompress, fresh synovial fluid is drawn in. This hydraulic pumping nourishes the avascular articular cartilage — the mechanism for the "revitalized back" and "alert and supple spine" noted in the source material.

3. Thoracic kyphosis correction: the primary structural benefit in a population with chronic thoracic kyphosis. The full bridge arch drives the thoracic spine into maximum extension, stretching the shortened anterior thoracic structures (pectorals, anterior intercostals, short head of biceps) and strengthening the chronically lengthened posterior structures (rhomboids, middle and lower trapezius, thoracic erectors). Over months of practice, thoracic kyphosis measurably reduces.

Anterior body opening — respiratory and energetic

The thoracic and abdominal expansion in the full backbend:

  • Maximally expands lung volume — both vital capacity and total lung capacity increase during the hold
  • Stretches the respiratory diaphragm in its central tendon, improving diaphragmatic range of motion
  • Opens the costocostal intervals, preventing intercostal rigidity

The "great energy, vitality and lightness" described in the source material corresponds to the acute cardiovascular effect: heart rate increases significantly during Chakrasana (the large muscle mass demand), then drops sharply post-hold as the parasympathetic rebound occurs — producing the characteristic post-backbend sense of lightness and energy.

Neuroendocrine stimulation

The combination of strong cervical extension (thyroid), strong lumbar extension over the kidneys (adrenal zone), and the overall sympathetic activation of a demanding posture creates a profound neuroendocrine stimulus. The adrenal glands, stimulated by the lumbar extension, release cortisol and epinephrine acutely during the posture; the subsequent relaxation produces a rebound anti-stress effect. The thyroid stimulation from cervical extension contributes to the metabolic activation effects seen in Diabetes and Obesity protocols.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — maximal full spinal extension; anterior body opening; posterior spinal strengthening; shoulder and wrist loading.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the fully open chest and forward-facing upper body in the arch is the most complete expression of prana vayu and anahata chakra opening in the entire asana system; the intense activation of the posterior energy channels (pingala) in the full backbend is associated with the invigoration and energy expansion that practitioners consistently report.

Asthma (A007) Maximal thoracic expansion; diaphragmatic stretch; lung volume increase

6. Contraindications (Strong)

  • Beginners: absolutely do not attempt without mastery of Bhujangasana, Dhanurasana, and Ustrasana; sudden full backbends without preparation can seriously injure spinal muscles
  • Acute back pain / disc prolapse: the full extension under load is contraindicated in active disc herniation or facet joint arthritis with radiculopathy
  • Frozen shoulder / rotator cuff tear: the overhead shoulder loading in full external rotation is contraindicated
  • Wrist pain / carpal tunnel: the full-extension wrist loading; use fists instead of flat palms to reduce wrist extension if wrist pain prevents flat-palm support
  • High blood pressure (uncontrolled): the combined isometric effort and inverted head position significantly raises blood pressure
  • Abdominal hernia: the abdominal stretch during the arch
  • Pregnancy: the full supine backbend with the abdomen stretched is contraindicated from the second trimester onward

7. Variations & Progressions

Preparation sequence (mandatory before Chakrasana): Bhujangasana → Dhanurasana → Ustrasana → Setu Bandhasana → Chakrasana. Each posture in this sequence progressively increases the spinal extension demand.

Setu Bandhasana (Bridge Pose): The intermediate between Ustrasana and Chakrasana — the crown of the head on the floor with the body bridged between head and feet. This provides most of the thoracic extension without the wrist/shoulder demand.

Standard Chakrasana (as described): Full bridge on palms and soles, arms straight. 30 seconds–1 minute.

Advanced: From the fully lifted position, walk the hands toward the feet to increase the arch; or from standing, drop back into the full arch without the supine setup.

Counterpose (essential): After Chakrasana, always perform Sasankasana (Child's Pose, P0097) and/or Pavanamuktasana (wind-relieving pose) to decompress the posterior spinal elements that were under high compression during the arch.
