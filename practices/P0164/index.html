Nadanusandana (A-kara, U-kara, M-kara, AUM Chanting)

Nadanusandana — literally "tracing or inquiring into sound" — is a structured practice of systematic acoustic resonance through the sequential chanting of the three primordial syllables A, U, and M, followed by their synthesis as AUM (OM). Each syllable is paired with a specific hasta mudra that tunes the practitioner's awareness to a distinct region of the body: A resonates in the abdomen and lower body, U in the chest and mid-body, M in the head and cranial cavity, and AUM sweeps the whole from base to crown. The practice uses sound as a vibratory instrument, not merely as devotional chant: the pitch is adjusted until true acoustic resonance is felt throughout the target region, and the post-resonance silence is used to deepen awareness and release subtle tensions. Nadanusandana is distinct from Bhramari (which uses the N-kara nasal hum) and from OM Meditation (which is silent, mental repetition).

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrasana (may also be performed in Padmasana or Sukhasana). Spine erect. Eyes closed. Face relaxed and smiling.

A. A-kara Chanting (9 rounds)

  • Adopt Cin Mudra: touch the tip of the index finger to the tip of the thumb; other fingers straight. Rest hands on the thighs.
  • Inhale slowly and completely.
  • While exhaling, chant 'AAA' — a low-pitched, open vowel sound — allowing the sound to vibrate freely in the abdomen and lower thorax.
  • Feel the resonance spreading through the abdomen, pelvis, and lower body.
  • At the end of exhalation, remain briefly in the post-resonance silence. Notice the subtle continuation of the vibration.
  • Repeat nine rounds.

B. U-kara Chanting (9 rounds)

  • Adopt Cinmaya Mudra: tip of index finger to tip of thumb (as Cin Mudra); middle, ring, and small fingers folded into the palm. Hands on thighs.
  • Inhale slowly and completely.
  • While exhaling, chant 'UUU' — a mid-pitched, rounded vowel — directing awareness to the chest and mid-body.
  • Feel the resonance in the sternum, ribs, and thoracic cavity.
  • Rest in post-resonance silence.
  • Repeat nine rounds.

C. M-kara Chanting (9 rounds)

  • Adopt Adi Mudra: fold the thumb into the centre of the palm; close all four fingers over the thumb. Hands on thighs.
  • Inhale slowly and completely.
  • While exhaling, chant 'MMM' — a high-pitched, nasal-resonant consonant — directing awareness to the entire head region.
  • Feel the resonance in the skull, sinuses, and cranial vault.
  • Rest in post-resonance silence.
  • Repeat nine rounds.

D. AUM Chanting (9 rounds)

  • Adopt Brahma Mudra: touch the tips of all four fingers to the base of the thumbs, both fists resting on the thighs knuckle-upward (or hands joined in Brahma Mudra position).
  • Inhale slowly, filling the lungs completely.
  • While exhaling, chant 'A-U-M' as a single continuous sound — beginning with A (abdomen resonance), transitioning through U (thoracic resonance), and completing with M (cranial resonance).
  • Feel the complete sweep of resonance from the base of the body to the crown of the head.
  • Rest in post-resonance silence.
  • Repeat nine rounds.

Note on pitch adjustment: The pitch should be adjusted during practice until a fine resonance — a perceptible bodily vibration — is felt throughout the target region. Resonance occurs only when the frequency of the produced sound matches the natural resonant frequency of the body cavity. The post-resonance silence after each round deepens awareness and releases subtle tensions more completely than the sound itself.

2. Anatomical Activation

  • Larynx and vocal cords: Generate the fundamental frequency; fine-grained muscular control is required to find the resonant pitch.
  • Abdominal and thoracic cavities: Act as the primary resonating chambers for A and U respectively; the diaphragm, intercostals, and abdominal wall modulate the resonating volume.
  • Cranial resonance chambers: Sinuses, nasal passages, hard palate, and skull vault resonate with the M-kara's high-frequency component. Cranial bones conduct vibration.
  • Mudra-specific regions:
    • Cin Mudra (fingers open) → relaxed hand → associated with abdominal breathing / Muladhara-Svadhisthana resonance field.
    • Cinmaya Mudra (fingers folded) → proprioceptive shift in the hand activates thoracic awareness.
    • Adi Mudra (thumb enclosed) → deepest proprioceptive signal; associated with clavicular and head region.
    • Brahma Mudra (fists) → integrates all three levels.
  • Respiratory system: Nine slow full exhalations per syllable constitute an extended pranayama-level practice that recruits the parasympathetic nervous system through prolonged vagal stimulation.

3. Physiological Effects

Resonance as the central mechanism: When a sound wave's frequency matches the natural vibratory frequency of a body cavity, resonance occurs — the amplitude of the wave doubles, and the vibration can be felt far from its source. The key discipline of Nadanusandana is not to chant loudly but to chant at the right pitch. The practitioner must actively search for this resonant frequency, making the practice an exercise in acoustic proprioception — learning to feel one's own body as a tuned instrument.

A-kara and the abdominal-pelvic resonance field: The vowel 'A' is produced with the mouth wide open and the vocal tract at its largest diameter, producing the lowest fundamental frequencies of the three syllables. At these low frequencies (typically 80–200 Hz), vibration propagates most effectively through dense, fluid-filled tissue — the abdominal organs, pelvic floor, and bowel. The gentle mechanical stimulation of visceral resonance activates vagal afferents innervating the gut-brain axis, promoting parasympathetic tone, peristalsis, and reduction of visceral tension. The Cin Mudra, associated with abdominal breathing in the SVYASA system, reinforces this abdominal awareness through tactile proprioceptive signalling from the fingertips.

U-kara and thoracic resonance: The vowel 'U' is produced with rounded lips and a narrowed oral cavity, raising the fundamental frequency to mid-range. The thoracic cavity — with its air-filled lungs, rigid rib cage, and pericardium — resonates most readily in this range. Thoracic resonance stimulates the cardiac plexus, the pulmonary vagal fibres, and the intercostal nerves, producing a gentle massage effect on the heart and lungs. This is associated with reduced heart rate, increased HRV, and improved respiratory efficiency. The Cinmaya Mudra, linked to thoracic breathing, reinforces this mid-body focus.

M-kara and cranial-neural resonance: The 'M' phoneme involves closure of the lips with sustained nasal resonance, generating the highest frequencies of the three syllables. These high frequencies propagate most efficiently through bone and the fluid-filled cranial cavity. Skull bone conduction transmits vibration directly to the cochlea, activating the inner ear and its vestibular connections. The nasal cavities and sinuses vibrate intensely, stimulating the olfactory bulb and its direct connections to the limbic system. The cribriform plate vibration may stimulate the vagal portion innervating the meninges. The Adi Mudra (deep proprioceptive fist) consolidates this high-resonance signal.

AUM synthesis — longitudinal coherence: The sequential A→U→M chant creates a controlled longitudinal wave of resonance that sweeps the entire body from the pelvic floor to the cranial vault. This body-wide acoustic sweep acts as a neuromuscular scan, releasing residual tension wherever resonance encounters rigidity. The Brahma Mudra integrates all four respiratory modes (abdominal, thoracic, clavicular, complete), aligning the hand mudra hierarchy with the full resonance sweep. The resulting state — characterised by full-body vibration followed by deep silence — is among the most effective methods for inducing the 3-dimensional body awareness described in PET.

Post-resonance silence: Each set of nine rounds ends in a silence that is qualitatively different from ordinary quiet. The vibrational afterglow of resonance continues to propagate through the body's tissues for several seconds after the sound ceases, creating a heightened sensitivity to subtle interoceptive signals. This is the moment when "even subtle tensions" are released (as the PGDYTP 101 source notes). Neurologically, this corresponds to the post-activation rebound of the parasympathetic system following the mild sympathetic arousal of chanting, resulting in deeper-than-baseline relaxation.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Pranamaya Kosha — the resonant vibration of A, U, and M-kara directly stimulates the pranic body through its relationship with the respiratory system and the five vayu regions (Apana → Prana → Udana).

Secondary: Manomaya Kosha — the prolonged auditory-kinesthetic feedback of resonance trains the mind in the "sound dimension" of awareness, complementary to MSRT's mantra-based approach; Annamaya Kosha — the physical body benefits from visceral, thoracic, and cranial stimulation.

5. Indicated For

  • Anxiety, panic disorder, chronic mental restlessness
  • Asthma and respiratory disorders (thoracic resonance, extended exhalation)
  • Irritable bowel syndrome and functional digestive disorders (visceral resonance via A-kara)
  • Hypertension (vagal activation, ANS rebalancing)
  • Insomnia (the transition from resonance to silence deepens sleep onset)
  • Chronic fatigue, low prana states
  • Preparation for deeper meditation (clearing the body-pranic field)
  • Voice disorders and vocal fatigue (improves resonance and projection)
  • Tinnitus management (the resonance practice can recalibrate auditory sensitivity over time)

6. Contraindications

  • Acute laryngitis or vocal cord pathology (reduce volume; if chanting is impossible, substitute mental (anahata) resonance)
  • Severe tinnitus (loud resonance may temporarily worsen perception; start at very low volume)
  • Uncontrolled hypertension (the expiratory effort of chanting is mild; contraindication is relative)
  • Acute asthma attack (defer; Nadanusandana is beneficial between attacks but should not be practised during acute bronchospasm)
  • Third trimester of pregnancy (abdominal resonance with A-kara may be uncomfortable; modify by reducing volume and switching to U-kara and M-kara only)

7. Variations & Progressions

  • Beginner: Start with A-kara and AUM only, 5–7 minutes. Focus on finding the resonant pitch rather than volume.
  • Intermediate: Full four-part sequence (A, U, M, AUM), nine rounds each, 20–25 minutes.
  • Advanced: Precede with Bhramari (9 rounds) to open the resonance sensitivity; follow with OM Meditation for the mental dimension.
  • MSRT integration: Nadanusandana (external, body-specific resonance) and MSRT (mantra-level resonance progressing to silence) share the same resonance principle. Nadanusandana can serve as the preparatory foundation for MSRT.
  • Group chanting: In group settings, the collective resonance creates a shared acoustic field that amplifies individual experience and builds group cohesion.
  • Therapeutic modifications:
    • For respiratory conditions: extend U-kara rounds; practise in Vajrasana or seated on a bolster.
    • For neurological conditions: emphasise M-kara; add gentle ear-covering (Sanmukhi Mudra-lite) to intensify cranial resonance awareness.
    • For digestive conditions: emphasise A-kara; practise in Vajrasana (which itself promotes abdominal circulation).
