Head Rolling

Head Rolling (Griva Bhramana / Cervical Rotation)

Griva = neck, bhramana = circular movement/rotation. A gentle full-range circular movement of the head in which the neck is taken through its complete range of motion in a continuous arc — forward, to one side, backward, to the other side, and returning to centre — tracing a circle with the top of the head. Mobilises all cervical facet joints through their complete range; stretches the full perimeter of the neck musculature; promotes synovial fluid distribution in the cervical joints. A standard component of the Sithilikarana Vyayama neck series. Important contraindication in cervical spondylosis, where the backward (extension) arc must be avoided.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Dandasana or Vajrasana — sit erect. Or standing in Tadasana.

Practice:

  1. Let the head rest in the neutral upright position.
  2. Gently drop the chin toward the chest (forward flexion).
  3. From the chin-down position, slowly roll the head to the right — the right ear moves toward the right shoulder.
  4. Continue rolling the head to the right and backward — taking the head into cervical extension (looking upward) if there are no contraindications.
  5. Continue rolling to the left — the left ear toward the left shoulder.
  6. Complete the circle by returning to the chin-forward position.
  7. This is one round. Perform 5 clockwise circles.
  8. Repeat 5 anti-clockwise circles.
  9. Relax in Sithila Dandasana.

Notes:

  • The movement should be slow, gentle, and continuous — no jerking or sudden movements.
  • Keep the shoulders completely relaxed and lowered throughout.
  • In cervical spondylosis: omit the backward extension arc entirely. Roll only from the forward-flexed position through lateral flexion on each side, without going into extension. This creates a semicircular movement (forward → side → side → forward) rather than a full circle.
  • Do not force any range of motion.
  • Close the eyes and move with full awareness of the sensations in the neck.

Duration: 5 circles clockwise; 5 anti-clockwise. Approximately 2 minutes.

2. Anatomical Activation

Cervical joints mobilised

All six cervical movement directions are traversed in one complete head-roll:

  • Flexion (chin to chest): C2–C7 facets; anterior intervertebral joints
  • Extension (head back): posterior cervical facets; atlantoaxial joint
  • Right lateral flexion: right cervical facets and intertransverse joints
  • Left lateral flexion: left side equivalents
  • Combined rotation: the circular movement creates continuous rotation at each segment

Muscles stretched in sequence

The circular movement progressively stretches each perimeter muscle:

  • Suboccipitals: stretched in forward flexion
  • Sternocleidomastoid (contralateral): stretched in lateral flexion
  • Upper trapezius and levator scapulae: stretched in lateral flexion with contralateral tilt
  • Scalenes: stretched in lateral flexion and extension
  • Deep cervical extensors (splenius, semispinalis): stretched maximally in the full forward flexion portion

3. Physiological Effects

Complete cervical joint lubrication

A single head-roll circle distributes synovial fluid to all cervical facet joints through their full range in approximately 10–15 seconds. This is more efficient than the individual directional exercises (forward-backward, side-to-side) because the diagonal and combined movements activate the minor accessory gliding motions of the facet joints that straight-plane movements cannot reach.

Morning stiffness relief

Head rolling is particularly effective as a morning practice for patients with cervical stiffness from RA, early cervical spondylosis, or occupational neck strain. The gradual, circular warm-up:

  • Restores the lubrication layer in the facet joints after overnight immobility
  • Gently stretches the shortened morning-stiff muscles without the abrupt demand of stronger exercises
  • Activates the cervical proprioceptors (muscle spindles and Golgi tendon organs), resetting the cervical muscle tone

Venous drainage and lymphatic clearance

The circular movement of the cervical muscles acts as a pump on the internal jugular veins and the cervical lymph nodes. The improved venous drainage from the head reduces the morning facial and cerebral congestion experienced by many patients and may reduce the frequency of morning headaches of cervical origin.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — cervical joint mobilisation; neck muscle flexibility.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the neck is the site of the Vishuddhi chakra and the gateway through which prana flows between the head and trunk; restoring cervical mobility facilitates pranic circulation.

6. Contraindications

Significant contraindication:

  • Cervical spondylosis (cervical OA with osteophytes or disc narrowing): Full backward extension in the head-roll is contraindicated. The extension arc can impinge osteophytes against the posterior disc annulus, intervertebral foramina (compressing nerve roots), or even the vertebral arteries (causing vertebrobasilar ischaemia in severe cases). Practice only the partial circle (flexion-lateral-lateral-flexion without extension).

Other contraindications:

  • Atlantoaxial instability (RA): cervical rotation beyond midrange is contraindicated; practice with specialist guidance
  • Acute cervical disc prolapse with radiculopathy: avoid end-range rotation and extension; practice gentle midrange movements only
  • Severe vertigo or vestibular disorders: the circular head movement may induce dizziness; perform very slowly and discontinue if vertigo is triggered

7. Variations

Partial circle (cervical spondylosis version):

  • Drop the chin forward.
  • Roll only to the right (lateral flexion) — pause.
  • Return to centre (forward).
  • Roll to the left — pause.
  • Return to centre.
  • This D-shape movement mobilises all except the extension arc.

With breath synchronisation: Inhale during the upward arc (side → back → side); exhale during the downward arc (sides → forward). Connects head rolling to the Sithilikarana breathing series.
