Bhujangasana Breathing

Bhujangasana Breathing (Dynamic Cobra Breathing)

The dynamic (repetitive) version of Bhujangasana (P0083), in which the cobra raise is synchronised with inhalation and the lowering of the trunk with exhalation — performed for 10 rounds rather than held statically. The rhythmic rise-and-lower cycle applies a repeated pumping action to the dorsal spine and thoracic cage, mobilising the posterior vertebral chain with each breath cycle, and massaging the abdominal organs against the floor on each descent. Used across all prone-series protocols.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Prone posture (Makarasana) — lie face-down with legs together, forehead resting on the floor.

Practice:

  1. Place both palms flat on the floor beside the lower chest, near the last rib. Fingers point forward, elbows close to the body.
  2. Inhaling: raise the head and trunk progressively upward (from forehead → chin → chest → navel level), arching the dorsal spine. Minimum pressure should be on the palms — the lift is primarily from the dorsal spinal muscles. Elbows may remain slightly bent, or extended, depending on the practitioner's back flexibility. Raise to the navel level (not beyond the navel off the ground).
  3. Exhaling: slowly lower the trunk and head back to the starting prone position. Head returns last.
  4. This is one round. Repeat 10 rounds.

Variation (harder): If possible, place the hands behind the back with fingers interlocked and perform the same raise-and-lower. This removes the arm support entirely, making it a fully muscular spinal exercise.

Notes:

  • Synchronise movements and breathing with eyes closed.
  • The body below the navel remains in contact with the floor throughout.
  • Do not strain — the height of the raise should be within comfortable range.

Duration/rounds: 10 rounds per session.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Erector spinae (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis): concentrically active on the rise; eccentrically control the descent
  • Multifidus: deep segmental stabiliser throughout the movement
  • Rhomboids and middle trapezius: retract the scapulae during the raise
  • Deep neck extensors: raise the head into cervical extension
  • Abdominal organs (passive massage): compressed against the floor on each lowering phase

Compared to the static Bhujangasana (P0083)

The dynamic version provides:

  • Less sustained isometric load on the spinal extensors → more accessible for weak spinal muscles early in rehabilitation
  • A rhythmic pumping effect on the spinal joints and intervertebral discs
  • Repeated abdominal organ massage with each floor contact
  • Active breath-movement training (exhalation on the return)

3. Physiological Effects

Dynamic spinal extension rehabilitation

The repeated concentric-eccentric cycle of the spinal extensors provides a graded strengthening stimulus. For patients in the early stages of back pain rehabilitation or with thoracic hyperkyphosis, the dynamic version allows them to work through the full extension range without sustaining the fatiguing isometric hold of the static pose.

Diaphragmatic and thoracic pumping

Each inhalation-rise cycle expands the thoracic cage anteriorly (cobra), and each exhalation-lower cycle brings the chest back to neutral. This oscillation applies rhythmic stretch-relaxation cycles to the bronchial tree and pleura, producing a mild secretion-clearing effect useful in chronic respiratory conditions.

Abdominal organ massage

The repetitive contact of the abdomen with the floor on each lowering phase provides a mechanical massage to the abdominal contents — particularly the liver, stomach, and small intestine. In the 10-round version, this is 10 compressions per session, which is sufficient to stimulate peristalsis and digestive enzyme activity.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — dorsal spine strengthening, thoracic expansion, abdominal organ massage.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — thoracic opening on each inhalation supports prana vayu circulation.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning

Asthma / Respiratory Disorders (A007) Thoracic pumping; bronchial stretch; respiratory muscle training
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Abdominal organ massage; adrenal stimulation
Obesity (A002) Abdominal toning; organ stimulation
Hypertension (A005) Moderate sympathetic stimulation from spinal extension; used in hypertension IAYT protocol prone section
All IAYT protocols Component of the standard prone breathing exercise sequence

6. Contraindications

Same as Bhujangasana (P0083):

  • Recent abdominal surgery — avoid prone position for minimum 2 months
  • Cervical spondylosis — limit cervical extension range
  • Pregnancy (second trimester onward) — prone position not suitable
  • Acute lumbar disc prolapse — extension may aggravate; assess individually

7. Relationship to Static Bhujangasana

Feature Dynamic (P0066) Static (P0083)
Duration of hold No hold — continuous rise-lower 1-minute hold
Breath Inhale on rise, exhale on lower Inhale to rise; hold with normal breathing
Load on spinal muscles Concentric-eccentric dynamic Sustained isometric
Rounds 10 1–2
Difficulty Lower (early rehabilitation) Moderate-higher

Progression: From Bhujangasana Breathing (P0066) to static Bhujangasana (P0083) to Dhanurasana (P0084).
