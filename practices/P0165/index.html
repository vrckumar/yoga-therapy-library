Self Management of Excessive Tension (SMET)

SMET — Self Management of Excessive Tension — is SVYASA's signature integrated yoga-based stress management programme, designed as both a standalone module and a daily self-practice tool. Developed over more than three decades, SMET synthesises selected asanas, pranayama, relaxation techniques (IRT, QRT, DRT), Cyclic Meditation, and the science of emotions into a 30–35 minute daily protocol grounded in the Mandukya Upanishad's principle of alternating stimulation and relaxation. The practice is built around the insight that 80% of human stress is internal (generated by the mind's reactive patterns) rather than external, and that systematic alternation between activation and rest — applied across the Annamaya, Pranamaya, and Manomaya koshas — reduces this internal stress load far more efficiently than symptomatic management alone. Half an hour of SMET practice has been claimed, in SVYASA research, to produce three times the rest of six hours of good sleep.

1. Technique

SMET is a multi-step integrated protocol. The daily practice is typically 30–35 minutes and follows a fixed sequence that moves from gross (physical) to subtle (mental) and back to rest.

Step 1 — Prayer (Starting prayer):
Lie in Savasana or sit comfortably. Recite or hear the Mandukya Upanishad karika (3.44):
Laye sambodhayeccittam vikshiptam shamayetpunah | Sakashayam vijaniyt-samaprapt-na chalayet.
Meaning: "In the state of oblivion, awaken the mind. When agitated, pacify it. Recognise the innate stagnations. If the mind has reached equilibrium, do not disturb it again." This principle governs the entire practice structure.

Step 2 — IRT (Instant Relaxation Technique) — Annamaya Kosha:
Lie on the back. Bring legs together, hands by the sides. Smile.

  • Sequentially tighten from toes to head: stretch toes → tighten ankles and calf muscles → pull up knee caps → tighten thighs → squeeze buttocks → exhale and suck in abdomen → make fists → inhale and expand chest → tighten shoulders and neck → compress the face → tighten the WHOLE body: tighten… tighten… tighten…
  • Release: legs apart, arms apart, palms facing roof. Let the whole body collapse. Enjoy the contrast-induced relaxation. IRT uses the principle of post-isometric relaxation — voluntary tonic contraction followed by sudden release produces a rebound parasympathetic state deeper than passive relaxation.

Step 3 — Standing asanas (stimulation phase):
Selected standing postures are performed with breath awareness — typically Tadasana, Ardha Kati Chakrasana, Pada Hastasana, Ardha Chakrasana — each paired with inhalation (expansion) and exhalation (release). These postures stretch the trunk, activate the spine, stimulate abdominal and thoracic organs, and sharpen proprioceptive awareness. They constitute the Rajasic (activating) phase.

Step 4 — Sitting asanas:
Selected sitting postures — Vajrasana, Sasankasana, Ardha Matsyendrasana — provide continued stimulation with an element of introspection. The forward-folding postures (Sasankasana) bring the focus inward and begin the transition from stimulation to relaxation.

Step 5 — QRT (Quick Relaxation Technique) — Pranamaya Kosha:
Transition to Vajrasana or Padmasana with arm movements:

  • Stretch the arms up (Hastauttanasana variation): inhale, stretch arms overhead, feel the expansion; exhale, return, feel the inward gathering.
  • The movement is linked with breath-sensation awareness. The practitioner transitions from external body awareness (asana) to internal breath-prana awareness.
  • Brief Bhramari or breath-watching may be incorporated to quieten the Pranamaya Kosha.

Step 6 — AUM chanting (transition to Manomaya Kosha):
Sit in Vajrasana or Padmasana. Inhale; chant 'AAA' with arms raised in Brahma Mudra (or similar). Feel the resonance throughout the body. Slowly return to sitting. This step uses the resonance principle from Nadanusandana to create a bridge between the body-breath dimension and the mental dimension.

Step 7 — DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) — Annamaya/Pranamaya Kosha:
Slide down to Savasana. Let the whole body collapse. Guided auto-suggestion:

  • Awareness travels from toes to crown, releasing each part.
  • Breath slows spontaneously; bodily weight increases; warmth spreads.
  • The practitioner is guided to visualise expanding into the infinite blue sky — the prana diffuses outward and dissolves. This is the deepest rest phase of the practice.
  • Duration: 10–15 minutes.

Step 8 — Closing prayer:
Om sarve bhavantu sukhinah | sarve santu niramayah | sarve bhadrani pasyantu | ma kashcit duhkhabhaga bhavet | Om shantih shantih shantih.

(Full Cyclic Meditation may replace or extend Steps 2–7 in the advanced SMET format.)

2. Anatomical Activation

  • Musculoskeletal system: IRT and standing asanas systematically contract and release all major muscle groups. The tension-release cycle targets chronic holding patterns (muscular armour) that accumulate under stress.
  • Respiratory system: QRT and the chanting step retrain respiratory mechanics and rhythm, improving diaphragmatic excursion and reducing upper-chest (stress-mode) breathing patterns.
  • Autonomic nervous system: The alternating activation (asana, IRT contraction) and relaxation (DRT, QRT) phases directly exercise the sympathetic-parasympathetic oscillation, restoring its normal dynamic range which is compressed under chronic stress.
  • Central nervous system: The progression from gross motor activity (standing asanas) to refined awareness (QRT breath-observation) to sensory withdrawal (DRT expanding blue sky) mirrors the Pratyahara process — progressive withdrawal of the senses from external stimulation toward internal quiescence.
  • Limbic system: The AUM chanting step and the DRT sky visualisation engage the same limbic deactivation mechanisms as meditation, producing measurable reductions in amygdala reactivity.

3. Physiological Effects

The stimulation-relaxation principle — the Mandukya blueprint: The foundational principle of SMET is drawn directly from the Mandukya Upanishad karika 3.44: the mind oscillates between two problematic poles — tamas (dull, stagnant, under-activated) and rajas (agitated, distracted, over-activated). Neither pole is conducive to health or awareness. The SMET protocol alternates deliberate activation (asana, IRT contraction) with deliberate relaxation (IRT release, QRT, DRT) in a rhythmic cycle that models and re-educates the nervous system's oscillatory regulation. Just as a tuning fork must be struck (stimulated) to produce its truest resonance, the body-mind must be activated before it can relax into its deepest stillness.

Post-isometric relaxation and the IRT mechanism: The IRT's full-body contraction followed by sudden release exploits the neurological principle of post-isometric relaxation (PIR). During voluntary maximum contraction, the Golgi tendon organs detect tension and, upon release, trigger a rebound inhibition of the contracted muscles that is disproportionately deep — deeper than passive relaxation from a resting state. This is why IRT produces a more rapid and profound relaxation than simply lying down. The whole-body nature of IRT ensures that this rebound reaches not just peripheral muscles but the deep postural and visceral musculature, including the diaphragm and pelvic floor, which are primary carriers of chronic tension.

Cyclic Meditation and stress hormones: SVYASA research has demonstrated that 22.5 minutes of Cyclic Meditation (the formal version of SMET's CM component) produces changes in oxygen consumption, cortisol, and EEG coherence exceeding those of comparable periods of conventional sleep. The mechanism involves: (a) deepened parasympathetic activation during the relaxation phases reducing cortisol; (b) heightened alertness (alpha-theta coherence) during the meditative awareness phase maintaining metabolic efficiency without the cortisol demand of wakefulness; and (c) the oscillation between the two phases training the nervous system's regulatory flexibility.

Internal vs external stress — the 80:20 insight: SMET's core teaching is that approximately 80% of perceived stress is internally generated: the same external event produces profound distress in one person and equanimity in another. The difference lies in the conditioned reactivity of the Manomaya Kosha. SMET works on this internal reactivity through three mechanisms: (1) reducing the baseline physiological stress burden (asana, IRT, DRT), making the system less reactive to new stressors; (2) training the capacity to observe rather than be swept by reactions (QRT breath awareness); and (3) cultivating an expanding awareness (DRT's blue sky) that provides an experiential reference point larger than the stressor.

Brainwave coherence and the "thrice the rest" claim: In deep DRT and meditation, alpha and theta brainwaves increase in amplitude and coherence. These states are characterised by reduced metabolic rate, lowered cortisol and catecholamines, increased growth hormone, and enhanced immune surveillance. The "three times the rest of six hours of sleep" claim reflects the depth of metabolic restoration achievable in an alert meditative state compared to sleep, where restoration occurs but conscious direction of awareness is absent. In SMET, the DRT-cum-meditation provides this deep restoration without the risk of sleep-induced loss of awareness.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — the asana and IRT/DRT components directly address the physical body, releasing muscular tension, restoring postural alignment, and retraining breathing mechanics.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — QRT, Bhramari, and AUM chanting address the pranic body; Manomaya Kosha — the DRT's expanding-awareness phase and the overall meditative direction address the mental sheath. SMET is unique in spanning all three lower koshas in a single integrated module.

5. Indicated For

  • Occupational stress, burnout, and corporate wellness (SMET was specifically designed for working professionals)
  • Hypertension (reduced sympathetic tone, improved HRV)
  • Diabetes mellitus (cortisol reduction, improved insulin sensitivity)
  • Anxiety and anxiety disorders
  • Insomnia and sleep disorders
  • Psychosomatic disorders broadly
  • Prevention of non-communicable disease (NCDs) in high-stress populations
  • Post-illness or post-surgical rehabilitation
  • Healthy individuals seeking systematic stress resilience training
  • All IAYT programmes as the Annamaya-level foundation module

6. Contraindications

  • IRT (contraction phase): Avoid in acute cardiac events, uncontrolled hypertension, recent abdominal surgery, pregnancy (modify — avoid abdominal squeeze). Persons with IHD, slipped disc, spondylosis should use gentle, partial contraction only.
  • Standing asanas: Standard contraindications per individual posture apply (see respective asana files).
  • DRT: No significant contraindications; the lying-down posture must be comfortable (use props as needed for low back conditions).
  • General SMET: Acute febrile illness — defer physical components; continue breath awareness and DRT. Severe psychiatric instability — practise only with clinical supervision.

7. Variations & Progressions

  • 10-day beginner programme: Days 1–2: Steps 4 and 5 only; Days 3–4: partial practice; Days 5–8: full practice; Days 9–10: consolidation with Q&A.
  • 30-minute daily practice (maintenance): IRT → standing asanas → QRT → AUM → DRT → closing prayer.
  • 1-day CM module: 8 hours covering the theory and full practice of Cyclic Meditation, which forms the meditative core of advanced SMET.
  • 2-day SMET: Full programme covering all steps plus lectures on stress concept, guna theory, and evidence base.
  • Advanced SMET (3 days) and SMET-R (5 days, Avartan): Progressive deepening through more extended CM sessions and group dynamics.
  • Teachers Training (TT-SMET, 1 month): Full certification programme for teaching SMET to others.
  • IAYT integration: SMET forms the Annamaya Kosha foundation of the five-kosha integrated protocol. The advanced techniques (PET, MSRT, MIRT, MEMT, VISAK, ANAMS) are added sequentially as the practitioner's capacity deepens.
