Neck Bending

Neck Bending (Greeva Sanchalana — Parsva)

Cervical lateral flexion — bringing the ear toward the shoulder. Mobilises the frontal plane of cervical movement, stretching the contralateral scalenes and upper trapezius, and activating the ipsilateral cervical lateral flexors. Addresses the lateral neck tightness common in stress, poor sleeping posture, and occupational neck strain.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana or seated. Spine erect.

Practice:

  1. Keep the head facing forward (do not rotate). Keep shoulders level and still.
  2. While exhaling, slowly tilt the right ear toward the right shoulder — pure lateral flexion. The left side of the neck stretches. Go only as far as comfortable without lifting the left shoulder.
  3. While inhaling, return to centre.
  4. While exhaling, tilt the left ear toward the left shoulder.
  5. While inhaling, return to centre.
  6. This constitutes one round. Perform 10 rounds.

Breathing: Exhale on each tilt; inhale returning to centre.

Duration: 10 rounds.

2. Anatomical Activation

Lateral flexion to the right

  • Right sternocleidomastoid: ipsilateral lateral flexion
  • Right scalenes (anterior, middle, posterior): primary ipsilateral lateral flexors; also assist inspiration by elevating the first and second ribs
  • Right upper trapezius: lateral flexion and elevation of the cervical spine
  • Left upper trapezius: eccentrically lengthened on the left side — stretched
  • Left scalenes: stretched during right lateral flexion

Joints mobilised

  • Cervical lateral facets: the lateral aspects of the C2–C7 facet joints that restrict lateral flexion
  • Uncovertebral joints (joints of Luschka): these joints, unique to the cervical spine, are mobilised during lateral flexion and particularly affected in cervical spondylosis

3. Physiological Effects

Scalene and upper trapezius elongation

The scalene muscles are primary accessory muscles of inspiration. In chronic upper chest breathing (common in anxiety, asthma, and stress), the scalenes become hypertrophied and shortened. Lateral neck flexion provides a daily elongation stimulus for the scalenes — improving their resting length and restoring diaphragmatic breathing dominance.

Thoracic outlet considerations

The scalenes form the posterior boundary of the anterior scalene triangle through which the brachial plexus and subclavian artery pass. Chronic scalene shortening can compress these structures — contributing to thoracic outlet syndrome (arm numbness and pain). Lateral neck bending stretches the scalenes, decompressing the thoracic outlet.

Levator scapulae conditioning

The levator scapulae connects the upper cervical transverse processes to the superior angle of the scapula. Lateral flexion stretches the contralateral levator scapulae — one of the muscles most commonly implicated in the "stiff neck" pattern that accompanies forward head posture and occupational neck strain.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — cervical lateral facet mobilisation; scalene and upper trapezius elongation; uncovertebral joint mobilisation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the lateral movement of the neck activates the lateral cervical nadis (ida and pingala channels) in their ascending path through the neck.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Cervicogenic Headache Scalene and upper trapezius elongation; lateral facet mobilisation
Arthritis — Cervical Spine (A003) Uncovertebral joint mobilisation; lateral cervical range maintenance
Thoracic Outlet Syndrome Scalene lengthening; brachial plexus decompression
Occupational Neck Strain Lateral neck flexibility maintenance for desk workers
Asthma / Breathing Disorders Scalene elongation improves diaphragmatic breathing pattern
All IAYT protocols Lateral cervical range preparation

6. Contraindications

  • Cervical spondylosis with radiculopathy — lateral flexion may narrow the intervertebral foramen ipsilaterally and aggravate nerve root compression; reduce range and avoid end-range
  • Acute torticollis — do not attempt to force correction through lateral bending
  • Scalene myofascial pain — reduce range during acute phase

7. Variations & Progressions

Standard alternating (10 rounds): As described.

Sustained hold (advanced): 30-second sustained lateral tilt each side for a deeper scalene and upper trapezius stretch.

Gravity-assisted (variation): Seated, let the weight of the head increase the lateral tilt without muscular assistance — pure passive stretch.

Sequencing context: Follows P0023–P0024. Progresses to P0026 (combined neck movement) and P0027–P0029.
