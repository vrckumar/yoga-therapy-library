Alternate Bhujangasana and Parvatasana (Cobra-to-Mountain Flow)

A vigorous dynamic practice in which the body alternates between Bhujangasana (Cobra Pose — prone spinal extension) and Parvatasana (Mountain/Inverted-V — hip elevation with posterior chain stretch). Each alternation — from cobra up to inverted-V and back — constitutes one round. Performed rapidly (10–20 repetitions), this creates a powerful spinal mobilisation through the full arc from maximum extension (cobra) to maximum flexion-traction (inverted-V), massaging all spinal segments and stimulating the entire anterior and posterior kinetic chains alternately. A key stamina-building and spinal rehabilitation practice in the vigorous IAYT prescription.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Prone posture (Makarasana) — lie face-down with legs together. Bring the palms on either side of the chest, fingers pointing forward.

Stage I — Bhujangasana:

  1. Inhaling progressively: raise the head, then the upper trunk, then the back — arching the spine as far back as comfortable until the elbows are straight. This is the full Bhujangasana (Cobra Pose).

Stage II — Parvatasana: 2. Exhaling: without moving the hands or feet, raise the buttocks upward toward the ceiling. Lower the head to look at the toes. Press the heels toward the floor. The body forms the inverted-V (Parvatasana / Mountain Pose).

  1. This cobra-to-mountain arc constitutes one round.

  2. Repeat rapidly 10 to 20 times. The speed increases progressively as the rounds continue, and slows again toward the end.

  3. Return to prone Sthiti position.

  4. Relax in Makarasana.

Notes:

  • Hands and feet do not move throughout the practice — they remain in fixed position.
  • The transition from Bhujangasana to Parvatasana is fluid — no pause at either extreme initially; flow continuously.
  • Allow the breathing to be rhythmic and natural — inhale in cobra, exhale in inverted-V.
  • Gradually increase speed; come back gradually.

Duration/rounds: 10–20 rounds; approximately 1–2 minutes.

2. Anatomical Activation

The two-phase movement

Bhujangasana phase (inhalation — extension):

  • Erector spinae, multifidus: concentrically produce the spinal extension
  • Triceps: lock the elbows as the trunk rises
  • Pectoral region: eccentrically lengthened
  • Hip flexors (iliopsoas): eccentrically stretched

Parvatasana phase (exhalation — flexion/traction):

  • Gluteus maximus and hamstrings: concentrically raise the hips
  • Serratus anterior: protracts and stabilises the scapulae in the inverted-V
  • Hamstrings and gastrocnemius: under strong stretch as the heels press toward the floor
  • Thoracolumbar fascia: longitudinally stretched under the body weight

Dynamic cycling across the full spinal arc

The alternation between the two extreme positions produces a spinal mobilisation through the complete range of motion — from full extension (cobra) to full traction-flexion (inverted-V). No single static pose produces this complete arc.

3. Physiological Effects

Comprehensive spinal mobilisation

The dynamic oscillation between maximum extension and inverted-V traction:

  1. Distributes synovial fluid throughout the facet joints through the full extension-flexion cycle
  2. Mobilises the intervertebral discs through alternating anterior (cobra) and posterior (inverted-V) pressure distribution
  3. Stretches the entire posterior kinetic chain (hamstrings, calves, thoracolumbar fascia) on every inverted-V phase
  4. Strengthens the thoracic and lumbar erector spinae on every cobra phase

Stamina and cardiovascular conditioning

At 10–20 rapid repetitions, this practice is significantly aerobic. The large muscle groups of the posterior chain (gluteals, hamstrings, erectors) generate substantial metabolic demand. Heart rate elevation and increased respiratory rate are expected and intended effects in the vigorous IAYT prescription for obesity and diabetes.

Neural mobilisation

The alternating full spinal extension and traction positions mobilise the neural tissues (spinal cord and nerve roots) through their full excursion range. The spinal cord lengthens in the inverted-V and shortens in the cobra. This neural mobilisation is considered beneficial for spinal nerve root irritation and is analogous to the neural mobilisation techniques used in physiotherapy.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — vigorous spinal mobilisation, posterior and anterior chain alternating activation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the oscillation between extension (prana vayu activation) and traction (vyana vayu activation) generates strong pranic circulation along the spinal channel.

Obesity (A002) Cardiovascular conditioning; large muscle group activation

General stamina building Vigorous dynamic sequence used in energising protocols

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar disc prolapse: the combination of vigorous extension and inverted-V may be too demanding; use modified slower version
  • Wrist injury: high body weight through the wrists at speed; contraindicated until healed
  • Pregnancy: prone position in cobra and the vigorous inversion-extension arc both contraindicated
  • Recent abdominal surgery: avoid for 2–3 months
