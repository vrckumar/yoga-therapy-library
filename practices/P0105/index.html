Simha Mudra

Simha Mudra (Lion Pose / Lion's Gesture)

Simha (Sanskrit) = lion. The Lion Pose — a seated practice in which the practitioner assumes the facial expression and vocal projection of a roaring lion: wide eyes, mouth stretched open, tongue extended fully downward toward the chin, and a forceful exhalation on "HAAA" or "KSHAA" (the lion's roar). Performed from Vajrasana. The combination of maximum facial muscle stretch, full tongue protrusion, wide-open eyes, and the exhalation roar activates the oropharyngeal, hypoglossal, glossopharyngeal, and vagal nerves — making this the primary yogic practice for throat, thyroid, and vocal cord conditions. Classified as a Kriya Mudra in the Advance Yoga Technique curriculum.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrasana — sit on the heels with the spine erect, palms placed on the knees.

Practice:

  1. Inhale deeply through the nose.
  2. Open the mouth wide to its maximum extent.
  3. Protrude the tongue fully downward toward the chin — extend it as far as possible.
  4. Open the eyes wide (wide-eyed gaze, as if surprised or fierce).
  5. Exhale forcefully through the mouth with a "HAAA" sound — the lion's roar — projecting the breath and the sound forward.
  6. Simultaneously, some versions include a gentle lean forward from the waist as the roar is projected.
  7. After the exhalation, close the mouth, relax the tongue, and breathe normally for 2–3 cycles.
  8. Repeat 3–5 times.

Notes:

  • The tongue extension should be maximum — gently pulling the tongue downward at the root.
  • The sound is produced on the exhalation, not the inhalation.
  • The wide-eyed gaze activates the external oculomotor muscles (especially the frontalis and levator palpebrae).

Duration: 3–5 rounds per session; each roar lasts 3–5 seconds.

2. Anatomical Activation

Oropharyngeal structures

  • Tongue (genioglossus, hyoglossus, styloglossus): maximum extension activates the full tongue musculature; the root of the tongue is particularly stretched
  • Soft palate (palatoglossus, levator veli palatini): stretched by the wide mouth opening
  • Palatine tonsil region and faucial pillars: the wide oral opening with tongue protrusion stretches and stimulates the tonsillar region
  • Pharyngeal constrictors: stretched and toned by the forceful exhalation through the wide mouth
  • Larynx and vocal cords: the "HAAA" exhalation vibrates the vocal cords and exercises the laryngeal muscles

Cranial nerves activated

  • Hypoglossal nerve (CN XII): direct activation via full tongue movement
  • Glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX): stimulated by the posterior oropharyngeal stretch
  • Vagus nerve (CN X): the pharyngeal and laryngeal branches of the vagus nerve are activated by the forceful exhalation and vocal production — this is the basis for the vagal / parasympathetic effect
  • Facial nerve (CN VII): activates the wide-eyed expression (frontalis, orbicularis oculi)
  • Trigeminal nerve (CN V): proprioceptive feedback from the masseter and pterygoid muscles during maximum jaw opening

Thyroid gland

The forward cervical extension combined with the wide mouth and tongue protrusion in Simha Mudra increases blood flow to the anterior throat, stimulating the thyroid gland mechanically and via the cervical sympathetic chain.

3. Physiological Effects

Throat and vocal cord rehabilitation

The vigorous "HAAA" exhalation exercises the laryngeal musculature in a way that no other yoga practice replicates. For patients with:

  • Hoarseness or vocal cord paresis — the forceful exhalation strengthens the thyroarytenoid muscles
  • Chronic throat clearing or post-nasal drip — the wide pharyngeal opening and exhalation mechanically clears the posterior pharynx
  • Thyroid dysfunction — the increased anterior cervical blood flow combined with the cervical extension position stimulates thyroid perfusion

Facial muscle toning and nerve activation

The maximum facial expression (wide eyes, wide mouth, protruded tongue) is the most complete facial exercise in yoga practice. The activation of cranial nerve motor pathways in this pose is relevant for:

  • Post-stroke facial paresis rehabilitation (used in stroke rehabilitation protocols)
  • Bell's palsy recovery
  • Temporomandibular joint dysfunction (relaxation of the masseter after the maximum opening)

Vagal activation via exhalation

The forceful extended exhalation ("HAAA") stimulates the vagal branches in the pharynx and larynx. This produces a parasympathetic effect — heart rate slows briefly after the roar, similar to the mechanism of humming (Bhramari) or the "haaa" in Yoga Nidra exhalation. In thyroid protocols, this vagal activation helps regulate the sympathetic overdrive associated with hyperthyroid states.

Psycho-emotional effects

The act of "roaring like a lion" with wide eyes and full tongue extension is deliberately disinhibiting — it breaks psychological inhibition and self-consciousness. In IAYT for anxiety and social withdrawal, Simha Mudra is used to release emotional tension held in the throat (the Vishuddhi chakra region associated with self-expression).

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — oropharyngeal stretching and toning; thyroid stimulation; facial muscle activation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the exhalation roar activates udana vayu (upward-moving vital energy governing speech and the throat); stimulates the Vishuddhi (throat) chakra.

Tertiary: Manomaya Kosha — the disinhibiting expression releases repressed emotions and facilitates self-expression.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Thyroid conditions (hypo- and hyperthyroid) Primary indication; anterior cervical blood flow stimulation; thyroid gland activation
Throat conditions (chronic pharyngitis, tonsillitis) Tonsil and pharynx stimulation; lymphoid tissue activation
Vocal cord problems (hoarseness, paresis) Laryngeal muscle strengthening; forceful exhalation
Anxiety (A006) Throat chakra release; disinhibition of held self-expression
Stroke rehabilitation Facial nerve and hypoglossal nerve activation; orofacial muscle retraining

6. Contraindications

  • Severe cervical spondylosis: the cervical extension and forward lean should be gentle; avoid if neck pain worsens
  • Temporomandibular joint disorder (TMJ): maximum mouth opening may aggravate TMJ pain; work within a comfortable range
  • Acute sore throat or oral infections: avoid direct practice during acute infection

7. Classification Notes

Simha Mudra is classified as a Kriya Mudra in the Advance Yoga Technique curriculum — a mudra associated with cleansing (kriya) functions, specifically the cleansing of the oropharyngeal and nasal passages. It is distinct from a purely symbolic hand gesture (hasta mudra) and functions simultaneously as an asana, a pranayama, and a bhavana (visualisation of the lion's fearless nature).
