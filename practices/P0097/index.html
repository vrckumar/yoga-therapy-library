Sasankasana

Sasankasana (Hare Pose / Child's Pose)

Śaśāṅka (Sanskrit) — hare, rabbit. The posture mimics the form of a resting hare: starting from Vajrasana, the forehead is brought to the ground in front of the knees with a full spinal forward flex. The posture appears in IAYT protocols primarily as a counterpose to all backward-bending practices (Bhujangasana, Dhanurasana, Ustrasana) and as an independent therapeutic intervention for lumbar decompression, adrenal quieting, and anxiety relief. It is also the fifth and ninth position of the S-VYASA Sūryanamaskāra.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Daṇḍāsana, then establish Vajrāsana.

Practice:

  1. Sit in Vajrasana — heels under the buttocks, soles facing upward, palms resting on thighs.
  2. Take the hands behind the back; make a loose fist with the right hand and hold the right wrist with the left palm. (Alternative: stretch the arms forward alongside the head in the final position.)
  3. Relax the shoulders.
  4. Inhale and lean slightly backward from the waist, creating a gentle anterior chest opening.
  5. Exhale slowly and bend forward from the waist, bringing the forehead down to rest on the ground in front of the knees. Collapse the shoulders fully — allow the shoulder blades to round outward. The buttocks remain in contact with the heels throughout.
  6. Maintain this position for approximately 1 minute with slow, normal breathing. Allow the lower back to completely release.
  7. Inhale slowly and rise back to the vertical Vajrasana position.
  8. Release the hands and relax in Vajrasana or Dandhasana.

Arms-forward variation (used in Surya Namaskar): From Caturanga Dandasana position, bend the knees, rest buttocks on heels, and extend both arms forward on the floor, forehead down.

Breathing: Exhale into the forward fold; slow, deep breathing during hold, allowing the lumbar spine to lengthen on each exhalation; inhale on rising.

Duration/rounds: 1 minute hold; 1–2 rounds; or as a counterpose immediately after backward bends.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Thoracic and lumbar erector spinae: placed under a sustained eccentric lengthening stretch; the posterior spinal muscles fully release in this position, achieving a neurological down-regulation of chronic paraspinal tension
  • Multifidus: similarly released; in back pain patients with hyperactive multifidus co-contraction, this relief is immediately therapeutic
  • Gluteus maximus: stretched as the hip flexes fully and the buttocks remain on the heels
  • Posterior shoulder capsule and rhomboids: in the arms-forward variation, the scapulae protract and the posterior shoulder capsule is gently stretched
  • Intertransversarii and interspinales (deep segmental muscles): the complete lumbar-thoracic flexion places each segmental muscle under maximal stretch, allowing intra-segment tension to release

Organs and structures affected

  • Adrenal glands: positioned at the upper poles of the kidneys; in Sasankasana the abdominal aorta and its renal and suprarenal branches are mildly compressed by the folded abdomen-on-thighs contact. Upon rising, reactive vasodilation occurs. The IAYT clinical note is that this compression-release cycle reduces adrenal over-activity in stressed individuals — the counterpart to backbend-induced adrenal stimulation
  • Lumbar intervertebral discs: full spinal flexion creates a posterior widening of the intervertebral spaces and an anterior compression. For healthy discs this is beneficial traction; posterior disc derangements must be contraindicated
  • Brain circulation: the head-down position modestly increases cerebral blood flow; the slow breathing and internal quietude activates the default mode network, associated with introspective rest

3. Physiological Effects

Lumbar decompression — the counterpose mechanism

The primary therapeutic value of Sasankasana in the Back Pain protocol is its role as the essential counterpose to lumbar extension postures. Bhujangasana, Dhanurasana, Ustrasana, and Supta Vajrasana all load the posterior spinal structures (facet joints, posterior annulus, interspinous ligaments) under extension — adaptive stress that strengthens and decompresses the anterior disc but simultaneously compresses the posterior elements. Without an adequate flexion counterpose, the posterior elements accumulate fatigue stress.

Sasankasana provides:

  1. Distraction of posterior facet joints: full lumbar flexion opens the posterior facet joint spaces, relieving the compression from extension. In facet-mediated back pain, this is immediately analgesic.
  2. Posterior annular and ligament stretch: the posterior longitudinal ligament, supraspinous, and interspinous ligaments are placed under gentle tension that maintains their extensibility.
  3. Paraspinal muscle release: the sustained position with minimal muscular effort triggers the inverse stretch reflex in the lumbar paraspinal muscles, producing a deep, progressive release of chronic paraspinal muscle spasm — a major contributor to non-specific low back pain.

Adrenal quieting — the autonomic mechanism

In Sasankasana, the entire body is folded inward, the head is below the heart, the limbs are gathered — the physiological posture of interoceptive withdrawal (pratyahara at the body level). This body configuration sends a powerful safety signal to the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis:

  • Baroreceptor loading: with the head below the heart, arterial pressure in the carotid sinus rises slightly, triggering baroreceptor-mediated vagal activation and HPA axis quieting
  • Cortisol reduction: prolonged parasympathetic activation suppresses corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) secretion from the hypothalamus, leading to reduced ACTH and cortisol production
  • Sympathoadrenal quieting: the compression of the adrenal region on the thighs, combined with the profoundly reduced muscular and mental activity, reduces catecholamine (epinephrine, norepinephrine) output

This is the mechanistic basis for the IAYT prescription of Sasankasana in Anxiety, Depression, and all stress-related conditions — it is a direct neurophysiological antidote to the sympatho-adrenal hyperactivation that underlies these disorders.

Respiratory benefit

With the trunk folded forward on the thighs, the abdominal contents press upward against the diaphragm, reducing its downward excursion. This forces the breath to shift to thoracic (costal) breathing, expanding the posterior and lateral chest wall in a way that is rarely achieved in upright postures. The posterior lung zones — the most vascular regions, responsible for most of the actual gas exchange — are expanded and ventilated selectively. This posterior respiratory loading complements the anterior apical loading of Hastauttanasana.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — lumbar spinal flexion and decompression; posterior fascial chain lengthening; adrenal and renal compression-release.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the forward fold compresses the apana vayu region (lower abdomen and pelvic floor), and the slow exhalation-dominated breathing in this posture strengthens apana function, which governs elimination and the downward-cleansing energy.

Tertiary: Manomaya Kosha — the complete physical withdrawal and forward folding creates the felt sense of safe interiority, reducing anxiety (manomaya tension). It is an embodied pratyahara — a withdrawal of the senses inward.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning

Anxiety / Psychological conditions (A006) Adrenal quieting; parasympathetic activation; embodied withdrawal and safety posture
Depression (A006) Calming overactivated HPA axis; rest and restoration of the nervous system

General relaxation / counterpose Used after every backward-bending sequence in all 8 IAYT protocols
Insomnia HPA quieting and vagal activation; appropriate as a pre-sleep practice

6. Contraindications

  • Gastritis and peptic ulcer: the compression of the abdomen against the thighs increases intra-gastric pressure; may aggravate active gastric irritation
  • Acute knee pain: Vajrasana base requires comfortable knee flexion; avoid if knee flexion beyond 90° is painful
  • Severe hypertension: the mild cranial blood flow increase may be undesirable; use with caution
  • Pregnancy (second trimester onward): the enlarged abdomen cannot comfortably compress between the thighs; substitute with a supported version (cushion under the chest)
  • Posterior lumbar disc prolapse with acute radicular signs: the forward flexion under load can worsen posterolateral disc herniation

7. Variations & Progressions

Supported (Balasana variant): Place a folded blanket between the thighs and calves to reduce knee flexion, or rest the forehead and chest on a bolster for full-body support. Suitable for those with knee or ankle limitations.

Standard (as described): Vajrasana base, hands clasped behind back, forehead on floor, 1 minute. The arms-behind variation maintains the chest compression and shoulder-blade stretch.

Arms-forward variation: Both arms extended forward alongside the ears, forehead on floor. This adds a shoulder and thoracic lengthening component, useful in shoulder pain or frozen shoulder (gentle mobilisation).

In Sūryanamaskāra: Position 5 (after Caturanga Dandasana) and position 9 (as transition back from Parvathasana to lunge) — performed as a brief transit position rather than a sustained hold.

Progression: Paschimottanasana — seated forward bend with legs extended — is a more intense version of the forward-fold principle, providing greater hamstring loading alongside the spinal lengthening.
