Neck Twisting

Neck Twisting (Greeva Sanchalana — Vama Dakshina)

Cervical rotation — turning the head to the right and left. Mobilises the transverse plane of cervical movement, activating the sternocleidomastoid (contralateral), deep cervical rotators, and posterior cervical muscles. The most functionally important cervical movement for activities of daily living (driving, crossing the road, social communication).

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana or seated. Spine erect.

Practice:

  1. Keep the head level (do not tilt). Keep the shoulders still.
  2. While exhaling, slowly turn the head to the right — looking over the right shoulder as far as comfortable. Chin remains at the same level throughout.
  3. While inhaling, return to centre.
  4. While exhaling, turn to the left.
  5. While inhaling, return to centre.
  6. This constitutes one round. Perform 10 rounds.

Breathing: Exhale on each rotation; inhale returning to centre.

Duration: 10 rounds (alternating right and left).

2. Anatomical Activation

Rotation to the right

  • Left sternocleidomastoid (SCM): the contralateral SCM is the primary cervical rotator — when the left SCM contracts, the head turns right
  • Left splenius capitis and cervicis: ipsilateral splenius rotates the head ipsilaterally; but here the left splenius acts as agonist for right rotation
  • Right semispinalis capitis: contralateral deep rotator
  • Deep suboccipital muscles (left obliquus capitis inferior): fine rotational control at the atlantoaxial joint

The atlantoaxial joint — 50% of cervical rotation

Approximately 50% of total cervical rotation occurs at the C1–C2 (atlantoaxial) joint — the pivot joint formed by the dens (odontoid process) of C2 rotating within the ring of C1. The other 50% is distributed among the lower cervical segments. Cervical rotation exercises mobilise both the atlantoaxial and the lower cervical facet joints.

3. Physiological Effects

Cervical rotational mobility — functional significance

Cervical rotation is the movement most frequently reported as restricted in cervical arthritis and cervicogenic headache. The normal range is approximately 70–80° to each side; restriction to less than 45° significantly impairs driving, working at a desk, and social interaction. Regular neck twisting maintains this functionally critical movement.

SCM and deep rotator conditioning

The sternocleidomastoid is frequently tight in forward head posture and in individuals who sleep with neck twisted. The alternating contraction-relaxation of the left and right SCMs in this exercise dynamically balances their resting tone and reduces the asymmetric tension that contributes to cervicogenic headache and neck pain.

Vertebral artery consideration

The vertebral arteries pass through the transverse foramina of C1–C6 and can be mechanically affected by cervical rotation. At normal rotation ranges, vertebral artery flow is not significantly compromised. However, at extreme rotational ranges in the presence of atherosclerosis or anatomical variants, flow can be transiently reduced. The practice instruction to rotate "as far as comfortable" — not to force range — is the safety guideline.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — atlantoaxial and lower cervical facet joint mobilisation; SCM and deep cervical rotator conditioning.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the turning of the head is associated with expansion of the visuddha and ajna centres in yogic anatomy — activating the higher pranic centres of the cervical and cranial region.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Cervical Spine (A003) Rotational range maintenance; atlantoaxial joint mobilisation
Cervicogenic Headache SCM and suboccipital rotator conditioning; rotational restriction correction
Torticollis (subacute) Gentle bilateral rotation rebalances SCM tone
Back Pain (A004) — cervical component Cervical rotation affects thoracic and lumbar rotational patterns
All IAYT protocols Cervical rotation preparation before pranayama (nose-alternating) and meditation

6. Contraindications

  • Vertebral artery insufficiency — dizziness during or after rotation; stop immediately
  • Cervical disc herniation — rotation may aggravate radicular symptoms; reduce range
  • Severe cervical spondylosis — limit range; avoid end-range rotation

7. Variations & Progressions

Standard bilateral rotation: As described — 10 rounds alternating.

Sustained hold (variation): Hold maximum rotation for 5 breaths each side — deeper muscle elongation.

Sequencing context: Follows P0023 (forward-backward). Together with P0025–P0029 forms the complete cervical mobilisation group.
