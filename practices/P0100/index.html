Salabhasana

Salabhasana (Locust Pose)

Salabha (Sanskrit) — locust, grasshopper. The prone leg-raising posture that complements Bhujangasana by shifting the extension load from the thoracic spine to the lumbar spine, gluteal muscles, and posterior thigh — making it the primary posterior-chain strengthening practice in IAYT protocols.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Prone (Makarasana). Lie face-down with legs together, chin on the floor.

Practice:

  1. Make fists with both hands and slide them under the thighs, with the back of the hands (knuckles down) resting against the floor. This elevates the pelvis slightly and provides a mechanical fulcrum for the leg raise.
  2. Inhale and raise both legs simultaneously, keeping the knees straight. The key instruction is to pull up the kneecaps — contracting the quadriceps to lock the knees — and to squeeze the buttocks — engaging the gluteus maximus throughout the lift.
  3. The legs should be raised as high as comfortable without bending the knees. Only the fists and the front of the body (chest to abdomen) remain in contact with the floor.
  4. Maintain the posture for approximately 1 minute with normal breathing.
  5. Exhale and slowly lower the legs to the floor. Rest in Makarasana.

Breathing: Inhale into the raise; breathe normally throughout the hold; exhale on release.

Duration/rounds: 1 minute hold; 1–2 rounds per session. A half-posture variation (one leg at a time) may be performed in early stages.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Gluteus maximus: primary hip extensor; the squeeze instruction is designed to maximally recruit this muscle
  • Hamstrings (biceps femoris, semimembranosus, semitendinosus): assist hip extension and maintain knee extension under load
  • Erector spinae (lumbar portion) — iliocostalis lumborum, longissimus: isometrically stabilise the lumbar spine against the pulling force of the elevated legs
  • Multifidus: segmental lumbar stabilisation — particularly important in protecting the lumbar facet joints
  • Quadriceps: the kneecap-pulling instruction activates quadriceps to prevent knee flexion, creating a rigid lever through the lower limb
  • Gluteus medius and minimus: stabilise the pelvis laterally during bilateral leg raise

Organs directly affected

  • Kidneys: the lumbar extension and posterior abdominal wall toning produces a rhythmic mechanical stimulation of the kidneys, which are embedded in perirenal fat adjacent to the psoas muscle
  • Hip musculature and sciatic nerve pathway: the gluteal stretch and strengthening directly targets the anatomical region of the sciatic nerve

Spinal structures

Lumbar facet joints extend under load; the intervertebral discs are loaded posteriorly. The sacroiliac joints are stabilised through the coordinated contraction of the posterior chain (gluteus maximus, hamstrings, erector spinae).

3. Physiological Effects

Posterior chain strengthening — the primary therapeutic mechanism for low back and sciatica

The lumbar spine is chronically underloaded in flexion-dominant lifestyles (sitting, forward bending). Salabhasana provides a unique form of resisted lumbar extension — the weight of both legs creates a lever-arm load that the lumbar erectors and multifidus must overcome. This is mechanically equivalent to a physiotherapy "prone hip extension" exercise, performed without equipment.

The specific effects:

  1. Multifidus recruitment: The segmental stabiliser most commonly found atrophied in chronic low back pain patients is the multifidus. Prone leg raises with a straight knee require high-level multifidus activation — this is the direct structural mechanism for improvement in back pain.
  2. Sacroiliac stabilisation: The simultaneous contraction of gluteus maximus and hamstrings compresses and stabilises the sacroiliac joint — a common source of posterior pelvic pain and referred sciatic-type symptoms.
  3. Sciatic nerve mobilisation: The sustained hip extension with knee locked places the sciatic nerve under tension in its full course from the lumbar nerve roots through the sciatic notch to the popliteal fossa. This neural tension, held with normal breathing, acts as a gentle neural flossing mechanism — reducing adhesions and improving neural conduction along the sciatic pathway.

Renal stimulation mechanism

The kidneys receive approximately 20% of cardiac output and are highly sensitive to changes in local perfusion. The posterior abdominal wall compression achieved through sustained lumbar extension and gluteal contraction in Salabhasana:

  • Rhythmically compresses the perirenal fat and renal capsule via tightening of the erector spinae and quadratus lumborum
  • This mechanical stimulation, analogous to abdominal organ compression in seated twists, is proposed to improve renal blood flow and filtration efficiency

This aligns with the stated benefit of kidney toning in the source material and with the use of Salabhasana in metabolic protocols.

Cardiovascular and contraindications rationale

The bilateral isometric leg raise against gravity creates a significant increase in intra-abdominal and intrathoracic pressure — similar in mechanism to a Valsalva-type manoeuvre. This transiently increases venous return to the heart and raises cardiac preload. In healthy individuals this is beneficial (improves stroke volume); in cardiac patients or those with uncontrolled diabetes (where autonomic cardiac neuropathy may impair cardiovascular adaptation), this acute pressure rise is the basis for the stated caution. The practice should be modified to unilateral (one leg at a time) in these populations.

Autonomic effect

Prolonged prone isometric contraction with normal breathing activates a mild sympathetic response (effort) balanced by the parasympathetic background of the prone position. The practice is therefore energising without being arousing — appropriate for mid-sequence placement.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — intensive posterior chain muscular loading; mechanical stimulation of kidneys and sciatic pathway.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the lumbar region corresponds to the muladhara (root) and svadhisthana chakra regions; sustained activation here builds apana vayu (downward/eliminative vital energy) and is associated with grounding and stability.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain / Sciatica (A004, A009) Lumbar extensor strengthening; multifidus activation; sciatic nerve mobilisation via sustained hip extension
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Renal toning supports metabolic waste elimination; posterior chain activation improves insulin sensitivity in large muscle groups
Obesity (A002) Large muscle group activation (gluteals, hamstrings) increases metabolic demand; reduces fat accumulation in gluteal-thigh region
Arthritis — Hip and Lumbar (A003) Strengthens muscles supporting hip and sacroiliac joints
Asthma (A007) Prone position with chest on floor provides postural drainage; posterior thoracic expansion
GID / Digestive Disorders (A014) Abdominal floor contact stimulates viscera; posterior abdominal wall toning improves organ position

6. Contraindications

  • Cardiac conditions — avoid bilateral leg raise; modified to unilateral or Ardha Salabhasana (half locust) with breath attention
  • Uncontrolled diabetes — caution due to autonomic cardiovascular involvement; use unilateral modification
  • Acute low back pain — paradoxically, acute flare requires rest before strengthening; use once subacute
  • Pregnancy — prone position contraindicated from second trimester onward
  • Abdominal aortic aneurysm — avoid all prone compression postures

7. Variations & Progressions

Beginner (Ardha Salabhasana — Half Locust): Raise one leg at a time, alternating sides. This reduces the intra-abdominal pressure and isolates the gluteal activation unilaterally.

Standard (as described): Both legs raised simultaneously, kneecaps pulled up, buttocks squeezed.

Intermediate: Extend the arms forward (Superman position) to add upper back and posterior shoulder engagement, increasing the total posterior chain load.

Progression: Dhanurasana — the arms reach back to grasp the ankles, combining the hip extension of Salabhasana with the spinal arc of Bhujangasana in a single integrated posture.
