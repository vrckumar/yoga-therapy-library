Parsva Konasana

Parsva Konasana (Side Angle Pose)

Pārśva (Sanskrit) — side, flank; Koṇa — angle. From a wide-legged stance with the front knee bent to 90°, the trunk extends laterally along the line of the front thigh, one hand on the floor beside the foot, the other arm stretching overhead in line with the body. The posture creates a single, unbroken lateral arc from the outer heel of the rear foot to the fingertips of the overhead arm. It is the primary lateral strengthening posture in the IAYT standing repertoire, combining a deep hip lunge, lateral trunk extension, chest expansion, and intestinal stimulation simultaneously.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tāḍāsana.

Practice:

  1. Move the right leg to approximately 1.5 metres away from the left foot (wider than Virabhadrasana).
  2. Turn the right foot toward the right side so the toes point away from the body (90°).
  3. Exhale and bend the right knee until a right angle is formed between the right thigh and the right calf — the right thigh is parallel to the floor, the right lower leg perpendicular. The right knee should be directly above the right heel.
  4. Lower the trunk toward the right, resting it on the right thigh. Place the right hand on the ground beside the right foot (or on a block if the floor is not accessible).
  5. Inhale and stretch the left arm over the head, biceps touching the left ear, palm facing downward. The left arm, trunk, and left leg form one continuous lateral line.
  6. Look at the tips of the left hand's fingers.
  7. Maintain the posture for approximately 1 minute with normal breathing. The left leg remains fully extended and straight, the left knee not bending.
  8. Return to Sthiti by inhaling and rising.
  9. Repeat to the left side.

Key alignment note: In the final position, there should be a right angle between the bent thigh and calf, and the extended rear leg must not bend.

Breathing: Exhale into the lateral bend; normal breathing during hold; inhale on rising.

Duration/rounds: 1 minute each side; 1 round per session.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Quadriceps (front leg): isometrically active at 90° knee flexion — high-intensity thigh strengthening
  • Gluteus medius and minimus (rear leg): prevent the pelvis from sagging toward the floor; primary pelvic stabilisers
  • Lateral trunk (obliques, quadratus lumborum, lateral intercostals): the lateral extension of the trunk stretches the entire lateral myofascial chain on the upper side; the lower side is isometrically active, supporting the chest against gravity
  • Serratus anterior: the overhead arm position activates serratus anterior to stabilise the scapula against the thoracic wall; this muscle is essential for shoulder health and is often weakened in back pain patients
  • Latissimus dorsi (stretched, upper side): the overhead arm position elongates the latissimus from the iliac crest to the humerus
  • Hip adductors (rear leg, medial): the fully extended rear leg with foot flat on floor loads the adductors eccentrically
  • Tensor fasciae latae and IT band (front leg): laterally stabilise the front knee

Organs and structures affected

  • Lateral abdominal wall and colon: the lateral extension places the intestinal contents under a unilateral gravitational shift; the descending colon (on the left lateral bend) or ascending colon (on the right) is mobilised by gravity and the mechanical pressure of the trunk-on-thigh contact
  • Liver and spleen: on the respective side bends, the liver (right) and spleen (left) are compressed against the diaphragm, stimulating hepatic and splenic circulation
  • Intercostal spaces: the lateral extension creates a visible widening of the intercostal spaces on the upper side, passively expanding lung volume in the upper (and often hypoventilated) lateral segments

3. Physiological Effects

Lateral thoracic expansion — the intercostal mechanism

Parsva Konasana is the only commonly prescribed IAYT posture that provides a sustained, weight-bearing stretch of the lateral thoracic wall. The overhead-stretched arm, combined with the lateral trunk extension and the opened intercostal spaces, creates an active lateral expansion of the lung that is not achievable in upright standing or seated postures.

The lateral lung zones — the middle portions of each lung from base to apex — are ventilated preferentially in the lateral position but are not selectively expanded in any upright posture. Parsva Konasana addresses this gap: the upper lateral intercostal spaces are stretched open, the ribs are elevated on the upper side, and the lateral pleural lining is expanded. This is particularly relevant in:

  • Asthma: where bronchospasm and secretion pooling affects the lower and lateral lung zones
  • Post-operative patients: where lateral splinting pain reduces ventilation of the lateral zones
  • Obesity: where the weight of abdominal fat restricts lateral rib cage expansion

Hip strengthening and prevention mechanism

The 90° front-knee lunge in Parsva Konasana is functionally identical to the movement pattern of stair climbing, walking uphill, and single-leg squatting. Training this pattern under the sustained isometric hold of the posture builds:

  1. Quadriceps endurance: the primary functional deficit in knee osteoarthritis patients who report difficulty with stairs and rises from chairs
  2. Gluteal strength: the rear leg glutes prevent Trendelenburg gait — the characteristic hip-dropping gait seen in hip arthritis and weakness
  3. Lateral hip stabilisation: the iliotibial band and tensor fasciae latae on both legs are loaded in their functional lateral-stability role

Intestinal peristaltic stimulation

The source material explicitly notes: "stimulates intestinal peristalsis." The mechanism is the combination of:

  1. Lateral gravitational displacement: the abdominal contents shift toward the dependent side under gravity, creating a passive stretching of the mesenteric ligaments and a mechanical stimulus to the intestinal wall
  2. Compression of one side of the colon: the trunk-on-thigh contact on the front side compresses the lateral abdominal wall, increasing local intra-colonic pressure
  3. Diaphragmatic motion enhancement: the expanded lateral chest wall improves diaphragmatic excursion on the upper side, which provides rhythmic massaging of the abdominal viscera with each breath

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — lateral thoracic expansion; quadriceps and hip strengthening in functional lunge pattern; lateral myofascial chain stretch; intestinal peristaltic stimulation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the lateral extension activates the side channels (ida on the left, pingala on the right); the serratus anterior activation and chest opening support the anahata region.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain (A004) Quadratus lumborum and lateral trunk strengthening; hip stabiliser strengthening reduces lumbopelvic instability; lateral fascial chain mobilisation
General strengthening Quadriceps, gluteal, and lateral trunk conditioning in a functional weight-bearing pattern
Digestive Disorders / GID (A014, A015) Intestinal peristalsis stimulation; lateral colon mobilisation
Asthma (A007) Lateral thoracic expansion; intercostal stretching; lateral lung zone ventilation
Obesity (A002) High-metabolic-demand posture; lateral trunk toning

6. Contraindications

  • Cardiac conditions (early): the isometric high-load on large muscle groups is strenuous; introduce gradually after initial cardiac stability is established
  • Severe knee pain: the 90° front knee load; use a reduced angle or chair support
  • Acute lumbar disc injury: the lateral bending under load may worsen lateral disc protrusion; avoid in acute phase

7. Variations & Progressions

Beginner: Use a yoga block under the lower hand to reduce the lateral lean degree. Alternatively, perform the lateral extension with the lower hand on the front thigh rather than the floor.

Standard (as described): Lower hand on floor beside the front foot; overhead arm stretching; right angle at front knee. 1 minute each side.

Intermediate: Bring the lower forearm to rest on the front thigh rather than the hand on the floor, then progressively work toward palm on floor.

Progression: Parivrtta Parsva Konasana — Revolved Side Angle Pose — adds a full spinal rotation to the lateral extension, dramatically increasing abdominal organ stimulation.
