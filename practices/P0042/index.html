Straight Leg Raising

Straight Leg Raising (Uttanpadasana Kriya — Unilateral)

Single-leg straight leg raise from supine — one leg raised to 90° while the other remains on the floor. Combines hip flexor strengthening, quadriceps activation (knee maintained in full extension), lumbar stabilisation, and hamstring lengthening in a single movement.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine Posture. Arms stretched above head or by the sides.

Practice:

  1. Lie flat. Keep the left leg on the floor throughout.
  2. While inhaling slowly, raise the right leg — keeping the knee fully extended — to 45°. Pause.
  3. Continue inhaling; raise to 90° (perpendicular to floor). Pause.
  4. While exhaling, lower the right leg slowly — to 45°, pause; then to the floor.
  5. Relax for 2 breaths. Repeat with the left leg.
  6. Perform 5–10 repetitions per leg.

Note (from PGDYTP 101 source): Do not bend the knee throughout. Do not disturb the leg lying on the floor. Synchronise breathing with movement perfectly. Even if you can, do not raise the leg beyond 90°.

Breathing: Inhale on raising (two phases: 0°→45°, 45°→90°); exhale on lowering.

Duration: 5–10 repetitions per leg.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Iliopsoas: primary hip flexor — concentrically raises the leg
  • Rectus femoris: hip flexion and knee extension — dual-function in this exercise
  • Quadriceps (all four heads): isometric knee extension throughout the raise
  • Abdominal muscles: stabilise the lumbar spine against the psoas pull on the lumbar vertebrae

Lumbar stabilisation mechanism

  • As the hip flexors raise the leg, they exert an extension moment on the lumbar spine via their attachment to the lumbar transverse processes
  • The abdominals must co-contract to prevent lumbar hyperextension — this is the core stabilisation demand of the exercise
  • The contralateral leg remaining on the floor provides a stable base

3. Physiological Effects

Hip flexor strengthening

The single-leg straight raise is the simplest, lowest-risk hip flexor strengthening exercise available. The leg weight (approximately 16% body weight) creates a gravitational moment arm that increases as the leg approaches the horizontal. The quadriceps maintains knee extension, removing the quadriceps-deficit confounder and isolating the hip flexor.

Lumbar assessment tool

The degree to which the lumbar spine hyperextends during the straight leg raise (assessed by watching for the lumbar spine lifting off the floor or the abdominal bulge) is a clinical assessment of core stabilisation capacity. In clinical yoga therapy, the ability to perform a pain-free straight leg raise to 90° without lumbar compensation is a primary functional outcome measure in back pain protocols.

Hamstring neural tension assessment

When the straight leg raise is limited to less than 70–80° by posterior leg tension (not lumbar pain), this indicates neural tension in the sciatic nerve or tightness in the hamstring muscles. Progressive, pain-free straight leg raising over weeks gradually elongates the sciatic neural tensioning component.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — hip flexor strengthening; quadriceps isometric activation; lumbar stabilisation training; hamstring and sciatic neural tension assessment and conditioning.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the slow, breath-coordinated raising of the leg is a controlled prana-generating movement; the Straight Leg Raise Breathing (Pratikriya) in PGDYTP 101 is specifically described as a breathing practice using this movement.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain (A004) Core stabilisation training; hip flexor strengthening; lumbar assessment
Arthritis — Hip and Knee (A003) Safe lower limb strengthening without weight-bearing joint compression
Sciatica Hamstring and sciatic nerve gentle mobilisation
Post-operative rehabilitation Hip flexor and quadriceps reactivation from day 1 post-surgery
All IAYT protocols Initiates the supine leg group; explicitly described in PGDYTP 101 as a breathing practice

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar disc herniation — hip flexor contraction increases intradiscal pressure; avoid in acute phase; use with extreme caution in subacute phase
  • Lumbar spondylolisthesis — hip flexor traction on lumbar vertebrae; limit range to 45°
  • Hip labral tear (acute) — full flexion to 90° loads the labrum at end range

7. Variations & Progressions

0°→45° only (modified): For acute back pain — half the hip flexor moment; safe starting point.

Alternate legs (P0043): Standard progressive form.

Bilateral (P0044): Advanced — both legs simultaneously; contraindicated in back pain.

Sequencing context: First supine leg exercise. Progresses to P0043 (alternating), then the hip-opening and lumbar stretching series (P0046–P0056).
