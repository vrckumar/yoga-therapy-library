Vertical Stretch of the Knee

Vertical Stretch of the Knee (Januphala Kriya)

Slow straight-leg raises from supine — the leg is lifted with the knee in full extension. This combines quadriceps strengthening (the leg's weight provides resistance) with hip flexor activation and lumbar stabilisation. The "vertical stretch" refers to the vertical trajectory of the leg and the lengthening (stretch) of the posterior knee and hamstring chain.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine Posture. Legs together on the floor.

Practice:

  1. Lie flat. Keep the left leg straight on the floor.
  2. Tighten the right quadriceps (kneecap tightening, as in P0008).
  3. Inhaling, slowly raise the right leg — keeping the knee fully extended — to 45°, pause; continue to 90° (perpendicular to floor).
  4. Exhaling, slowly lower the right leg back to the floor.
  5. Repeat 10 times on the right. Then 10 times on the left.

Note on breath: The PGDYTP 101 text specifies raising to 45° on first inhalation, then to 90° on second inhalation — two-phase elevation. Lowering in two phases (to 45° exhale, then to floor exhale).

Duration: 10 repetitions per leg.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Quadriceps femoris (all four heads): isometrically maintaining full knee extension against gravity throughout the raise
  • Iliopsoas (iliacus + psoas major): hip flexion — lifting the entire leg from the floor; the primary active muscle during the raise
  • Rectus femoris: dual function — both hip flexor and knee extensor — maximally engaged in this exercise
  • Tensor fasciae latae: assists hip flexion and abduction

Secondary activation

  • Abdominal muscles (rectus, obliques): stabilise the lumbar spine against the hip flexor pull on the lumbar vertebrae (psoas attaches to lumbar transverse processes)
  • Hamstrings of contralateral leg: isometric contraction pressing the straight leg into the floor to stabilise the pelvis

3. Physiological Effects

Quadriceps strengthening — the key mechanism

The straight leg raise is a classic quadriceps strengthening exercise that loads the quadriceps isometrically throughout the full range of hip flexion. Unlike deep knee bends that create high patellofemoral compression, the straight leg raise loads the quadriceps at zero knee flexion — with minimal joint compression. This makes it the optimal quadriceps exercise for knee arthritis patients who cannot perform weight-bearing knee exercises.

The load is provided by the weight of the leg itself — approximately 16% of body weight — creating a significant gravitational moment arm at the knee as the leg rises. The quadriceps must maintain isometric extension against this increasing moment throughout the movement.

Hip flexor activation and psoas reactivation

In sedentary individuals, the iliopsoas becomes both shortened (due to prolonged sitting) and neurologically inhibited (paradoxically weak despite being shortened). The slow, controlled straight leg raise reactivates the iliopsoas through its full range — from lengthened (leg on floor) to shortened (leg at 90°) — providing both strengthening and normalisation of the hip flexor motor pattern.

Lumbar stabilisation demand

The psoas major shares its attachment points with the lumbar spine (L1–L5 transverse processes). When the hip flexors contract during straight leg raising, they generate a compressive and extension force on the lumbar spine. The abdominal muscles must co-contract to prevent lumbar hyperextension — training the core stabilisation reflex that is essential for back pain prevention.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — quadriceps strengthening; hip flexor activation; lumbar stabilisation training; posterior hamstring and knee elongation (stretch).

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the controlled raising of the leg is understood as a prana-generating movement; the held position at 90° is the same as Uttanpadasana, a classical pranayama support posture.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Knee (A003) Safest quadriceps strengthening; zero patellofemoral compression; reduces load during weight-bearing
Knee Osteoarthritis Each 1 kg of quadriceps strength reduces knee loading by approximately 3–4 kg; direct clinical benefit
Back Pain (A004) Hip flexor reactivation; lumbar stabilisation training; hamstring flexibility
Hip Flexor Weakness Iliopsoas and rectus femoris activation through full functional range
Post-knee surgery First strengthening exercise prescribed — safe from post-operative day 1

6. Contraindications

  • Lumbar herniated disc with radiculopathy — the hip flexor contraction increases intradiscal pressure; avoid in acute phase
  • Severe hip flexor tightness with lumbar hyperlordosis — the lumbar spine must be monitored; place a small towel under the lumbar curve if needed
  • Femoral hernia — the hip flexion may aggravate

7. Variations & Progressions

Single leg (standard): One leg at a time as described — allows lumbar monitoring.

Both legs together (advanced, P0044): Bilateral straight leg raise — doubles the quadriceps and core demand; avoid in low back pain.

Ankle weight added (strength progression): Adding 0.5–2 kg ankle weight increases resistance progressively.

Sequencing context: Final exercise in the knee series (P0006–P0012). Bridges to the hand/wrist/elbow group (P0014 onward) in the full Sithilikarana sequence.
