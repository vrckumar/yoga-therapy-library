Ankle Bending

Ankle Bending (Goolf Naman)

Rhythmic dorsiflexion and plantarflexion of the ankle joint. The primary ankle mobilisation exercise in the Sithilikarana Vyayama series — systematically pumps the talocrural joint through its principal axis of motion and activates the gastrocnemius-soleus complex as a venous return pump.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Dandasana (seated with legs extended) or Supine Posture. Legs together, heels on the floor.

Practice:

  1. Keep both legs straight on the floor. Flex both feet simultaneously — pull the toes and forefoot toward the shin (dorsiflexion) as far as comfortable.
  2. Then point both feet away from the body (plantarflexion) — extend the ankle, pointing the toes as far as comfortable.
  3. This constitutes one round. Perform 10 rounds slowly and rhythmically.
  4. Relax in Sithila Dandasana.

Breathing: Exhale on dorsiflexion; inhale on plantarflexion — or breathe normally throughout.

Duration/rounds: 10 rounds.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Gastrocnemius and soleus (triceps surae): plantarflexion — concentrically active pulling the heel away from the shin
  • Tibialis anterior: dorsiflexion — concentrically active pulling the forefoot toward the shin; stretched during plantarflexion
  • Peroneus longus and brevis: assist plantarflexion; also evert the foot slightly
  • Extensor digitorum longus: assists dorsiflexion

Joint mobilised

  • Talocrural (ankle) joint: the primary sagittal plane hinge joint — dorsiflexion (talar dorsal surface rolls anteriorly in mortise) and plantarflexion (rolls posteriorly)
  • Subtalar joint: minor participation during plantarflexion

3. Physiological Effects

Gastrocnemius-soleus venous pump — the primary clinical mechanism

The calf muscles constitute the largest peripheral venous pump in the body. During plantarflexion, the gastrocnemius and soleus contract and compress the deep venous plexus of the calf — the soleal sinusoids — squeezing blood superiorly toward the femoral vein. During dorsiflexion, the muscles relax and the venous plexus refills from the capillary bed. This rhythmic compression-release constitutes the peripheral venous return mechanism that augments cardiac preload.

In sedentary individuals, post-operative patients, and during prolonged travel, this pump is inactive — leading to venous pooling, oedema, and risk of deep vein thrombosis. Ankle Bending is the most efficient activation of this pump in a non-weight-bearing position, making it the standard DVT prevention exercise prescribed in immobilised patients.

Talocrural joint synovial fluid distribution

The talocrural joint bears the highest load per unit area of any joint in the body. Despite this, its articular cartilage depends on the same movement-driven synovial fluid imbibition as all synovial joints. The alternating dorsiflexion-plantarflexion distributes synovial fluid across both the dome of the talus and the tibial plafond — the two primary articular surfaces.

Anterior tibial compartment activation

Dorsiflexion actively loads tibialis anterior — the muscle that resists foot drop and stabilises the ankle during the swing phase of gait. In elderly individuals and those with neurological conditions, this muscle weakens progressively. Regular Ankle Bending maintains its activation and prevents the gait instability associated with tibialis anterior weakness.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — talocrural joint mobilisation; calf venous pump activation; tibialis anterior and triceps surae conditioning.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — apana vayu, the downward vital force governing the lower extremities and elimination, is stimulated by the rhythmic foot movements.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Ankle (A003) Talocrural joint mobilisation; synovial fluid distribution; morning stiffness reduction
Varicose Veins / Venous Insufficiency Calf venous pump activation — direct mechanical assistance to venous return
Post-surgical / Post-immobilisation DVT prevention; venous return stimulation; ankle ROM maintenance
Oedema of the lower limbs Venous and lymphatic pump activation reduces dependent oedema
Diabetes (A001) — neuropathy prevention Peripheral circulation improvement; sensorimotor foot reactivation
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) Ankle mobilisation before weight-bearing; calf pump activation

6. Contraindications

  • Acute ankle sprain (ligamentous) — avoid active plantarflexion/inversion which loads the damaged lateral ligaments; dorsiflexion only may be permitted after medical clearance
  • Achilles tendon rupture — plantarflexion loads the healing tendon; avoid until cleared
  • Acute ankle arthritis — rest the joint in the most inflamed phase; gentle movement may resume in subacute phase

7. Variations & Progressions

Bilateral simultaneous (standard): Both feet together — the most common form.

Alternating: Right then left — allows observation of any range asymmetry.

Resistance band added (advanced): Loop a band around the forefoot; adds mild resistance to dorsiflexion — tibialis anterior strengthening progression.

Standing heel raises (weight-bearing progression): Rising on the toes (plantarflexion) and lowering — the weight-bearing equivalent that maximally activates the soleal pump.

Sequencing context: Follows P0001–P0002 (toe practices). Progresses to P0004 (Ankle Rotation) then P0005 (combined ankle sequence).
