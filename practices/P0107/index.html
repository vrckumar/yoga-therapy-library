Sarvangasana

Sarvangasana (Shoulder Stand)

Sarva (Sanskrit) — all, entire; anga — limb, body part. The "queen of asanas" in classical hatha yoga — the full shoulder-supported inversion that reverses gravitational effects on the entire body. The chin lock (jalandhara bandha) created by the chin in the suprasternal hollow is the defining feature that differentiates this from other inversions and produces its specific thyroid and nervous system effects.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine (Shavasana). Lie flat on the back, legs together, arms beside the body.

Practice:

  1. Raise both legs together to 45°, then continue to 90° (perpendicular to the floor), keeping the knees straight.
  2. Press the palms firmly into the floor and swing the hips upward, raising the buttocks and trunk off the floor.
  3. Support the back with both hands, placing the palms on the mid-back, elbows on the floor shoulder-width apart.
  4. Continue raising the trunk until the body is fully erect — legs, hips, and trunk in one vertical line above the shoulders.
  5. The chin rests in the suprasternal hollow (the notch at the top of the sternum) — this is jalandhara bandha (throat lock), a natural chin lock that compresses the carotid sinus and has specific cardiovascular and endocrine effects.
  6. Walk the hands up the back toward the shoulder blades to support a more vertical position. The weight is borne on the shoulders and upper arms — not the neck.
  7. Maintain for 1–3 minutes with normal breathing.
  8. To release: slowly lower the knees toward the forehead, place the palms on the floor, and roll down vertebra by vertebra. Rest in Shavasana before returning to upright.

Breathing: Normal breathing throughout. The chin lock restricts throat breathing slightly, emphasising abdominal/diaphragmatic breathing.

Duration/rounds: 1–3 minutes; 1 round. Always follow with Matsyasana as a counter-posture.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Triceps brachii: hold the elbows in position on the floor
  • Posterior shoulder musculature (rhomboids, lower trapezius): stabilise the shoulder girdle as the foundation
  • Core (transverse abdominis, multifidus): maintain the vertical alignment of the trunk against gravitational pull
  • Neck flexors (sternocleidomastoid, anterior scalenes): the chin-locked position requires active neck flexor engagement to maintain the head position

Organs and structures affected

  • Thyroid and parathyroid glands: the chin lock creates direct pressure on the thyroid cartilage and increases blood pooling in the thyroid region — a passive mechanical effect
  • Pelvic organs: full inversion reverses the gravitational loading on the pelvic floor; the uterus, ovaries, bladder, and rectum are passively repositioned
  • Lower limb venous system: full inversion drains venous blood from the lower limbs completely — the most efficient single posture for venous return

Structural considerations

The cervical spine is held in mild flexion with the full body weight transmitted through the shoulder joint complex. The cervical vertebrae bear compressive forces, and the posterior cervical ligaments are on stretch — this is the anatomical basis for the hypertension and spondylosis contraindications.

3. Physiological Effects

Thyroid stimulation — the primary endocrine mechanism

The jalandhara bandha (chin lock) in Sarvangasana produces a specific and clinically significant sequence of effects on the thyroid:

  1. Mechanical compression: the chin pressing into the suprasternal notch directly compresses the thyroid gland (which sits over the trachea at this level). This compression temporarily reduces blood outflow from the gland.
  2. Venous pooling: with the body inverted, blood accumulates in the head and neck region. The thyroid gland, being highly vascularised (it receives more blood per gram of tissue than almost any other organ), pools this additional blood within its follicles.
  3. Decompression on release: when the posture is released, the compressed vessels rebound and blood flows freely through the gland. This compression-release cycle is proposed to mechanically stimulate thyroid follicular cells, improving secretory capacity.
  4. Parathyroid: the four parathyroid glands, embedded in the posterior surface of the thyroid, receive the same mechanical stimulation — potentially improving calcium-regulating PTH secretion.

This mechanism is the direct physiological basis for Sarvangasana's therapeutic role in thyroid conditions and the downstream metabolic effects (metabolism, growth, development) of thyroid hormone.

Cardiovascular — the venous drainage mechanism

In the upright position, the venous blood in the lower limbs must travel against gravity, relying on skeletal muscle pumping (the calf pump) and venous valves. In Sarvangasana, the full inversion means gravity now assists venous return from the entire lower body:

  • Varicose veins (distended, valve-incompetent superficial veins) receive complete drainage — the pooled blood empties into the deep venous system
  • Haemorrhoidal venous plexuses at the anorectal junction are similarly drained — the basis for the stated piles (haemorrhoids) benefit
  • Pelvic congestion, a common driver of menstrual irregularities, is relieved by gravitational inversion of the pelvic venous plexuses

Carotid sinus stimulation and blood pressure

The chin lock compresses the carotid sinus — a baroreceptor located at the bifurcation of the common carotid artery in the neck. Carotid sinus stimulation triggers a reflex parasympathetic response (via the glossopharyngeal nerve → nucleus tractus solitarius → dorsal vagal nucleus):

  • Heart rate decreases
  • Peripheral vascular resistance decreases
  • Blood pressure decreases

This is the neurological basis for the calming effect of Sarvangasana and, paradoxically, why uncontrolled hypertension is a contraindication — if blood pressure is already unstable, adding a hypotensive carotid reflex could cause adverse events.

Pelvic organ repositioning

In conditions such as uterine prolapse, retroversion, or menstrual cramping, the gravitational reversal of pelvic organ positioning provides relief of pressure on the pelvic floor and stretches the broad ligaments and round ligaments of the uterus. This accounts for the stated benefit in menstrual disorders.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — comprehensive gravitational reversal affecting all organ systems; thyroid and pelvic organ stimulation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — inversion is associated with reversing apana vayu (downward-moving energy) toward the higher chakras; vishuddha (throat) chakra activation through the chin lock is the energetic parallel of the thyroid stimulation.

6. Contraindications

  • Cervical spondylosis — the neck bears body weight in a flexed position; cervical degeneration can be aggravated; substitute Viparita Karani
  • Hypertension (uncontrolled) — carotid sinus stimulation may cause unpredictable blood pressure responses; use Viparita Karani instead
  • Low back pain (acute) — the lumbar spine is in full flexion under load; avoid in acute disc or muscle injury
  • Glaucoma / detached retina — intraocular pressure increases in inversion
  • Pregnancy — inversion poses risk of placental disruption and is contraindicated beyond first trimester
  • Menstruation (active flow) — some traditions avoid inversions during menstruation; individual assessment recommended

7. Variations & Progressions

Modified (chair-supported): Use a folded blanket under the shoulders to take cervical pressure off; use a chair for the legs to rest on, creating a supported partial inversion.

Standard (as described): Full vertical shoulder stand with chin lock.

Counter-posture (mandatory): Matsyasana — Fish Pose — must follow Sarvangasana to counter-extend the cervical spine. Halasana may precede or follow within the same inversion sequence.

Alternative for contraindications: Viparita Karani — Legs Up the Wall — provides similar venous drainage and partial thyroid stimulation without neck compression.
