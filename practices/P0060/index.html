Tiger Breathing

Tiger Breathing (Vyaghra Svasana)

An all-fours (quadruped) breathing exercise in which spinal extension (concave back, head raised) is synchronised with inhalation and spinal flexion (arched back, chin to chest) with exhalation — essentially the yoga cat-cow movement performed with breath coordination. One of the most widely used practices in IAYT back pain, obesity, and respiratory protocols. Trains breath-spine coordination, mobilises the thoracic and lumbar vertebrae, and directly addresses the dysfunctional breathing patterns associated with chronic back pain.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Dandasana (sitting on the floor with legs outstretched), then transition to Vajrasana (sitting on the heels).

Practice:

  1. From Vajrasana, lean forward and place both palms flat on the floor directly in front of the knees, in line with the shoulders. Fingers point forward. Arms, thighs, and heels are approximately one shoulder-width apart. Arms and thighs are perpendicular to the floor. This is the "all-fours" table position.
  2. Inhaling: raise the head and look upward at the ceiling. Simultaneously depress the spine (let it sag downward), making the back concave — the classical "cow" extension.
  3. Exhaling: arch the spine upward toward the ceiling and bring the head downward, chin moving toward the chest — the classical "cat" flexion.
  4. This completes one round. Repeat 5 rounds.
  5. After completing the rounds, assume Sasankasana (child's pose / hare pose) and relax.

Notes:

  • Do not bend the arms or shift the thighs forward or backward during the movement — the hands and knees remain stationary.
  • Co-ordinate the movements precisely with the breathing.
  • Keep the eyes closed and maintain breath awareness throughout.
  • Ensure comfort in the "all-fours" position before beginning.

Duration/rounds: 5 rounds at a slow, deliberate pace. 1 minute total.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

Inhalation phase (extension / concave back):

  • Erector spinae (bilateral): concentrically active to produce the lumbar and thoracic extension
  • Deep neck extensors: raise the head into cervical extension
  • Diaphragm: inhalation is facilitated by the spinal extension, which reduces thoracic compression and allows diaphragmatic descent

Exhalation phase (flexion / arched back):

  • Rectus abdominis and obliques: draw the spine upward into flexion
  • Deep neck flexors: bring the chin toward the chest
  • Thoracic and lumbar flexors: produce the posterior arch of the spine

Joint mobility

  • Thoracic and lumbar facet joints move through their full flexion-extension range in a weight-supported, decompressed position — the four-point position unloads the intervertebral discs, allowing freer vertebral movement than in upright postures.

3. Physiological Effects

Spinal mobilisation with breath

The quadruped position removes axial loading from the spine while allowing the full flexion-extension range. This produces the classic benefits of spinal mobilisation (increased synovial fluid distribution, reduced stiffness, improved segmental mobility) while simultaneously training respiratory coordination. For chronic back pain patients, the spinal mobility gain is amplified by the association with slow, rhythmic breathing, which reduces protective muscle guarding.

Respiratory mechanism

  • In the extension phase, the concave back expands the anterior thorax, facilitating inhalation.
  • In the flexion phase, the arched back passively compresses the posterior thorax and assists exhalation.
  • This mechanical breathing-spine coupling is the same principle used in physiotherapy respiratory techniques for patients with COPD and chest physiotherapy.

Indicated for back pain — spinal decompression

The all-fours position is one of the few positions that simultaneously:

  1. Decompresses the intervertebral discs (gravity-assisted traction effect)
  2. Mobilises the facet joints through full range
  3. Activates the deep spinal stabilisers (multifidus) in their optimal length-tension position

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — spinal mobilisation, thoracic wall movement, and abdominal toning.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the oscillation between spinal extension (inhalation) and flexion (exhalation) mirrors the natural breath-wave movement of the spine, restoring samana vayu (balancing vital energy in the middle region).

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain (A004) Primary indication; decompresses the spine, mobilises all vertebral segments, reduces muscle guarding
Asthma / Respiratory Disorders (A007) Trains the thoracic breathing mechanism; coordinates breath with chest movement
Obesity (A002) Abdominal compression in flexion phase stimulates abdominal organ motility
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Abdominal organ stimulation in the flexion phase activates the pancreatic region
Anxiety / Depression (A006) Rhythmic, grounded four-point movement with breath produces rapid calming; reduces sympathetic arousal

6. Contraindications

  • Wrist injury or carpal tunnel syndrome: the quadruped position places body weight through the wrists; use fists or wedge supports, or transition to a forearm-supported variant
  • Acute knee injury: padding under the knees; may not be appropriate if knee flexion is severely restricted
  • Acute lumbar disc prolapse with radiculopathy: assess carefully; flexion may aggravate some disc patterns

7. Variations & Progressions

Tiger Stretch (Type I): A faster, more vigorous version in which the spinal extension is achieved with a quick inhalation through the nose and the flexion is released with a forceful blast of exhalation through the mouth, using a jerk. Performed 10–20 rounds with increasing speed. Used in vigorous/stamina protocols.

Progression: Alternate Bhujangasana and Parvatasana extends the same principle to a prone-to-inverted-V movement.
