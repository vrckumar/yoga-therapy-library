Neck Side Movement

Neck Side Movement (Greeva Parsva Sanchalana)

Isolated, slow, sustained cervical lateral flexion — the pure side movement emphasised as a therapeutic exercise rather than a preparation movement. Focuses on the lateral cervical structures (scalenes, levator scapulae, lateral facets) with slow, breath-assisted elongation.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Seated. Spine erect. Shoulders level and relaxed.

Practice:

  1. Exhale slowly; during the exhalation, allow the right ear to drop toward the right shoulder — pure lateral flexion, no rotation. The left shoulder remains down.
  2. At the end range, breathe naturally for 3–5 breaths, allowing the lateral stretch to deepen with each exhalation.
  3. Inhale and return to centre.
  4. Exhale and lower the left ear toward the left shoulder.
  5. Hold 3–5 breaths on the left side.
  6. Return to centre. Repeat 5 rounds each side.

Breathing: Slow, conscious breathing enhances the therapeutic stretch effect.

Duration: 5 rounds each side; 3–5 breath holds at end range.

2. Anatomical Activation

Structures stretched (contralateral side during each lateral tilt)

  • Upper trapezius: the most superficial lateral neck muscle; frequently the source of referred headache pain when shortened
  • Levator scapulae: connects upper cervical spine to the scapula; a primary site of myofascial trigger points in occupational neck strain
  • Scalenes (anterior, middle, posterior): primary lateral flexors and accessory inspiratory muscles
  • Splenius capitis: posterior lateral neck muscle
  • Sternocleidomastoid (contralateral): stretched in pure lateral flexion without rotation

3. Physiological Effects

Myofascial trigger point release — the therapeutic emphasis

P0025 performs lateral flexion as part of a dynamic 10-round sequence. P0028 is the slower, held version — the therapeutic form used when the lateral cervical muscles (upper trapezius, levator scapulae) are the primary source of pain and trigger points. Sustained stretching at end range of lateral flexion — maintained for 3–5 breaths — is the duration required for myofascial trigger point deactivation and sustained muscle length change (as opposed to the rapid elastic recoil from a brief dynamic movement).

Scalene respiratory function restoration

The scalenes' dual role — cervical lateral flexors and accessory inspiratory muscles — means that when they are chronically shortened, they impair both neck mobility and breathing efficiency. The sustained lateral tilt specifically elongates the scalenes at rest length, gradually restoring diaphragmatic dominance.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — sustained myofascial elongation of lateral cervical muscles; trigger point deactivation; scalene resting length restoration.

Secondary: Manomaya Kosha — the slow, breath-assisted hold requires sustained attention and breath awareness — initiating the meditative quality of practice.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Cervicogenic Headache Sustained upper trapezius and levator scapulae elongation — direct trigger point treatment
Occupational Neck Strain (computer/desk work) Lateral cervical myofascial release
Arthritis — Cervical Spine (A003) Sustained lateral facet mobilisation
Tension Headache Pericranial muscle elongation
Asthma / Breathing Disorders Scalene elongation improving diaphragmatic breathing pattern

6. Contraindications

  • Cervical radiculopathy — lateral flexion to the side of the radiculopathy narrows the foramen ipsilaterally; tilt only to the contralateral side during acute phase
  • Cervical spondylosis — gentle mid-range only; avoid end-range sustained holds

7. Variations & Progressions

Gravity-assisted (deepening): After reaching end range, place the hand of the same side on the head (gently, not pulling) to use the arm weight to deepen the stretch.

Breathing progression: Increase hold to 10 breaths as tolerance improves.

Sequencing context: More therapeutic version of P0025's lateral bending. Used in protocols where cervicogenic headache or neck muscle pain is the primary indication.
