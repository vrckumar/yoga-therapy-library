Dorsal Stretch

Dorsal Stretch (Vaksha Sthala Naman)

Prone spinal extension — lifting the head, chest, and upper back off the floor while in the prone position. The prone counterpart to all the supine flexion stretches (P0051–P0055); provides anterior thoracic and abdominal wall elongation and posterior thoracic extensor muscle activation. A milder version of Bhujangasana as a preparatory loosening exercise.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Prone Posture. Legs together, chin on the floor. Arms bent at elbows beside the chest.

Practice:

  1. Lie prone. Place the palms beside the lower chest (slightly below the shoulder level).
  2. While inhaling slowly, press the palms gently into the floor and lift the head, then the neck, then the chest off the floor — sequentially arching the spine from the upper to the lower.
  3. Arch upward as far as comfortable — the navel may or may not remain on the floor depending on flexibility and intent.
  4. Hold for 3–5 breaths with normal breathing. Feel the anterior chest and abdominal elongation.
  5. While exhaling, slowly lower the chest, then the neck, then the head back to the floor.
  6. Relax in Makarasana. Repeat 3–5 times.

Breathing: Inhale on lifting; normal during hold; exhale on lowering.

Duration: 3–5 repetitions.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged (extension)

  • Erector spinae (thoracic and cervical): concentrically active raising the trunk
  • Rhomboids and middle trapezius: scapular retraction during the lift
  • Posterior deltoid and triceps: pressing the hands into the floor (elbow extension component)
  • Posterior cervical muscles (semispinalis, splenius): cervical extension

Structures elongated

  • Anterior abdominal wall (rectus abdominis, obliques): stretched as the spine extends
  • Anterior thoracic fascial chain: pectoralis major, anterior intercostals, anterior longitudinal ligament (posterior boundary of the anterior extension stretch)
  • Hip flexors (iliopsoas): the prone extension position elongates the hip flexors — the same mechanism as Bhujangasana

3. Physiological Effects

Anterior thoracic elongation — the complement to supine flexion series

The preceding exercises P0051–P0055 systematically elongated the posterior thoracolumbar structures (erector spinae, thoracolumbar fascia, posterior disc annulus). Dorsal Stretch now provides the essential counter-direction — elongating the anterior structures (abdominal wall, anterior thoracic fascial chain, hip flexors) and activating the posterior extensors concentrically. Without this counter-direction stretch, the posterior elongation series would create an unbalanced therapeutic outcome favouring excessive kyphotic flexibility.

Prone extension and disc mechanics

In the prone extension position, the posterior disc space is compressed (narrowed) and the anterior disc space is widened. This is the opposite of the lumbar flexion that dominated the P0051–P0055 series. The extension position is specifically therapeutic for posterior disc herniations — the extension force centralises the nuclear material away from the posterior herniation direction. This is the McKenzie extension principle, and Dorsal Stretch is its yogic implementation.

Posterior thoracic extensor activation

The thoracic erector spinae are among the muscles most weakened by modern sedentary postures (thoracic kyphosis). The prone extension reactivates these muscles in their functional concentric role — lifting the thorax against gravity from a prone position.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — anterior thoracic and abdominal elongation; posterior extensor muscle activation; disc anterior widening (McKenzie principle); anterior thoracic fascial chain mobilisation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the chest-opening, spine-arching movement is specifically associated with the expansion of prana vayu in the thoracic region; Dorsal Stretch activates the anahata (heart) and vishuddha (throat) energy centres.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain (A004) — posterior disc herniation McKenzie extension centralisation; posterior disc decompression by anterior widening
Thoracic Kyphosis / Rounded Posture Posterior extensor reactivation; anterior fascial elongation
Asthma / Respiratory Disorders Anterior chest expansion; thoracic mobility for breathing
Arthritis — Thoracic Spine (A003) Posterior thoracic extensor activation; thoracic extension ROM maintenance
All IAYT protocols Final preparatory exercise — balances the flexion-dominant supine series with extension

6. Contraindications

  • Acute posterior disc herniation — with progressive neurological deficit — extension may increase nerve root pressure in lateral recess stenosis; medical assessment first
  • Spinal stenosis (lumbar) — extension narrows the central canal; avoid in symptomatic stenosis
  • Spondylolisthesis — posterior element compression in extension; reduce range significantly
  • Recent abdominal surgery — anterior wall elongation in extension; avoid until fully healed

7. Variations & Progressions

Partial (head and neck only): Lifting only the head and neck — minimal spinal loading; safe starting point.

Full (as described): Head, neck, and upper chest — standard therapeutic form.

Bhujangasana (advanced): Elbows straight, full thoracic extension — the complete asana version; prescribed as a therapeutic asana rather than a loosening exercise.

Sequencing context: Final exercise in the supine leg and lumbar series (P0042–P0056). Completes the full Sithilikarana Vyayama loosening sequence. Transitions to the formal asana series.
