Tongue Movement

Tongue Movement (Jivha Chalana)

Jivha = tongue, chalana = movement. A series of deliberate tongue exercises — extension, lateral movement, rotation, and retraction — performed as part of the facial rehabilitation sequence in IAYT. The tongue is the only muscle group in the body directly controlled by the hypoglossal nerve (Cranial Nerve XII), and tongue exercises are a primary tool in the rehabilitation of speech, swallowing, and oropharyngeal function after neurological events (stroke, head injury). Also used in the thyroid and throat protocols as preparatory practice before Simha Mudra.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrasana or seated in a chair, spine erect, mouth relaxed.

Practice:

Stage I — Forward extension:

  1. Open the mouth and protrude the tongue fully forward — stretch it as far out of the mouth as possible, pointing toward the chin.
  2. Hold for 5 seconds. Retract.
  3. Repeat 5 times.

Stage II — Lateral movement:

  1. With the mouth open, move the tongue to the right corner of the mouth as far as possible.
  2. Return to centre. Move to the left corner.
  3. This is one round. Repeat 10 rounds (alternating left and right).

Stage III — Up-down movement:

  1. Extend the tongue upward toward the nose tip.
  2. Then extend downward toward the chin.
  3. One round. Repeat 10 times.

Stage IV — Circular movement (tongue rotation):

  1. With the mouth open, rotate the tongue in a full circle — moving right, up, left, down, right — tracing the inner surface of the lips.
  2. Perform 5 clockwise circles, then 5 anti-clockwise circles.

Stage V — Palate touching:

  1. Touch the tip of the tongue to the hard palate (front) and progressively move it backward toward the soft palate.
  2. This activates different regions of the palate and posterior tongue root.

Notes:

  • Perform slowly and with full awareness of the tongue's position in each movement.
  • After each stage, rest the tongue briefly on the floor of the mouth.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles

  • Genioglossus: the largest intrinsic tongue muscle; the primary protruder (Stage I); activated in all movement stages
  • Hyoglossus: retracts and depresses the tongue
  • Styloglossus: retracts and elevates the tongue
  • Inferior and superior longitudinal tongue muscles: shorten and curl the tongue in different directions
  • Transverse and vertical intrinsic muscles: change the shape and width of the tongue

Cranial nerve activation

  • Hypoglossal nerve (CN XII): the motor nerve for all tongue movements; all stages directly activate and rehabilitate CN XII function
  • Glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX): stimulated by the posterior tongue and palate contact
  • Lingual nerve (branch of CN V): sensory feedback from the tongue surface

3. Physiological Effects

Hypoglossal nerve rehabilitation (stroke recovery)

After a stroke, the hypoglossal nerve motor pathway is frequently affected, resulting in:

  • Dysarthria (slurred speech due to weak tongue)
  • Dysphagia (swallowing difficulty due to impaired tongue thrust)
  • Facial asymmetry of the tongue (deviation toward the weak side)

Systematic tongue exercises as described here constitute the foundational rehabilitation for these deficits — identical in principle to the speech therapy oropharyngeal exercises prescribed post-stroke.

Speech and articulation improvement

The tongue is responsible for the majority of consonant articulations. Weakened tongue mobility reduces speech intelligibility. Tongue movement exercises improve:

  • Speed and precision of tongue movement
  • Range of motion in all directions
  • Muscle endurance for sustained speech

Oropharyngeal tone and swallowing

The posterior tongue-to-palate contact (Stage V) strengthens the tongue's role in the swallowing reflex (propulsion of the food bolus). This is relevant in:

  • Elderly patients with reduced swallowing efficiency
  • Post-stroke dysphagia
  • Obstructive sleep apnoea (where tongue base weakness contributes to airway collapse)

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — tongue musculature rehabilitation; hypoglossal nerve activation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — tongue movements in yoga tradition are associated with the Khechari Mudra pathway and udana vayu (upward-moving vital energy governing speech).

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Stroke rehabilitation Primary — hypoglossal nerve reactivation; dysarthria and dysphagia rehabilitation
Thyroid conditions Oropharyngeal stimulation; preparatory to Simha Mudra
Chronic pharyngitis / throat conditions Posterior pharyngeal and tongue root stimulation
Speech difficulties Tongue strength, speed, and range of motion
Obstructive sleep apnoea Tongue base strengthening

6. Contraindications

  • Acute glossitis (tongue inflammation): reduce to gentle movements within the pain-free range
  • Oral ulcers: avoid lateral movements that contact the affected area
  • No significant contraindications for the basic forward extension and lateral movements

7. Related Practices

  • Tongue Massaging (P0145): self-massage of the tongue and palate — complementary practice
  • Simha Mudra (P0105): maximum tongue protrusion with roar — the advanced version
  • Lip Stretch (P0146): facial muscle exercises that include the perioral muscles surrounding the tongue space
