Cyclic Meditation

Cyclic Meditation (CM)

Cyclic Meditation is an integrated mind-body practice developed at SVYASA (Swami Vivekananda Yoga Anusandhana Samsthana) based on the principle articulated in the Mandukya Upanishad and operationalised through the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali: "Laye sambodhayet cittam, vikṣiptam śamayet punaḥ; sakāśāyam vijānīyāt, samprāptam na cālayet" — "In the state of oblivion awaken the mind; when agitated, pacify it; when the mind has reached the state of perfect equilibrium, do not disturb it."

The technique alternates active standing postures (stimulation) with guided supine relaxation (deactivation) in a structured cycle. Clinical research at SVYASA has documented significant blood glucose reduction, blood pressure normalisation, reduced anxiety scores, and improved sleep quality with regular Cyclic Meditation practice. It is one of the most clinically validated yoga techniques in the IAYT system.

1. Technique

Starting position: Supine — Śavāsana (Corpse Pose).

Approximate duration: 35–45 minutes for a complete session.

Step I — Starting Prayer (supine)

Lie on the back. Relax and collapse the whole body on the ground; legs apart; hands apart; palms facing the ceiling; face smiling.

Chant the starting verse (the Mandukya principle above) and feel the resonance throughout the body. The prayer establishes the theoretical framework of the practice as an intention.

Step II(a) — Instant Relaxation Technique (IRT) (supine)

  1. Bring legs together; join the heels, toes; palms by the side of the thighs. Face smiling.
  2. Bring awareness to the tip of the toes. Progressively tighten the entire body: stretch the toes, tighten the ankles, tighten the calf muscles, pull up the kneecaps, tighten the thighs, compress and squeeze the buttocks. Exhale and suck in the abdomen. Make fists of the palms; tighten the arms. Inhale and expand the chest. Tighten the shoulders, neck muscles, compress the face. Tighten the whole body from toes to head — tighten...tighten...tighten...
  3. Release and relax: legs go apart, arms go apart, palms facing the ceiling. The most comfortable position; let the whole body sink down. All muscle groups beautifully relax. Collapse the whole body. Enjoy the relaxation.

Step II(b) — Linear Awareness (transition from supine to standing)

  1. Slowly raise the left hand above the head along the ground.
  2. Turn over to the left side. Place the head on the left biceps; the right leg on the left leg; right palm on the right thigh. Let the whole body relax — fine linear awareness of the weight contacting the left side.
  3. Slowly come up to Tāḍāsana. Let all movements be slow and continuous. Eyes remain closed. Feel the changes as you stand up: the flow of blood downward from the heart; the heartbeat; the pulse in the feet.
  4. Chant Bhramari (Mmm...) to generate a three-dimensional vibrational awareness — feel the resonance throughout the body.

Step II(c) — Centering

  • Slowly lean forward — feel the weight on the toes. Pointed awareness.
  • Lean backwards — feel the weight on the heels.
  • Come to the centre.
  • Lean right, lean left, come to the centre.
  • Now the whole body is centered: weight equally distributed across the entire sole. Shoulders collapsed, arms hanging freely. Face smiling.

Step III — Standing Postures (stimulation phase)

Ardha Kaṭi Cakrāsana (Half-Wheel Pose) — bilateral:

  • Slowly raise the right arm up sideways from the hanging position, through horizontal, to vertical (biceps touching right ear). Twist the palm at the horizontal to face upward.
  • At vertical position: stretch the arm upward from the fingertips. The entire right side of the body is stretched. Face smiling.
  • Slowly bend left; left palm slides down the left thigh. Feel the beautiful stretch of the right waist muscles and the compression on the left side.
  • Return slowly to vertical; feel the blood and nerve impulses spreading.
  • Bring the arm down slowly; hang it freely.
  • Repeat on the left side.

(The detailed guided narration of sensations — "pointed awareness at the wrist," "surface awareness on the deltoid," "feel the blood gushing down the arm" — is an integral part of the technique that trains interoception throughout each movement.)

Step IV — Quick Relaxation Technique (QRT) (return to supine)

  1. Slowly sit down and lie back to Śavāsana from the right side — slow, continuous movement.
  2. Back in supine: all the movements settling.

Phase I — Observing abdominal movement:

  • Bring awareness to the abdominal muscles moving up and down with breathing. Recognise the haphazardness and jerky quality of unregulated breath. Count 5 rounds (1 inhalation + 1 exhalation = 1 round) mentally. Do not manipulate the breath — simply observe.

Phase II — Associating with breathing:

  • Associate the awareness with the breathing rhythm. Gradually, as attention is placed on the breath, the breath naturally begins to slow and become more regular. Notice how the mere act of observing the breath begins to regulate it.

Phase III — Slowing the breath:

  • Now deliberately slow the breath further with each exhalation. Allow the exhalation to be longer than the inhalation. Feel the body sinking deeper into relaxation with each breath cycle.

Phase IV — Silence:

  • The breath has slowed to 4–6 breaths per minute. The mind is quiet. Remain in this inner silence. Observe the quality of the silence — not blank or dull, but alert and expansive.

Step V — Second cycle of Standing Postures → QRT

The sequence in Step III–IV may be repeated with additional standing postures (e.g., Ardha Cakrasana, Padahastasana, Trikonasana) and a second guided relaxation. The number of stimulation-relaxation cycles defines the depth of the practice.

Step VI — Deep Relaxation Technique (DRT) (closing supine)

Extended Śavāsana with systematic body awareness from toes to head — the final deep rest before the session closes.

2. The Stimulation-Relaxation Principle

The theoretical basis of Cyclic Meditation is explicitly drawn from the Yoga Sutras. The starting verse articulates a psychological principle that SVYASA has operationalised as a physiological training method:

  • When the mind-body system is in a state of oblivion (Laya — torpor, dullness, hypo-arousal): activate it with stimulating postures, movement, and awareness challenges.
  • When the mind-body system is in a state of agitation (Vikṣipta — scattered, over-aroused): calm it with guided relaxation, breath regulation, and body awareness.
  • When the system reaches equilibrium (Samāhita): do not disturb it — maintain the silence.

In physiological terms, this translates to:

Stimulation phase (standing postures): the large-muscle isometric and isotonic demands of the standing postures activate the sympathetic nervous system — heart rate increases, blood pressure rises, cortisol and epinephrine are secreted. The adrenals are activated, peripheral circulation is increased.

Deactivation phase (guided supine relaxation): the abrupt shift to supine with guided body awareness and slow breathing activates the parasympathetic system — heart rate drops, blood pressure falls (the baroreceptor-mediated vagal response to lying supine), and the adrenal output falls sharply. The contrast between the stimulated and deactivated states is the therapeutic mechanism.

3. Physiological Effects

Blood glucose reduction — the documented clinical mechanism

Cyclic Meditation is one of the few yoga techniques with randomised controlled trial (RCT) documentation of acute blood glucose reduction. SVYASA studies have shown that a single 35-minute session of Cyclic Meditation reduces fasting blood glucose by 10–15 mg/dL in type 2 diabetic subjects.

The mechanisms proposed are:

  1. Adrenergic-insulin cycling: The stimulation phase activates adrenal catecholamine secretion (epinephrine, norepinephrine), which briefly mobilises hepatic glucose (glycogenolysis). The subsequent relaxation phase allows insulin to clear this mobilised glucose more efficiently than at baseline rest. The net effect is an improvement in hepatic glucose regulation through the exercise-recovery cycle applied at the metabolic level.

  2. GLUT-4 upregulation: The isometric muscle work of the standing postures is sufficient to activate AMP-kinase (AMPK) in the working muscles, which translocates GLUT-4 glucose transporters to the muscle cell surface independently of insulin. This insulin-independent glucose uptake persists for hours after the session, improving the post-session glucose tolerance curve.

  3. Cortisol normalisation: The alternating stimulation-relaxation cycling trains the HPA axis to respond proportionately — activating cortisol in the stimulation phase and suppressing it in the relaxation phase — normalising the chronically elevated basal cortisol of stress-related Type 2 diabetes.

Blood pressure normalisation

In hypertensive subjects, the repeated baroreceptor stimulation during the transitions from standing to supine (and the acute blood pressure changes during standing postures) trains the baroreflex sensitivity — the speed and magnitude of the cardiovascular system's response to blood pressure perturbations. Improved baroreflex sensitivity is directly associated with reduced resting blood pressure and reduced cardiovascular risk.

Anxiety and sleep improvement

The structured alternation of activation and deactivation teaches the nervous system to identify, regulate, and shift between arousal states. In chronic anxiety, the nervous system is "stuck" in sympathetic overdrive — it has lost the flexibility to down-regulate. Cyclic Meditation provides repeated, successful practice of the down-regulation transition (from standing stimulation to supine quiet), demonstrating to the nervous system that the transition is possible and safe. Over sessions, this flexibility generalises to daily life: anxious patients report improved ability to "let go" of stressful arousal outside the practice session.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — the standing postures activate the physical body; the supine relaxation provides physical rest; the IRT's systematic tension-release is a whole-body Annamaya practice.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the QRT's breath-observation and breath-regulation phases work directly on the pranic body; the shift from unregulated to regulated, slow breathing in the relaxation phase is a pranayama practice embedded within the larger cycle.

Tertiary: Manomaya Kosha — the entire cycling between stimulation and deactivation, observed by continuous inner awareness, is a Manomaya practice; the practitioner develops the capacity to observe their own arousal states from the witness position — the beginning of mental self-regulation.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Documented blood glucose reduction; adrenergic-insulin cycling; GLUT-4 upregulation; cortisol normalisation
Hypertension (A005) Baroreflex retraining; blood pressure normalisation; parasympathetic conditioning
Anxiety (A006) Arousal-state regulation training; nervous system flexibility; demonstrated anxiety score improvement in RCTs
Obesity (A002) Metabolic activation through repeated stimulation-deactivation cycling; AMPK activation in muscle
Depression (A006) Stimulation component activates the sympathetic system from depressive hypo-arousal; the cycling prevents over-activation
Insomnia The deep body awareness and breath regulation of QRT and DRT phases are among the most effective non-pharmacological interventions for sleep onset difficulties

6. Contraindications

  • Acute medical emergency: CM requires physical standing postures; not appropriate during acute illness or medical instability
  • Severe orthopedic limitations: the standing posture components can be modified to seated or supine versions; the therapeutic benefit is partially preserved even without standing
  • Acute psychiatric episodes: the activation-deactivation cycling may not be appropriate in states of florid psychosis; use under supervision

7. Variations & Progressions

Abbreviated CM (20 minutes): IRT → single AKC round → QRT → brief DRT. Suitable for clinical settings with time constraints.

Standard CM (35–45 minutes, as described): Full sequence with bilateral postures and complete QRT phases.

Extended CM (60 minutes): Multiple posture rounds with progressively deeper relaxation phases; used in residential SVYASA programs for chronic disease.

Audio-guided CM: SVYASA has produced standardised audio recordings of the complete CM technique (referenced in PGDYTP 101 as "audio cassettes of SVYASA") for home practice. These ensure consistent delivery of the guided narration elements that are integral to the practice.
