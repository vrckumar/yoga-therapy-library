Hastauttanasana

Hastauttanasana (Raised Arms Pose)

Hasta (Sanskrit) — hand; Uttana — intense stretch upward. From Tāḍāsana, both arms are raised overhead with the biceps touching the ears, and a controlled backward arch is introduced through the thoracic and lumbar spine. The posture is the second and eleventh position of Sūryanamaskāra, giving it the highest frequency of practice of any single standing backbend in the IAYT repertoire. As a standalone practice it serves as the primary counterpose to all forward bends and as an independent opener for the thoracic cage and the lung apices.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tāḍāsana. Stand erect, legs together, arms at sides.

Practice:

  1. Inhale slowly and raise both arms sideways.
  2. At the horizontal level, turn the palms upward.
  3. Continue to inhale deeply and raise the arms further overhead until the biceps touch the ears. Turn the palms forward (facing ahead).
  4. Stretch the entire body upward from the waist — feel the elongation through the spine from coccyx to crown.
  5. With the inhalation complete, allow the thoracic and lumbar spine to arch gently backward. Drop the head backward in line with the arch — the gaze goes upward and slightly behind. The arch is initiated from the upper thoracic region, not by collapsing the lumbar spine.
  6. Maintain the posture for approximately 30–60 seconds with normal breathing, continuing to stretch upward through the arms while sustaining the backward arch.
  7. Exhale and return the arms to Tāḍāsana.

In Sūryanamaskāra context (positions 1 and 12): Performed as a flowing movement — inhale, sweep arms upward and arch back; the movement is continuous rather than held. Exhale leads into Padahastasana.

Breathing: Inhale into the raise and arch; maintain normal breathing in hold; exhale on return.

Duration/rounds: 30–60 seconds as a standalone; flowing in Sūryanamaskāra.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Erector spinae (thoracic and lumbar): concentrically active to produce and maintain the backward arch of the spine
  • Latissimus dorsi and teres major: extend the arms overhead; assist in the overall posterior chain elongation
  • Serratus anterior and lower trapezius: stabilise the scapulae against the thoracic wall during arm elevation, preventing winging and allowing the chest to open maximally
  • Anterior deltoids and biceps (eccentrically): controlled lengthening as the arms go overhead; actively stretched in the backward-bend position
  • Pectoralis major and minor: stretched across the entire anterior chest as the arms elevate and the thorax arches backward
  • Anterior intercostal muscles: passively stretched as the ribcage lifts and expands; the superior (apical) intercostals of the upper thorax receive the greatest stretch
  • Rectus abdominis and hip flexors (iliopsoas): eccentrically stretched in the backward arch; engage to control the degree of lumbar extension
  • Deep neck extensors (semispinalis, splenius): active in maintaining and continuing the cervical arch

Organs and structures affected

  • Lung apices: as the arms elevate and the thorax arches backward, the superior poles of the lungs — the apices — are expanded. The apical lung zones are the least-ventilated regions during tidal breathing; this posture specifically expands them, improving ventilation-perfusion matching in the upper lobes
  • Thyroid and parathyroid glands: the cervical extension with the head backward creates a mild anterior cervical stretch and passive compression of the posterior cervical structures, promoting blood flow through the thyroid bed
  • Heart and mediastinum: thoracic opening shifts the heart and great vessels slightly posteriorly; the pericardium is gently stretched; this contributes to the cardiac tone noted in IAYT literature

3. Physiological Effects

Anterior thoracic expansion — the apical lung mechanism

The most important and clinically specific physiological effect of Hastauttanasana is the selective expansion of the superior thoracic cage and the lung apices. In normal quiet breathing, the diaphragm performs the vast majority of ventilatory work, expanding the lower and middle lung zones most. The upper lobes — bounded superiorly by the clavicle and first rib, posteriorly by the upper thoracic spine — are ventilated relatively little. In conditions of chronic respiratory disease (asthma, post-infective fibrosisic changes) or simply with poor posture (forward-rounded shoulders), the apical regions become even less ventilated and may develop secretion pooling.

When both arms are elevated overhead with the biceps pressing the ears, the clavicles are lifted and the first ribs are elevated, mechanically expanding the thoracic inlet. The backward arch simultaneously lifts the sternum and further increases the anterior-posterior diameter of the upper thorax. The result is a selective positive-pressure stretch of the apical lung parenchyma: the alveoli are expanded passively, fresh air is drawn into lung segments that are typically hypoventilated, and accumulated secretions can be loosened and mobilised. This is the physiological rationale for including Hastauttanasana specifically in the IAYT Asthma protocol.

Anterior body lengthening — the fascial and neural mechanism

The stretch in Hastauttanasana runs from the plantar fascia of the feet, up through the anterior tibialis, the quadriceps, iliopsoas, rectus abdominis, anterior intercostals, pectorals, and anterior cervical muscles — the entire anterior myofascial line. Sustained elongation of this line:

  1. Reduces anterior postural compression: modern sedentary life chronically shortens the anterior body — the psoas tightens, the chest collapses, the cervical spine protrudes forward. Hastauttanasana directly reverses all three of these patterns in a single posture.
  2. Improves ventilatory mechanics: the tight anterior fascia is a significant limiting factor in thoracic expansion. By lengthening the pectorals, anterior intercostals, and abdominal fascia, the posture increases thoracic compliance — the lungs can expand more fully with less respiratory muscle effort.
  3. Stimulates the sympathetic chain via thoracic extension: the gentle backward arch mobilises the thoracic paravertebral tissues and the sympathetic chain ganglia, producing an invigorating, alerting autonomic effect appropriate for the opening posture of Sūryanamaskāra.

Counter-gravitational spine elongation

The upward stretch component of the posture — biceps pressing ears, the body elongating from coccyx to fingertips — creates a mild axial traction of the intervertebral spaces. Gravity continuously compresses the spine throughout the day; Hastauttanasana provides a counter-compressive force that temporarily increases intervertebral disc height and reduces the compressive load on facet joints. This is one mechanism underlying its inclusion in back pain protocols.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — anterior thoracic expansion, apical lung activation, anterior fascial chain elongation, spinal axial traction.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the upward stretch with full inhalation fills the lung apices, directly expanding prana vayu (inward, upward moving vital energy) and the vishuddha (throat) and anahata (heart) chakra regions; the opening of the upper chest is associated in yoga anatomy with the liberation of held emotional contraction in the cardiac region.

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar disc prolapse: the backward arch loads the posterior annulus; avoid strong extension if there is active radicular pain
  • Severe cervical spondylosis: limit or avoid the head-backward component; keep the neck in neutral if cervical extension is symptomatic
  • Vertigo: the head-backward position may provoke vertigo in susceptible individuals; keep the head neutral
  • Pregnancy (third trimester): the full forward stretch of the abdomen with arms overhead is uncomfortable and straining in late pregnancy; use a supported version

7. Variations & Progressions

Beginner: Raise arms only to shoulder height, no backward arch. Gradually increase arm elevation and spinal arch over weeks of practice.

Ardha Cakrasana (Half Wheel): A more intense variant — support the lower back with the palms at the waist, fingers pointing forward, and arch backward more deeply. The arms above the head version (Hastauttanasana) is generally gentler and more accessible.

Standard (as described): Arms overhead, biceps to ears, gentle thoracic and lumbar arch, head back. 30–60 seconds.

In Sūryanamaskāra flow: Position 1 (inhale, arms sweep up and back) and Position 12 (inhale, rise from Padahastasana into arm arch), performed as breath-linked movement transitions.

Progression: Chakrasana (P0158-chakrasana.md) — full backbend — represents the maximal expression of the anterior body opening initiated in Hastauttanasana.
