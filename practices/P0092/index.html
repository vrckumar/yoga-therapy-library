Trataka

Trataka (Concentrated Gazing Practice)

Trāṭaka (Sanskrit) — to gaze steadily, from trāṭa (looking intently). Trataka is both the sixth of the classical Shatkarmas (purificatory practices) and an independent therapeutic and concentration practice. In SVYASA's IAYT protocols, it is prescribed as Jyoti Trataka — gazing at a candle flame — performed in three progressive stages: effortless gazing (Stage I), intensive focusing on the tip of the wick (Stage II), and de-focusing with expanded awareness (Stage III). The practice simultaneously rehabilitates the extraocular muscles, trains the optic nerve, and cultivates the one-pointed concentration (dharana) that is the first stage of meditation.

1. Technique

Preparations:

  • Obtain a candle, stand, and matches. Wash the eyes with cool, clean water before beginning.
  • When practising in a group, sit in a circle at 1.5–2 metres from the candle; maximum 10 participants per group; candle at eye level.
  • Sit in any comfortable meditative posture (or chair if necessary).
  • Remove glasses and wristwatches.
  • Spine, neck, and head aligned. Shoulders relaxed.
  • Close the eyes; adopt Namaskara Mudra. Maintain a smile throughout.
  • Begin with the opening prayer (Om sahanāvavatu... — the teacher-student protection prayer).
  • Perform preparatory eye exercises (vertical, horizontal, diagonal, and rotational eyeball movements — 10 rounds each, with palming between each exercise) before Trataka proper.

Stage I — Effortless Gazing (Dharana):

  • Open the eyes gently; look first at the floor, then shift the gaze to the base of the candle stand, up to the candle, then slowly to the flame.
  • Gaze at the whole flame without effort. Do not blink. If tears appear, allow them to flow — this is a sign of good practice and cleanses the tear glands.
  • Gaze for approximately 30 seconds.
  • Close the eyes; palm (press-and-release palming with breath).
  • Repeat.

Stage II — Intensive Focusing (Dharana):

  • Open the eyes; shift the gaze from the floor to the flame as before.
  • Now focus on the tip of the wick — the small dark cord at the base of the flame. This is one-pointed concentration: fix attention on a single, small point.
  • Gaze for approximately 30 seconds without blinking. Use will power. Allow tears.
  • Close the eyes; apply constant-pressure palming with deep breathing.

Stage III — De-focusing (Dhyana preparation):

  • Open the eyes; gaze at the flame; then gradually widen the vision.
  • Slowly de-focus from the flame into a soft, expanded awareness. Observe all details simultaneously: the colour of the flame, its shape, the aura around the flame, the small light particles. Observe the aura expanding.
  • After one minute: focus intensely on the flame; close the eyes and retain the image at the eyebrow centre (ājñā region). When the image fades, begin palming with Bhramari: constant pressure around eyes, inhale, then hum 'Mm...' feeling resonance in the eye and head region.

Stage IV — Silence:

  • Remain in inner silence, aware of the changes taking place. Recognise that the mind has become calm, concentration has improved, and sharpness of eyesight has increased.
  • Close with the protection prayer and Namaskara.

Tips:

  • Practise in darkness, preferably in the evening.
  • The flame must be steady (no draughts).
  • Always open eyes with a few blinks; start by looking at the floor before shifting to the flame.
  • During palming, palms must not press the eyeballs — only cup the orbits.

Breathing: Slow, deep, diaphragmatic throughout all stages.

Duration/rounds: Full Trataka session (including eye exercises): 20–30 minutes.

2. Anatomical Activation

Extraocular muscles (six per eye)

All six extraocular muscles — medial rectus, lateral rectus, superior rectus, inferior rectus, superior oblique, inferior oblique — are systematically exercised in the preparatory eye movement phases (vertical, horizontal, diagonal, rotational). These muscles are the most fine-tuned proprioceptive muscles in the human body; their training directly improves the precision of gaze control, the speed of saccadic movements, and the accuracy of tracking.

Ciliary muscle and lens

The sustained focusing on a single flame at a fixed distance (approximately 1.5–2 metres) provides a sustained isometric exercise for the ciliary muscle, which controls lens accommodation. In modern life, the ciliary muscle is rarely exercised at intermediate distances; it is either relaxed for distance (screen-free environments) or over-contracted for near work (screens). Trataka provides a middle-distance sustained hold that restores ciliary muscle flexibility and lens accommodation range.

Lacrimal system

The sustained gaze without blinking, which normally triggers the blink reflex at 4–6 seconds, produces a mild hyperosmotic stress on the corneal epithelium, stimulating the lacrimal gland to produce tears. The resulting tear flow flushes the conjunctival sac, clears debris from the tear film, and exercises the nasolacrimal drainage system. This is the mechanism for the "tears washing out impurities" described in the source text.

Optic nerve and visual cortex

The sustained, high-contrast gaze at a small bright point (the candle wick) provides maximal stimulation to the foveal cones — the highest-acuity photoreceptors in the central retina. The sustained foveal stimulation trains the optic nerve transmission pathway from retina to the lateral geniculate nucleus (in the thalamus) to the primary visual cortex (V1). This pathway is the basis for the "strengthening of the optic nerve" described in IAYT literature.

3. Physiological Effects

Visual system rehabilitation — the multi-mechanism effect

Trataka acts on the visual system at four anatomically distinct levels:

1. Refractive correction mechanism: Errors of refraction (myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism) are in part maintained by habitual accommodative patterns — the ciliary muscle learns a dysfunctional resting tension. Trataka interrupts this by providing a sustained, voluntary, well-defined focal target at a consistent distance, retraining the ciliary muscle to find its natural resting length and improving the lens's range of accommodation. The source material states: "Errors of refraction get corrected. It strengthens the ability of the lens to adjust better to distances."

2. Extraocular muscle conditioning: Just as any other skeletal muscle improves its strength, endurance, and coordination with systematic exercise, the six extraocular muscles improve their precision of movement through the preparatory eye exercises. In convergence insufficiency, amblyopia, and strabismus, strengthening the weaker extraocular muscles through targeted gaze exercises is a standard therapeutic approach. Trataka's preparatory exercises are a yoga-based implementation of this principle.

3. Neural attention pathway strengthening: The sustained focused gaze — particularly Stage II's one-pointed concentration on the flame tip — activates the anterior cingulate cortex and the prefrontal cortex's attentional executive function. Maintaining the gaze without blink or diversion for 30 seconds requires suppression of the automatic saccadic system (which ordinarily moves the eyes every 200–500 ms). This suppression is an act of will that strengthens prefrontal inhibitory control — the same neural circuit that governs sustained attention, impulse control, and working memory. The source material notes: "Develops intense concentration and improves memory."

4. Autonomic nervous system regulation: The parasympathetic division of the autonomic nervous system governs the pupil constrictor and the ciliary muscle. The sustained, relaxed, non-blinking gaze at the flame — combined with the slow breathing and meditative posture — produces a progressive parasympathetic dominance as the session progresses. This is the mechanism for the "balances the nervous system, relieving nervous tension, anxiety, depression and insomnia" benefit in the source material.

The palming mechanism — active ocular recovery

The palming technique (pressing the warmed palms around the orbits without touching the eyeballs, creating complete darkness) provides active recovery between gazing periods. The warmth increases local vasodilation around the orbit; the complete darkness allows the photoreceptors to regenerate their bleached rhodopsin (in the rods) and iodopsin (in the cones); the gentle pressure regulates intraocular pressure. The synergy of warmth, darkness, and pressure constitutes a powerful ocular recovery stimulus between concentration phases.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — extraocular muscle exercise; ciliary muscle conditioning; lacrimal system activation; optic nerve stimulation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — in the de-focusing stage (Stage III), the visual prana (chakshu prana) is trained to move from narrow contracted focus (dharana) to expansive open awareness; this is the pranic equivalent of moving from object-bound attention to field awareness.

Tertiary: Manomaya/Vijnanamaya Kosha — Trataka is the classical practice for Dharana (concentration — the sixth limb of Ashtanga Yoga) and is explicitly described by Patanjali as a preparation for Dhyana (meditation). The ability to hold attention on a single point for 30 seconds, then 60 seconds, then progressively longer, is the building block of all deeper meditative practices. "An excellent preparation for meditation" (source material).

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Refractive errors of vision Ciliary muscle re-education; accommodation range improvement; optic pathway conditioning
Neurological conditions — concentration (A010) Prefrontal attentional training; anterior cingulate cortex activation; sustained focus capacity
Psychological conditions — anxiety, insomnia (A006) Parasympathetic activation; nervous system balancing; pre-sleep practice
Depression Voluntary attentional engagement interrupts depressive rumination; prefrontal engagement activates dopaminergic reward circuits
Meditation preparation The direct prerequisite practice for all deeper IAYT meditation techniques

6. Contraindications

  • Epilepsy: flickering candle flames may trigger photosensitive epilepsy; use a steady object (dot on wall) instead of a candle flame
  • Tension headache: Trataka may worsen tension headache through sustained visual effort; avoid during active headache episodes; wait for resolution
  • Insomnia (sensitive individuals): evening Trataka may over-stimulate; practise at least one hour before sleep and observe the individual response
  • Glaucoma / high intraocular pressure: the palming pressure technique should be modified (no direct orbital pressure); the gazing itself is acceptable

7. Variations & Progressions

Bindu Trataka (point gazing): Gaze at a black dot on a white wall instead of a flame. Eliminates the photostimulation variable; useful for photosensitive individuals or where candlelight is impractical. Provides all the muscle and attentional training without the flame-related benefits.

Standard Jyoti Trataka (as described): Candle flame at eye level, 1.5–2 metres distance. Three stages. 20–30 minutes total.

Akash Trataka (sky gazing): Advanced practice — gaze at an open blue sky (not the sun) in the early morning. The uniformly luminous field trains the de-focused expansive awareness of Stage III without an object.

Progression to meditation: Trataka is the natural progression point toward formal meditation — as the candle image stabilises behind closed eyes (the after-image of Stage III), the practitioner transitions directly into Om Meditation (P0119) or Cyclic Meditation (P0121), using the concentrated mind as the meditation foundation.
