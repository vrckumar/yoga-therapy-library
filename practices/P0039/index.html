Padasanchalana

Padasanchalana (Cycling Movement)

Supine leg cycling — lying on the back and moving the legs in a cycling (pedalling) motion. A non-weight-bearing dynamic exercise that simultaneously mobilises the hip and knee joints through a functional range, activates the abdominal muscles, and improves lower limb circulation.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine Posture.

Practice:

  1. Lie flat on the back. Bend both knees toward the chest.
  2. Begin cycling motion with both legs — as if pedalling a bicycle: right leg extends forward-downward while left knee draws to chest; then left leg extends while right knee draws — creating a smooth alternating pedalling cycle.
  3. The extended leg should descend to approximately 30–45° from the floor (not touching the floor).
  4. Perform 10–20 cycles forward (as if pedalling forward).
  5. Reverse direction: 10–20 cycles backward.
  6. Lower both legs to the floor. Relax in Savasana.

Breathing: Normal throughout. Some practitioners coordinate: exhale when both knees are bent; inhale when legs are extended.

Duration: 10–20 cycles each direction.

2. Anatomical Activation

  • Iliopsoas: hip flexion during the draw-to-chest phase
  • Rectus femoris: hip flexion and knee extension combined — dual function cycling muscle
  • Hamstrings: knee flexion during the heel-toward-buttock phase
  • Quadriceps: knee extension during the forward push phase
  • Abdominal muscles (rectus, obliques, transversus): stabilise the lumbar spine against the alternating hip flexor pull — the key core stabilisation demand

3. Physiological Effects

Combined hip and knee ROM — functional mobility pattern

Padasanchalana mimics the hip-knee movement pattern of actual cycling and walking — the most common functional lower limb movement. The cycling motion takes the hip through approximately 90° of flexion and extension and the knee through approximately 90° of flexion and extension simultaneously — in the same coordinated pattern used in ambulation. This makes it uniquely valuable as a functional rehabilitation exercise for patients who are not yet able to walk or cycle actively.

Abdominal engagement — core stabilisation

The alternating extension of each leg creates an asymmetric hip flexor pull on the lumbar spine. The abdominals must co-contract isometrically to prevent lumbar hyperextension — training the deep core stabilisation mechanism in a dynamic, movement-synchronised context. This is distinct from static abdominal exercises.

Lower limb circulation

The pumping action of the legs — repeated hip and knee flexion-extension — activates the calf and thigh venous pumps, improving venous return from the lower limbs. The non-weight-bearing position reduces venous pressure at the ankle while maintaining the pumping action.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — simultaneous hip and knee ROM; abdominal core stabilisation; lower limb venous pump; functional movement pattern maintenance.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the cycling motion harmonises prana flow through the lower limbs; in supine position, apana vayu is specifically stimulated.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Hip and Knee (A003) Combined non-weight-bearing hip and knee mobilisation
Back Pain (A004) Hip and knee ROM improvement; abdominal core engagement
Post-operative (non-ambulatory) Maintains lower limb function during bed rest; prevents DVT
Obesity (A002) Abdominal activation; lower limb exercise without joint loading
All IAYT protocols Supine hip-knee mobilisation in the preparatory series

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar disc herniation — hip flexor contraction increases intradiscal pressure; avoid in acute phase
  • Acute hip or knee joint injury — reduce range to comfortable

7. Variations & Progressions

Forward only (standard): Cycling in the forward direction simulating bicycle pedalling.

Both forward and backward: Adding reverse direction adds complexity and challenges the motor pattern.

Sequencing context: Follows P0038 (Crow Walking). Progresses to P0040 (Cycling — similar practice) and P0041 (Sit-up).
