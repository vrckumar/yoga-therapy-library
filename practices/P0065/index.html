Setubandhasana Breathing

Setubandhasana Breathing (Bridge Pose Breathing)

A supine breath-movement practice in which the hips are raised into the bridge position on inhalation and lowered back to the floor on exhalation. Coordinates spinal extension with inhalation — the rising of the pelvis and lumbar spine opens the anterior thorax and creates a reflex deepening of the inhalation. One of the most effective introductory spinal extension-breath coordination exercises for lumbar rehabilitation, as it works in the unloaded supine position and requires no upper body strength. Used in back pain, hypertension, and diabetes IAYT protocols.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine posture — lie flat on the back with legs together and hands relaxed by the sides.

Stage I — Setubandhasana Breathing (dynamic):

  1. Fold both legs at the knees, placing the heels on the floor near the buttocks (feet approximately hip-width apart or together).
  2. Inhaling: raise the buttocks and trunk upward as far as comfortable. The body from the knees to the shoulders forms a straight diagonal line (bridge). Keep the hands flat on the floor beside the body for support.
  3. Exhaling: slowly lower the buttocks and trunk back to the floor.
  4. This is one round. Repeat 5 rounds.

Stage II — Setubandhasana Stretch (extended hold with leg extension):

  1. As in Stage I, raise the hips while inhaling, but place the hands under the waist to support the body.
  2. Exhaling: slowly slide the feet forward inch by inch, extending the legs.
  3. Inhaling: slowly bend the knees again, drawing the feet back.
  4. Exhaling: lower the buttocks and trunk to the floor.
  5. This is one round. Repeat 5 rounds. Practice Stage II only when Stage I is well established.

Notes:

  • Keep the hands on the floor (Stage I) or under the waist (Stage II) throughout.
  • Feel the stretch in the lumbar region as the hips rise, and the release of the stretch as they lower.
  • Synchronise the breathing with movements precisely.

Duration/rounds: 5 rounds of Stage I per session; Stage II when ready.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Gluteus maximus: primary hip extensor; concentrically active as hips rise
  • Hamstrings: assist in hip extension; eccentrically control the descent
  • Erector spinae: extend the lumbar spine in the bridge position
  • Diaphragm: the hip elevation opens the anterior abdominal wall, allowing unrestricted diaphragmatic descent on inhalation
  • Hip flexors (iliopsoas): eccentrically lengthened as the hips rise — this is the stretch that many sedentary patients with anterior pelvic tilt most need

Spinal mechanics

  • Posterior chain activation: Bridge activates the kinetic chain from the ankles through the hamstrings, gluteals, and lower back — precisely the chain that is weakened in sedentary patients with chronic low back pain.
  • Sacroiliac joint: the bridge position applies a mild posterior shear force to the sacroiliac joint, helping to stabilise hypermobile SI joints.

3. Physiological Effects

Lumbar strengthening and mobilisation

The dynamic rise-and-lower cycle trains the gluteus maximus and erector spinae in their functional range — from supine lying (full flexion) to bridge (moderate extension). This is the foundational strength exercise for lumbar rehabilitation and is equivalent to the glute bridge exercise in physiotherapy.

Anterior chest and hip flexor opening

With the hips elevated, the anterior body from the pubis to the sternum is in a mild stretch. Over repeated practice this:

  • Stretches the hip flexors (psoas complex), which are chronically shortened in seated postures and are the primary contributor to anterior pelvic tilt and resultant lumbar hyperlordosis
  • Expands the anterior thorax, complementing the breathing function

Breath-spinal extension coupling

Synchronising inhalation with spinal extension (the rise of the bridge) exploits the natural respiratory-locomotor coupling in which extension movements are biologically linked to inhalation. This coupling, when disrupted (as in chronic low back pain), leads to breath-holding during movement — Setubandhasana Breathing specifically retrains this normal pattern.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — posterior chain strengthening, anterior body stretching, lumbar mobilisation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the bridge position with open chest is associated with prana vayu activation in the Anahata (heart centre) region.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain (A004) Primary indication; gluteal and lumbar strengthening; lumbar mobilisation in non-loaded position
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Abdominal organ stimulation; hip flexor stretching improves pelvic circulation
Hypertension (A005) Gentle supine spinal extension; calming, non-fatiguing practice
Obesity (A002) Posterior chain activation; abdominal organ stimulation
Elderly / Post-surgical rehabilitation Safe, supine position requires no balance or upper limb strength

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar disc prolapse with nerve root symptoms: assess carefully; spinal extension may centralise some disc patterns but aggravate extension-sensitive presentations
  • Recent abdominal surgery: avoid the abdominal wall tension involved in the bridge for 4–6 weeks
  • Severe cervical spondylosis: the bridge position places mild pressure through the upper back and neck; use neck padding if required
  • Knee ligament injury: folding the knees to the foot-near-buttocks position may be painful; increase the distance of the feet from the buttocks

7. Variations & Progressions

Supported bridge (static): After raising the hips, support the lower back with the hands and hold the bridge position for 30–60 seconds with normal breathing — the static version of Setubandhasana (Stage II). Increases the therapeutic intensity for hip flexor stretching.

Progression: Bhujangasana Breathing (P0066) follows in the prone position, extending the same inhalation-extension principle to a full spinal extension rather than just hip extension.
