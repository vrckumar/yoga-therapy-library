Patella Movement and Kneecap Tightening

A combined practice integrating passive patella mobilisation (P0006) with active VMO/quadriceps contraction (P0008) in sequence. The combination is clinically significant: passive mobilisation loosens the retinaculum and joint, then active tightening sets the patella in its corrected tracking position, reinforcing the mechanical correction.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Seated (Dandasana) or Supine. One leg extended, quadriceps relaxed.

Practice — Phase 1 (Passive Mobilisation):

  1. Perform patella mobilisation as in P0006 — superior, inferior, medial, lateral gliding, 5 rounds each direction.
  2. Complete with a final medial glide (to restore neutral or slightly medial tracking against habitual lateral tightness).

Practice — Phase 2 (Active Tightening): 3. Immediately after mobilisation, press the back of the knee down firmly into the floor. 4. Tighten the quadriceps by pulling the kneecap upward (superiorly) through voluntary quadriceps contraction — the patella visibly moves 1–2 cm upward. 5. Hold the contraction for 5 seconds. Release completely. 6. Repeat 5–10 times. 7. Repeat the full sequence (mobilisation then tightening) on the other knee.

Breathing: Normal during mobilisation; exhale during quadriceps contraction, inhale on release.

2. Anatomical Activation

Phase 1 (passive)

Same as P0006 — patellofemoral joint, retinaculum, Hoffa's fat pad.

Phase 2 (active)

  • Quadriceps femoris (all four heads): isometric contraction pulling the patella superiorly
  • VMO (vastus medialis obliquus) — specifically: the most distal and medially oriented fibres of vastus medialis that provide the medial stabilising force on the patella; the contraction emphasised in the tightening exercise

3. Physiological Effects

The mobilise-then-activate sequence — clinical rationale

Passive mobilisation increases patellar mobility within the groove. Active contraction immediately after mobilisation trains the quadriceps to move the now-freed patella in its corrected trajectory. This two-phase approach mirrors the clinical physiotherapy protocol for patellofemoral rehabilitation: mobilise the restriction, then strengthen the motor control.

VMO activation — the medial patellar stabiliser

The VMO is the only quadriceps component with a significant medial pull on the patella. It fires last in the quadriceps activation sequence and is the first component to atrophy in knee pain conditions. The tightening exercise — pressing the knee down and pulling the patella up — specifically emphasises VMO activation through the terminal extension mechanism.

See P0008 for the full physiological explanation of VMO-specific effects.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — combined retinacular mobilisation and VMO neuromuscular reactivation; patellofemoral joint preparation for weight-bearing.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome Combined mobilisation + VMO strengthening — the most complete conservative approach
Arthritis — Knee (A003) Synovial fluid distribution + quadriceps activation before weight-bearing
Post-knee surgery rehabilitation Patellar mobilisation prevents post-surgical adhesions; VMO reactivation is standard early post-op goal
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) Comprehensive kneecap preparation — when both P0006 and P0008 are not performed separately

6. Contraindications

  • Acute knee injury with effusion — avoid
  • Acute prepatellar bursitis — avoid pressure on patella

7. Variations & Progressions

Sequential (standard): Mobilisation then tightening as described.

Simultaneous (advanced): While holding a gentle medial patellar glide, contract the VMO — combines the mechanical correction and motor retraining simultaneously.

Sequencing context: The combined form is used when protocol time is limited. When time permits, P0006 and P0008 are performed separately for greater specificity.
