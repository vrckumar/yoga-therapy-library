Parivritta Trikonasana

Parivritta Trikonasana (Revolved Triangle Pose)

Cross-reference / alternative transliteration: This entry uses the spelling Parivritta Trikonasana. The same pose is also rendered as Parivrtta Trikonasana (P0091) — a slight variant in romanisation of the Sanskrit parivrtta (परिवृत्त, meaning "revolved" or "turned around"). Both IDs refer to the same pose. See P0091 for the primary technical description. This entry provides supplementary cross-reference and notes for the alternate spelling.

1. Pose Identity

Pari = around, vritta/vṛtta = turned/revolved, trikona = triangle, asana = pose.

Parivritta Trikonasana is the revolved variant of Trikonasana — distinguished from the straightforward (non-revolved) Trikonasana by the rotation of the trunk. While in Utthita Trikonasana the chest opens in the same direction as the front leg, in Parivritta Trikonasana the trunk rotates so the opposite hand reaches toward the front foot, creating a full spinal rotation.

2. Technique (Summary)

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana.

Practice:

  1. Inhaling: spread the legs approximately one metre apart by stepping the right leg away from the left. Simultaneously raise both arms sideways to shoulder level.
  2. Exhaling: take the right hand down to the ground on the outside of the left foot (the rotation point — the right hand crosses to the opposite side), while the left arm rises vertically.
  3. Turn the face upward to look at the raised (left) hand.
  4. Maintain the final posture for 1 minute with normal breathing.
  5. Return to Sthiti and repeat on the other side (left hand to the outside of the right foot).

Key alignment point: The hand goes to the outside of the opposite foot (cross-body), producing the full spinal rotation that distinguishes this pose from Utthita Trikonasana (where the hand reaches toward the same-side foot).

3. Primary Benefits

  • Gives full rotational movement to the thoracic and lumbar spine
  • Improves kidney function through the deep lateral and rotational compression-release cycle
  • Strengthens the thigh and hip musculature (particularly the quadriceps and hip abductors of the front leg)
  • Stimulates the abdominal organs through the rotational compression

4. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Abdominal organ compression and rotation; pancreatic and hepatic stimulation
Back Pain (A004) Spinal rotation mobility; strengthens lateral stabilisers
Obesity (A002) Lateral and rotational organ stimulation
Kidney/renal health Rotational compression of the renal region

5. Contraindications

  • Spinal problems (disc prolapse, acute lumbar strain) — the rotational load may aggravate
  • Heart conditions and severe hypertension — avoid prolonged holds
  • Dizziness or vertigo during the rotation phase

6. Transliteration Note

The Sanskrit root parivrtta (परिवृत्त) is romanised variously in different SVYASA-affiliated texts:

  • Parivrtta — closer to the IAST standard
  • Parivritta — common simplified romanisation in older PGDYTP materials

Both refer to the same word and the same pose. In the SVYASA database, P0091 uses Parivrtta and P0112 uses Parivritta — these are identical practices.
