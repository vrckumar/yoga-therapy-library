Knee Bending

Knee Bending (Janu Naman)

Active and passive knee flexion-extension through full available range — the tibiofemoral joint counterpart to the patellofemoral work in P0006–P0008. Systematically distributes synovial fluid across the medial and lateral compartments of the knee and maintains full ROM in the tibiofemoral articulation.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Standing (Tadasana) — most common form. Or Supine.

Practice — Standing form:

  1. Stand in Tadasana. Hold a support (wall or chair) if needed for balance.
  2. Bend the right knee, bringing the heel toward the buttock — full knee flexion as far as comfortable.
  3. Extend the knee fully, returning to standing.
  4. Repeat 10 times on the right leg, then 10 times on the left.

Practice — Supine form:

  1. Lie supine. Bend the right knee, sliding the foot along the floor toward the buttock (heel to buttock).
  2. Extend the right leg fully back to the floor.
  3. Repeat 10 times on right, then 10 on left.

Breathing: Exhale on flexion; inhale on extension.

Duration: 10 repetitions per leg.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Hamstrings (biceps femoris, semitendinosus, semimembranosus): concentrically active during knee flexion
  • Quadriceps femoris: concentrically active during knee extension; eccentrically controlling flexion
  • Gastrocnemius: assists knee flexion (proximal attachment on femoral condyles)
  • Popliteus: unlocks the extended knee at the start of flexion

Joint surfaces mobilised

  • Medial tibiofemoral compartment: articular cartilage of medial femoral condyle on medial tibial plateau
  • Lateral tibiofemoral compartment: lateral femoral condyle on lateral tibial plateau
  • Menisci: the medial and lateral menisci translate posteriorly during flexion — moved through their gliding range

3. Physiological Effects

Tibiofemoral synovial fluid distribution

The knee joint is the largest synovial joint in the body. Its articular surfaces — the two femoral condyles and the two tibial plateaus — cover a large area, and synovial fluid must be circulated across this entire surface for adequate cartilage nutrition. Full knee flexion-extension distributes fluid across the posterior aspects of the femoral condyles (which bear load in mid-to-deep flexion) and across the posterior menisci. Static or limited-range movement leaves these areas nutritionally deprived.

Meniscal conditioning

The menisci are fibrocartilaginous shock absorbers that improve congruency and distribute load across the tibiofemoral joint. They translate anteriorly during extension and posteriorly during flexion. Regular full-range flexion-extension maintains the mobility of these translation movements — reduced meniscal mobility contributes to posterior knee stiffness and increases the risk of meniscal tears with sudden movements.

Hamstring and quadriceps coordination

The cyclic contraction of hamstrings in flexion and quadriceps in extension trains the reciprocal inhibition pattern of the knee — when one group contracts, the other relaxes. This coordination is impaired in knee pain conditions and is fundamental to smooth, controlled ambulation.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — tibiofemoral joint mobilisation; meniscal conditioning; hamstring-quadriceps coordination; synovial fluid distribution in the largest joint of the body.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the knee is the functional midpoint of the lower limb channel; full ROM mobilisation maintains the prana flow through this junction.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Knee (A003) Synovial fluid distribution; ROM maintenance in tibiofemoral compartments
Knee Osteoarthritis Non-weight-bearing or low-load ROM maintenance; delays progression of stiffness
Post-knee surgery ROM restoration — standard goal after meniscectomy, ACL repair, total knee replacement
Back Pain (A004) Knee mobility affects lumbar mechanics during sitting and rising from floor
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) Knee warming before standing postures, seated postures, and supine practices

6. Contraindications

  • Acute knee injury with haemarthrosis — avoid forced flexion until effusion resolves; gentle ROM within pain-free range is acceptable
  • Total knee replacement (acute phase) — follows specific ROM protocol; usually begins within days under physiotherapy guidance
  • Posterior cruciate ligament tear — avoid deep flexion which loads the PCL

7. Variations & Progressions

Standing (standard): As described — combines balance challenge with ROM.

Supine (modified): For those who cannot stand safely; eliminates balance challenge.

Supported flexion (modified): Sitting in a chair, let gravity increase knee flexion — useful for stiff knees.

Heel-to-buttock measure: Tracking distance from heel to buttock as a ROM measurement tool.

Sequencing context: Follows kneecap series (P0006–P0008). Progresses to P0010 (Knee Rotation) then P0011 (combined) and P0012 (vertical stretch).
