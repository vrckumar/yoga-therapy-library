Full Butterfly

Full Butterfly (Purna Titali Asana / Baddha Konasana — Dynamic)

Bilateral hip external rotation mobilisation — both knees bent with soles of feet together, knees moving up and down rhythmically. The dynamic loosening version of Baddha Konasana. Provides deep groin and inner thigh mobilisation bilaterally.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Dandasana.

Practice:

  1. Sit in Dandasana. Bend both knees and bring the soles of the feet together as close to the groin as comfortable.
  2. Hold the feet with both hands.
  3. Gently flap both knees up and down simultaneously — like butterfly wings. Do not force.
  4. Perform 20–30 gentle flaps.
  5. Then hold the knees close to the floor for 5–10 breaths — applying gentle downward pressure with the elbows on the inner thighs (or hands on knees).
  6. Release and extend the legs.

Breathing: Normal during flapping; normal during the sustained hold.

Duration: 20–30 flaps; 5–10 breath hold.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary structures

  • Bilateral hip external rotators: all the external rotators symmetrically elongated
  • Adductor group (all five adductors): bilateral simultaneous stretch — maximum inner thigh mobilisation
  • Gracilis: hip adduction and knee flexion — a secondary target in this position
  • Hip joint capsule (inferior): bilaterally elongated

The symmetrical bilateral position of Full Butterfly provides a deeper and more complete adductor stretch than unilateral Half Butterfly because both groins are simultaneously opened — the pelvic floor cannot compensate for one side by shifting.

3. Physiological Effects

Bilateral adductor mobilisation — the defining effect

The adductors are the most commonly shortened muscles in individuals who sit for prolonged periods (the seated position places the adductors in an intermediate shortened position). In Baddha Konasana position with both knees open, the adductors are placed in maximum elongation — simultaneously stretching all five adductors (adductor longus, brevis, magnus, pectineus, and gracilis).

Pelvic floor mobilisation

The pelvic floor muscles are gently stretched in the Baddha Konasana position — the levator ani and coccygeus are elongated as the hips externally rotate and abduct. This pelvic floor stretch is relevant to pelvic floor dysfunction, dysmenorrhea, and preparation for childbirth.

Inner thigh circulation

The Baddha Konasana position compresses the femoral triangle (anterior hip region) and then releases it with each flap — a gentle pumping action that improves circulation to the inner thigh and inguinal region.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — bilateral adductor and hip external rotator elongation; pelvic floor mobilisation; hip joint bilateral external rotation ROM.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — bilateral hip opening activates the svadhisthana chakra region; pelvic floor mobilisation is directly associated with apana vayu.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Hip (A003) Bilateral hip external rotation ROM maintenance
Back Pain (A004) Adductor and hip rotator flexibility reduces lumbar-pelvic tension
Dysmenorrhea Pelvic floor relaxation; inner thigh release; improved pelvic circulation
Preparation for Padmasana The essential preparation exercise for lotus position
All IAYT protocols Standard hip opening before supine practices and pranayama

6. Contraindications

  • Acute adductor strain — bilateral stretch of acutely injured adductors; reduce range
  • Symphysis pubis dysfunction — the bilateral abduction position may aggravate SI joint or pubic symphysis instability; use Half Butterfly (unilateral) instead

7. Variations & Progressions

Dynamic (flapping) → Static hold: Begin with gentle flapping; progress to sustained hold as range improves.

Forward fold (Baddha Konasana progression): Add a forward fold from the Full Butterfly position — deepens the adductor and groin stretch.

Sequencing context: Follows P0046 (Half Butterfly). Progresses to P0048 (Hip Stretch) and the lumbar stretching series.
