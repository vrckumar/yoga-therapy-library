DRT — Deep Relaxation Technique

The Deep Relaxation Technique is the most comprehensive and multi-layered of the three IAYT relaxation practices (IRT, QRT, DRT). A seven-phase guided body scan, DRT moves sequentially from the peripheral physical body through the energy body, the mental body, and ultimately into expanded states of awareness — closing every IAYT session with a complete physiological, psychological, and spiritual reintegration.

Unlike IRT (which works through contrast) and QRT (which works through breath-synchronised interoceptive awareness), DRT works through progressive parasympathetic induction — a systematic, region-by-region relaxation of the entire body, combined with AUM-component chanting that uses sound vibration to address successively deeper layers of the self (koshas).

Duration: 7 minutes. Position: Savasana. Closes every IAYT therapeutic session.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Lie in Savasana — supine, symmetrically arranged. Arms away from the body at 30–45 degrees, palms facing upward (the supine position of complete receptivity). Feet naturally fall apart. Eyes closed. Face and jaw completely relaxed.

Phase 1 — Lower body relaxation with A-kara (Annamaya Kosha)

Direct awareness progressively through the lower body, pausing at each region to consciously release tension:

Feet and toes: Bring awareness to both feet simultaneously. Feel the weight of the feet against the floor. Allow the toes to uncurl. Feel the ankles release. Take a slow breath in — and on the exhalation, chant A-kara internally (or gently aloud): 'AAA' — a low, open, reverberant vowel. Feel the vibration in the feet and lower legs as you chant.

Calves: Awareness moves upward to the calves. Feel the gastrocnemius and soleus muscles release against the mat. On the next exhalation — 'AAA'. Feel the vibration move upward through the calves.

Knees: The kneecap areas. Let the knees be heavy, sinking. 'AAA'.

Thighs: The entire thigh — quadriceps, hamstrings, inner thighs. Completely heavy and still. 'AAA'.

Hips and buttocks: The entire pelvic girdle, the gluteal muscles, the hip joints. 'AAA'.

After the lower body is relaxed — feel the entire region from toes to hips as a unified, heavy, warm, released mass. One final 'AAA' that resonates through the entire lower body.

Phase 2 — Middle body relaxation with U-kara (Pranamaya Kosha)

Abdomen: Awareness enters the abdominal region. Feel the natural rise and fall of the abdomen with each breath. Do not control the breath — simply allow it. On the exhalation — 'UUU' (U-kara — 'OOO' sound). Feel the vibration now in the chest and middle torso.

Lower back and lumbar spine: Feel the lumbar region release against the mat. The natural lumbar curve may increase slightly as all muscles around the spine let go. 'UUU'.

Upper back and thoracic spine: The entire back of the ribcage heavy against the floor. 'UUU'.

Chest: Feel the heart beating in the chest — a gentle, slow, even rhythm. The ribcage is heavy and open. 'UUU'.

Arms and hands: Awareness moves along both arms simultaneously — upper arms, elbows, forearms, wrists, palms, fingers. Each finger heavy and still. Palms completely open, facing the ceiling. 'UUU' — the vibration moves through the arms.

Shoulders and base of neck: The shoulder joints release. The muscles of the upper trapezius and the cervical base release. 'UUU'.

After the middle body is relaxed — the region from abdomen to shoulders is unified in heaviness and stillness. Final 'UUU' resonating through the entire middle body.

Phase 3 — Head relaxation with M-kara (Manomaya Kosha)

Jaw: The masseter muscles — the most chronically tense muscles in the stressed body — are released. Allow the jaw to sag. The teeth part slightly inside the closed mouth. 'MMM' (M-kara — a nasal humming). Feel the vibration in the jaw, teeth, and skull.

Tongue: Release the tongue from the roof of the mouth. Allow it to rest heavily on the floor of the mouth. 'MMM'.

Palate and throat: The soft palate releases, the pharynx relaxes, the larynx drops. 'MMM'.

Lips: The lips are gently closed but completely slack — no muscular tension. 'MMM'.

Nose: The nostrils relax. The breath flows in and out without effort. 'MMM'.

Cheeks: The cheek muscles release. The face becomes heavy and smooth. 'MMM'.

Eyes: The eyes are closed and completely still — no flicker of movement behind the eyelids. The eyeballs rest in their sockets. The orbital muscles release. 'MMM'.

Forehead: The frontalis muscle — often chronically contracted in anxious or concentrating individuals — becomes smooth and heavy. The furrowed brow releases. 'MMM'.

Temples: The temporal muscles (the chewing muscles on the sides of the skull) release. 'MMM'.

Crown of head: Awareness reaches the top of the skull — the entire cranial vault is relaxed. 'MMM' — the vibration fills the entire head.

Phase 4 — Whole body integration with AUM

The practitioner becomes aware of the whole body lying on the ground — toes to crown, front to back, left to right — as a single, unified, deeply relaxed entity.

Chant AUM in a single complete breath: Inhale deeply. On the exhalation, chant the full AUM — 'A-U-M' — in a single sustained breath. The 'A' resonates in the lower body, the 'U' rises through the chest and middle body, the 'M' fills the head and then: silence. The silence after AUM is held — a natural Kumbhaka of the sound, the vibration still reverberating through the body.

Feel the resonance of AUM throughout the entire body simultaneously — a whole-body vibrational experience. The body is a single resonating chamber.

Phase 5 — Body consciousness dissolution (Vijnanamaya Kosha)

Come out of the awareness of individual body parts. You are no longer attending to the legs, or the chest, or the head — you are aware of the whole body, from the outside, as if observing it.

Visualisation: Imagine seeing your body lying on the ground, from above or from the side. You are the observer; the body is the observed. There is a gentle distance between the awareness and the physical form — the awareness is not inside the knee or the jaw — it encompasses the whole.

This phase initiates the Vijnanamaya Kosha level — the layer of discriminative wisdom and witness consciousness (sakshi). The practitioner begins to identify not with the physical sensations but with the awareness that perceives them.

Phase 6 — Expanded awareness (Anandamaya Kosha / sky visualisation)

Release the image of the body entirely. Bring the awareness to the vast blue sky — immense, limitless, cloudless, luminous. Become the sky. There are no boundaries to awareness. There is no inside or outside. The sky does not know where it ends. Be that.

Remain here in stillness and expansion — for as long as the protocol allows (30–60 seconds in a standard 7-minute DRT; longer in extended meditation practices).

This phase corresponds to the Anandamaya Kosha — the bliss body — and to the Turiya state (the fourth state of consciousness, beyond waking, dreaming, and deep sleep). The "bliss" of this phase is the yogic ananda — not excitement or pleasure, but the quiet, unchanging joy of pure awareness.

Phase 7 — Return and emergence

The practitioner returns from expanded awareness back to the physical body and the present moment:

  1. Deep inhalation — one full breath that re-establishes the sense of the body.
  2. Chant AUM — one complete AUM, reinstating the whole-body vibrational awareness.
  3. Gently move the body — fingers, then toes, then wrists, then ankles — slowly reactivating proprioceptive awareness of the physical form.
  4. Turn to the right side — roll gently onto the right side (always the right side, to allow the heart to be uppermost as the practitioner rises). Rest for a moment.
  5. Rise slowly — press up to sitting. Avoid any sudden movement.
  6. Close with a collective AUM chant (if in a group session), or a moment of grateful silence.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary structures engaged

Autonomic nervous system — complete parasympathetic shift: DRT produces the most complete and sustained parasympathetic activation achievable through yoga practice. The seven-phase progression creates a cumulative parasympathetic effect — each phase building on the autonomic state established by the previous one.

Hypothalamus and HPA axis: The extended, deep parasympathetic state of DRT suppresses hypothalamic CRH secretion, reducing the HPA axis output (cortisol, ACTH). This is the most direct non-pharmacological method for reducing chronic cortisol elevation — a factor in virtually every lifestyle disease managed by IAYT.

Limbic system: The body scan (Phases 1–3) and the expanded awareness state (Phases 5–6) progressively reduce limbic arousal — particularly amygdala reactivity. The amygdala's threat-detection function requires attention to external stimuli and to future projection; when awareness is absorbed in present-moment internal body sensations and then expanded to boundless sky, there is no material for the amygdala to process. Limbic quietening is the basis of DRT's profound emotional effects.

Skeletal musculature — complete resting tone reduction: The region-by-region body scan, with deliberate release at each location, produces a systematic reduction in resting muscle tone across the entire body. The final Phase 4 AUM chanting and Phase 5 body observation allow this reduced tone to stabilise — unlike IRT (which achieves reduction through contrast) or QRT (which achieves partial reduction through breath synchronisation), DRT achieves the deepest and most sustained reduction in resting motor tone.

Prefrontal cortex: The deliberate maintenance of relaxed, inward-directed awareness across 7 minutes strengthens the prefrontal cortex's capacity for executive regulation of the limbic system. DRT is, in part, a training of the prefrontal cortex — its ability to hold a calm, non-reactive witnessing state in the face of physical sensation and thought.

3. Physiological Effects

Progressive parasympathetic induction through sequential body-region awareness — the primary mechanism

DRT achieves its effects through a pathway fundamentally different from IRT (contrast) and QRT (breath-synchronisation). The DRT mechanism is progressive top-down neural inhibition of motor and autonomic outflow through the combined effects of:

1. Directed somatic attention and motor neuron inhibition When the practitioner directs conscious attention to a body region with the intention of releasing tension, the frontal cortex sends inhibitory signals to the corresponding motor cortex representations. This top-down motor neuron inhibition reduces the efferent discharge to the muscles of that region — reducing muscle tone below the resting baseline. Each region of the body scan (Phases 1–3) involves this frontal-motor cortex inhibitory pathway.

Over the course of the 7-minute scan, the cumulative inhibitory effect from all body regions produces a whole-body reduction in motor neuron excitability — the deepest available state of muscular rest short of anaesthesia.

2. A-kara, U-kara, M-kara — layer-specific vibro-acoustic activation The three components of AUM are assigned to specific body regions and koshas:

  • A-kara (lower body — Annamaya Kosha): The low, open 'AAA' sound produces vibration in the thoracic and abdominal regions, with its overtones reaching the lower limbs through bone conduction. Annamaya Kosha = physical body = waking state = lower body.
  • U-kara (middle body — Pranamaya Kosha): The 'OOO' sound resonates in the chest cavity — heart, lungs, and thoracic great vessels. Pranamaya Kosha = energy body = dream state = middle body (breath and energy).
  • M-kara (head — Manomaya Kosha): The nasal 'MMM' resonates maximally in the cranial vault, sinuses, and brain. Manomaya Kosha = mental body = deep sleep state = head (mind, sense organs).

Each chanting phase vibrates the specific region being relaxed, providing vibroacoustic stimulation that simultaneously signals the associated kosha — creating a simultaneous physical, energetic, and mental relaxation of that layer.

3. The AUM silence — access to Vijnanamaya and Anandamaya The moment of silence after the full AUM chant (Phase 4) is the most important moment in DRT. In yogic physiology, the AUM has four components: A + U + M + Silence (the Turiya — the fourth). The Turiya is not a sound but the awareness that remains when all sounds end. The silence after AUM is the door to:

  • Vijnanamaya Kosha — the layer of pure knowing, witnessing awareness
  • Anandamaya Kosha — the bliss body, the most subtle layer of the individual self
  • Turiya — the state of pure consciousness beyond all three ordinary states of mind

Phases 5 and 6 are the extended exploration of these layers — made accessible by the deep physical, pranic, and mental relaxation achieved in Phases 1–4.

4. Cortisol reduction — the lasting therapeutic mechanism Studies on yoga nidra (the closest research parallel to DRT) consistently show:

  • Significant acute cortisol reduction after a single session (20–30% reduction measurable in saliva)
  • Cumulative reduction in baseline cortisol with regular practice (weeks to months)
  • Improvement in sleep architecture (increased slow-wave sleep, reduced sleep latency)
  • Reduction in anxiety scores (State-Trait Anxiety Inventory) with 8-week practice

The mechanism is the HPA axis dampening achieved through the sustained parasympathetic state — identical in principle to what occurs during DRT.

5. Immune system support Cortisol is immunosuppressive at elevated chronic levels: it reduces T-lymphocyte proliferation, reduces natural killer cell activity, and increases susceptibility to infection and malignancy. The cortisol reduction achieved through regular DRT practice has been associated with improved immune parameters — increased T-cell count, improved NK cell activity, and reduced inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6).

This is the basis of DRT's inclusion in all IAYT protocols regardless of condition — it addresses the chronic stress-immunity compromise that underlies or complicates virtually every psychosomatic disease.

4. Kosha Level

DRT is unique in the IAYT system for systematically addressing all five koshas in a single practice:

Phase 1 (lower body, A-kara) → Annamaya Kosha — physical body relaxation

Phase 2 (middle body, U-kara) → Pranamaya Kosha — pranic/energy body relaxation; breath awareness

Phase 3 (head, M-kara) → Manomaya Kosha — mental body relaxation; sense withdrawal (Pratyahara)

Phase 4 (whole body AUM) → Integration of Annamaya + Pranamaya + Manomaya — the three ordinary states of consciousness (waking, dreaming, deep sleep) integrated in one sound

Phase 5 (body observation) → Vijnanamaya Kosha — witness consciousness; discriminative wisdom; identification with the observer rather than the observed

Phase 6 (vast blue sky) → Anandamaya Kosha — bliss body; boundless awareness; Turiya state

Phase 7 (return) → Samadhipada — the return from expanded awareness back to ordinary consciousness, carrying the relaxed equanimity into daily life

No other single practice in the IAYT system works across all five koshas sequentially and systematically. This is why DRT closes every session — it integrates all the work of the session at every layer of the self.

6. Contraindications

  • No absolute contraindications. DRT is the safest and most universally applicable of all IAYT practices. It can be adapted for any physical condition.
  • Acute psychosis or mania — the expansive states of Phases 5–6 can be destabilising for patients in acute psychiatric crisis. In these cases, use only Phases 1–3 (body scan), omitting the expanded awareness phases.
  • Dissociative disorders — as with QRT, intensive body awareness can occasionally trigger dissociation in trauma survivors. In trauma-sensitive contexts, modifications (eyes open, guided partner contact, abbreviated phases) are appropriate.
  • Falling asleep: The most common practical challenge. For patients with severe insomnia or extreme fatigue, DRT often induces sleep — which is therapeutic in those contexts. For patients who need to remain awake (e.g., to continue the session), gentle background music or occasional verbal guidance cues from the therapist prevent sleep.

7. Variations & Progressions

Extended DRT (15–20 minutes): For insomnia, cancer rehabilitation, and intensive stress management protocols, DRT is extended to 15–20 minutes, with more time allocated to each phase. This extended format approaches Yoga Nidra in depth.

Yoga Nidra: The full yogic sleep practice — the advanced, longer form of DRT. Includes Sankalpa (intention setting), Rotation of consciousness through the body, Pairs of opposites, Visualization, and extended Anandamaya-level practice. DRT is the simplified, accessible IAYT form; Yoga Nidra is the complete classical form for advanced practitioners.

DRT with Sankalpa: Adding a personal healing intention (Sankalpa) at the beginning and end of DRT (stated during Phase 4 or Phase 5, when the subconscious mind is most receptive). Used in protocols for chronic conditions where psychological attitude toward healing is a significant factor.

DRT without chanting (audio recording): DRT can be effectively delivered through a recorded guide — useful for home practice between sessions. The therapist records a 7-minute DRT audio for the patient, who uses it daily between clinic sessions. Research on yoga nidra consistently shows that recorded guidance produces effects comparable to live guidance.

DRT in chair or supported position: For patients who cannot lie flat (cardiac conditions, late pregnancy, respiratory distress), DRT is performed in a reclining chair or with the upper body elevated on bolsters. The somatic effects are slightly reduced but the psychological and energetic effects are equivalent.
