Trikonasana

Trikonasana (Triangle Pose)

Trikona (Sanskrit) — triangle, three-angle. The standing posture that forms a triangle with the floor — legs as one side, the trunk-and-arm as the hypotenuse, and the extended upper arm completing the form. The most comprehensive standing posture in the IAYT repertoire for simultaneous lateral spinal, lower limb, and postural effects.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana — stand erect with feet together.

Practice:

  1. Inhale and spread the feet approximately 1 meter apart (or slightly wider than the hips — the exact distance depends on the practitioner's height; the rear foot may be slightly turned in for stability).
  2. Raise both arms horizontal, in a straight line from shoulder to fingertip, palms facing downward.
  3. Exhale and bend the trunk to the right, keeping both arms straight and in the same plane as the body (no forward lean).
  4. The right hand reaches toward or contacts the right foot (or ankle, shin — wherever the hand reaches without bending the knee or the elbow). The left arm rises in a straight line upward, perpendicular to the floor.
  5. Look upward along the left hand's fingertips. The gaze (drishti) is directed toward the upper hand.
  6. Both arms are in a single vertical line. The upper arm actively reaches toward the ceiling, creating a length between the hips.
  7. Maintain for approximately 1 minute with normal breathing.
  8. Inhale and return to upright with arms extended. Exhale, repeat on the left side.

Breathing: Exhale into the bend; breathe normally throughout the hold; inhale to return.

Duration/rounds: 1 minute each side; 1 round per side.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Quadratus lumborum (working/concave side): primary lateral flexor; concentrically contracts to hold the lateral bend
  • Obliques (concave side external, convex side internal): assist the lateral trunk flexion
  • Hamstrings (bilateral): stretched by the wide stance and trunk side-bend; the rear-leg hamstring is on a diagonal stretch
  • Hip abductors (gluteus medius and minimus): stabilise the pelvis in the wide stance position; prevent the pelvis from dropping
  • Quadriceps: isometrically contract to maintain straight knees in the wide stance
  • Gastrocnemius and soleus: stretched by the wide-stance plantar pressure pattern
  • Calf and plantar arch muscles (foot-contact side): engaged by the foot-to-floor contact; the "prevents flat foot" benefit comes from the weight-bearing load on the medial arch

Organs and structures affected

  • Liver (right bend): right-side lateral compression of the right upper abdominal quadrant
  • Spleen and pancreas (left bend): left-side lateral compression
  • Lateral thoracic lymph nodes: the lateral thoracic opening facilitates lymphatic flow along the lateral chest
  • Sciatic nerve (wide stance side): the combination of hip abduction, knee extension, and trunk lateral flexion places the sciatic nerve on a unique oblique tension

Spinal structures

Lateral flexion of the lumbar and thoracic spine, with a component of thoracic rotation (the upper arm reaching upward naturally creates mild thoracic rotation). The intervertebral foramina on the convex side are maximally widened, allowing lateral nerve root decompression.

3. Physiological Effects

Lateral spinal mobilisation with rotational component — the comprehensive mechanism

Trikonasana achieves what Ardhakati Chakrasana achieves in a simple bilateral lateral bend, but now with:

  1. Weight-bearing in a wide stance: this adds gravitational load to the hip abductors and medial arch structures, strengthening them under functional conditions
  2. Rotational component: the gaze toward the upper hand and the reaching of the upper arm toward the ceiling adds a thoracic rotation element to the pure lateral bend. This combined lateral-and-rotational loading is unique to Trikonasana and produces a more complex intervertebral disc stress than simple lateral flexion alone — comparable to the combined load experienced in many functional activities (reaching sideways for an object, turning to look while leaning)

This combined motion pattern is the mechanism of the stated "corrects curvatures of back" benefit: by loading the spine in the lateral-rotational plane that is most neglected in rehabilitation, Trikonasana addresses the functional mobility that scoliotic or kyphotic postures most restrict.

Lower limb strengthening — the weight-bearing mechanism

The wide stance places significant demands on the lower limb musculature:

  1. Hip abductors: the 1-meter stance requires sustained gluteus medius contraction to prevent Trendelenburg-pattern hip drop. This is the mechanism of "strengthens calf and thigh muscles"
  2. Medial arch loading (plantar fascia and intrinsic foot muscles): the weight-bearing through the wide stance, particularly on the lower foot as it contacts the floor during the lateral bend, provides a specific stimulus to the medial arch. This is the direct mechanism of "prevents flat foot" — the loaded arch stimulus promotes the intrinsic foot muscle activation needed to maintain the arch

This makes Trikonasana the most effective standing posture for lower limb conditioning in the IAYT system — it simultaneously loads the hip abductors, thigh musculature, and plantar arch.

Lateral organ compression — liver and spleen stimulation

The right-side lateral bend achieves a specific hepatic compression: with the wide stance allowing greater trunk travel toward the floor, and the arm-to-foot contact creating a closed-chain lateral bend, the liver receives a more complete right-side compression than in Ardhakati Chakrasana. The left-side bend equivalently stimulates the spleen and pancreatic region. The sequential bilateral practice (right then left) ensures both the hepatic glucose regulation mechanism and the pancreatic region are addressed.

Hypertension — postural and vascular mechanism

Trikonasana's inclusion in the hypertension protocol rests on several mechanisms:

  • The wide-stance weight-bearing provides a significant proprioceptive demand that, via cerebellar-vestibular pathways, induces autonomic regulation
  • The lateral thoracic opening improves baroreflex sensitivity (the ability of the cardiovascular system to rapidly modulate blood pressure)
  • The 1-minute sustained hold with normal breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system via respiratory sinus arrhythmia — the normal fluctuation in heart rate with breathing that reflects vagal tone
  • The postural challenge produces moderate physical effort without excessive cardiovascular load

Waist musculature

The sustained quadratus lumborum contraction on the working side and the passive lengthening of the opposite quadratus lumborum (which is typically asymmetrically tight in most people) provides both strengthening and lengthening of the lateral waist. The stated "strengthens waist muscles" refers to this combined bilateral loading pattern.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — lateral spinal mobilisation; lower limb strengthening; plantar arch loading; liver/spleen stimulation; waist musculature conditioning.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the extended arms in a horizontal plane open both the right and left pranic channels simultaneously; the gaze upward is associated with ajna (third eye/pituitary) activation; the postural balance demand focuses the mind on prana management.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Lateral hepatic and pancreatic stimulation; large muscle group activation improving insulin sensitivity
Back Pain (A004) Lateral-rotational spinal mobilisation; quadratus lumborum strengthening; disc decompression on convex side
Hypertension (A005) Parasympathetic activation via sustained breath-coordinated hold; baroreflex improvement
Arthritis (A003) Hip abductor strengthening; knee stability in wide stance; spinal lateral mobility
Obesity (A002) Large muscle group activation; waist conditioning; organ stimulation
Flat foot / plantar fasciitis Weight-bearing medial arch stimulus; intrinsic foot muscle activation
Scoliosis (mild functional) Specific lateral-rotational loading of restricted spinal segments

6. Contraindications

  • Recent abdominal surgery — lateral abdominal compression and wide-stance effort increase intra-abdominal pressure; avoid until healed
  • Slip disc (acute lumbar disc prolapse) — the combined lateral flexion and rotational disc loading may aggravate herniated disc material; avoid in acute phase
  • Acute sciatica — the wide stance with lateral trunk bend creates sciatic nerve tension; avoid or significantly modify
  • Severe balance disorders — the wide stance improves stability relative to single-leg balance postures, but severe balance disorders require wall support

7. Variations & Progressions

Beginner: Narrow the stance. Allow the lower hand to rest on the shin or knee rather than the foot. Reduce hold time to 30 seconds per side.

Standard (as described): 1-meter stance, hand to foot, upper arm perpendicular, gaze along upper arm, 1-minute hold.

Wall-supported: Back heel and back of the body touching a wall — this removes the balance challenge and allows focus on the lateral bend.

Progression: Parivrtta Trikonasana — the revolved triangle — adds a full spinal rotation to the lateral bend, combining the mechanisms of Trikonasana with those of a standing twist. Significantly more demanding.

Preparatory: Ardhakati Chakrasana — the simpler lateral bend without the wide stance or arm-to-foot reach.
