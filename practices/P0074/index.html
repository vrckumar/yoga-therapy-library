Cooling Pranayama — Sitali, Sitkari, and Sadanta

Sheetali / Sitali (Sanskrit) — cooling, calming; from sheeta — cool, cold. Sitkari from the sibilant hissing sound produced during inhalation. These three cooling pranayamas share a common mechanism: inhalation through the oral cavity, where the air passes over a moistened tongue surface and is significantly cooled before entering the throat and lungs. They form a functional group — therapeutic alternatives depending on the practitioner's anatomical ability to roll the tongue.

In the IAYT system, this pranayama group is the primary intervention for conditions characterised by excess heat: fever, hypertension (a Pitta excess condition in Ayurvedic terms), inflammatory arthritis, and stress-related hyperthermia. They are the direct therapeutic counterpart to Surya Anuloma Viloma.

1. Technique

Sitali Pranayama (Rolled Tongue Cooling Breath)

Prerequisites: The ability to roll the tongue into a tube shape is a genetic trait — approximately 60–70% of people can do this. Those who cannot should use Sitkari or Sadanta instead.

Starting position: Sit comfortably in any meditative posture with spine erect. Eyes may be gently closed.

Process:

  1. Open the mouth. Roll the tongue lengthways into a tube (like a straw). Protrude the tongue slightly beyond the lips.
  2. Inhale slowly and steadily through the rolled tongue — the air passes over the moist inner surface of the tongue, cooling significantly before entering the throat. A distinctly cool stream is felt in the throat and chest.
  3. At the end of inhalation, withdraw the tongue and close the mouth. Hold briefly (natural Kumbhaka — not forced).
  4. Exhale slowly through both nostrils.
  5. This completes one round.

Rounds: 9 rounds in IAYT protocols. Extend to 18–27 rounds for fever or acute hypertensive episodes.

Sitkari Pranayama (Hissing Breath)

For those who cannot roll the tongue:

Process:

  1. Fold the tip of the tongue inward and slightly upward, pressing it horizontally against the upper palate behind the teeth (not rolled).
  2. Allow the sides of the tongue to remain extended, and part the teeth slightly — creating a narrow channel through which air enters on each side of the tongue.
  3. Inhale through the sides of this opening — a sibilant 'SSSS' or hissing sound is produced. The cool air streams over the moist lateral tongue surfaces and diffuses through the oral cavity and throat.
  4. Close the mouth, exhale through the nostrils.
  5. One round.

Rounds: 9 rounds standard.

Sadanta Pranayama (Alternative Cooling Breath)

For those who cannot do either Sitali or Sitkari effectively:

Process:

  1. Keep the teeth slightly parted and the tongue flat (not curled, not rolled).
  2. Inhale slowly through the slightly parted teeth — the air cools as it passes through the narrow dental opening.
  3. Close mouth, exhale through nostrils.
  4. One round.

Sadanta produces a similar cooling effect through a simpler mechanism — the narrow passage between the teeth creates a cooler, more diffuse airstream than unrestricted oral breathing.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary structures engaged

Tongue (lingua): The primary heat exchange surface. The tongue is richly vascularised (via the lingual artery and its branches) and covered with moist mucous membrane. When cool, dry air passes over this surface during inhalation:

  • Evaporative cooling occurs — water from the tongue surface evaporates into the inspired air, absorbing heat energy from the tongue tissue and from the submucosal blood supply.
  • The cooled and humidified air then passes into the pharynx and trachea, where it continues to cool the surrounding mucosa and blood vessels.

Pharynx and larynx: The cooled air contacts the posterior pharyngeal wall, soft palate, and larynx — areas richly supplied with parasympathetic nerve endings. Cooling these structures activates cold-sensitive receptors (TRP channels — transient receptor potential channels, particularly TRPM8) that trigger parasympathetic reflexes.

Carotid sinus: The internal carotid arteries pass through the neck immediately posterior to the pharynx. Cooling of pharyngeal tissue transmits to the carotid blood, which then reaches the carotid sinus — the primary baroreceptor for blood pressure regulation. This is a direct pathway by which pharyngeal cooling can reflexively lower blood pressure.

Hypothalamus (thermoregulatory centre): The hypothalamus receives temperature-sensing input from the body's core. Repeated introduction of cool air into the core respiratory tract provides a sustained cooling signal to the hypothalamic thermostat, downregulating heat-generating sympathetic activity.

Upper respiratory mucosa and submucosal blood vessels: All three practices cool and moisten the oral and pharyngeal mucosa, reducing local inflammation and providing direct relief in upper respiratory inflammatory conditions.

3. Physiological Effects

Evaporative cooling and thermoregulation — the primary mechanism

The fundamental mechanism of all cooling pranayamas is evaporative cooling — the same principle as sweating, but applied to the respiratory tract. Each inhalation passes air over a moist, warm surface (the tongue and oral mucosa). Water evaporates from this surface into the incoming air, absorbing latent heat of vaporisation (approximately 2,260 joules per gram of water). This energy is taken from the tongue tissue and its blood supply, cooling the blood flowing through the lingual and pharyngeal vessels.

Over 9 rounds (approximately 3–4 minutes), this creates a measurable reduction in:

  • Core body temperature (reduced by 0.5–1.0°C in fever states)
  • Blood temperature in the carotid circulation (which directly cools the hypothalamus)
  • Local inflammatory temperature in any inflamed mucosal tissue

1. Fever reduction In fever, the hypothalamic thermostat has been reset upward by prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) signalling from immune cells. Cooling pranayamas provide a direct counter-signal: the cooled blood reaching the hypothalamus partially overrides the upward-set thermostat, producing a mild antipyretic effect. This is not as powerful as pharmacological fever reducers, but is useful as a complementary intervention and carries no side effects.

2. Hypertension: the carotid cooling pathway The cooling of pharyngeal and laryngeal tissues cools the blood in the carotid artery as it passes through the neck. The carotid sinus — a baroreceptor bulge at the bifurcation of the common carotid artery — detects blood pressure. A secondary effect of cooling the carotid blood is a slight increase in baroreceptor sensitivity, triggering a vagal reflex that slows the heart and dilates peripheral blood vessels.

Additionally, the parasympathetic activation from cold-receptor stimulation in the pharynx (via TRP channels) directly reduces sympathetic outflow — the mechanism most relevant to hypertension reduction. This is why cooling pranayama is a specific inclusion in the IAYT hypertension protocol alongside Bhramari and Nadi Suddhi.

3. Reduction of sympathetic arousal — the anti-stress mechanism The autonomic nervous system has strong temperature-sensation connections. Cold-receptor activation in the upper respiratory tract (a visceral sensory pathway) reliably triggers parasympathetic responses — reduced heart rate, reduced blood pressure, reduced cortisol. This is the physiological basis of the traditional teaching that cooling pranayama "cools blood and nervous system."

In the context of anger (a state of acute sympathetic arousal with raised body temperature and increased catecholamine levels), 9 rounds of Sitali can produce rapid autonomic cooling — addressing both the thermal and the neurochemical components of the agitated state.

4. Arthritis: reduction of inflammatory heat Inflammatory arthritis (both rheumatoid and osteoarthritis during flare) involves elevated prostaglandin production and increased local and systemic temperature. The systemic cooling effect of cooling pranayama — via reduced core temperature and reduced sympathetic-driven inflammatory signalling — is used in the IAYT arthritis protocol to reduce the overall inflammatory burden. This is adjunctive to the primary anti-inflammatory practices but contributes to the overall heat-reducing strategy of the protocol.

5. Reduction of hunger and thirst A lesser-known but consistently reported effect of Sitali and Sitkari is suppression of hunger and thirst. The mechanism is hypothalamic: the cooling of the oral and pharyngeal mucosa and the stimulation of cold receptors provides a signal to the lateral hypothalamus that temporarily reduces hunger drive. This is utilised in the obesity management protocol where cooling pranayama is used to reduce appetite between meals.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — the temperature regulation and anti-inflammatory effects operate at the physical body level through direct physiological mechanisms.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — in yogic physiology, these practices reduce Pitta (fire element) excess and activate Chandra (lunar, cooling, Ida Nadi) qualities. The exhalation through both nostrils after the cooling inhalation completes an energetic cycle that balances the solar-lunar pranic currents.

The PGDYTP 102 places cooling pranayama in the context of Pitta management — the Ayurvedic interpretation of inflammation, fever, anger, and hypertension as conditions of excess heat (tejas) — and uses Sitali as the primary pranayamic intervention for Pitta disorders.

6. Contraindications

  • Chronic tonsillitis or pharyngitis — repeated introduction of cool air into an inflamed throat can exacerbate the condition. Use with caution; avoid in acute flare.
  • Asthma and COPD — cold air can trigger bronchospasm in cold-sensitive asthmatic airways. Avoid during cold weather or if there is a known cold-air bronchospasm trigger. Warm the air by reducing the degree of mouth opening or avoid entirely in cold-sensitive asthmatics.
  • Extreme cold weather — practise indoors in a warm room; avoid in cold environments where the inspired air is below room temperature.
  • Low blood pressure (hypotension) — the blood pressure–reducing effect can cause faintness in patients with already low blood pressure. Avoid or use with minimal rounds only.
  • Low body temperature / Kapha constitution — constitutionally cold individuals with slow metabolism should use Surya Anuloma Viloma and Bhastrika rather than cooling practices.
  • No contraindication for the oral mechanics themselves — Sitkari and Sadanta are used by those unable to roll the tongue.

7. Variations & Progressions

Sitali — for those with tongue-rolling ability. The most effective cooling method due to the tube shape concentrating the air stream.

Sitkari — for those who cannot roll the tongue. Achieves the same therapeutic goal through a lateral-tongue mechanism.

Sadanta — for those who cannot perform either of the above. The simplest version; effective and suitable for all patients.

Khechari Mudra variant: For advanced practitioners — during Sitali inhalation, the tongue tip is folded back to touch the soft palate before closing the mouth (partial Khechari). This further concentrates cooling at the nasopharyngeal region, adjacent to the pituitary gland and hypothalamus.

With Kumbhaka: After inhalation through the rolled tongue, close the mouth and retain the breath briefly (3–5 seconds) before exhaling through the nostrils. The retained cool air maximises heat transfer from the blood to the inspired air. Used in fever management.

Extended rounds (therapeutic dose): 18–27 rounds in acute hypertensive crisis (used with supervision as an adjunct to medical management). 18 rounds for fever management. Always followed by warm water intake to replace the moisture lost through evaporative cooling.
