Side Bending

Side Bending (Parsva Naman)

Standing lateral spinal flexion — bending to the right and left with the arm raised overhead. Mobilises the lateral thoracolumbar fascia, quadratus lumborum, and intercostal muscles. The lateral-plane counterpart to forward-backward bending (P0033) and twisting (P0034), completing the three-plane spinal mobilisation.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana. Feet one meter apart. Arms at sides.

Practice:

  1. Raise the arms sideways to horizontal while inhaling.
  2. While exhaling, bend the trunk to the right until the right hand reaches toward the right heel. Bend in the same plane — do not rotate. Look at the upward-pointing left hand palm.
  3. Inhale; come up.
  4. Exhale; bend to the left in the same manner.
  5. Repeat 4–5 times to each side alternately.
  6. Relax in Tadasana.

Note (from source): Bend in the frontal plane — do not lean forward or backward. The left hand directs forward (palm facing forward) as a reference for maintaining the frontal-plane alignment.

Breathing: Exhale on each lateral bend; inhale returning to vertical.

Duration: 4–5 rounds each side (alternating).

2. Anatomical Activation

Right lateral bending

  • Right quadratus lumborum: the primary lateral flexor of the lumbar spine; connects the 12th rib, lumbar transverse processes, and iliac crest
  • Right erector spinae (iliocostalis): lateral component of the erector spinae
  • Right internal and external obliques: assist lateral flexion
  • Right intercostals: right-side rib approximation during the bend
  • Left side tissues stretched: left quadratus lumborum, left intercostals, left lateral thoracolumbar fascia, left serratus anterior

3. Physiological Effects

Quadratus lumborum mobilisation

The quadratus lumborum (QL) is the most commonly shortened muscle in low back pain and the most common site of acute "back muscle" spasm. Located deep in the posterior abdominal wall, the QL is a lateral flexor, extensor, and inspiratory accessory muscle. Lateral bending directly elongates the contralateral QL — the most effective non-invasive QL stretch available. Regular lateral bending prevents QL shortening and maintains lumbar lateral flexibility.

Intercostal and thoracic lateral mobilisation

The intercostal muscles between each rib are stretched during lateral bending. In restricted thoracic expansion (asthma, emphysema, post-rib fracture), intercostal tightness limits chest expansion. Side bending is the most effective exercise for intercostal elongation and thoracic lateral expansion.

Thoracolumbar lateral fascia elongation

The lateral thoracolumbar fascia — including the thoracodorsal fascia and the lateral fascial compartment — is elongated maximally during ipsilateral lateral flexion. This fascial component is frequently restricted in chronic back pain and contributes to the "stuck" quality of lateral movement.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — quadratus lumborum elongation; intercostal mobilisation; lateral thoracolumbar fascia conditioning; lateral spinal facet mobilisation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — lateral bending is associated with the nadis running along the lateral body; stretching the lateral chest opens the respiratory function of prana vayu.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain — Lumbar (A004) QL elongation; lateral lumbar facet mobilisation; paraspinal balance
Asthma / Respiratory Disorders Intercostal elongation; lateral thoracic expansion
Obesity (A002) Lateral abdominal and trunk mobilisation; waist stimulation
Arthritis — Thoracolumbar (A003) Lateral facet joint mobilisation
All IAYT protocols Explicitly listed in PGDYTP 101 as standard loosening exercise (Side Bending)

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar strain — lateral loading of acutely injured QL or lateral paraspinals
  • Acute rib injury — intercostal stretching may aggravate rib fracture or contusion
  • Severe lumbar scoliosis — lateral bending toward the convex side of the curve may be very restricted; gentle range only

7. Variations & Progressions

Standard (one arm raised, one down): As described.

Both arms overhead: Greater spinal elongation and lateral stretch depth.

Sequencing context: Final exercise in the trunk series (P0030–P0035). Transitions to the aerobic/dynamic group (P0036–P0041).
