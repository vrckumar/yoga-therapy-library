Dog Breathing

Dog Breathing (Shvana Svasana)

A seated (Vajrasana) vigorous panting exercise performed with the mouth open wide, tongue fully protruded, and spine slightly concave. Rapid forceful inhalation and exhalation expand and contract the abdomen vigorously, mimicking the natural panting of a dog. A form of hyperventilation that generates heat, activates the sympathetic nervous system, and vigorously exercises the respiratory muscles. Indicated primarily in asthma protocols and energising sessions. Named for the canine panting posture it replicates.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrasana — sit on the heels with the spine erect.

Practice:

  1. Place both palms flat on the floor beside (or just in front of) the knees.
  2. Make the spine slightly concave (mild extension / sway-back position) and fix the gaze straight ahead.
  3. Open the mouth wide. Push the tongue fully out of the mouth, pointing it downward toward the chin.
  4. Practice rapid, forceful inhalation and exhalation through the open mouth, expanding and contracting the abdomen vigorously with each breath. The breathing is entirely through the mouth (not the nose).
  5. Continue for 30 seconds.
  6. Close the mouth and relax in Vajrasana.

Notes:

  • The movement is predominantly abdominal — the abdomen actively pumps with each breath cycle.
  • The tongue should be pushed to its full protruded length.
  • Eyes may be wide open (as in Simha Mudra) or relaxed.

Duration: 30 seconds per round; 1 round per session.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Diaphragm: the primary driver of the rapid cyclic pressure changes; alternately descends (inhalation) and ascends (exhalation) rapidly
  • Transversus abdominis and rectus abdominis: vigorously contract on each rapid exhalation to drive the diaphragm up
  • External intercostals: active during rapid inspiratory phases
  • Tongue (genioglossus, hyoglossus): fully elongated and protruded — this stretches the tongue root, pharynx, and activates the hypoglossal nerve

Oropharyngeal effects

The wide-open mouth and protruded tongue create a fully patent upper airway. This position:

  • Stretches the soft palate and posterior pharynx
  • Reduces nasopharyngeal resistance
  • Stimulates the oropharyngeal mucosa and cranial nerves (glossopharyngeal X, hypoglossal XII)

3. Physiological Effects

Energising and heat generation

The rapid abdominal pumping produces a vigorous thermogenic effect. The metabolic rate increases sharply during the 30 seconds of practice. This is the intended effect in the energising phase of IAYT protocols — similar to Bhastrika pranayama in mechanism.

Hypercapnia management in asthma

The rapid ventilation lowers CO₂ temporarily. In asthmatic patients during stable (non-acute) periods, controlled brief hyperventilation followed by relaxation can reset the respiratory rate set-point and reduce the hypersensitivity of the respiratory centre to CO₂. Used within the specific asthma IAYT protocol as one of several techniques to recondition respiratory control.

Bronchial clearance

The vigorous oscillatory pressure changes in the thorax — similar in principle to oscillating positive expiratory pressure (OPEP) devices used in physiotherapy — help dislodge secretions from the bronchial tree.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — vigorous respiratory muscle exercise; abdominal pump; oropharyngeal stretching.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — rapid breath cycling activates all five prana vayus simultaneously; associated with strong samana and vyana activation.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Asthma / Respiratory Disorders (A007) Primary indication; bronchial secretion mobilisation; respiratory muscle conditioning; used in Asthma IAYT protocol
Fatigue / Low energy states Energising; heat-generating; sympathetic activation
Depression (A006) Sympathetic stimulation; activates and energises; counteracts hypo-arousal

6. Contraindications

Significant contraindications:

  • Epilepsy: hyperventilation lowers the seizure threshold; absolutely contraindicated
  • Hypertension (A005): vigorous hyperventilation raises blood pressure; contraindicated in uncontrolled hypertension
  • Acute asthma attack: not during acute bronchospasm; only in stable, non-acute periods
  • Heart disease (severe): contraindicated due to the sympathetic activation and blood pressure spike
  • Pregnancy: avoid due to hypocapnia risk and abdominal pressure changes
  • Vertigo: hyperventilation can precipitate vertigo episodes

7. Variations

Modified version (gentle panting): For those who cannot tolerate rapid abdominal pumping, the speed is reduced to a comfortable panting rate. Benefits are proportionally reduced but the practice remains accessible.

Relationship to Simha Mudra: Dog Breathing and Simha Mudra (P0105) share the element of tongue protrusion and wide mouth opening. In Dog Breathing, the emphasis is on vigorous breath; in Simha Mudra, the emphasis is on the exhalation-roar and facial muscle activation.
