Parivrtta Trikonasana Stretch (Extended/Dynamic Revolved Triangle Stretch)

A dynamic or extended-hold variant of Parivritta/Parivrtta Trikonasana (P0091, P0112) — the Revolved Triangle. While the static pose is held for up to 1 minute, the "stretch" variant describes the entry-and-release action performed dynamically as part of a vigorous flow protocol, or denotes a deeper final-stretch variation in which the rotational reach is extended to its maximum. Used in the PGDYTP vigorous exercise prescription for diabetes and back pain.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana.

Dynamic (stretch) version:

  1. Inhaling: step the legs apart approximately one metre. Raise both arms sideways to shoulder level.
  2. Exhaling: rotate the trunk and take the right hand to the outside of the left foot (cross-body). Left arm rises vertically. Turn the face upward.
  3. Inhaling: return to the arms-spread standing position.
  4. Exhaling: repeat on the other side — left hand to the outside of the right foot.
  5. This dynamic alternation is performed with increasing flow and rhythm — 5–10 alternating repetitions.

Extended-hold stretch version:

  1. Enter the revolved triangle as above.
  2. In the final position, actively extend the reach of the lower hand beyond the foot (or slide it further along the floor) while the upper arm stretches vertically.
  3. Maintain the position for 30–60 seconds with normal breathing.
  4. Deepen the stretch on each exhalation by rotating the trunk slightly further.

2. Primary Stretches and Activations

Rotational chain

  • Trunk rotators (internal and external obliques): the primary movers of the spinal rotation
  • Thoracic spine: the point of maximum rotation is typically at the mid-thoracic vertebrae (T6–T8)
  • Posterior hip musculature (piriformis, deep external rotators): stretched on the front-leg side
  • Hamstrings: the forward-bent, legs-apart position stretches the hamstrings bilaterally; the front-leg hamstring more than the back

Renal region stretch

The lateral-rotational position of Parivrtta Trikonasana places the renal (kidney) region on each side under alternating compression (near-side) and traction (far-side). The renal stimulation benefit noted in the source materials refers to this alternating mechanical effect on the kidney parenchyma and its fascial surrounding.

3. Physiological Effects

Spinal rotation rehabilitation

Dynamic alternating rotations (as in the stretch version) produce greater range of motion gains than static holds alone, because the oscillatory movement prevents the stretch reflex from building protective guarding. For patients with rotational stiffness (common in chronic low back pain), the dynamic alternating version is the appropriate entry point.

Lateral thoracic stretching

The combined lateral bend and rotation produces a three-dimensional stretch of the thoracic cage — particularly the intercostals and costovertebral joints. This is the mechanism for the reported respiratory benefit.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — spinal rotation; hamstring and lateral chain stretch; renal region stimulation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the lateral-rotational expansion of the thoracic cage during the held stretch activates vyana vayu (expansive vital energy in the peripheral body).

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Abdominal organ rotation; liver and pancreatic stimulation
Back Pain (A004) Rotational mobility; lateral chain flexibility
Obesity (A002) Vigorous standing dynamic practice in the obesity protocol
Kidney function (general) Alternating renal compression-traction

6. Contraindications

Same as P0091 and P0112:

  • Acute disc prolapse or nerve root compression
  • Severe hypertension (prolonged holds)
  • Recent abdominal surgery

7. Relationship to P0091 and P0112
