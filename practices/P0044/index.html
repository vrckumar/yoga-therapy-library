Alternate and Both Leg Raising

Alternate and Both Leg Raising (Uttanpadasana — Purna)

The complete leg raising sequence — alternating single-leg raises (P0043) followed by bilateral simultaneous leg raises. The bilateral form doubles the hip flexor and abdominal demand and is contraindicated in low back pain.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine Posture.

Practice — Part 1 (alternating, as P0043): 5 rounds alternating.

Practice — Part 2 (bilateral):

  1. While inhaling, raise both legs simultaneously — keeping knees extended — to 45°. Pause.
  2. Continue to 90°.
  3. While exhaling, lower both legs slowly — to 45°, pause; then to the floor.
  4. Repeat 5 times.

Note (from PGDYTP 101 source on bilateral leg raises): "As you exhale and bring down the legs, there will be a tendency for them to fall down too rapidly as they come close to the floor. So use strength and have control over the movement."

Important: Avoid this exercise if you have low back pain (as stated in PGDYTP 101 source).

Breathing: Inhale on raising; exhale on lowering.

2. Anatomical Activation

Bilateral simultaneous — additional demands

  • Both iliopsoas muscles simultaneously: bilateral psoas contraction creates a strong lumbar extension moment — the maximum lumbar challenge in the leg-raising series
  • Rectus abdominis: maximum isometric demand to prevent lumbar hyperextension
  • Transversus abdominis: the primary deep stabiliser — must activate pre-emptively before bilateral leg lowering

3. Physiological Effects

Maximum hip flexor and core demand

The bilateral leg raise provides the maximum hip flexor and abdominal demand in the supine leg series. The combined weight of both legs (approximately 32% of body weight) creates a large gravitational moment at the hip. The eccentric control during lowering — specifically cited in the source as requiring "strength and control" — is the primary strengthening stimulus.

Lumbar shear assessment

The bilateral leg raise is the exercise that most reliably identifies lumbar instability and inadequate core stabilisation — the lumbar spine will visibly hyperextend (lift off the floor) if the abdominals cannot maintain neutral spine. This makes it both a therapeutic exercise and a clinical assessment tool.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — maximum hip flexor and core strengthening; lumbar stabilisation under bilateral load.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Core Strengthening (healthy individuals) Maximum abdominal demand
Obesity (A002) Strong core activation; abdominal engagement
Post-rehabilitation (advanced) When alternating raises are mastered and back pain has resolved

6. Contraindications

  • Low back pain (explicitly stated in PGDYTP 101 source) — avoid this exercise; use alternating (P0043) instead
  • Lumbar disc herniation — bilateral hip flexion creates maximum intradiscal pressure
  • Weak core — lumbar hyperextension injury risk; master P0043 first

7. Variations & Progressions

Sequence: Always begin with alternating (P0043) before progressing to bilateral.

Sequencing context: Follows P0042–P0043. Progresses to P0045 (Side Leg Raising) and hip opening series.
