Ardha Sirsasana

Ardha Sirsasana (Half Headstand / Dolphin Pose)

Ardha (Sanskrit) — half; Sirsa — head. The forearms are placed on the floor, the interlaced hands form a cradle, and the crown of the head is placed lightly on the floor between the hands — the hips are lifted high while the legs remain on the floor. The body forms an inverted V at the hips, the weight is borne primarily on the forearms (not the head), and the spine aligns with the floor at approximately 30–45° to the vertical. Ardha Sirsasana provides the essential cerebral blood flow and organ-inversion benefits of full Sirsasana without the cervical loading, balance demand, or cardiovascular stress that make full headstand inappropriate for many patient populations.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Prone (face down) or from Vajrasana.

Practice:

  1. Come to a kneeling position. Place the forearms on the floor, interlacing the fingers firmly to form a stable basket. The elbows should be no wider than shoulder-width.
  2. Place the crown (top) of the head on the floor within the interlaced hand basket. The back of the head rests in the palms.
  3. Tuck the toes under, press through the forearms, and raise the hips upward, straightening the knees.
  4. Walk the feet in toward the head until the hips are as high as possible (ideally above the shoulders), the back is straight, and the legs are as vertical as possible while still keeping the feet on the floor.
  5. The crown of the head barely touches the floor — the primary support comes from the forearms and elbows, not the head. This is the key distinction from full Sirsasana.
  6. Hold for 30 seconds to 1 minute with slow, normal breathing.
  7. To release: bend the knees, lower the hips, return to the kneeling position. Do not raise the head abruptly; remain in a forward-fold or Sasankasana for 30 seconds before standing.

Breathing: Deep, slow breathing is encouraged; the inverted position makes exhalation easier and inhalation slightly more effortful.

Duration/rounds: 30 seconds to 2 minutes; 1 round per session; gradually increase duration.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Triceps and shoulder flexors: the forearm-supported position keeps the elbows on the floor; the triceps and anterior deltoids are moderately loaded to maintain the inverted V position
  • Serratus anterior: critical for maintaining protracted (pushed-forward) scapulae; prevents the scapulae from collapsing onto the spine (the "shrugging" error in Dolphin Pose)
  • Rhomboids and lower trapezius: balanced activation against the serratus; maintain stable scapulothoracic positioning
  • Core (transversus abdominis, multifidus): the inverted V hip position requires sustained core activity to maintain spinal alignment
  • Hamstrings and calf complex: the legs must remain as straight as possible; the hamstrings are loaded in hip-flexion/knee-extension (the same as Downward Dog)
  • Deep cervical flexors: gently active to maintain the neutral cervical position; the head should not be pressed hard into the floor

Organs and structures affected

  • Brain and cerebral circulation: with the head below the heart, cerebral arterial pressure increases — the same mechanism as Sirsasana but at reduced intensity (less inversion angle); nevertheless, significantly greater than upright standing
  • Pituitary and hypothalamus: the increased cranial blood flow particularly benefits the highly vascular pituitary gland, which requires arterial pressure-driven filtration for optimal hormone secretion
  • Lymphatic system: the elevated lower limb position (relative to the heart) assists lymphatic drainage from the legs and pelvis; gravity-assisted lymphatic return is enhanced
  • Cervical spine: in Ardha Sirsasana the crown lightly touches the floor but the primary weight is on the forearms; the cervical spine is in a gentle neutral-to-slight-flexion position — NOT in compression as in full headstand

3. Physiological Effects

Inversion benefits without cervical risk — the progressive pathway

Ardha Sirsasana occupies the critical intermediate position in the IAYT inversion progression: it provides genuine, clinically meaningful inversion benefits while eliminating the structural risks that make Sirsasana contraindicated for a large proportion of the patient population.

Cerebral blood flow: The partial inversion (hips above heart, head below heart at approximately 30–45° from horizontal) increases mean arterial pressure at the level of the brain by approximately 15–25 mmHg above standing values. This produces:

  • Improved perfusion of borderline ischaemic cerebral territories (relevant in early cerebrovascular disease)
  • Increased CSF circulation via increased choroid plexus filtration pressure
  • Stimulation of the carotid sinus baroreceptors, initiating the parasympathetic reflex that lowers blood pressure and heart rate post-posture — the mechanism underlying the mild hypertension benefit

The key safety advantage over Sirsasana: In full headstand, the cervical spine bears 20–30% of the body weight — approximately 10–20 kg sustained on a region with natural lordosis now compressed under load. In cervical spondylosis, cervical disc pathology, or osteoporosis, this load can cause significant harm. In Ardha Sirsasana, the forearms bear nearly all the load; the head contact is a gentle orientation guide, not a weight-bearing point. The cervical spine experiences no compressive loading, making the posture safe in populations where Sirsasana is absolutely contraindicated.

Pituitary-hypothalamic stimulation

The pituitary gland is the master endocrine regulator. It secretes growth hormone, TSH (thyroid stimulating), FSH, LH, prolactin, ACTH, and ADH — hormones that govern metabolic rate, reproductive function, stress response, water balance, and tissue repair. The pituitary receives its blood supply from a specialised portal system (the hypophyseal portal) that is exquisitely pressure-sensitive.

In the inverted position, cerebral blood flow increases and the pressure gradient through the hypophyseal portal vessels is enhanced. This is proposed in IAYT literature as the basis for the "stimulation of the pituitary and hypothalamus" attributed to inversion postures. While direct clinical evidence is limited, the anatomical basis is sound, and the documented improvements in endocrine function (particularly in thyroid and adrenal disorders) with regular inversion practice support this mechanism.

Upper body and shoulder strengthening

Ardha Sirsasana is an effective scapular stabiliser exercise. The serratus anterior and lower trapezius must work together to maintain the protracted, depressed shoulder blade position with the arms in this forearm-overhead loaded position. These muscles are chronically weak in postural dysfunction patients — the same population that most benefits from IAYT back pain protocols.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — partial inversion with cerebral blood flow enhancement; forearm and shoulder strengthening; cervical-safe inversion mechanics.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the inversion begins to direct prana vayu from the periphery toward the ajna (third eye) and sahasrara (crown) chakra regions; the practice bridges the physical and meditative dimensions of inversion.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Hypertension (mild, A005) Baroreceptor-mediated vagal activation; cardiovascular parasympathetic effect post-posture — safe where full inversion is contraindicated
General energy and vitality Cerebral blood flow enhancement; pituitary stimulation; upper body strengthening
Neurological conditions (A010) Cerebral perfusion; upper extremity neuromuscular training; proprioceptive challenge
Preparation for Sirsasana The forearm-balance mechanics, serratus activation, and inversion tolerance are all direct prerequisites for full headstand
Anxiety (A006) The parasympathetic rebound after the baroreceptor-mediated vagal activation produces a marked calming effect

6. Contraindications

  • Severe hypertension (uncontrolled): the inversion is contraindicated in stage 2+ hypertension; appropriate only for mild hypertension with medical supervision
  • Acute eye conditions / glaucoma: raised intraocular pressure with inversion
  • Cervical spondylosis (severe): in Ardha Sirsasana the cervical load is minimal, making it far safer than Sirsasana; however, the slight neck flexion should be avoided if cervical symptoms are severe
  • Acute sinusitis / ear infection: the head-below-heart position increases sinus and middle ear pressure

7. Variations & Progressions

Preparatory (Forearm plank): Maintain the forearm position with the body horizontal rather than inverted — this builds serratus, triceps, and core strength without any inversion.

Dolphin Push-ups: From the Ardha Sirsasana position, bend the elbows to lower the head toward the floor, then press back up — a dynamic version that builds the upper body strength required for full Sirsasana.

Standard (as described): Crown of head lightly on floor, forearms bearing primary weight, hips as high as possible. 30 seconds–2 minutes.

Progression to Sirsasana: When Ardha Sirsasana can be held for 2 minutes with no head/neck discomfort, no elevated blood pressure response, and stable forearm support, the practitioner is ready to attempt Sirsasana progression — tucking one knee to the chest, then the other, before extending legs to vertical.
