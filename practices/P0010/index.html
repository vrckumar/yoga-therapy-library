Knee Rotation

Knee Rotation (Janu Chakra)

Gentle tibial rotation performed with the knee in semi-flexion — a small-amplitude rotational movement at the tibiofemoral joint that mobilises the menisci and the collateral ligaments in their rotational plane. This is the most subtle knee mobilisation in the series and requires careful execution to avoid ligamentous stress.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Seated with one knee bent to 90° (chair or floor sitting with foot flat).

Practice:

  1. Sit with the right knee bent to approximately 90° — foot flat on the floor.
  2. Place both hands on the right knee.
  3. Gently rotate the lower leg (tibia) in a small clockwise circle — the foot traces a small circle on the floor. The rotation is slight — the knee is not designed for large rotation, especially under load.
  4. Perform 5 rounds clockwise, then 5 rounds anti-clockwise.
  5. Repeat on the left knee.

Alternative — supine form:

  1. Lie supine. Bend the knee to 90° with the foot off the floor.
  2. Gently rotate the lower leg in small circles — the foot traces a circle in the air.

Breathing: Normal throughout.

Duration: 5 rounds each direction.

2. Anatomical Activation

Structures mobilised

  • Medial and lateral menisci: the menisci rotate with the tibia — medial meniscus moves laterally on internal rotation; lateral meniscus moves medially — this rotation mobilises the menisci through their attachment points
  • Medial collateral ligament (MCL): slightly loaded during external tibial rotation
  • Lateral collateral ligament (LCL): slightly loaded during internal tibial rotation
  • Posterior capsule: mobilised through the rotational movement
  • Anterior cruciate ligament: mildly tensioned at extremes of rotation in extension

Muscles involved in tibial rotation

  • Popliteus: the primary medial tibial rotator; unlocks the knee from extension
  • Biceps femoris (short head): external tibial rotation
  • Semitendinosus and gracilis: internal tibial rotation

3. Physiological Effects

Meniscal mobilisation — the key effect

The menisci are frequently cited as a source of knee pain in osteoarthritis. Their peripheral vascularity is limited to the outer third — the inner (avascular) portions depend on synovial fluid for nutrition. Tibial rotation moves the menisci laterally and medially within the joint, stimulating synovial fluid flow to their avascular portions and maintaining meniscal tissue health.

Rotational capsular mobilisation

The posterior knee capsule and the capsular ligaments become fibrosed in chronic knee stiffness and arthritis. Rotational mobilisation stretches these capsular structures in a plane not accessed by sagittal-plane knee bending, providing a more complete joint mobilisation.

Popliteus activation

The popliteus muscle — the primary medial rotator of the tibia — is often neglected in knee rehabilitation. It is the muscle that unlocks the extended, screw-home knee before flexion begins. Weak or inhibited popliteus leads to altered knee mechanics and posterior knee pain. The gentle rotational exercise reactivates popliteus as a functional muscle.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — meniscal mobilisation; rotational capsular stretching; popliteus activation.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Knee (A003) Meniscal and posterior capsular mobilisation; synovial fluid to avascular meniscal zones
Knee Stiffness (non-acute) Rotational component accesses restriction planes not addressed by sagittal exercises
Medial Knee Pain Popliteus reactivation; medial meniscal mobilisation
Back Pain (A004) Knee mechanics affect lumbar-pelvic alignment; full-plane knee mobilisation improves gait

6. Contraindications

  • Acute ligamentous injury (MCL, LCL, ACL, PCL) — rotational loading on healing ligaments is contraindicated; avoid until healed
  • Acute meniscal tear — rotation loads the torn meniscal tissue; avoid
  • Large knee effusion — rotation may increase intra-articular pressure; wait until effusion reduces

7. Variations & Progressions

Seated at 90° (standard): As described — safest, most controlled form.

Supine (alternative): Foot off the floor — gravity-assisted but slightly less controlled.

Sequencing context: Follows P0009 (Knee Bending). Progresses to P0011 (Knee Bending and Rotation combined), then P0012 (Vertical Stretch).
