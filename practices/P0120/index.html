Meditation

Meditation (General Meditative Practice)

Meditation in the yoga therapy context refers to the broad set of inward-directed practices that progressively withdraw attention from sensory experience toward sustained inner stillness. This general entry covers the foundational principles shared across all meditative practices used in IAYT protocols, including OM Meditation, Nadanusandana, Cyclic Meditation, MSRT, and others documented separately.

In the Patanjali Ashtanga framework, meditation encompasses three ascending stages: Dharana (concentration — fixing attention on a single object), Dhyana (meditation — sustained, unbroken flow of awareness toward that object), and Samadhi (absorption — merger of the meditator with the object of meditation). IAYT practice begins at the Dharana level and progressively deepens.

1. Technique

General instruction for all meditative practices:

  1. Choose a comfortable seated posture — Vajrasana, Sukhasana, Padmasana, or a chair. Spine erect, body relaxed, eyes gently closed.
  2. Allow 2–3 rounds of deep diaphragmatic breathing to settle the body.
  3. Withdraw attention progressively from external sounds → body sensations → breath → the object of meditation (breath, sound, mantra, or inner stillness depending on the specific technique).
  4. When the mind wanders (it will), gently return attention to the chosen object without self-criticism. This returning is the practice.
  5. Duration: begin with 5 minutes; gradually extend to 10–20 minutes with regular practice.
  6. Close the practice with 3 deep breaths, gentle body awareness, and a moment of gratitude before opening the eyes.

In IAYT protocols: meditation appears as the closing practice of every session (OM Meditation or Nadanusandana, 10 minutes) and in the mid-session mind-body technique slot (Cyclic Meditation, MSRT, or VISAK, 20–30 minutes).

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary structures engaged

Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) — the executive brain region responsible for attention regulation, impulse control, and emotional modulation. Regular meditation practice measurably increases grey matter density in the PFC (documented in neuroimaging studies). This is the anatomical substrate of improved self-regulation.

Default Mode Network (DMN) — the brain network active during mind-wandering, self-referential thought, and rumination. Meditation reduces DMN hyperactivity, which is correlated with depression, anxiety, and chronic pain amplification.

Amygdala — the brain's threat-detection centre. Chronic stress enlarges the amygdala. Meditation practice reduces amygdala reactivity and, in long-term meditators, reduces amygdala volume — the neuroanatomical correlate of reduced anxiety.

Insula — interoceptive cortex; awareness of internal body states. Meditation increases insular activation, improving the mind-body awareness that underpins effective self-regulation.

Hypothalamus — mediation practice reduces hypothalamic CRH release, dampening the HPA axis and lowering cortisol.

3. Physiological Effects

The autonomic shift — the central mechanism

All meditative practices share a common physiological outcome: shift of autonomic balance from sympathetic dominance to parasympathetic tone. This occurs through multiple pathways:

  1. Slow breathing (the breath naturally slows during meditation to 4–6 cycles per minute) → vagal activation → reduced heart rate → increased HRV → parasympathetic dominance
  2. Reduced cortical arousal → reduced hypothalamic CRH → reduced pituitary ACTH → reduced adrenal cortisol
  3. Amygdala calming → reduced amygdala-driven sympathetic activation → lower adrenaline and noradrenaline

Brainwave changes

EEG studies of meditation consistently show:

  • Increased alpha waves (8–12 Hz) — associated with relaxed alertness, creative insight
  • Increased theta waves (4–8 Hz) during deep meditation — associated with the hypnagogic state between waking and sleep; profound relaxation and insight
  • Reduced beta waves (13–30 Hz) — the stress/analytical mind quietens

Cyclic Meditation specifically produces deep theta/delta EEG states equivalent to several hours of sleep, which accounts for its documented blood glucose reduction in diabetes patients.

Neuroendocrine effects

Regular meditation practice (8 weeks and beyond) measurably reduces:

  • Cortisol (primary stress hormone) → improved insulin sensitivity, reduced inflammation
  • Adrenaline and noradrenaline → lower resting heart rate and blood pressure
  • Inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) → reduced systemic inflammation

And increases:

  • DHEA (anti-aging adrenal hormone)
  • Melatonin → improved sleep quality
  • Serotonin and dopamine precursors → improved mood

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Manomaya Kosha — meditation operates directly at the mental sheath, quieting the fluctuations (vrittis) of the mind described in Patanjali's Yoga Sutras (Yogaś citta-vrtti-nirodhaḥ — Yoga is the cessation of the modifications of the mind).

Secondary: Vijnanamaya Kosha — sustained practice cultivates the witness awareness (Sakshi bhava) — the capacity to observe mental activity without being identified with it. This is the intellectual/wisdom dimension of meditation.

Deepest: Anandamaya Kosha — in moments of genuine absorption (Dhyana merging into Samadhi), the sense of separation between meditator and the object of meditation dissolves. This is the Anandamaya experience — the bliss that is intrinsic to awareness itself, independent of circumstances.

6. Contraindications

  • Acute psychotic states (schizophrenia in acute phase) — meditation may intensify dissociation; use only under clinical supervision
  • Severe PTSD — meditation can surface traumatic memories; trauma-sensitive approach required
  • No physical contraindications — meditation is universally safe when approached gradually

7. Variations & Progressions

Entry level: Body scan awareness (DRT format) — attention on body sensations. No mantra, no visualisation. Duration: 5 minutes.

Breath awareness: Follow the breath at the nostrils or abdomen without controlling it. Simpler than mantra meditation.

Mantra meditation (OM Meditation): Silent repetition of OM or Soham (I am that). Provides the mind an object to anchor attention.

Sound meditation (Nadanusandana): Listen to internal sound. Progressively inward — from external music to internal nada.

Trataka: Gaze-based Dharana — prepares concentration for deeper meditation.

Cyclic Meditation: Structured alternation of active/passive states. More accessible for restless beginners than silent sitting.

MSRT/VISAK/ANAMS: Progressive Manomaya and Vijnanamaya level techniques for deeper psychological work.

Advanced (Dhyana → Samadhi): Extended seated practice with Kumbhaka support. Requires sustained personal guidance from an experienced teacher.
