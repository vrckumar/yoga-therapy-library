Mani Bandha Sakti Vikasaka

Mani Bandha Sakti Vikasaka (Wrist Strengthening)

Mani = jewel/wrist, bandha = joint/lock, sakti = strength, vikasaka = developer. The wrist-strengthening exercise of the Sakti Vikasaka series. Mani Bandha refers to the wrist joint (the "jewel joint" — so named in the yogic anatomical tradition because of its central role in precise manual function). The exercise specifically strengthens the wrist flexors and extensors through a vigorous up-down pumping movement from the clenched-fist position.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana.

Practice:

  1. Stretch both arms straight out in front of the chest at shoulder level, keeping them parallel to the ground.
  2. Make tight fists of both hands with the palms facing downward.
  3. Now move both fists up and down vigorously from the wrists — wrist flexion on the down-stroke, wrist extension on the up-stroke. The forearms remain stationary.
  4. Repeat 10 rounds.

Notes:

  • The forearms should not move — only the wrist moves.
  • Maintain tight fists throughout.
  • Normal breathing.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles

  • Wrist flexors (flexor carpi radialis, flexor carpi ulnaris, palmaris longus): concentrically active on the down-stroke
  • Wrist extensors (extensor carpi radialis longus/brevis, extensor carpi ulnaris): concentrically active on the up-stroke
  • Grip muscles (flexor digitorum superficialis/profundus, flexor pollicis longus): isometrically active in maintaining the tight fist throughout
  • Radiocarpal joint: moved through its full flexion-extension arc

The fist position

Making a tight fist before the movement activates the finger flexors and pre-loads the forearm muscles. This increases the effective resistance against which the wrist flexors and extensors must work, amplifying the strengthening stimulus compared to a relaxed hand position.

3. Physiological Effects

Wrist joint rehabilitation

The radiocarpal joint is among the most frequently affected joints in rheumatoid arthritis and the most important for upper limb function. The vigorous wrist pumping:

  1. Strengthens the wrist flexors and extensors dynamically
  2. Distributes synovial fluid throughout the radiocarpal joint
  3. Mobilises the wrist through its full range, reducing adhesion formation
  4. Activates the proprioceptors (Ruffini endings) of the wrist joint capsule, improving joint position sense

Forearm muscle conditioning

The forearm musculature (flexor and extensor compartments) is the primary source of functional wrist power. Wrist conditioning through Mani Bandha Sakti Vikasaka directly improves activities of daily living that require wrist stability — writing, carrying, opening jars, using tools.

Carpal canal mobilisation

The rhythmic wrist flexion-extension movement pumps the carpal canal — the fibro-osseous tunnel through which the median nerve and flexor tendons pass. In subacute carpal tunnel syndrome, this gliding movement helps reduce neural and tendon oedema within the canal.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — wrist flexor and extensor strengthening; radiocarpal joint mobilisation.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Rheumatoid Arthritis / Wrist OA (A003) Primary — wrist joint strengthening and mobilisation
Carpal tunnel syndrome (subacute) Tendon gliding; wrist mobilisation
Post-Colles fracture rehabilitation Progressive wrist strengthening after immobilisation
Repetitive strain injury (RSI) Wrist flexor-extensor balance

6. Contraindications

  • Acute RA wrist flare (hot, swollen, actively inflamed): reduce to gentle range; avoid vigorous pumping
  • Acute carpal tunnel syndrome: avoid sustained wrist flexion or extension if it increases paresthesia

7. Series Context

Fourth in the Sakti Vikasaka sequence. Preceded by Kara Prista (P0139); followed by Kaphoni (P0141).
