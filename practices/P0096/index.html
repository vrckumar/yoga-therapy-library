Tulasana

Tulasana (Scale Pose / Balance Pose)

Tula (Sanskrit) = balance, scales. The Scale Pose — a seated arm balance in which the entire body is lifted off the floor by pressing both palms firmly into the ground beside the hips, balancing on the hands alone. Traditionally performed from Padmasana (lotus), but accessible in a cross-legged seated position for those who have not yet mastered lotus. Builds considerable wrist, forearm, and core strength; demands deep hip compression in the leg-crossing position and full scapular depression. One of the more demanding seated balances in the IAYT sequence.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Padmasana (Lotus Pose) or comfortable cross-legged seated position (Sukhasana).

Practice:

  1. Sit in Padmasana or cross-legged with the spine erect and the seat grounded.
  2. Place both palms flat on the floor beside the hips (directly beside the outer thighs), fingers pointing forward or slightly outward.
  3. Exhale. Press the palms firmly into the floor, depress the scapulae (push the shoulder blades down away from the ears), and straighten the arms fully.
  4. Lift the entire body off the floor — buttocks, thighs, and crossed feet rise. The body is balanced only on the two hands. Keep the legs in their crossed position.
  5. Hold for 10–30 seconds with normal breathing.
  6. Lower back to the floor and relax.

Notes:

  • The key action is scapular depression and full elbow extension — pressing the palms and floor away from each other rather than pulling the arms up.
  • Gaze forward at a fixed point for balance.
  • The legs remain in the original cross-legged or lotus position throughout.
  • Begin with a brief hold (5 seconds) and gradually increase duration.

Duration: 10–30 seconds; 2–3 attempts per session.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Triceps brachii: must be fully contracted to lock the elbows in extension; the primary limiter for beginners
  • Serratus anterior: critical for scapular protraction and depression during arm balance; weakness in this muscle is a key reason students cannot lift
  • Lower trapezius: depresses the scapulae and prevents shoulder elevation
  • Wrist flexors and intrinsic hand muscles: stabilise the wrist joint under load; full body weight passes through the wrist
  • Core musculature (rectus abdominis, transversus abdominis, hip flexors): hold the leg cross in position and prevent the legs from swinging
  • Hip external rotators (piriformis, obturator group): in Padmasana, maintain the lotus position while the legs are lifted

Wrist loading

The wrist receives the full body weight in slight extension (the natural wrist position when palms are flat on the floor). The carpal tunnel and distal radioulnar joints must be healthy for safe practice.

3. Physiological Effects

Upper body and core strength

Tulasana is a straightforward strength-building practice. The lift requires:

  • Sufficient triceps strength to lock the elbows against body weight
  • Sufficient serratus/lower trapezius to generate scapular depression
  • Sufficient core strength to maintain the leg position and prevent trunk collapse

For IAYT patients who are sedentary or deconditioned, working toward Tulasana over weeks provides a measurable upper body and core strength goal — a motivating progressive challenge.

Wrist conditioning

The sustained wrist-loaded position improves wrist extensor endurance and prepares the joints for other weight-bearing practices such as Bakasana (crow pose) or advanced vinyasa transitions.

Concentration and focus

Arm balances require undivided drishti (focal gaze) and proprioceptive concentration. Tulasana, despite being brief, trains the quality of focused attention that is the gateway to dharana (concentration) practice.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — upper body and wrist strength; core stabilisation.

Secondary: Manomaya Kosha — sustained concentration required for the balance; the pose develops one-pointed focus.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Obesity (A002) Upper body and core strengthening; builds functional arm strength
General fitness Progressive upper body challenge; measurable strength goal
Concentration difficulties Brief but demanding balance pose trains attentional focus

6. Contraindications

  • Wrist injury or carpal tunnel syndrome: full body weight through the wrist is contraindicated; this is an absolute contraindication
  • Elbow hyperextension or injury: the locked-elbow position under load requires stable elbow joints
  • Shoulder instability: significant shoulder pathology (rotator cuff tear, labral lesion) contraindicated
  • Not appropriate for patients in acute pain phases of any IAYT protocol — introduced only after basic stability is established

7. Variations & Progressions

Preparation (progressive):

  1. Sit cross-legged and simply press the palms hard enough to hover the buttocks for 1–2 seconds.
  2. Gradually increase the hover time and height.
  3. Work toward full Padmasana lift once cross-legged lift is stable.

From Padmasana: In Padmasana, the legs are already compact and well-supported, making the full lift easier than from loose cross-legged.
