Griva Sakti Vikasaka

Griva Sakti Vikasaka (Neck Strengthening)

Griva = neck/cervical region, sakti = strength, vikasaka = developer. The neck-strengthening exercise of the Sakti Vikasaka series. The cervical spine is the most proximal joint in the Sakti Vikasaka progression (finger → wrist → elbow → shoulder → neck). Unlike the distal joint exercises which use free movement against air resistance, neck strengthening is performed using manual resistance — the practitioner's own hands provide resistance against which the neck muscles push isometrically. This self-resisted isometric approach is the safest method for cervical strengthening in arthritic conditions.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrasana or seated in a chair, spine erect.

Practice:

Anterior (Forward) — Strengthens neck flexors:

  1. Place both palms against the forehead.
  2. Press the forehead forward against the hands while the hands resist. Hold 5–10 seconds.
  3. Maintain isometric pressure — no movement occurs.
  4. Release and relax. Repeat 3–5 times.

Posterior (Backward) — Strengthens neck extensors:

  1. Place both hands at the back of the head (occiput region).
  2. Push the head backward against the hands while the hands resist.
  3. Hold 5–10 seconds isometrically. Repeat 3–5 times.

Lateral (Side) — Strengthens lateral neck flexors:

  1. Place one hand against the side of the head (temporal region).
  2. Push the head laterally against the hand resistance.
  3. Hold 5–10 seconds. Repeat both sides 3–5 times.

Rotational — Strengthens deep cervical rotators:

  1. Place one hand against the jaw/cheek region.
  2. Attempt to rotate the head toward the hand while the hand resists.
  3. Hold 5–10 seconds. Repeat both sides.

Notes:

  • No actual head movement should occur — this is pure isometric exercise.
  • Normal breathing throughout; do not hold the breath.
  • Begin with gentle pressure and progressively increase over sessions.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles

Flexors (anterior resistance):

  • Sternocleidomastoid (SCM): the primary cervical flexor and rotator; often weak in cervical spondylosis
  • Longus colli and longus capitis (deep cervical flexors): the deep stabilisers most critical for cervical spine health; these are specifically strengthened by isometric anterior resistance

Extensors (posterior resistance):

  • Suboccipital muscles (rectus capitis posterior, oblique capitis): the deep cervical extensors; often short and hypertonic in chronic desk posture, but need strengthening in their mid-range
  • Splenius capitis and splenius cervicis, semispinalis: the large posterior cervical extensors
  • Upper trapezius: participates in posterior resistance; already overactive in most patients — this exercise trains it in conjunction with the deeper muscles rather than in isolation

Lateral flexors:

  • Scalenes (anterior, middle, posterior): the primary lateral flexors; also respiratory muscles — strengthening them improves respiratory capacity
  • Levator scapulae: lateral neck flexion and scapular elevation

3. Physiological Effects

Deep cervical flexor rehabilitation — the critical clinical application

Research in cervical spondylosis and chronic neck pain consistently identifies deep cervical flexor weakness (longus colli and longus capitis) as a central finding. These muscles:

  • Are the primary stabilisers of the cervical vertebrae against shear forces
  • Maintain the cervical lordosis in its optimal midrange
  • Are selectively inhibited by neck pain — even mild neck pain reflexively reduces their motor drive

The isometric anterior resistance (forehead-push against hands) specifically activates the deep cervical flexors — the same technique used in physiotherapy "deep cervical flexor training" protocols.

Cervical joint protection in RA

Rheumatoid arthritis can affect the atlantoaxial joint (C1-C2), creating atlantoaxial instability. In such cases, careful assessment is required before neck strengthening — the exercises should be performed with guidance and should not include forceful end-range movements. Isometric exercises within the pain-free midrange are appropriate.

Scalene activation and respiratory benefit

The scalene muscles (strengthened in lateral resistance) are also primary respiratory muscles — they elevate the upper two ribs during forced inhalation. Improving scalene strength through Griva Sakti Vikasaka has a secondary benefit for respiratory capacity, relevant in COPD and asthma protocols.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — cervical stabiliser strengthening; deep neck flexor rehabilitation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the neck houses the Vishuddhi chakra (throat energy centre) and the entry to the sushumna channel; strengthening the cervical structures provides a stable foundation for pranayama and meditation practices.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Rheumatoid Arthritis — cervical involvement (A003) Primary; cervical stability; deep flexor strengthening (with atlantoaxial assessment)
Cervical Spondylosis (A004) Deep cervical flexor training; postural correction
Neck Pain (chronic) Cervical stabiliser rehabilitation
Headache (cervicogenic) Reduced suboccipital muscle tension through balanced strengthening

6. Contraindications

  • Atlantoaxial instability (RA): must be assessed by a physician before neck exercises; any instability at C1-C2 requires specialist clearance
  • Acute cervical disc prolapse with myelopathy: isometric exercises should be supervised; significant pressure against resistance may be contraindicated
  • Recent cervical spine surgery: avoid until cleared by the surgical team

7. Series Context

Final (seventh) exercise in the Sakti Vikasaka sequence, completing the distal-to-proximal joint progression. Preceded by Bhujabanda (P0142).

Full series: Anguli (P0137) → Kara Tala (P0138) → Kara Prista (P0139) → Mani Bandha (P0140) → Kaphoni (P0141) → Bhujabanda (P0142) → Griva (P0143).
