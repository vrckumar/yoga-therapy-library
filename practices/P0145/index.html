Tongue Massaging

Tongue Massaging (Jivha Mardana)

Jivha = tongue, mardana = pressing/massaging. Self-massage of the tongue using the fingers — pressing, kneading, and stretching the tongue tissue to stimulate its musculature, activate the taste receptors, improve surface blood flow, and stimulate the vagal and glossopharyngeal nerve pathways accessible through the tongue base. Performed as part of the morning oral hygiene and facial rehabilitation sequence. A component of the traditional Danta Dhauti (dental-oral cleansing) practice system.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Seated in Vajrasana or in a chair.

Practice:

Stage I — Surface massage:

  1. Protrude the tongue fully.
  2. Using the right index finger and thumb, grasp the tongue at the tip.
  3. Gently knead the tongue from tip to middle, moving the fingers in small circular pressing motions.
  4. Work systematically along the dorsal (upper) surface and the lateral edges.
  5. Continue for 30–60 seconds.

Stage II — Lateral edge massage:

  1. Using the thumb on the dorsal surface and the index finger on the ventral (under) surface, firmly knead along the lateral edges of the tongue from tip to base.
  2. The lateral edges of the tongue contain the taste receptors most sensitive to sour and salt — stimulating this region improves taste sensitivity.
  3. Repeat 5–10 times on each side.

Stage III — Palate massage (optional extension):

  1. After the tongue massage, use the tip of the index finger to press and rub along the hard palate from the back of the front teeth to the soft palate.
  2. This stimulates the palatal reflex and the vagal pathways accessible through the soft palate.

Stage IV — Cleaning (Danta Dhauti context):

  1. As part of the morning Dhauti practice, the tongue is wiped clean using the right hand's fingers scraped from back to front — removing the coating (ama/tongue coating) that accumulates overnight.
  2. This is the traditional morning oral hygiene component.

Notes:

  • Wash hands thoroughly before tongue massage.
  • The pressure should be firm but not painful.
  • Morning practice before eating is most effective (removes overnight tongue coating).

2. Anatomical Activation

Structures stimulated

  • Taste buds (papillae): the mechanical stimulation of the tongue surface activates the taste receptor cells in the fungiform and circumvallate papillae — this resets their sensitivity and improves taste perception
  • Tongue musculature: the kneading pressure provides passive traction on the intrinsic and extrinsic tongue muscles — a form of soft tissue mobilisation
  • Sublingual and submandibular salivary gland ducts: the tongue ventral massage stimulates the opening of these ducts, increasing salivary flow
  • Lingual veins and lymphatics: the tongue has rich venous and lymphatic drainage; massage promotes clearance of metabolic waste from the tongue tissue

Reflex pathways

  • Glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX): the posterior tongue and palate stimulation activates CN IX, which carries taste sensation from the posterior third of the tongue and contributes to salivary reflex
  • Vagus nerve (CN X): the soft palate stimulation (Stage III) activates the vagal branches, producing a mild parasympathetic response

3. Physiological Effects

Tongue coating removal and digestive health

In Ayurvedic and yogic medicine, a coated tongue (ama on the tongue) indicates accumulation of metabolic waste. Morning tongue scraping and massage:

  • Removes the bacteria-rich biofilm that forms overnight (dental plaque precursor)
  • Reduces the microbial load in the oral cavity, decreasing the risk of periodontal disease and aspiration pneumonia
  • Stimulates the salivary glands to initiate the digestive process — saliva contains amylase, the first enzyme of carbohydrate digestion

Taste sensitivity restoration

Regular tongue surface stimulation through massage has been shown to improve taste sensitivity by:

  • Clearing obstructing biofilm from the taste papillae surface
  • Increasing blood flow to the papillae
  • Mechanically stimulating the microvilli of the taste cells, resetting their receptor sensitivity

Reduced taste sensitivity is a common complaint in elderly patients and those on multiple medications — tongue massage is a simple intervention that can partially restore it.

Vagal activation

The palate massage (Stage III) provides non-invasive vagal stimulation through the palatal branches of the vagus nerve. This is associated with improved GI motility, reduced nausea, and a mild calming effect — relevant in GID protocols.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — tongue muscle mobilisation; oral mucosa stimulation; salivary gland activation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — stimulation of the taste pathways (prana flow related to the gustatory sense, rasana tanmatra).

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
GID / Digestive disorders (A014) Salivary gland stimulation; improved taste sensitivity; morning digestive preparation
Oral hygiene (all protocols) Ama removal; dental health; reduced oral bacteria
Stroke rehabilitation Tongue muscle mobilisation; sensory stimulation for dysarthria
Elderly / Reduced taste Taste sensitivity restoration
Morning Sadhana practice Traditional component of Danta Dhauti oral cleansing

6. Contraindications

  • Active oral infections (oral thrush, herpes stomatitis): avoid massage of infected areas
  • Bleeding gums or oral ulcers: reduce pressure; avoid affected areas
  • Nausea or strong gag reflex: reduce pressure at the posterior tongue

7. Related Practices

  • Tongue Movement (P0144): active tongue exercises
  • Simha Mudra (P0105): maximum tongue protrusion
  • Mukha Dhouti (P0163): complete mouth cleansing practice of which tongue massaging is a component
