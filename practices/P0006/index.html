Patella Movement

Patella Movement (Januphala Naman)

Manual mobilisation of the patella — the practitioner uses the hands to gently slide the kneecap superiorly, inferiorly, medially, and laterally within the trochlear groove of the femur. The only exercise in the IAYT system that specifically mobilises the patellofemoral joint rather than the tibiofemoral (hinge) joint of the knee.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Seated (Dandasana) or Supine. Knee fully extended (leg straight). The quadriceps must be completely relaxed — the patella can only be moved when the quadriceps tendon is slack.

Practice:

  1. Sit with one leg extended and completely relaxed. Let the knee relax into the floor.
  2. Place the thumb and index finger (or index and middle finger) of both hands around the edges of the patella.
  3. Gently slide the patella upward (superiorly) toward the thigh. Hold for 2 seconds.
  4. Gently slide the patella downward (inferiorly) toward the shin. Hold for 2 seconds.
  5. Gently slide the patella medially (toward the other knee). Hold briefly.
  6. Gently slide the patella laterally (away from midline). Hold briefly.
  7. Perform 5–10 rounds of superior-inferior gliding, then 5–10 rounds of medial-lateral gliding.
  8. Repeat on the other knee.

Breathing: Normal throughout. Relax the quadriceps completely during all movements.

2. Anatomical Activation

Structures mobilised passively

  • Patellofemoral joint: the articulation between the posterior surface of the patella and the trochlear groove of the femur — mobilised in all four directions
  • Patellar retinaculum (medial and lateral): fibrous bands that restrain patellar motion; stretched in the opposite direction of each glide
  • Infrapatellar fat pad (Hoffa's fat pad): mobilised by inferior patellar gliding; often a source of anterior knee pain when impinged
  • Synovial folds (plica): gently mobilised — reduces plica syndrome symptoms

3. Physiological Effects

Patellar chondromalacia prevention — the primary mechanism

The posterior surface of the patella is covered with articular cartilage that must distribute load evenly across the trochlear groove during knee flexion. When the patella tracks improperly — typically due to tight lateral retinaculum or weak VMO pulling the patella medially — abnormal focal pressure is applied to a limited area of articular cartilage. This focal overloading causes cartilage softening and degeneration known as chondromalacia patellae.

Patella Movement directly addresses the mechanical restriction: by mobilising the tight lateral retinaculum through medial gliding, and by generally restoring patellar mobility within the groove, normal tracking is facilitated. The cartilage also receives synovial fluid nutrition through this passive movement — critical as the posterior patellar cartilage has no direct blood supply.

Retinacular tightness release

The most common cause of patellar maltracking is tightness of the lateral patellar retinaculum combined with relative weakness of the VMO. The medial glide component of patella movement specifically stretches the lateral retinaculum — the conservative management technique for patellofemoral pain syndrome that is also used by physiotherapists as patellar taping and mobilisation.

Hoffa's fat pad mobilisation

The infrapatellar fat pad is frequently impinged between the patellar tendon and the tibial plateau in hyperextension and in repetitive loading. The inferior patellar glide decompresses the fat pad and mobilises it within its fascial compartment, reducing Hoffa's syndrome symptoms.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — patellofemoral joint mobilisation; retinacular conditioning; articular cartilage nutrition through synovial fluid.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the knee is the janu sandhi (knee joint) in yogic anatomy — a critical energy junction of the lower limb channel.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome / Chondromalacia Patellae Direct patellar mobilisation; lateral retinaculum stretching; tracking improvement
Arthritis — Knee (A003) Synovial fluid distribution to patellofemoral articular surfaces; stiffness reduction
Knee Osteoarthritis Patellar mobilisation before weight-bearing exercise; reduces patellofemoral loading
Back Pain (A004) — with knee stiffness Knee preparation before seated and supine practices
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) First kneecap-specific exercise; prepares patellofemoral joint before tibiofemoral work

6. Contraindications

  • Acute knee injury with haemarthrosis — avoid any manipulation until effusion resolves
  • Recent knee surgery (within 6 weeks) — follow surgical protocol; early patellar mobilisation is sometimes prescribed, but only under physiotherapy guidance
  • Acute prepatellar bursitis — pressure on the patella is painful and may aggravate inflammation

7. Variations & Progressions

Four-directional (standard): Superior, inferior, medial, lateral as described.

Clockwise/anticlockwise circular glide (advanced): Move the patella in a smooth circle within the groove — the most comprehensive mobilisation.

Sequencing context: The first kneecap exercise — follows the ankle group. Progresses to P0007 (Patella Movement and Kneecap Tightening combined) and P0008 (Kneecap Tightening/VMO activation).
