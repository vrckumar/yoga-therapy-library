Dhanurasana

Dhanurasana (Bow Pose)

Dhanu (Sanskrit) — bow. The body forms the arc of a bow with the arms as the bowstring — the full prone backbend that integrates spinal extension, hip extension, and abdominal rocking into a single dynamic posture. Among the most therapeutically comprehensive prone postures in the IAYT system.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Prone (Makarasana). Lie face-down, legs together, forehead on the floor.

Practice:

  1. Bend both knees and bring the heels toward the buttocks.
  2. Reach both arms back and grasp the ankles firmly. Do not bend the elbows — the arms remain straight throughout. This is a key alignment cue that ensures the lift comes from the spine and hips, not from arm leverage.
  3. Inhale and simultaneously raise the head and chest off the floor while pulling the legs outward and backward against the grip of the hands. This antagonistic tension — the legs trying to straighten, the hands holding the ankles — creates the arc of the bow.
  4. The trunk arches backward into a full spinal extension. The body comes to rest balanced on the abdomen (the soft region between the lower chest and the pelvis).
  5. Maintain the posture for approximately 30 seconds with normal breathing. The natural breath creates a gentle rocking motion on the abdomen — this is intentional and therapeutic.
  6. Exhale and gently release, returning to Makarasana.

Breathing: Inhale into the lift; breathe normally throughout the hold — the rocking that results from breathing cycles is part of the practice; exhale on release.

Duration/rounds: 30 seconds; 1–2 rounds per session.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Erector spinae (full length): the entire posterior spinal column is in concentric extension — this is the most comprehensive spinal extensor loading of any prone posture
  • Gluteus maximus and hamstrings: contract to extend and hold the hips; they also create the pulling force that the hands resist
  • Quadriceps (eccentric): are stretched by the knee flexion and attempt to straighten the leg against the arm grip
  • Anterior thoracic structures (passive stretch): pectorals, anterior intercostals, rectus abdominis, and hip flexors (psoas, rectus femoris) are all passively lengthened in the bow arc
  • Posterior shoulder and rotator cuff: the arms reaching back and holding straight require posterior deltoid, teres minor, and infraspinatus activation

Organs directly affected

  • Abdominal viscera: the entire abdominal contents (stomach, small intestine, large intestine, liver, pancreas, kidneys) are rhythmically compressed and released with each breath cycle as the body rocks on the abdomen
  • Pancreas: the retroperitoneal pancreatic tissue receives direct mechanical stimulation from the abdominal floor contact
  • Adrenal glands: the lumbar extension and posterior abdominal wall activation directly stimulates the perirenal adrenal region

Spinal structures

Full-range thoracic and lumbar extension stretches the anterior longitudinal ligament along its entire length. The cervical spine is extended by the head lift. The sacroiliac joints are compressed and stabilised.

3. Physiological Effects

Abdominal visceral massage — the defining therapeutic mechanism

Dhanurasana's therapeutic superiority to other prone postures for metabolic and digestive conditions lies in the abdominal rocking produced by normal breathing while balanced on the abdomen. Each breath cycle creates:

  1. Inhalation: the diaphragm descends, abdominal pressure increases, and the viscera are pressed against the floor — compression phase
  2. Exhalation: the diaphragm ascends, abdominal pressure decreases, and the viscera rebound — decompression/release phase

This rhythmic compression-release cycles 12–15 times per minute across the pancreas, stomach, intestines, and liver simultaneously. The mechanical effect on each organ:

  • Pancreas: retroperitoneal compression stimulates both the exocrine (enzyme secretion) and proposed endocrine (insulin secretion) functions — the basis for Dhanurasana's inclusion in the diabetes protocol
  • Liver: compression improves hepatic blood flow and bile secretion into the duodenum — addressing the digestive/metabolic component of diabetes and GID
  • Gastrointestinal tract: the rhythmic mechanical stimulation is equivalent to a vigorous abdominal massage, stimulating peristalsis throughout the small and large intestine

This mechanism — repetitive rhythmic compression of all abdominal organs simultaneously — makes Dhanurasana the most efficient single posture for gastrointestinal stimulation in the IAYT system.

Full-arc spinal loading

Unlike Bhujangasana (thoracic emphasis) or Salabhasana (lumbar emphasis), Dhanurasana loads the entire spinal extensor chain from sacrum to occiput in a single eccentric-concentric arc. The curved geometry of the "bow" means:

  • Thoracic extensors work at shortened range (maximum effort)
  • Lumbar extensors work at lengthened range (maximum stretch)
  • The intervertebral joints throughout the spine achieve maximum extension ROM

This comprehensive loading makes Dhanurasana the progression posture for spinal rehabilitation, used after Bhujangasana and Salabhasana have built baseline extensor strength.

Hip flexor lengthening

The knee flexion combined with hip extension creates a unique combined stretch of the rectus femoris (which crosses both hip and knee joints). The knee bending lengthens the distal attachment while the hip extension lengthens the proximal attachment — producing a full-length rectus femoris stretch. Since hip flexor tightness is a primary driver of anterior pelvic tilt and consequent lumbar hyperlordosis, this stretch has corrective postural implications beyond the practice session.

Autonomic and endocrine effects

The combined stimulation of adrenal tissue (via posterior abdominal wall compression) and abdominal sympathetic ganglia (via the spinal extension) produces a moderate sympathetic activation. The resulting catecholamine release and subsequent rebound parasympathetic response after release is proposed as a mechanism for adrenal toning — improving the HPA axis responsiveness that is impaired in chronic diabetes and metabolic syndrome.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — full posterior chain activation; comprehensive abdominal organ massage; hip flexor lengthening.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the full spinal arc engages all primary chakra regions simultaneously; the chest opening at anahata and abdominal stimulation at manipura make this a posture of energetic integration across the pranic column.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning

Arthritis (A003) Full spinal mobilisation; hip joint range of motion preservation

6. Contraindications

  • General debility — proceed cautiously; this is a demanding posture; ensure Bhujangasana and Salabhasana are comfortable before introducing Dhanurasana
  • Recent abdominal surgery — avoid; abdominal floor pressure is contraindicated
  • Peptic ulcer (active) — direct abdominal compression may aggravate; avoid in acute phase
  • Pregnancy — prone position contraindicated from second trimester onward
  • Severe lumbar disc prolapse — full spinal extension may aggravate posterior disc herniation; assess individually

7. Variations & Progressions

Beginner (Half Dhanurasana): Grasp one ankle at a time, raising one leg while the opposite thigh stays on the floor. This reduces the spinal arc and abdominal compression.

Standard (as described): Both ankles held, arms straight, balanced on abdomen, 30-second hold.

Intermediate: Rock deliberately with each breath cycle, exaggerating the inhalation-driven forward rock and exhalation-driven backward rock. This increases the visceral massage effect.

Preceding postures: Bhujangasana (spinal extension warm-up), Salabhasana (posterior chain preparation).
