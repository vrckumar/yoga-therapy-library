Laghu Shankhaprakshalana

Laghu Shankhaprakshalana (Short Intestinal Cleanse)

Laghu (Sanskrit) — short, light; Shankha — conch shell (metaphor for the intestinal tract); Prakshalana — to wash through, to clean. Laghu Shankhaprakshalana is the shorter version of the full Shankhaprakshalana — the complete intestinal cleanse. Where the full version washes the entire GI tract from mouth to anus (using 10–15 litres of saline over 2–3 hours), the Laghu version performs a targeted cleanse of the GI tract using 2–3 glasses of saline and a specific asana sequence that moves the water progressively through the digestive system.

The five-asana sequence is the defining feature of Laghu Shankhaprakshalana — these specific postures apply sequential mechanical pressure to different sections of the GI tract, moving the saline water from the stomach through the small intestine, through the ileocaecal valve, and into the large intestine, ultimately triggering defecation.

1. Technique

Timing: Early morning, completely empty stomach. No food or liquids (other than the prescribed saline water) should have been taken since the previous evening.

Saline preparation: 2–3 glasses of warm saline water — 1 teaspoon of salt per 500 ml. Temperature: comfortably warm.

Process:

Step 1 — Drink: Drink 2 glasses of warm saline water rapidly. Do not sip — drink quickly to ensure the water reaches the stomach as a bolus.

Step 2 — Perform the five-asana sequence (8 rounds each):

Asana 1 — Tadasana with lateral bend (Spinal twist standing): Stand in Tadasana. Interlock the fingers and raise the arms overhead, palms facing up. Inhale, stretch upward. Exhale and bend slowly to the right. Inhale back to centre. Exhale, bend to the left. This is one round. Perform 8 rounds. Mechanism: The lateral bending compresses alternating sides of the stomach and upper GI tract, pushing the water from the stomach into the duodenum.

Asana 2 — Kati Chakrasana (Standing spinal rotation): Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, arms extended at shoulder level. Twist the trunk to the right, looking over the right shoulder. Return to centre, twist to the left. 8 rounds. Mechanism: The rotational compression of the upper abdomen further moves water through the duodenum and into the jejunum.

Asana 3 — Bhujangasana (Cobra pose): Lie prone. Place palms beside the chest. Inhale, raise the head and chest (cobra position). Exhale, lower. 8 rounds. Mechanism: The prone position with chest raised compresses the lower abdomen, pushing water through the jejunum and into the ileum. The posterior abdominal wall pressure moves water anteriorly and inferiorly.

Asana 4 — Dhanurasana variation (Abdominal rotation in prone): Lie prone with hands beside the hips. Raise the right leg and simultaneously turn the head to the left. Lower, then raise the left leg and turn the head to the right. 8 rounds. (Some versions use a simplified Dhanurasana or the lying twist.) Mechanism: The alternating leg raises with trunk rotation compress and release different quadrants of the lower small intestine and ileocaecal junction, facilitating water passage from the ileum into the caecum.

Asana 5 — Utkatasana variation (Squatting or low squat): Squat with feet apart, hands on knees. Rotate the knees alternately — right knee drops inward (medially) while left goes outward, then reverse. Or perform a simple deep squat rising and lowering. 8 rounds. Mechanism: The squatting position increases intra-abdominal pressure and opens the ileocaecal region. The rotation distributes this pressure through the ascending, transverse, and descending colon, moving water toward the sigmoid and rectum.

Step 3 — Defecation: After completing the five-asana sequence, attend to the toilet. The saline water, having been moved through the GI tract by the asanas, typically triggers bowel action within 15–30 minutes of the first sequence.

Step 4 — Repeat: Return and drink another 2 glasses of warm saline. Repeat the five-asana sequence × 8 rounds each. Return to the toilet. Continue this cycle 2–3 times, until the stool expelled is essentially clear or light brown saline water — indicating that the intestinal contents have been fully flushed.

Step 5 — Post-practice meal (essential): Immediately after completing the cleanse, eat a prescribed meal of khichdi — rice and lentils (moong dal) cooked to a soft, semi-liquid consistency with a small amount of ghee. This meal:

  • Provides an easily digestible carbohydrate-protein combination that does not stress the freshly cleansed GI tract
  • The ghee coats the intestinal wall, providing a protective lubricating layer after the washing
  • Restores normal intestinal flora nutrition (the flora have been partially washed out and need simple nutrients to re-establish)
  • Prevents the reactive hypoglycaemia that can occur if the post-cleanse stomach is left empty

Do not skip the khichdi meal — eating nothing after Laghu Shankhaprakshalana leaves the intestinal wall unprotected and can cause cramping, weakness, or electrolyte imbalance.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary structures engaged

Entire GI tract from stomach to rectum: Unlike Kunjal Kriya (which addresses only the stomach) or enemas (which address only the rectum and sigmoid colon), Laghu Shankhaprakshalana works the entire length of the GI tract. Each section is sequentially targeted by a specific asana:

  • Stomach → duodenum: Tadasana lateral bend
  • Duodenum → jejunum: Kati Chakrasana rotation
  • Jejunum → ileum: Bhujangasana prone pressure
  • Ileum → caecum: Dhanurasana variation
  • Ascending, transverse, descending colon → sigmoid: Utkatasana squatting

Ileocaecal valve: This one-way valve between the small intestine (ileum) and the large intestine (caecum) is a key anatomical checkpoint. In normal digestion, passage through this valve is controlled by peristalsis and the ileocaecal sphincter. The Dhanurasana and Utkatasana asanas specifically apply pressure to the right iliac fossa region (where the ileocaecal junction sits), facilitating valve opening and water passage into the colon.

Intestinal wall smooth muscle: The mechanical pressure of the water bolus, enhanced by each asana, stretches the intestinal wall. This activates the myenteric plexus (Auerbach's plexus) — the enteric nervous system network embedded in the intestinal smooth muscle — triggering propulsive peristaltic waves ahead of the water bolus.

Colonic mucosa: The warm saline in contact with the colonic mucosa:

  • Softens and dislodges adherent mucus and faecal material
  • Stimulates goblet cells to secrete fresh mucus
  • Activates enteroendocrine cells (L cells and I cells) that secrete gut hormones (GLP-1, PYY, CCK) involved in satiety, insulin regulation, and gut motility

Gut microbiome environment: The washing effect temporarily reduces bacterial density throughout the GI tract. After the cleanse, the intestinal environment (pH, water content, temperature) is reset, providing a refreshed substrate for the recolonisation of beneficial bacteria — particularly relevant for diabetics whose gut flora composition is altered by chronic hyperglycaemia.

3. Physiological Effects

Complete GI tract reset — mechanical, neural, and microbiome pathways

1. Mechanical removal of intestinal contents — deep GI cleansing The progressive movement of saline through the entire GI tract removes:

  • Accumulated faecal material from all colon segments (not just the sigmoid, as with an enema)
  • Adherent mucus from the intestinal wall that can harbour bacteria and inflammatory mediators
  • Food residue from the small intestine that has not been properly digested (ama — undigested matter in Ayurvedic terms)
  • Fermentation byproducts from dysbiotic bacteria in the colon

This comprehensive removal of accumulated material is unlike any other cleansing practice in the IAYT system. It addresses the entire length of the gut simultaneously, making it unique in its capacity for deep GI reset.

2. Intestinal mucosal regeneration After the saline wash, the intestinal mucosa is exposed to a clean, warm, isotonic environment. This:

  • Stimulates the turnover of enterocytes (intestinal epithelial cells, which normally replace themselves every 3–5 days but can become sluggish in poor gut health)
  • Improves the integrity of the intestinal barrier (tight junctions between enterocytes), reducing intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") that contributes to systemic inflammation in metabolic disorders
  • Removes the accumulated mucus that impairs nutrient absorption

3. Gut microbiome reset and improved absorption The gut microbiome in diabetics and obese individuals is dysbiotic — an altered ratio of beneficial to harmful bacteria, often with excess Firmicutes relative to Bacteroidetes. This dysbiosis contributes to:

  • Increased extraction of calories from food (Firmicutes-dominant flora are more efficient at breaking down polysaccharides into absorbable simple sugars)
  • Increased intestinal permeability
  • Increased production of inflammatory lipopolysaccharides (LPS) from gram-negative bacteria that enter the bloodstream through a permeable gut wall

Laghu Shankhaprakshalana transiently reduces the total bacterial load and, through the khichdi post-meal (easily digestible, non-fermentable), creates a substrate environment that preferentially supports the re-establishment of Bacteroidetes-rich beneficial flora. This is not a permanent microbiome reset with a single session, but repeated weekly practice in the diabetes protocol progressively shifts the gut environment.

4. Hypoglycaemic state reset in diabetes — the daily Laghu version The PGDYTP 101 describes a "daily version" of Laghu Shankhaprakshalana used specifically in the IAYT diabetes protocol to "help reset the hypoglycaemic state." This refers to the fasting-state GI cleanse that:

  • Removes the overnight gastric and intestinal contents that can impair morning insulin sensitivity
  • Stimulates GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) release from L cells in the ileum and colon — GLP-1 increases insulin secretion, reduces glucagon, and slows gastric emptying (improving post-meal glycaemic control)
  • Reduces the morning rise in blood glucose (dawn phenomenon) by removing the intestinal substrate that would otherwise be absorbed after breakfast

5. Removal of deep-seated toxins — ama physiology In Ayurvedic physiology, ama is the primary pathogenic factor in chronic disease — a sticky, heavy, undigested residue that accumulates in the GI tract, blocks the channels (srotas), and impairs metabolic function. Laghu Shankhaprakshalana is the most direct Ayurvedic practice for removing accumulated ama from the GI tract. The physiological correlate is the adherent biofilm, dysbiotic bacterial colonies, and faecal material that accumulate in the intestinal folds (particularly the valvulae conniventes of the small intestine and the colonic haustra) — sites inaccessible to normal peristalsis or enema.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — the most comprehensive physical cleansing practice in the IAYT system. The entire GI tract from stomach to rectum is directly engaged.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the clearing of accumulated ama from the GI tract removes the most significant obstruction to the flow of Samana Vayu (digestive prana) and Apana Vayu (eliminative prana). The lightness and clarity reported after Laghu Shankhaprakshalana is a Pranamaya experience — the prana flows freely through a system that has been cleansed of its physical blockages.

Tertiary: Manomaya Kosha — the profound mental clarity and sense of lightness that follows Laghu Shankhaprakshalana extends beyond the physical. The gut-brain axis (the bidirectional neural and hormonal communication between the enteric nervous system and the central nervous system) means that a cleansed, well-functioning gut directly supports mental clarity, reduced anxiety, and improved mood — effects reported consistently by practitioners.

6. Contraindications

  • Active gastric or duodenal ulcer — the saline wash directly contacts and can irritate an active ulcer. Absolute contraindication.
  • Acute GI infection (gastroenteritis, colitis) — the mechanical stimulation can worsen inflammation. Wait until resolved.
  • Full Shankhaprakshalana (longer version) — requires guidance — the longer version uses 10–15 litres of water, takes 2–3 hours, and requires close supervision by an experienced teacher. Laghu (shorter) version is appropriate for self-practice with basic instruction.
  • Hernia — the increased intra-abdominal pressure during the asana sequence can worsen any hernia.
  • Ileostomy or colostomy — the practice is anatomically inappropriate; the normal GI route does not exist.
  • After meals — absolutely only on empty stomach.
  • Pregnancy — absolute contraindication. The abdominal pressure and bowel stimulation are dangerous.
  • Acute appendicitis or diverticulitis — any acute abdominal inflammatory condition is a contraindication.
  • Electrolyte imbalance (known) — the repeated washing can alter sodium and potassium levels. Avoid in patients on diuretics or with known electrolyte disorders without medical supervision.

7. Variations & Progressions

Laghu (short — 2–3 glasses): As described — the standard form appropriate for weekly or daily use in the diabetes protocol. Two to three cycles of drink + asanas + defecation.

Full Shankhaprakshalana (Purna — 10–15 litres): The complete intestinal wash. Performed quarterly or annually in intensive yoga therapy programs. Requires a full day, experienced supervision, and a specific post-practice diet of khichdi for three days. Not for self-practice.

Asana sequence modification: The five asanas may be modified for practitioners with limited mobility. The standing twists (Tadasana and Kati Chakrasana) can be done seated on a chair; the prone asanas (Bhujangasana, Dhanurasana) can be replaced with supine twists for patients with spinal conditions.

Post-practice khichdi recipe: 1 cup rice + ½ cup moong dal (split green lentils), cooked to a porridge consistency with 4–5 cups water. Add 1 teaspoon ghee, light salt, and turmeric. No strong spices. This meal is sacred to the post-practice recovery and must not be skipped or substituted.

Integration in the IAYT diabetes protocol: Laghu Shankhaprakshalana is used in the IAYT diabetes protocol as a weekly Sunday practice — complementing the daily Neti, Kapalabhati, asana, and pranayama routine with a weekly deep GI reset that maintains the gut environment in an optimal state for insulin sensitivity and metabolic health.
