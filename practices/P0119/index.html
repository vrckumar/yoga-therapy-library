OM Meditation

OM Meditation (Oṃkāra Dhyāna)

Oṃ — the primordial sound, the universal syllable of Brahman; Dhyāna — meditation, sustained contemplation. OM Meditation is the universal closing practice of every IAYT therapeutic session across all eight conditions and protocols. Its consistent placement at the end of the practice sequence is not incidental: it is prescribed as the integration and consolidation phase that allows all the physiological effects produced by the preceding asana, pranayama, and relaxation practices to settle into the system. The silent mental repetition of OM, sustained for 10 minutes, produces a coherent alpha/theta brainwave state that is the neurophysiological equivalent of restorative rest.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Any comfortable meditative posture — Vajrāsana, Sukhasana, Padmasana, or sitting in a chair. Spine erect; shoulders relaxed; eyes gently closed.

Practice:

Phase I — Active Japa (repetition):

  • Close the eyes and begin chanting OM mentally — silently, with full inner awareness.
  • Allow the mind to repeat OM continuously without any break. If distractions arise, chant OM faster — not giving any opportunity for the distraction to take hold.
  • Keep the OM repetition smooth, effortless, and rhythmic.

Phase II — Slowing (transition):

  • After a period of sustained mental repetition, the chanting naturally begins to slow. Allow this — do not force either speed or slowness.
  • The gaps between successive OMs begin to widen.

Phase III — Gap awareness:

  • As the OM repetition slows further, observe the gap between two OMs. Notice that the gap is a moment of silence.
  • As the repetition slows further still, the gap becomes progressively wider and deeper.
  • "The gap goes on becoming wider and wider to diffuse into silence" (source text).

Phase IV — Silence:

  • The experience of inner silence expands from the three-dimensional awareness of the body to an all-pervasive awareness beyond the limits of the body.
  • The "bed of silence becomes deeper and more expansive."
  • Visualise and feel an ocean of silence with gentle soft waves of OM on its surface.
  • Now merge into complete silence — the stage of Ajapa (no-repetition). This silence is described in the source as "the source of Creativity, Power, Knowledge and Bliss."

Phase V — Return and conclusion:

  • From this deep ocean of silence, allow one OM to emerge as an audible sound that diffuses into the entire body and the space all around.
  • Enjoy the beautiful vibrations.
  • Blink the eyes slowly; gently open the eyes and return.

Between sessions (note from source): "Try to touch this state of deep rest and inner silence several times in the day whenever you have some free time."

Duration: 10 minutes as the standard IAYT closing practice; may be extended to 20–30 minutes for experienced practitioners or specific neurological conditions.

2. Anatomical / Neurological Activation

Brainwave shift mechanism

The silent mental repetition of a single, non-semantic sound (OM) is a well-validated technique for inducing a specific brainwave state:

  • Beta waves (14–30 Hz): the normal waking cognitive state — planning, problem-solving, anxiety, discursive thought
  • Alpha waves (8–13 Hz): relaxed alert awareness; the first shift observed in EEG studies of mantra meditation
  • Theta waves (4–7 Hz): deep relaxation, borderline sleep, creativity, and deep meditation access
  • Delta waves (0.5–3 Hz): dreamless deep sleep; occasionally observed in very advanced meditators

Research at SVYASA has documented the shift from beta to alpha-theta coherence during OM Meditation, with simultaneous reduction in skin conductance (sympathetic arousal marker), decreased heart rate, and reduced respiratory rate. This is the EEG correlate of the subjective experience described in the practice.

Default Mode Network (DMN)

The DMN — a network of brain regions active during mind-wandering, autobiographical rumination, and unfocused thought — is the neural substrate of the "busy mind" that meditation seeks to quieten. OM Meditation, by anchoring attention on a single repetitive mental sound, suppresses DMN activity and activates instead the task-positive network (TPN), which governs focused attention. Over time, regular practitioners show structural changes in the DMN — reduced grey matter density in the medial prefrontal cortex and posterior cingulate — corresponding to the experiential reduction in rumination.

3. Physiological Effects

Coherent brainwave induction — the primary mechanism

The specific power of OM as a meditation object lies in the properties of the syllable itself. OM is composed of three phonemes — A, U, and M — that produce resonance in sequentially higher regions of the body: A in the lower abdomen and thorax, U in the thoracic and neck region, M in the cranial vault (as the lips close). When this vibration is internalised as a mental sound rather than vocalised, the physiological resonance shifts inward: it becomes a pattern of neural oscillation rather than an acoustic oscillation.

The mental repetition of OM entrains neural oscillations toward the natural resonant frequency of the alpha band (approximately 10 Hz) — which is close to the dominant EEG frequency of wakeful rest. This alpha entrainment produces:

  1. Coherent inter-hemispheric synchrony: the left and right hemispheres of the cortex begin to oscillate in phase, reducing the functional asymmetry associated with anxiety (dominant right amygdala) and depression (reduced left prefrontal activity). The clinical consequence is emotional balance.

  2. Hypothalamic-pituitary axis quieting: sustained alpha-theta activity inhibits the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus — the primary source of CRH (corticotrophin-releasing hormone), which drives the cortisol stress response. Reduced CRH → reduced ACTH → reduced cortisol. This is the mechanistic pathway through which regular OM Meditation reduces the chronic HPA hyperactivation that underlies every psychosomatic condition in the IAYT spectrum.

  3. Default mode suppression: The anchoring of attention on the OM repetition suppresses the default-mode network (DMN), reducing ruminative thinking, anxiety about the future, and regret about the past. This suppression is measurable in fMRI studies and corresponds to the subjective experience of the mind quietening.

Closing function in the IAYT sequence

The specific placement of OM Meditation as the final practice in every IAYT session is functionally essential. All preceding practices — asanas (physical stimulation), pranayama (energy regulation), relaxation techniques (deep rest) — have disturbed the system in therapeutic ways: circulation has been altered, organs have been compressed and released, the nervous system has been stimulated and quieted. OM Meditation provides the final integration phase, allowing all these temporary perturbations to settle into a new, improved steady state. The 10-minute silence is the consolidation window — analogous to the synaptic consolidation period after learning, during which temporary short-term changes become permanent structural adaptations.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Manomaya Kosha — direct regulation of the mental sheath through conscious attention management; suppression of discursive thought; cultivation of a quiet mental field.

Secondary: Vijnanamaya Kosha — as the gaps between OMs widen into silence, the practitioner begins to encounter the witness consciousness (sakshi) that observes mental activity without being caught in it; this is the first experiential access to the Vijnanamaya (intellect/wisdom) level.

Tertiary: Anandamaya Kosha — the "bliss of silence" described in Phase IV corresponds to the Anandamaya level: a state of contentment and fullness that is independent of external objects or events.

6. Contraindications

  • Acute psychosis: in florid psychosis, internal mental practices that reduce critical self-monitoring may exacerbate symptoms; use only under direct clinical supervision
  • Very early practice (beginners): 10 minutes of sustained internal attention may be initially challenging; begin with 5 minutes and extend gradually

7. Variations & Progressions

Beginner (5 minutes): Focus only on Phases I and II — active mental OM repetition with gentle attention, without requiring the silence phases.

Standard IAYT (10 minutes, as described): Full five-phase OM Dhyana as closing practice.

Extended session (20–30 minutes): Used in specific neurological and cancer protocols; longer duration increases the depth and duration of theta access.

With Nadanusandana integration: OM Meditation is practised immediately after Nadanusandana (P0164) in many IAYT protocols; the A-U-M sound resonance of Nadanusandana prepares the auditory and neural pathways, making the subsequent mental OM repetition more immediate and resonant.
