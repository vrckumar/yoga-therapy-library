Sasankasana Breathing

Sasankasana Breathing (Hare Pose Breathing / Shashankasana Breathing)

A kneeling breath-movement practice performed in Vajrasana. The spine extends backward on inhalation (chest opens) and folds forward into the full Sasankasana (child's pose / hare pose) on exhalation. The forward bend on exhalation produces deep lumbar decompression and diaphragmatic compression, activating a strong parasympathetic response. Widely used in all IAYT protocols as a breath-synchronised lumbar mobiliser and nervous system calmer.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrasana — sit on the heels with the spine erect.

Hand position: Take both hands behind the back. Make a fist of the right hand and hold the right wrist with the left hand. Relax the shoulders completely.

Practice:

  1. Inhaling: bend backward from the waist, opening up the chest and lifting the sternum. Allow the thoracic spine to extend.
  2. Exhaling: slowly bend forward from the waist, bringing the forehead toward the ground in front of the knees. Collapse the shoulders deliberately — let them fall passively inward. Continue to exhale completely.
  3. Inhaling: slowly come up to the vertical (seated) position and then lean slightly backward.
  4. This forms one complete round. Continue for 10 rounds slowly.

Notes:

  • Keep the shoulders completely collapsed and relaxed, especially in the forward bend.
  • Knees may be separated slightly to avoid unnecessary pressure on the abdomen and chest during the forward fold.
  • Synchronise the breathing with the movement precisely — movement should follow breath, not the other way around.
  • Keep the eyes closed and maintain breath awareness throughout.

Duration/rounds: 10 rounds; approximately 2–3 minutes.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

Extension phase (inhalation):

  • Erector spinae: extend the thoracic and lumbar spine
  • Serratus posterior superior and intercostals: facilitate ribcage elevation during inhalation
  • Anterior thorax (pectorals, anterior intercostals): stretched during the backward lean

Flexion phase (exhalation):

  • Gluteus maximus: eccentrically controls the forward fold in kneeling
  • Erector spinae: eccentrically lengthened during the forward bend
  • Abdominal organs: compressed between the thighs and chest in full Sasankasana
  • Lumbar discs: the forward fold in the kneeling position decompresses the posterior disc annulus and gently stretches the posterior longitudinal ligament

3. Physiological Effects

Lumbar decompression on exhalation

In Sasankasana (full forward fold), the weight of the trunk folds over the fixed thighs. This:

  • Produces gentle traction on the lumbar spine, separating the vertebral segments
  • Reduces posterior disc compression — the opposite of the anterior compression produced by typical seated slouching
  • Stretches the paraspinal musculature and thoracolumbar fascia

The synchronisation of this decompression with the exhalation phase is therapeutically significant: at end-exhalation, the diaphragm is relaxed and the intrathoracic pressure is lowest, allowing the lumbar stretch to be received without muscular resistance.

Parasympathetic activation

The forward-fold position with collapsed chest compresses the abdominal viscera against the thighs. This mechanical stimulation of the vagus nerve through intra-abdominal pressure changes is one of the most reliable non-pharmacological parasympathetic activators in IAYT practice. The response includes:

  • Slowing of the heart rate
  • Reduction in sympathetic muscle tone
  • Subjective sense of calm and inner withdrawal

Breath training

The alternation between chest-opening (inhalation) and chest-compression (exhalation in forward fold) trains the full thoracic excursion of the breath, correcting the common dysfunction of restricted exhalation in anxious or tense breathing patterns.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — lumbar decompression, thoracic spinal mobilisation, abdominal organ compression.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the forward-fold exhalation is associated in IAYT literature with deepening of apana vayu (downward-moving vital energy) and the activation of Svadhisthana and Muladhara energy centres.

Tertiary: Manomaya Kosha — the inward-turning posture of Sasankasana produces pratyahara (sense withdrawal), supporting meditative stillness.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain (A004) Lumbar decompression; posterior chain stretching; gentle facet mobilisation
Anxiety / Depression (A006) Strong parasympathetic activation on the exhalation-forward-fold; calming effect on the nervous system
Asthma / Respiratory Disorders (A007) Full thoracic excursion; chest compression on exhalation trains complete exhalation
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Abdominal organ compression stimulates pancreatic and digestive function
Hypertension (A005) Parasympathetic activation lowers blood pressure; forward fold reduces cardiac preload

6. Contraindications

  • Severe knee problems (acute meniscal injury, arthritic knees): Vajrasana base may not be comfortable; practice from a kneeling pad or avoid if kneeling causes pain
  • Pregnancy (third trimester): the abdominal compression of full Sasankasana is not appropriate; practice only the backward extension phase
  • Acute vertigo or cervical problems: head-lowering should be cautious; keep the forehead on a block rather than the floor if needed

7. Variations & Progressions

Static Sasankasana hold: Instead of a dynamic 10-round practice, hold the full forward position for 1–3 minutes as a restorative posture — used in the anxiety and hypertension protocols as an extended parasympathetic practice.

Related: Rabbit Breathing (P0063) is the non-synchronised seated counterpart in which rapid chest-breathing (panting) occurs in the partial Sasankasana position.
