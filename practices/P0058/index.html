Hand Stretch Breathing

Hand Stretch Breathing (Hasta Prasarana Svasana)

A three-stage breath-movement exercise performed in Tadasana with interlocked fingers. The hands are stretched forward, diagonally upward, and finally vertically overhead — each stage progressively activating a higher zone of the chest, culminating in clavicular (upper-lobe) breathing. A key practice for patients with asthma, COPD, or habitual thoracic restriction.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana — stand erect, feet together. Gently bring both hands to the front of the chest, interlock the fingers, and place the palms against the sternum. Collapse and relax the shoulders. Close the eyes.

Stage I — Horizontal:

  1. Inhaling: stretch the interlocked arms straight forward at shoulder level, simultaneously twisting the hands so the palms face outward. Fully extend the arms without strain.
  2. Exhaling: reverse the movement, bringing the palms back onto the chest. Collapse the shoulders again.
  3. Repeat 5 rounds.

Stage II — At 135°:

  1. Inhaling: stretch the arms diagonally upward in front of the body at approximately 135° (between horizontal and vertical, roughly at forehead level).
  2. Exhaling: return the palms to the chest.
  3. Repeat 5 rounds.

Stage III — Vertical:

  1. Inhaling: stretch the arms directly overhead so they are fully vertical. As the hands move up and down, the interlocked palms pass very close to the tip of the nose.
  2. Exhaling: bring the palms back to the chest.
  3. Repeat 5 rounds.

Notes:

  • Collapse the shoulders at the beginning and end of each cycle.
  • Maintain perfect awareness of the breathing throughout.
  • Exhalation should be slightly longer than inhalation.
  • May also be performed seated in a chair when Tadasana is not possible.

Duration: 15 rounds total (5 per stage); approximately 2 minutes.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Upper trapezius and serratus anterior: active in Stage III as arms reach overhead; elevate the shoulder girdle and ribs
  • Sternocleidomastoid and scalenes: in Stage III, assist in lifting the upper ribs — the anatomical basis of clavicular (apical) breathing
  • Intercostals: progressively recruited from lower to upper ribcage across the three stages
  • Latissimus dorsi and teres major: eccentrically loaded as arms extend overhead, gently stretching the lateral thorax

Breathing zones targeted

  • Stage I → Thoracic (mid-lobe) breathing — horizontal arm extension laterally expands the mid-chest
  • Stage II → Transitional zone — engages both thoracic and upper-lobe recruitment
  • Stage III → Clavicular (upper-lobe) breathing — overhead arm stretch activates the scalenes and lifts the uppermost ribs

3. Physiological Effects

Progressive lung recruitment

The three-stage design systematically opens the three zones of the lung from base to apex. In healthy breathing, all three zones should contribute; in obstructive conditions (asthma, COPD), the upper lobes are frequently under-ventilated. By mechanically linking arm elevation to inhalation, the exercise retrains the neuromuscular pathways for upper-lobe recruitment.

Intercostal stretching

As the arms are extended with interlocked fingers — especially in Stage III — the stretch is transmitted through the latissimus dorsi, teres major, and lateral intercostals, producing a therapeutic stretch across the lateral chest wall. This is particularly valuable in patients with rib restriction following prolonged bed rest or thoracic kyphosis.

Shoulder girdle relaxation

The deliberate "collapse of the shoulders" on exhalation teaches the patient to release chronic shoulder-elevation tension (upper trapezius holding), which is a common somatic marker of anxiety and chronic respiratory insufficiency.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — mechanical stretching of intercostals, shoulder girdle, and ribcage across all three stages.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — Stage III activates udana vayu (upward-moving vital energy associated with the throat and upper chest).

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Asthma / Respiratory Disorders (A007) Primary indication; opens all three lung zones; stretches bronchi; counters barrel-chest posture
Anxiety / Depression (A006) Shoulder relaxation on exhalation; rhythmic overhead movement releases upper trapezius tension
Hypertension (A005) Slow overhead arm movement with breath synchronisation lowers sympathetic tone
Back Pain / Cervical issues (A004) Stage II and III gently traction the cervical and upper thoracic spine via arm elevation

6. Contraindications

  • Frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis): Stage III (overhead) may not be achievable; limit to Stage I or II until mobility improves
  • Severe cervical spondylosis: Stage III extension of the overhead arm may be modified by keeping the arms slightly forward of vertical
  • No significant contraindications for Stages I and II

7. Variations & Progressions

Chair-seated version: Same three stages performed seated — equally effective for bed-bound or elderly patients.

Progression: Slow the count to extend the inhalation phase (4–6 counts per stage) to build the breath duration and increase the therapeutic stretch.
