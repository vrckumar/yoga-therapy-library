Shoulder Movement

Shoulder Movement (Skandha Chakra)

Forward and backward arm circles — full circumduction of the glenohumeral and shoulder girdle complex. The primary shoulder mobilisation exercise in the Sithilikarana series, activating all four rotator cuff muscles and distributing synovial fluid across the large articular surface of the glenohumeral joint.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana (standing).

Practice:

  1. Stand erect with arms by sides.
  2. Raise both arms sideways, making large forward circles — arms go forward, up overhead, backward, and down in a full circle. The circles should be as large as possible.
  3. Perform 10 rounds forward (anterior circumduction).
  4. Reverse: 10 rounds backward (posterior circumduction).
  5. Relax the arms. Relax in Sithila Tadasana.

Breathing: Inhale on the upward arc; exhale on the downward arc. Or breathe normally throughout.

Duration: 10 rounds each direction.

2. Anatomical Activation

Forward circles (anterior circumduction)

  • Anterior deltoid: initiates forward flexion phase
  • Pectoralis major: assists forward and downward arc
  • Supraspinatus: initiates abduction through the first 15°
  • All rotator cuff muscles: dynamically stabilise the humeral head throughout the arc
  • Serratus anterior: protracts the scapula during the forward/upward arc

Backward circles (posterior circumduction)

  • Posterior deltoid: initiates extension phase
  • Latissimus dorsi: assists downward arc from overhead
  • Infraspinatus and teres minor: external rotation component in the upper arc
  • Trapezius (lower fibres): depresses scapula in downward arc
  • Rhomboids: retract scapula in backward arc

3. Physiological Effects

Glenohumeral synovial fluid distribution

The shoulder joint has the largest ROM of any joint in the body — approximately 180° of elevation, 60° of extension, and near-360° of circumduction. This enormous range means that the articular cartilage covers a large area, and synovial fluid must be distributed across a correspondingly large surface. Full circumduction is the only movement that comprehensively covers all articular zones.

The glenohumeral joint is particularly prone to rotator cuff impingement syndrome at the subacromial space — the gap between the humeral head and the acromion. Full circumduction exercises the shoulder through the impingement zone, which paradoxically is the treatment for impingement in the early stages: restoring normal glenohumeral rhythm prevents the humeral head from rising and impinging against the acromion.

Rotator cuff activation

The four rotator cuff muscles (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis) function as the dynamic stabilisers of the glenohumeral joint — they prevent superior migration of the humeral head during deltoid contraction. Shoulder circles require the rotator cuff to activate cyclically through all positions of the arc — maintaining their neuromuscular activation and preventing the rotator cuff atrophy that predisposes to tears.

Scapular mobility

The scapula must rotate upward 60° for every 120° of glenohumeral elevation — the scapulohumeral rhythm. Shoulder circles train this coupled movement, mobilising the acromioclavicular and sternoclavicular joints and activating the serratus anterior and trapezius in their scapular rotation roles.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — glenohumeral joint comprehensive mobilisation; rotator cuff activation; scapulohumeral rhythm training; subacromial space decompression.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the shoulders govern vyana vayu (outward-diffusing vital force) in yogic physiology; shoulder opening directly influences the expansion of prana through the upper body.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Shoulder (A003) Glenohumeral synovial fluid distribution; ROM maintenance; rotator cuff reactivation
Frozen Shoulder (Adhesive Capsulitis) — subacute Progressive circumduction is the primary physical therapy for frozen shoulder
Rotator Cuff Impingement Restores scapulohumeral rhythm; prevents superior humeral head migration
Rounded Shoulders / Poor Posture Posterior shoulder muscles activated during backward circles
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) Shoulder warm-up before all arm-based postures

6. Contraindications

  • Acute rotator cuff tear — full circumduction with resistance may worsen the tear; gentle pendulum exercises are preferred in acute phase
  • Acute frozen shoulder — avoid forcing full range; work within pain-free range only
  • Unstable shoulder (recurrent dislocation) — large arm circles may approach the impingement direction; medical assessment first
  • Acute subacromial bursitis — full abduction through the impingement arc may aggravate

7. Variations & Progressions

Large bilateral circles (standard): Both arms simultaneously as described.

Alternating (variation): One arm circles forward while other circles backward — challenges coordination.

Pendulum (modified — Codman's): Lean forward, let arms hang, gentle gravity-assisted pendulum circles — used for frozen shoulder when full active circles are too painful.

Sequencing context: First shoulder exercise. Progresses to P0021 (Shoulder Rotation — more detailed), then P0022 (Shoulder Blade Tightening).
