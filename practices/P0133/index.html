Chair Neck Movement in Vajrasana (Neck Exercises with Chair in Vajrasana Position)

A variant of chair-supported neck exercises in which the practitioner sits in or approximates a Vajrasana (kneeling on heels) position while using the chair for support. In the Yoga-Chair-Breathing protocol for asthma (Steps 2 and 3), the patient first performs neck movements seated on the chair (chair-supported), then transitions to sitting on the heels in front of the chair without chair support — two complementary stages of progressive independence. This entry documents the transitional practice that bridges chair-supported neck movement and independent floor-seated practice.

1. Technique

Context: This practice appears as Step 3 of the Yoga-Chair-Breathing (YCB) sequence for asthma management.

Starting position:

  • Sit on the heels in Vajrasana position (kneeling, sitting back on the heels) in front of the chair, without using the chair back for support.
  • Hands may rest on the knees or hold the edge of the chair for balance if needed.

Practice (same movements as Chair Neck Movement, P0132, but now without chair back support):

1. Neck movements only (5 times):

  • Move the head forward and backward in a relaxed, unhurried way. No breath synchronisation.

2. Neck movements with breathing (5 times):

  • Inhale while moving the head backward (cervical extension).
  • Exhale while moving it forward (cervical flexion).
  • Synchronise perfectly.

3. Neck movements with 'U-kara' (5 times):

  • Chant the "U" sound (OO vowel) in a low voice while bending the head forward.
  • The vibration of the U-kara resonates in the throat and chest region — activating the Vishuddhi chakra and the laryngeal nerve endings.

2. Progressive Independence Design

The Yoga-Chair-Breathing sequence moves in deliberate stages:

  1. Step 2: Neck movements with chair support (seated on chair, head resting on chair if needed)
  2. Step 3 (this practice): Neck movements without chair support, in Vajrasana — the patient demonstrates sufficient stability to proceed without the chair back

This graduated independence is the therapeutic design principle — the chair is progressively withdrawn as the patient's capacity improves.

3. Anatomical Activation

Same as P0132 (Chair Neck Movement) plus:

  • Knee and ankle joints: the Vajrasana position requires comfortable kneeling on the heels — this position itself provides a mild quadriceps and ankle stretch
  • Core postural muscles: without chair back support, the erector spinae and abdominal muscles must work to maintain the upright position during neck movements

4. Physiological Effects

Progressive proprioceptive challenge

Performing neck movements without the support of the chair back requires the core muscles to stabilise the trunk against the reactive forces of head movement. This is a modest but meaningful increase in proprioceptive demand compared to the fully supported chair version — a step toward the independence needed for unaided floor practice.

Sound vibration therapy (U-kara)

The addition of "U-kara" (the OO vowel sound) on exhalation-forward-flexion produces:

  • Vibration in the chest and thoracic cavity — stimulating the vagus nerve and bronchial walls
  • Gentle nasal and sinus resonance — soothing to the inflamed bronchial mucosa in asthma
  • A parasympathetic calming effect through the auditory-vagal pathway

5. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — cervical mobilisation; core stabilisation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the U-kara chant activates udana vayu (upward-moving energy in the throat and chest).

Tertiary: Manomaya Kosha — the progression from chair-support to independent sitting embodies the therapeutic theme of progressive self-reliance.

6. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Asthma (A007) Primary context — Step 3 of the Yoga-Chair-Breathing protocol
Cervical Spondylosis (A004) Transition practice between chair and floor
Elderly/rehabilitation patients Gradual re-establishment of independent sitting

7. Contraindications

Same as P0132 plus:

  • Cannot kneel (knee replacement, severe knee OA): remain on chair; skip the Vajrasana transition
  • Balance impairment: keep hands on chair for security
