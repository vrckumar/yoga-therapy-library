Surya Namaskar

Surya Namaskar (Sun Salutation)

Sūrya (Sanskrit) — sun; Namaskāra — salutation, prostration. The Sun Salutation is a flowing 12-position dynamic sequence combining forward bends, backward bends, a lunge, a plank, and a prostration position in a single continuous cycle synchronised with the breath. Each round is performed with one of 12 Bīja (seed) mantras and the associated name of the Sun God. The YIC Manual describes it as "a combination of different Āsanas involving speed" that belongs to the dynamic type of yoga.

In IAYT, Surya Namaskar occupies a unique position as the primary cardiovascular conditioning and full-body mobilisation intervention in the Obesity protocol, and a key warm-up and metabolic activator in the Diabetes protocol. It is the only practice that provides cardiovascular conditioning, full-body joint mobilisation, and neuro-endocrine stimulation within the asana framework, completing what no single static posture can achieve.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tāḍāsana. Stand erect with legs together. Begin with Praṇāmāsana — bring the palms together in Namaste Mudrā at the chest, eyes softly closed. The round begins with the utterance of the appropriate Bīja mantra and the name of the Sun God.

The 12 Positions

Position 1 — Hastautthānāsana (Inhale) Take the arms above the head while inhaling; bend the trunk backward. Biceps touch the ears, palms face forward. Open the chest fully; gaze upward and slightly back. Anatomical effect: Thoracic extension; anterior chest opening; lung apex expansion; spinal axial traction.

Position 2 — Pādahastāsana (Exhale) Bend the body forward while exhaling. Touch the forehead to the knees. Place the palms flat on the floor on either side of the feet. Knees remain straight throughout. Anatomical effect: Hamstring stretch; spinal forward flexion; abdominal organ compression; mild cranial inversion.

Position 3 — Aśvasañcālanāsana (Inhale) Inhale and kick the right leg back. The right knee may rest on the floor in the beginner version; the right toes are on the floor, right shin inclined backward. The left leg is perpendicular to the ground (knee directly above the foot). Push the pelvis forward and downward. Look upward, arch the spine. Anatomical effect: Right hip flexor (iliopsoas) maximum stretch; left quadriceps strengthening; thoracic extension.

Position 4 — Caturanga Daṇḍāsana (Exhale) Exhale and take the left leg also back, resting only on palms and toes. Keep the body in a straight line from head to toes, inclined at approximately 30° to the ground. Neck in line with the back. Anatomical effect: Full core and shoulder isometric strengthening; plank position; bilateral hip-flexor and anterior body activation.

Position 5 — Śaśāṅkāsana (Exhale and normal breathing) While inhaling, bend the legs at the knees and rest them on the floor; buttocks rest on the inside surface of the feet with the heels touching the sides of the hips, without altering the positions of the palms and toes. Exhale as the forehead rests on the floor. Relax in normal breathing. Anatomical effect: Lumbar decompression; quadriceps and hip flexor release; adrenal quieting; spinal counterpose.

Position 6 — Aṣṭāṅga Namaskāra (Exhale and Bāhya Kumbhaka — external breath retention) Exhale; without shifting the positions of hands and toes, glide the body forward and hold the breath externally. Eight points of the body rest on the ground: forehead, chest, both hands, both knees, and both toes. The buttocks are raised off the ground. Anatomical effect: Spinal extension begins at the lower vertebrae; chest compressed; hip flexors and wrists loaded.

Position 7 — Bhujaṅgāsana (Inhale) Inhale; raise the head and trunk, making the spine concave upward without lifting the position of the hands and feet. Arch the back as far as possible until the elbows are straight (or semi-straight). Keep the knees off the ground. Anatomical effect: Thoracic spinal extension; chest opening; erector spinae strengthening; adrenal stimulation.

Position 8 — Parvatāsana (Exhale) Exhale; raise the buttocks, push the head downward until the heels approach the ground without shifting the position of hands and feet. The body forms an inverted V. Anatomical effect: Hamstring stretch; calf stretch; shoulder traction; mild inversion; Achilles tendon lengthening.

Position 9 — Śaśāṅkāsana (Exhale and normal breathing) Same as position 5 — repeat the child's pose transition. Anatomical effect: Second lumbar decompression; hip flexor release; transition phase.

Position 10 — Aśvasañcālanāsana (Inhale) Inhale and bring the right leg forward between the two hands, so the right knee is above the right foot. Arch the back concave upward as in position 3, but now with the right leg forward. Anatomical effect: Left hip flexor stretch (opposite side from position 3); bilateral balance of hip flexor lengthening over the complete round.

Position 11 — Pādahastāsana (Exhale) Exhale and bring the left foot forward to meet the right foot. Bring the upper body down to touch the forehead to the knees, palms on floor, as in position 2. Anatomical effect: Bilateral hamstring stretch; abdominal compression; cranial inversion.

Position 12 — Hastautthānāsana (Inhale) Take the arms above the head while inhaling and bend the trunk backward, as in position 1. Return to Praṇāmāsana on the exhale; then to Tāḍāsana Sthiti. Anatomical effect: Thoracic extension; anterior chain re-opening after the sustained forward flexion.

This completes one round. A minimum of 3 rounds constitutes a therapeutic practice session. In the Obesity protocol, progressive practice builds toward 12–24 rounds.

The prolonged pronunciation of Oṃkāra followed by the Bījākṣara 'Ha', and the sounds 'R' and 'M' which come in every mantra, influence and stimulate the nerve centres in the brain corresponding to the respiratory, circulatory, and digestive systems.

Breathing pattern summary:

  1. Inhale | 2. Exhale | 3. Inhale | 4. Exhale | 5. Exhale+Normal | 6. Exhale+Hold | 7. Inhale | 8. Exhale | 9. Exhale+Normal | 10. Inhale | 11. Exhale | 12. Inhale

Note: In the 10-position Sūryanamaskāra, positions 5 and 9 (Śaśāṅkāsana) are omitted.

2. Anatomical Activation — Position-by-Position Summary

Every major joint and muscle group in the body is addressed in one complete round:

Body region Positions primarily addressed
Spine — extension 1, 3, 7, 10, 12
Spine — flexion 2, 5, 8, 9, 11
Shoulders / chest 1, 4, 6, 7, 12
Hip flexors 3, 10
Hamstrings 2, 8, 11
Core / plank 4, 6
Wrists / arms 4, 6, 7, 8
Inversion / cranial 2, 8, 11
Relaxation / counterpose 5, 9

The bilateral symmetry across 12 positions ensures that both hips are stretched (position 3 stretches the right hip; position 10 stretches the left) and both directions of spinal flexion and extension are fully loaded.

3. Physiological Effects

Cardiovascular conditioning — the primary Obesity protocol mechanism

Surya Namaskar, when performed at a moderate-to-brisk pace (3–6 rounds per minute), constitutes a cardiovascular aerobic exercise equivalent to brisk walking at 4–5 km/h. The large muscle mass engaged — quadriceps, hamstrings, gluteals, deltoids, pectorals, paraspinals — creates an oxygen demand sufficient to raise the heart rate to 60–80% of maximum in a fit individual, producing genuine aerobic training adaptation.

In the Obesity protocol, where the primary physiology is caloric imbalance (energy intake > expenditure), Surya Namaskar addresses the expenditure side directly. Twelve rounds of Surya Namaskar performed at a moderate pace burns approximately 150–200 kcal — the equivalent of 30 minutes of aerobic exercise — while simultaneously providing all the joint mobility, spinal health, and organ-stimulation benefits of the individual asanas. No other single yoga practice achieves this combination.

Cardiorespiratory adaptation with regular practice:

  • Resting heart rate decreases (improved cardiac stroke volume)
  • VO₂ max improves (enhanced oxidative capacity of the muscles)
  • Lung ventilatory efficiency improves (through the repeated inflation-deflation of the full respiratory cycle with each position change)
  • Blood lipid profile improves (elevated HDL, reduced triglycerides, reduced LDL)

Neuro-endocrine coordination — the full-body hormonal arc

The 12 positions of Surya Namaskar produce a systematically varied hormonal stimulus that no single posture can replicate:

  1. Backward bends (positions 1, 3, 7, 10, 12): activate the sympathetic nervous system and stimulate the adrenal cortex; cortisol and epinephrine secretion increases acutely during each extension phase
  2. Forward bends (positions 2, 8, 11): activate baroreceptors (head below heart), triggering parasympathetic (vagal) activity; heart rate slows, insulin sensitivity increases
  3. Plank and prostration (positions 4, 6): isometric large-muscle loading stimulates growth hormone secretion (particularly in young practitioners), testosterone, and insulin-like growth factor
  4. Transition phases: the rapid changes between flexion and extension cycle the sympathetic-parasympathetic balance at 1–2 second intervals, training the autonomic nervous system to switch between states rapidly and completely — the mechanism for improved heart rate variability documented in SVYASA studies

This systematic alternation of sympathetic activation (backbends) and parasympathetic activation (forward bends) within a single round, repeated 3–24 times in a session, trains the autonomic nervous system to respond quickly, completely, and proportionately — the fundamental mechanism of the "regulatory effect" on all psychosomatic conditions.

Joint mobilisation — comprehensive therapeutic mechanism

Every major synovial joint in the body is taken through its full range of motion in one round of Surya Namaskar:

  • Spinal joints: full extension and full flexion with axial loading
  • Hip joints: full extension (positions 3, 10), full flexion (positions 2, 5, 9, 11)
  • Knee joints: full extension (positions 2, 8, 11) and flexion (positions 4, 5, 6)
  • Shoulder joints: full elevation and extension (positions 1, 7, 12), compression (positions 4, 6)
  • Ankle joints: dorsiflexion and plantar flexion across multiple positions
  • Wrist joints: full dorsiflexion loading (positions 4, 6, 7, 8)

This systematic joint mobilisation stimulates synovial fluid production, cartilage nutrition, ligament tensile strength, and joint proprioception — explaining the therapeutic value in Arthritis and joint-stiffness presentations.

Metabolic activation for Diabetes

The combination of large-muscle aerobic exercise, abdominal organ compression, and abdominal organ stimulation produces multiple parallel effects on glucose metabolism:

  1. Peripheral glucose uptake: working muscle (quadriceps, hamstrings, deltoids) takes up glucose via the insulin-independent GLUT-4 pathway during exercise; blood glucose falls during and immediately after Surya Namaskar
  2. Hepatic glucose regulation: the abdominal compression of positions 2, 3, 10, 11 (where the abdominal contents are compressed) stimulates hepatic portal circulation and hepatic glucose extraction
  3. Pancreatic stimulation: positions 3 and 10 (the lunge positions) briefly compress the retroperitoneal pancreatic region; the multiple abdominal compressions over 12 positions produce a cumulative pancreatic stimulation effect
  4. Insulin sensitisation: the increase in muscle GLUT-4 receptor expression following regular exercise training improves peripheral insulin sensitivity for 24–48 hours post-session

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — comprehensive full-body joint mobilisation; cardiovascular conditioning; abdominal organ compression and release; posterior and anterior spinal alternating strengthening.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the synchronised breath and movement pattern makes Surya Namaskar an intensive pranayama practice; each position links to a specific breath phase, creating a systematic expansion and compression of the entire pranic field; the 12 mantra-linked positions represent a complete cycle of solar energy through the body from sunrise (position 1) to full illumination and return (position 12).

Tertiary: Manomaya Kosha — the mantra, the solar devotion, and the focused breath-synchronised movement of Surya Namaskar engage the emotional-mental (bhakti) dimension; it is simultaneously asana, pranayama, and a form of moving meditation.

6. Contraindications

  • Severe hypertension (uncontrolled): the strenuous aerobic demand and the inverted positions in forward bends are contraindicated
  • Acute back pain / disc prolapse: avoid the dynamic loading of spinal extension and flexion in active disc herniation
  • Cardiac decompensation: the aerobic demand is equivalent to moderate exercise; not appropriate in acute cardiac insufficiency
  • Wrist pain (severe): the plank and prostration positions load the wrists in full dorsiflexion; modify with fists or forearm support
  • Pregnancy (second trimester onward): the progressive abdominal compression and prone positions are unsuitable; prenatal yoga substitutions recommended

7. Variations & Progressions

Beginner (3 rounds, slow pace): Learn each position separately before linking them in sequence. Begin with 3 slow rounds (3–4 minutes), focusing on correct breathing synchronisation.

Standard therapeutic dose (IAYT): 3–6 rounds. Maintains all benefits with manageable exertion for clinical populations.

Obesity protocol progressive target: Build from 3 rounds over weeks to 12–24 rounds (20–30 minutes of sustained Surya Namaskar practice), at which point the cardiovascular conditioning effect becomes therapeutically significant.

10-position variation: Omit positions 5 and 9 (Śaśāṅkāsana); suitable when knee flexion is restricted or in dynamic flow contexts requiring higher pace.

Mantra-integrated practice: Chanting the full Bīja mantra and solar name at the start of each round adds the manomaya (mental-emotional) and sound resonance dimensions, elevating the practice from physical exercise to integrated yoga.
