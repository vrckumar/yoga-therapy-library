Sit-up

Sit-up (Utthana Prasarana)

Yogic sit-up from supine — the yoga therapy version emphasises slow, breath-synchronised spinal articulation rather than rapid, repeated crunches. Functions as core strengthening and spinal segmental mobilisation simultaneously.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine Posture. Arms stretched above the head (biceps by ears).

Practice:

  1. Lie flat with arms extended overhead (or by sides — arms by sides is easier).
  2. While exhaling slowly, draw the chin toward the chest; then sequentially curl the spine upward — head, then neck, then thoracic spine, then lumbar spine — until the trunk is upright (seated position). Arms reach forward to parallel the legs.
  3. In the seated position, exhale fully. Stretch the arms forward.
  4. While inhaling, slowly uncurl back to supine — lowering the lumbar spine first, then thoracic, then head — reversing the articulation.
  5. Repeat 5–10 times.

Note: This is the yogic (Patanjali-style) sit-up — slow, sequential spinal articulation, not a rapid flexion movement. The head is the last to rise and first to lower.

Breathing: Exhale throughout the rise; inhale throughout the lowering. Long, controlled breath synchronised with slow movement.

Duration: 5–10 repetitions.

2. Anatomical Activation

Rising phase

  • Rectus abdominis: primary trunk flexor throughout the rise
  • Internal and external obliques: stabilise the trunk rotation; provide oblique trunk flexion force
  • Hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris): terminal phase of the rise from floor to seated (approximately 40° to 90° hip flexion)

Lowering phase (more therapeutically significant)

  • Rectus abdominis (eccentric): controls the rate of descent — the eccentric loading is the primary strengthening stimulus in slow controlled sit-ups

3. Physiological Effects

Sequential spinal articulation — the yogic mechanism

The yogic sit-up, performed as segmental spinal articulation, sequentially loads each intervertebral segment through flexion from the bottom (sacrum) upward. This sequential loading has a specific therapeutic function:

  1. Each intervertebral level is mobilised through a brief flexion-extension cycle as the spine curls and uncurls
  2. The multi-fidus segmental stabilisers are activated at each level in sequence
  3. The abdominal muscles work through their full range of motion — from fully elongated (supine) to fully shortened (seated)

Core strengthening — slow vs fast

Rapid crunches train high-velocity rectus abdominis contraction. Slow yogic sit-ups train the endurance component — the ability to maintain abdominal activation through a full range of motion, which is more relevant to posture and back pain than the rapid-contraction pattern.

Distinction from aggressive crunches

The yogic sit-up is distinguished from exercise crunches by: (1) full spinal ROM (full rise to seated, not just head off floor); (2) slow breath-synchronised movement; (3) sequential spinal articulation. This approach avoids the cervical strain and intervertebral disc compression associated with rapid, repeated flexion.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — rectus abdominis and oblique strengthening; sequential spinal articulation; abdominal organ compression and release.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the full-range abdominal engagement activates the manipura (solar plexus) energy centre through direct stimulation of the agni (fire) region of the navel.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain (A004) Abdominal core strengthening — the primary conservative back pain intervention
Obesity (A002) Abdominal muscle activation; visceral fat reduction
Diabetes (A001) Abdominal compression stimulates pancreatic and digestive organ function
Constipation / GID Abdominal compression and release improves colonic motility
Postural Dysfunction Core strengthening supports lumbar spine and prevents kyphotic collapse

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar disc herniation — sit-up significantly increases intradiscal pressure; contraindicated
  • Osteoporosis (vertebral) — spinal flexion loading on osteoporotic vertebral bodies; avoid in severe cases
  • Post-abdominal surgery — avoid until wound healing complete (minimum 6 weeks)
  • Diastasis recti — full sit-up increases intra-abdominal pressure and may worsen the diastasis; use modified (partial) version

7. Variations & Progressions

Arms by sides (easier): Shorter lever arm — less demand on rectus abdominis.

Arms forward (standard): As described — moderate lever arm.

Arms overhead (advanced): Longer lever arm — greatest abdominal demand.

Partial sit-up (modified): Rise only to 30° (head and shoulder blades off floor) — reduces intradiscal pressure; appropriate for early back pain rehabilitation.

Sequencing context: Final exercise in the aerobic/dynamic group (P0036–P0041). Transitions to the supine leg group (P0042 onward).
