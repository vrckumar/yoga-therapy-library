Salabhasana Breathing

Salabhasana Breathing (Locust Pose Breathing)

The dynamic version of Salabhasana (Locust Pose), performed as a repetitive breath-movement exercise in which both legs are raised on inhalation and lowered on exhalation. The legs-up phase powerfully activates the lumbar erectors, gluteals, and posterior hip muscles while stimulating the abdominal organs via floor compression. Unlike Bhujangasana Breathing (which works the upper body), Salabhasana Breathing works the lower half — complementing it in the standard prone exercise sequence. Includes both the single-leg (Ardha Salabhasana) and full bilateral version.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Prone posture — lie face-down with legs together, hands stretched out overhead with chin on the floor. Then make fists of both palms (thumbs tucked inside) and place them underneath the thighs for support.

Type I — Ardha Salabhasana Breathing (single leg):

  1. Inhaling: raise the right leg upward as far as comfortable without bending the knee. The left leg remains straight and relaxed on the floor.
  2. Exhaling: slowly return the right leg to the floor.
  3. Repeat with the left leg in the same way.
  4. This is one round. Repeat 10 rounds.

Type II — Salabhasana Breathing (both legs):

  1. Inhaling: raise both legs together as far as comfortable without bending at the knees.
  2. Exhaling: bring them back to the floor slowly and with control.
  3. This is one round. Repeat 10 rounds.

Notes:

  • Take firm support of the fists under the thighs to reduce the compression on the abdomen and to assist the lift.
  • Do not bend the knees at any point.
  • The leg on the floor (in Stage I) should remain relaxed and straight.
  • Do not strain or exert excessively.
  • Synchronise the movement and breathing; keep eyes closed and maintain breath awareness.
  • Practice Type I before Type II until sufficient strength is developed.

Duration/rounds: 10 rounds per type.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Gluteus maximus: primary hip extensor; concentrically active in raising the legs
  • Hamstrings (semi/biceps femoris): assist hip extension
  • Erector spinae (lower lumbar portion): strongly active to counteract the tendency for the pelvis to tilt anteriorly during leg raise
  • Deep lumbar stabilisers (multifidus, quadratus lumborum): protect the lumbar spine during the asymmetric single-leg version
  • Abdominal organs: pressed against the fist-supported thighs, receiving a massage on each lowering phase

Bilateral vs. single-leg

  • Single-leg (Ardha): lower demand; allows the contralateral lumbar stabilisers to work asymmetrically — useful for detecting and correcting side imbalances in back pain rehabilitation.
  • Both legs: significantly higher demand on the lumbar extensors and abdominal wall; produces stronger abdominal organ compression.

3. Physiological Effects

Lumbar extensor and gluteal strengthening

Salabhasana Breathing provides the strongest direct load on the posterior hip and lower lumbar chain of all the prone breathing exercises. The dynamic leg-raise cycle:

  1. Concentrically strengthens the gluteus maximus in its shortened range (legs up)
  2. Eccentrically trains the slow-lower control (legs down) — the eccentric phase most important for functional movement and injury prevention

Abdominal organ stimulation

The fists under the thighs create a firm base against which the abdominal organs are compressed during the leg-raise phases. The repeated compression-release cycle across 10 rounds produces a mechanical massage to the descending colon, sigmoid colon, and reproductive organs. This is the anatomical basis for its indication in GID and reproductive health protocols.

Pelvic and sacral mobilisation

The alternating single-leg version in Ardha Salabhasana creates a repeated torque at the sacroiliac joint, mobilising the SI junction and stretching the piriformis and deep hip external rotators — muscles that are chronically tight in sedentary patients and a common source of referred posterior leg pain.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — posterior hip and lumbar strengthening; abdominal organ massage; lower body activation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the prone, lower-body activation is associated with apana vayu (downward-moving, eliminative vital energy) in the pelvic and sacral region.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Back Pain (A004) Primary indication; gluteal and lumbar extensor strengthening; sacroiliac mobilisation
Diabetes Mellitus (A001) Abdominal organ compression (pancreatic region, liver); adrenal activation
GID / Constipation (A014) Lower abdominal organ massage; pelvic floor stimulation
Obesity (A002) Strong posterior chain activation; organ stimulation
All IAYT protocols Standard component of prone breathing exercise sequence

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lumbar disc prolapse with radiculopathy: leg-raise produces a strong posterior chain activation and may aggravate nerve root compression
  • Recent abdominal surgery: avoid prone position and abdominal compression for 2–3 months
  • Pregnancy (second trimester onward): prone position not appropriate
  • Severe hypertension: the Valsalva-like effort of lifting both legs may briefly spike blood pressure; use single-leg version with slow, coordinated breathing instead

7. Variations & Progressions

Progression from Ardha to full Salabhasana breathing: Begin with Type I (single leg, 10 rounds) until gluteal strength is sufficient, then add Type II (bilateral, 10 rounds).

Related static practice: Salabhasana (full hold) — the static version in which the raised position is maintained for up to 1 minute.
