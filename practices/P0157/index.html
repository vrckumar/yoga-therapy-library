Mayurasana

Mayurasana (Peacock Pose)

Mayūra (Sanskrit) — peacock. In the posture, the entire body is held horizontally in the air, balanced solely on two hands with the elbows pressing deeply into the abdomen at the navel — the body spreads out like a peacock's tail. Of all the classical hatha yoga postures, Mayurasana applies the most intense and concentrated mechanical pressure to the abdominal viscera and pancreas. In SVYASA's IAYT framework, it is the single most powerful posture for digestive activation and is designated as a primary intervention for Diabetes Mellitus, where its documented effects on post-prandial blood glucose and pancreatic function are most fully realised. The source material (YIC Manual 2) notes it is "very good for diabetes" and "vitalizes the endocrines in the abdomen."

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrāsana.

Practice:

  1. The first four steps are identical to Hamsasana (see P0156):

    • Stand on the knees approximately 10 inches apart
    • Place the hands between the knees, fingers pointing toward the feet, elbows close together
    • Bend the elbows and lean forward; press the elbows into the navel region; lower the head to the floor
    • Extend the legs back, toes on the floor

  2. From the Hamsasana preparation position, move the body forward on the toes, progressively shifting the centre of gravity forward over the hands.

  3. Continue this forward shift until the feet lift off the ground spontaneously — the entire body is now balanced horizontally on the two hands, elbows pressing the abdomen, body parallel to the floor.

  4. Keep the entire body rigid as a plank; the legs extend fully straight and are held together, knees tight. Look forward.

  5. Maintain the final position for as long as comfortable with normal breathing.

  6. Lower the feet back to the floor; sit back in Vajrasana; relax in leg-stretch position.

Important: In the final position, the muscles of the abdomen (not the chest) support the weight of the body on the elbows. The elbows function as a fulcrum; the legs and body form the lever arm.

Breathing: The elbows press into the diaphragm region, so breathing is primarily thoracic during the hold. Breathe in the upper chest.

Duration/rounds: 5–20 seconds (early practice) to 30+ seconds (advanced); 1–2 rounds per session.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Wrist flexors and extensors: the entire weight of the body (50–80 kg) is balanced on two small palm-surfaces; the wrist is in full dorsiflexion — the loading is extreme and requires progressive wrist conditioning
  • Triceps brachii: fully loaded to maintain elbow near-extension against the full body weight
  • Serratus anterior: prevents scapular winging under the very high shoulder-loading; critical for stability
  • Deltoids (anterior): loaded at the end range of shoulder extension (arms behind the shoulder in the body-forward lean)
  • Core complex (entire): the straight-body position requires maximum isometric engagement of all core muscles to prevent sagging or piking
  • Gluteus maximus and hamstrings: isometrically active to maintain the straight leg position
  • Deep neck flexors: maintain the horizontal head position

Organs and structures directly affected

  • Celiac plexus and aorta: the elbows press into the epigastric-umbilical region, directly compressing the anterior abdominal wall at the level of the abdominal aorta, celiac axis (T12–L1), and superior mesenteric artery (L1)
  • Pancreas: the tail and body of the pancreas extend from the duodenal curve to the splenic hilum along the posterior upper abdomen; the elbow pressure is applied precisely over this region
  • Stomach and duodenum: the gastric body and the duodenum are compressed; gastric motility is stimulated
  • Liver, spleen: receive indirect compression as the elbow pressure transmits through the abdominal wall
  • Liver portal circulation: the compression of the porta hepatis (the entrance of the portal vein and hepatic artery into the liver) by the forward-directed elbow load stimulates portal venous cycling

3. Physiological Effects

The abdominal pressure mechanism — the most intense digestive activation in yoga

Mayurasana is anatomically distinguished from all other yoga postures by the fact that the entire body weight (minus the small fraction supported by the toes in Hamsasana) is concentrated into two small points — the elbows — which press perpendicularly into the abdominal wall at the navel region. The resulting intra-abdominal pressure is estimated to reach 100–150 mmHg in the fully balanced position — compared with 10–20 mmHg in standing and 30–50 mmHg in Hamsasana. This extreme pressure:

1. Pancreatic compression and release cycle: The pancreatic parenchyma, including both acinar cells (enzyme-producing) and the islets of Langerhans (insulin-producing), is directly compressed with each Mayurasana hold. Upon release:

  • Venous blood that was expelled from the pancreatic sinusoids rushes back, bringing fresh oxygenated blood
  • Pancreatic interstitial fluid is compressed and then refills, carrying nutrients and hormonal signals
  • The mechanical deformation of the acinar and islet cells provides a direct physical stimulus to secretory activity

This compression-release mechanism is the most direct physical stimulation of pancreatic function available in yoga practice. SVYASA clinical studies have documented significant reductions in fasting blood glucose and post-prandial glucose peaks in diabetic patients practising Mayurasana regularly. The mechanism proposed is the combination of:

  • Increased pancreatic blood flow (reactive hyperaemia)
  • Direct mechanical stimulation of islet cell insulin secretion
  • Enhanced insulin receptor sensitivity in hepatic and peripheral tissues following the abdominal circulatory normalisation

2. Digestive fire activation: In Ayurvedic-yogic terminology, jatharagni (digestive fire) is centred in the stomach and small intestine — the region directly under elbow pressure in Mayurasana. The intense mechanical stimulation is understood as a direct fanning of the digestive fire. Clinically, this manifests as:

  • Improved appetite in cases of digestive weakness (the source text notes: "Beneficial for poor appetite")
  • Reduction of constipation through direct mechanical stimulation of the sigmoid colon
  • Reduction of intestinal gas through mechanical displacement of accumulated gas in the colon

3. Detoxification through celiac plexus activation: The celiac plexus — the largest sympathetic nerve network in the body, often called the "abdominal brain" — governs the entire digestive organ function. Its nerve fibres innervate the stomach, small intestine, liver, spleen, kidneys, adrenals, and pancreas. The elbow compression directly overlies the celiac plexus ganglia. The mechanical stimulation produces a normalisation of sympathetic outflow to all these organs — reducing the adrenergic over-stimulation that characterises stress-related digestive disease and restoring parasympathetic-sympathetic balance.

Upper body and wrist strength

The extreme upper body demand of Mayurasana — comparable to a push-up with the body held at maximal abdominal compression — produces significant training adaptations in:

  • Wrist extensor strength and joint stability
  • Triceps and deltoid strength
  • Serratus anterior endurance (preventing long-term shoulder impingement)
  • Core isometric endurance

These adaptations are valuable in general toning protocols and contribute to the postural improvements that support all other IAYT interventions.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — extreme abdominal pressure stimulation of pancreas, liver, intestines, and celiac plexus; maximum wrist, arm, and core strengthening.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the elbow pressing into the manipura chakra (the seat of samana vayu and digestive fire/agni) constitutes the most direct physical stimulation of the solar plexus energy centre available in yoga; Mayurasana is described in Hatha Yoga Pradipika as capable of burning and neutralising all toxic foods and substances ingested.

6. Contraindications

  • Pregnancy: the direct abdominal compression and extreme wrist/core demand are absolutely contraindicated throughout pregnancy
  • Hypertension (uncontrolled): the isometric full-body exertion significantly raises blood pressure during the hold; avoid in uncontrolled hypertension
  • Hernia (any type): the extreme intra-abdominal pressure is the most severe contraindication — hernia can strangulate under this pressure
  • Abdominal surgery (within 3 months): healing abdominal fascia cannot withstand this pressure
  • Menstrual disorders (heavy): avoid during heavy menstruation
  • Peptic ulcer / acute gastritis: direct gastric compression may worsen active ulceration
  • Weak wrists: progressive wrist conditioning (through Hamsasana) is required before Mayurasana

7. Variations & Progressions

Foundation: Hamsasana is the mandatory preparatory posture. Do not attempt Mayurasana without first mastering the 30-second hold in Hamsasana.

Progressive approach to lift: In Hamsasana, progressively shift body weight forward by moving the toes incrementally toward the hands; as the centre of gravity passes anterior to the elbows, the feet will lift spontaneously. This gradual approach is safer than attempting a sudden kick-up.

Standard (as described): Full horizontal balance on two hands, body parallel to floor, 5–30 seconds.

Advanced (elevated leg variation): In the final balanced position, further elevate the legs and feet higher than the horizontal by tightening the gluteals and arching the lower back — this increases the abdominal compression as the balance point shifts further anterior.

Progression: Advanced practitioners proceed to Padma Mayurasana — Mayurasana with the legs in Padmasana (lotus position) — which changes the balance mechanics and adds hip flexibility as an additional prerequisite.
