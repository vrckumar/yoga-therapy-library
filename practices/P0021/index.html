Shoulder Rotation

Shoulder Rotation (Skandha Naman)

Full-range shoulder rotation with the elbow bent — placing the hands on the shoulders and rotating the elbow tips in large circles. This form specifically emphasises the internal/external rotation range of the glenohumeral joint and is the focused rotational complement to the arm circles of P0020.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Tadasana (standing).

Practice:

  1. Place the fingertips of both hands on the corresponding shoulders — right fingers on right shoulder, left fingers on left shoulder. Elbows point outward.
  2. Rotate both elbows simultaneously in large forward circles — elbows go forward, up, backward, and down.
  3. Make the circle as large as possible — elbows passing close to the ears at the top.
  4. Perform 10 rounds forward.
  5. Reverse: 10 rounds backward.
  6. Release arms and relax.

Breathing: Inhale on the upward arc; exhale on the downward arc.

Duration: 10 rounds each direction.

2. Anatomical Activation

Forward elbow rotation

  • Infraspinatus and teres minor (external rotators): engaged at the top of the arc as elbows pass overhead
  • Subscapularis (internal rotator): engaged in the downward arc
  • Deltoid (anterior, middle, posterior): cyclic activation throughout
  • Pectoralis minor: scapular protraction component
  • Serratus anterior: scapular upward rotation at top of arc

The hand-on-shoulder position changes the mechanics

With hands on shoulders, the forearm weight is eliminated and the rotation is led by the elbow — reducing shoulder muscle load and allowing greater focus on the glenohumeral internal/external rotation range. This makes shoulder rotation more accessible for weak or painful shoulders than the full arm-weight circles of P0020.

3. Physiological Effects

Glenohumeral internal and external rotation

The most clinically significant limitation in frozen shoulder and shoulder arthritis is loss of external rotation. External rotation occurs at the top of the circumduction arc (when elbows are overhead) and requires full posterior capsule elongation. By leading the movement from the elbow tip in a large circle, this exercise systematically stretches the posterior capsule at the apex of each rotation.

Acromioclavicular and sternoclavicular joint mobilisation

The scapula's full circumduction around the thorax — required for the elbow to complete the large circles — mobilises both the acromioclavicular joint (junction of clavicle and acromion) and the sternoclavicular joint (junction of clavicle and sternum). These joints are frequently stiff in individuals with chronic neck and shoulder tightness; their mobilisation is essential for full shoulder girdle function.

Capsular volume increase in frozen shoulder

In adhesive capsulitis, the glenohumeral joint capsule contracts in volume — reducing from approximately 30 ml to sometimes less than 10 ml. Gentle progressive circumduction exercises, beginning with small circles and increasing to large circles over weeks, is the standard conservative treatment for gradually re-expanding the capsular volume.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — glenohumeral internal/external rotation mobilisation; posterior capsule elongation; acromioclavicular and sternoclavicular joint mobilisation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the shoulder girdle opens the chest, expanding the pranic space around the heart and lung — the seat of prana vayu.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Frozen Shoulder / Adhesive Capsulitis Primary conservative exercise — progressive circumduction to expand capsular volume
Arthritis — Shoulder (A003) Internal/external rotation ROM maintenance; synovial fluid distribution
Rotator Cuff Impingement Posterior capsule stretching corrects the anterior tilt and superior humeral migration
Cervicogenic Shoulder Pain Shoulder girdle mobilisation relieves tension transmitted to cervical muscles
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) More targeted shoulder rotation than P0020; used for shoulder-specific conditions

6. Contraindications

  • Acute frozen shoulder — begin with small circles; increase range progressively as tolerated
  • Acute rotator cuff tear — avoid resisted or large-arc rotation until assessed
  • AC joint injury (acute) — the scapular circumduction loads the AC joint

7. Variations & Progressions

Small circles progressing to large (frozen shoulder protocol): Begin with elbow circles the size of a saucer; gradually increase over weeks to circles the size of a wheel.

Alternating arms (coordination variation): One elbow circles forward while other circles backward.

Sequencing context: Follows P0020 (arm circles). Progresses to P0022 (Shoulder Blade Tightening — scapular retraction).
