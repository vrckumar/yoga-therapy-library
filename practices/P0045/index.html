Side Leg Raising

Side Leg Raising (Parsva Uttanpadasana)

Supine abduction leg raise — raising the leg sideways from supine. Activates the hip abductors (gluteus medius, tensor fasciae latae) and adductor muscles, targeting the frontal plane hip musculature not addressed by the forward leg raises.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine Posture. Both legs together.

Practice:

  1. Lie flat. Keep the right leg extended.
  2. While inhaling, raise the right leg sideways (abduction) — lift it outward and upward to approximately 45°, maintaining the knee extended and the leg in a neutral rotation.
  3. While exhaling, lower the right leg to the floor.
  4. Repeat 10 times on the right. Then 10 on the left.

Breathing: Inhale on raising; exhale on lowering.

Duration: 10 repetitions per leg.

2. Anatomical Activation

Hip abduction (raising)

  • Gluteus medius: the primary hip abductor; originates from the lateral ilium, inserts on the greater trochanter — a key hip stabiliser in gait and single-leg stance
  • Gluteus minimus: assists abduction; internal rotation component
  • Tensor fasciae latae: hip abduction with internal rotation; connects to the iliotibial band

Hip adductors (lowering — eccentric)

  • Adductor longus, brevis, magnus, gracilis: eccentrically control the leg's return to the midline — the primary strengthening stimulus for the adductors

3. Physiological Effects

Gluteus medius strengthening

The gluteus medius is the most clinically important hip abductor — it stabilises the pelvis during single-leg stance (preventing contralateral pelvic drop, known as the Trendelenburg sign). Weak gluteus medius is associated with: knee valgus (inward knee collapse during landing), IT band syndrome, hip osteoarthritis, and patellofemoral pain. Side leg raising is the primary gluteus medius strengthening exercise.

Adductor eccentric conditioning

The controlled lowering phase trains the hip adductors eccentrically — the loading mode that most effectively strengthens and conditions the adductors and reduces adductor strain injury risk.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — gluteus medius strengthening; hip abductor and adductor conditioning; frontal-plane hip stability.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Hip (A003) Gluteus medius strengthening — reduces hip joint loading through improved stabilisation
Back Pain (A004) Gluteus medius weakness is a primary contributor to lumbar compensation; strengthening reduces lumbar load
Knee Pain / Patellofemoral Syndrome Gluteus medius controls knee valgus — indirect knee protection
IT Band Syndrome Hip abductor strengthening reduces IT band tension

6. Contraindications

  • Acute hip bursitis (trochanteric) — abduction movement engages the lateral hip structures over the greater trochanter
  • Hip labral tear — lateral movement at extreme range

7. Variations & Progressions

Prone form: Lying on one side; gravity assists the abductor — different muscle length relationships.

With ankle weight: Resistance progression for gluteus medius strengthening.

Sequencing context: Completes the straight leg raising sub-group. Progresses to the hip opening group (P0046 onward).
