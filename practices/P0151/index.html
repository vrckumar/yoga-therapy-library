Butterfly (Baddha Konasana / Full Butterfly)

Baddha = bound, kona = angle. The Butterfly pose — a seated posture in which the soles of the feet are pressed together with the heels drawn toward the perineum, and the knees are gently bounced up and down like the wings of a butterfly. The full butterfly (Purna Titali) is the primary hip adductor and groin-opening practice in the Sithilikarana Vyayama series. Also used as a preparation posture for Padmasana and advanced seated practices. The inner thigh muscles (hip adductors) and groin are the primary structures stretched; pelvic floor and reproductive organs are directly stimulated by the position.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Dandasana — sit with both legs extended.

Setup:

  1. Bend both knees and draw the feet in along the ground toward the body until the soles of both feet press firmly against each other.
  2. Draw the heels as close to the perineum (the centre of the pelvic floor) as comfortable.
  3. Completely relax the inner thigh muscles — let the knees fall outward toward the floor under gravity.

Stage I — Clasping the feet:

  1. Clasp both feet firmly with the fingers (hands interlaced around the feet).
  2. Gently bounce the knees up and down rhythmically, like butterfly wings flapping.
  3. Try to touch the knees to the ground on the downward stroke.
  4. Breathe normally throughout.
  5. Practice 30–50 up-and-down movements.

Stage II — Hands on the knees:

  1. Place both palms on the knees.
  2. Using the weight of the palms, gently push the knees downward toward the ground.
  3. Allow the knees to spring back up naturally (do not pull them up).
  4. Normal breathing.
  5. Repeat 20–30 times.

Notes:

  • Do not force the movements — the inner thigh muscles are sensitive and the groin is easily strained.
  • Keep the back, neck, and head as straight as possible throughout.
  • Keep the trunk steady — do not rock the trunk as the knees bounce.

Duration: 30–50 bounces (Stage I); 20–30 presses (Stage II).

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles stretched

  • Hip adductors (adductor magnus, adductor longus, gracilis): the primary target; the abducted knee position places these muscles under a progressive sustained stretch
  • Pectineus: the uppermost adductor; particularly stretched as the knees approach the floor
  • Iliopsoas (medial fibres): stretched in the hip externally-rotated position
  • Hip external rotators (piriformis, obturator group): eccentrically resisted as the knees fall outward

Pelvic floor and reproductive organs

The Baddha Konasana position (heels pressed to the perineum) places the pelvic floor muscles in a maximally relaxed, open position:

  • The pubococcygeus and iliococcygeus muscles are at their lengthened end range
  • The perineal body (central tendon of the perineum) receives gentle pressure from the heel contact
  • Blood flow to the internal reproductive organs (ovaries, uterus in females; prostate, testes in males) is enhanced

3. Physiological Effects

Hip adductor flexibility — the defining benefit

Hip adductor tightness is one of the most common musculoskeletal limitations in sedentary adults, contributing to:

  • Reduced hip abduction range (difficulty sitting cross-legged, climbing stairs)
  • Medial knee stress (the valgus force from tight adductors)
  • Pelvic floor dysfunction (tight adductors restrict pelvic floor flexibility)

The butterfly's repeated gentle bouncing provides an oscillatory stretch — more effective than a single sustained hold for releasing the stretch reflex in the adductors, particularly in patients with significant tightness.

Pelvic organ stimulation

The sustained Baddha Konasana position is one of the specific yoga poses used in gynaecological health protocols. The combination of heel-to-perineum pressure, hip abduction, and external rotation:

  • Promotes circulation to the pelvic organs
  • Reduces venous congestion in the pelvis (relevant for varicocele, pelvic congestion syndrome)
  • Is used in menstrual irregularity, infertility, and urinary tract health in IAYT and traditional Hatha practice

Preparation for Padmasana

Full Padmasana (lotus) requires deep hip external rotation and adductor flexibility. Regular butterfly practice progressively opens the range of hip abduction and external rotation needed for safe lotus position — making it the essential preparatory practice before attempting Padmasana.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — hip adductor stretching; pelvic floor stimulation; groin opening.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the Baddha Konasana position activates apana vayu (downward-moving vital energy) in the pelvic region — the pranic function associated with reproductive health, elimination, and the root chakra (Muladhara).

6. Contraindications

  • Groin injury or adductor strain: reduce range; do not bounce — use static hold only until healed
  • Knee ligament injury (medial): the abducted position places the medial knee under stretch; use padding and limit range
  • Severe hip arthritis: modify range; avoid forced bouncing

7. Relationship to Half Butterfly (Ardha Titali)

Feature Half Butterfly (Ardha) Full Butterfly
Legs One leg bent, one extended Both legs in Baddha Konasana
Primary stretch Single hip adductor Bilateral hip adductors
When used Early rehabilitation; less flexible patients Standard Sithilikarana Vyayama
Movement Up-down at the knee Up-down with both knees together

Half Butterfly is typically performed first, then Full Butterfly, as part of the progressive hip opening sequence in the Sithilikarana Vyayama warm-up.
