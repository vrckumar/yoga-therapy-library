Viparita Karani

Viparita Karani (Legs Up the Wall)

Viparita (Sanskrit) — inverted, reversed; karani — doing, action. The "action of reversing" — a modified inversion that places the legs above the heart and head without the cervical compression of Sarvangasana. It is the clinically accessible inversion for populations where full shoulder stand is contraindicated, and carries its own distinct therapeutic effects through partial gravitational reversal.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Supine, positioned sideways close to a wall (or beside a folded bolster for the wall-free version).

Practice:

  1. Lie on the back with the hips close to the wall. Swing the legs up and rest them against the wall, adjusting so the hips are as close to the wall as comfortable.
  2. Press the feet lightly into the wall and use the hands on the floor to lift the hips, supporting the lower back with both hands (thumbs pointing forward, fingers toward the spine).
  3. Rotate the trunk perpendicular to the wall so the body forms approximately a 45° incline to the floor — the legs are vertical on the wall, the trunk is inclined, and the hips are supported by the hands.
  4. The legs rest fully on the wall. The shoulders and upper back are on the floor. The neck is neutral — no chin lock is performed.
  5. Maintain for 3–5 minutes with slow, relaxed breathing.
  6. To release: bend the knees, push gently from the wall, lower the hips, and roll to one side before sitting up.

Breathing: Long, slow, diaphragmatic breathing throughout. The partially inverted trunk facilitates abdominal breathing.

Duration/rounds: 3–5 minutes; the extended hold distinguishes this from Sarvangasana and allows deeper parasympathetic engagement.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • Spinal erectors and quadratus lumborum: isometrically active to maintain the trunk inclination
  • Hip flexors (passive): the legs are supported by the wall; hip flexors are in a mid-length, relaxed position
  • Posterior leg muscles (passive): hamstrings, calf muscles, and plantar fascia are in a sustained passive stretch as the legs rest vertically
  • Diaphragm: in the inclined trunk position, the abdominal organs partially shift toward the chest, slightly increasing the load on the diaphragm — this is a mild diaphragmatic strengthening stimulus

Organs and structures affected

  • Thyroid and parathyroid: the partial inversion increases blood flow to the neck and head region, though to a lesser degree than Sarvangasana (no chin lock, lower angle of inversion)
  • Pelvic organs: partial inversion shifts the uterus, ovaries, bladder, and sigmoid colon superiorly — relieving pelvic floor pressure
  • Lower limb venous and lymphatic systems: the vertical legs allow complete gravitational drainage of both venous blood and lymph from the feet and calves upward
  • Abdominal organs: the inclined trunk changes the resting position of the bowel and mesentery, improving circulation to mesenteric vessels

Structural considerations

Unlike Sarvangasana, the cervical spine is in a neutral position — no compressive load is placed on the cervical vertebrae. The lumbar spine is supported by the hands. This makes Viparita Karani structurally safe for populations with cervical and lumbar conditions that prohibit Sarvangasana.

3. Physiological Effects

Venous and lymphatic drainage — the primary circulatory mechanism

Approximately 70% of total blood volume is in the venous side of the circulation at any given time, and the lower limbs contain a significant portion of this. Venous return is driven by skeletal muscle pumping (the "peripheral heart" of the calf), venous valves, and respiratory pressure changes. In upright sitting or standing, all of these mechanisms work against gravity for the lower limb circulation.

Viparita Karani eliminates this gravitational resistance completely for the legs:

  1. Venous blood drains by gravity from the dorsal foot veins through the plantar arch, up the anterior and posterior tibial veins, through the popliteal and femoral veins, and into the inferior vena cava — all passively and without muscular effort
  2. Similarly, lymph (which has no muscular pump of its own, only tissue pressure gradients and breathing) flows gravitationally from the interstitial spaces of the lower limbs into the thoracic duct
  3. Oedema fluid accumulated during the day in dependent lower limb tissues reabsorbs via osmotic and hydrostatic forces as the hydrostatic pressure gradient reverses

This is the direct mechanism for the stated benefits in varicose veins, leg oedema, and pelvic organ congestion — and explains why this posture is particularly appropriate after prolonged standing or sitting.

Thyroid and parathyroid — partial stimulation without compression

The partial inversion (trunk at ~45°) increases cerebral and neck blood flow compared to upright, through the same gravitational mechanism that makes full inversion so potent. However, without the jalandhara bandha of Sarvangasana:

  • Thyroid stimulation is gentler and more sustained (long hold at 45° vs. short hold at 90°)
  • The parathyroid glands receive a similar mild circulatory improvement
  • The carotid sinus is not significantly compressed — so the hypotensive reflex of Sarvangasana is absent or minimal

This makes Viparita Karani appropriate for mild hypertension (where the posture provides venous drainage benefit without the blood pressure risk of carotid sinus compression), while severe hypertension requires further modification.

Parasympathetic activation — the extended-hold mechanism

The long hold (3–5 minutes) combined with the passive, supported position, complete leg muscle relaxation, and reduction in proprioceptive input from the lower limbs produces a profound shift toward parasympathetic dominance:

  • Heart rate decreases (without the reflex-mediated drop of Sarvangasana)
  • Cortisol levels fall (the stress response is incompatible with the body's experience of supported passivity)
  • Digestive motility increases — parasympathetic activation of the enteric nervous system

This makes Viparita Karani a genuinely restorative posture in the physiological sense — it reduces allostatic load.

Pelvic organ health mechanism

For menstrual disorders and pelvic congestion, the inversion repositions pelvic organs that have been held in a gravitationally loaded position throughout daily life. The uterus, in anteflexed or retroflexed positions that cause menstrual cramping, is partially repositioned by inversion. The broad and round ligaments, which suspend the uterus, are stretched in the opposite direction to their chronic gravitational lengthening. The ovarian vasculature, which is prone to congestion in sedentary patients, receives improved drainage.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — gravitational reversal of lower limb venous/lymphatic flow; pelvic organ repositioning; thyroid circulation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — apana vayu (downward-flowing vital energy governing elimination and reproduction) is reversed toward the higher chakras; this is the energetic rationale for the use of inversions in reproductive and eliminative disorders in the yogic framework.

6. Contraindications

  • Severe hypertension — even partial inversion increases cerebral blood pressure; substitute recumbent rest with legs elevated on pillows (no trunk inclination)
  • Glaucoma / retinal conditions — any inversion increases intraocular pressure
  • Severe neck problems (acute phase) — the neck-neutral position is generally safe, but neck injuries require individual assessment
  • Pregnancy — inversions beyond first trimester require experienced guidance
  • Active menstruation — same individual assessment as for Sarvangasana applies

7. Variations & Progressions

Simplest (supported supine legs elevated): Legs resting on a chair, bolster, or wall with the trunk fully horizontal — no hand support needed. The trunk-at-floor level reduces the inversion angle but retains most venous drainage benefits.

Standard (as described): Trunk inclined ~45°, legs on wall, hands supporting lower back.

Extended restorative (with props): Bolster under the sacrum, legs up the wall, arms open — held for 10–15 minutes in a fully supported position. This is the yin/restorative yoga version and maximises the parasympathetic and venous drainage effects.

Progression to: Sarvangasana — the full shoulder stand, when cervical and cardiovascular conditions permit, provides more intense thyroid compression and chin lock effects.
