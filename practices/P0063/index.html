Rabbit Breathing

Rabbit Breathing (Shasha Svasana)

A forward-fold panting exercise performed from Vajrasana, with the forearms on the floor and the abdomen pressed against the thighs. Rapid thoracic breathing (only upper chest moves) is performed through the slightly open mouth with tongue partially protruded, mimicking the characteristic rapid, shallow breathing of a rabbit. Unlike Dog Breathing (which uses abdominal pumping), Rabbit Breathing isolates the thoracic muscles while mechanically suppressing abdominal movement. Followed by rest in full Sasankasana, producing a strong parasympathetic rebound. Used in respiratory and back pain protocols.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Vajrasana — sit on the heels with the spine erect.

Practice:

  1. Keeping the knees together, bend forward and rest both forearms flat on the floor. The elbows are placed beside (or just forward of) the knees. Palms are flat on the ground.
  2. Maintain the head at approximately one hand's-length distance above the ground (chin approximately one hand's width from the floor). Do not drop the head to the floor.
  3. Open the mouth partially. Protrude the tongue partially — the tongue should rest on the lower lip, touching the lower set of teeth.
  4. Fix the gaze at a point on the floor about 2 feet (60 cm) ahead.
  5. Pant quickly using only the upper part of the chest (thoracic muscles). Feel the air moving in and out of the lungs. The abdomen presses against the thighs throughout — this mechanically prevents abdominal movement, ensuring the practice is purely thoracic.
  6. Continue for 20–40 breaths.
  7. Close the mouth and relax fully in Sasankasana (forearms remain forward, forehead rests on the ground). Allow the breath to return to normal naturally. Feel the relaxation of the chest and thorax.

Notes:

  • Breathe rapidly through the mouth only (not the nose).
  • The abdomen must remain pressed against the thighs throughout — this is the key technique point that isolates thoracic breathing.
  • Do not drop the head to the floor during the panting phase.

Duration: 20–40 rapid breaths; followed by 30–60 seconds of rest in Sasankasana.

2. Anatomical Activation

Primary muscles engaged

  • External intercostals and anterior scalenes: primary movers of the upper and mid-thoracic cage in rapid thoracic breathing
  • Accessory respiratory muscles (pectoralis minor, sternocleidomastoid): recruited during rapid thoracic breathing
  • Diaphragm: partially inhibited by the abdominal compression against the thighs (the abdomen acts as a mechanical stop); the diaphragm must work in a shortened range
  • Tongue (partial protrusion): stretches the tongue root and stimulates oropharyngeal nerve endings

Position effects

The abdominal compression against the thighs in the forward-fold position:

  • Mechanically inhibits the abdominal (diaphragmatic) breathing zone
  • Forces all respiratory effort into the thoracic cage
  • Creates a mild intra-abdominal pressure increase, similar to a gentle Uddiyana Bandha effect

3. Physiological Effects

Isolated thoracic muscle training

By mechanically blocking abdominal breathing, this exercise forces the thoracic respiratory muscles to work independently — a targeted strengthening exercise for the intercostals and accessory respiratory muscles. This is directly beneficial in asthma, where the thoracic breathing muscles are often weak or underused.

Respiratory rate training and normalisation

The rapid breathing cycle followed by complete cessation (the Sasankasana rest phase) creates a clear contrast between sympathetic activation (panting) and parasympathetic recovery (forward fold). This oscillation trains the autonomic nervous system's capacity to reset, and is particularly useful in patients with habitually elevated respiratory rates.

Parasympathetic rebound after the panting

The transition from rapid panting to complete rest in Sasankasana produces a powerful parasympathetic rebound — heart rate slows markedly, muscle tension releases, and the thoracic muscles enjoy a deep passive rest. This dual-phase structure (activation followed by deep rest) is more effective than slow breathing alone for resetting the respiratory control centre.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — isolated thoracic respiratory muscle exercise; abdominal organ compression; oropharyngeal stimulation.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the thoracic panting activates vyana vayu (expansive energy in the chest); the subsequent Sasankasana rest deepens apana vayu downward-grounding.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Asthma / Respiratory Disorders (A007) Primary indication; thoracic muscle training; alternating activation-rest cycle for respiratory retraining
Back Pain (A004) Forward-fold position decompresses the lumbar spine; combined breath-movement
Anxiety / Depression (A006) The dramatic parasympathetic rebound in the Sasankasana recovery phase produces rapid calming

6. Contraindications

  • Severe asthma (acute attack): not during active bronchospasm; practice only in stable periods
  • Knee problems: Vajrasana base requires comfortable knee flexion; use padding if needed
  • Vertigo: the head-lowered position may aggravate vertigo; keep the head higher

7. Relationship to Similar Practices

  • Dog Breathing (P0062): uses the same sitting position but the spine is concave, tongue fully protruded, and the breathing is abdominal — the opposite of Rabbit Breathing's thoracic isolation
  • Sasankasana Breathing (P0061): the slow, synchronised forward-fold version; Rabbit Breathing uses the same posture but with unsynchronised rapid panting rather than synchronised slow breathing
