Elbow Bending

Elbow Bending (Kehuni Naman)

Full elbow flexion-extension — the sagittal-plane mobilisation of the humeroulnar and humeroradial joints. Trains biceps-triceps reciprocal activation, distributes synovial fluid across the elbow joint surfaces, and maintains the full functional range of the elbow required for all activities of daily living.

1. Technique

Starting position (Sthiti): Standing (Tadasana) or Seated. Arms extended by sides.

Practice:

  1. Stand with arms hanging loosely by the sides, palms facing forward (supinated).
  2. Slowly bend the right elbow, bringing the forearm upward — full flexion (hand approaches shoulder). Squeeze the biceps.
  3. Slowly extend the elbow back to the starting position — full extension. Feel the triceps engage.
  4. Perform 10 times on the right, then 10 on the left. Then 10 bilateral (both simultaneously).

Breathing: Exhale on flexion; inhale on extension.

Duration: 10 repetitions per arm.

2. Anatomical Activation

Elbow flexion

  • Biceps brachii: primary elbow flexor; also supinates the forearm when contracting from a neutral/pronated position
  • Brachialis: powerful elbow flexor — the "workhorse" flexor regardless of forearm rotation
  • Brachioradialis: elbow flexion with the forearm in neutral (midpronation)

Elbow extension

  • Triceps brachii (all three heads): the sole elbow extensor — long head, lateral head, medial head
  • Anconeus: assists terminal extension; stabilises the elbow joint

Joints mobilised

  • Humeroulnar joint: trochlear-notch articulation — the primary hinge of elbow flexion-extension
  • Humeroradial joint: capitulum-radial head articulation — participates in flexion-extension and forearm rotation
  • Proximal radioulnar joint: forearm rotation; mildly mobilised during the supination inherent in this exercise

3. Physiological Effects

Elbow joint synovial fluid distribution

The elbow joint has a large, confluent synovial cavity that communicates between the humeroulnar, humeroradial, and proximal radioulnar joints. The flexion-extension movement distributes synovial fluid across all articular surfaces — the trochlea and olecranon fossa (posterior), the capitulum and radial fossa (lateral), and the coronoid fossa (anterior). Stiff elbows from immobilisation or arthritis rapidly develop reduced fluid distribution to the posterior compartment (where the olecranon sits in the fossa at full extension) — the full extension component of elbow bending specifically addresses this.

Biceps-triceps coordination

The biceps and triceps constitute the primary agonist-antagonist pair of the upper arm — the paradigm of reciprocal inhibition in physical rehabilitation. Smooth, controlled elbow bending trains both the concentric (biceps in flexion) and eccentric (biceps in controlled extension) phases, as well as the triceps' concentric extension. This coordination is essential for carrying, lifting, pushing, and pulling movements.

Elbow epicondyle tendon conditioning

The common flexor tendon (medial epicondyle origin — golfer's elbow) and the common extensor tendon (lateral epicondyle origin — tennis elbow) are both subjected to mild cyclic loading during elbow bending. This tendon loading stimulates collagen alignment and tendon health maintenance.

4. Kosha Level

Primary: Annamaya Kosha — humeroulnar and humeroradial joint mobilisation; biceps-triceps activation and coordination; epicondylar tendon conditioning.

Secondary: Pranamaya Kosha — the arms are prana-carrying limbs in yogic anatomy; mobilising the elbow joint is understood as opening the mid-arm nadi junction.

5. Indicated For

Condition Reasoning
Arthritis — Elbow (A003) Full ROM mobilisation; synovial fluid distribution across all elbow joint compartments
Lateral Epicondylitis (Tennis Elbow) Common extensor tendon conditioning; elbow ROM maintenance
Medial Epicondylitis (Golfer's Elbow) Common flexor tendon conditioning
Post-fracture Elbow (subacute) ROM restoration — standard rehabilitation goal
All IAYT protocols (preparatory) Elbow preparation before all arm-based postures and pranayama

6. Contraindications

  • Acute lateral or medial epicondylitis — avoid repetitive loading in the acute phase; subacute phase: gentle ROM is acceptable
  • Acute elbow fracture — follow immobilisation and post-fixation protocol
  • Nursemaid's elbow (radial head subluxation) — requires reduction before any active movement

7. Variations & Progressions

Unilateral then bilateral (standard): As described.

Pronation variation: Perform with forearms pronated (palms facing backward) — changes the relative contribution of brachialis vs biceps; more functional for daily lifting tasks.

Resistance added (advanced): Light hand weights (0.5–1 kg) — biceps and triceps strengthening progression.

Sequencing context: Follows wrist exercises (P0015–P0017). Progresses to P0019 (Loosening of Elbow — circumduction). Together these complete the elbow mobilisation.
