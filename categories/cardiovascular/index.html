Cardiovascular Disorders

The Cardiovascular System

The cardiovascular system is the body's primary transport network. The heart, blood vessels, and approximately five litres of blood together deliver oxygen and nutrients to every cell while removing carbon dioxide and metabolic waste. Cardiovascular disease is now the leading cause of death and disability worldwide, and its close association with modern lifestyle — chronic psychological stress, sedentary behaviour, dietary excess, and tobacco — makes it uniquely suited to yoga therapy intervention.

Heart Anatomy

The heart is a muscular, pear-shaped organ the size of a clenched fist, located in the chest slightly left of the midline and protected by the rib cage. It contains four chambers: two thin-walled atria (receiving chambers) above and two thick-walled ventricles (pumping chambers) below. The left and right sides are separated by septa — the interatrial septum above and the interventricular septum below — ensuring that oxygenated and deoxygenated blood do not mix.

One-way flow is maintained by four valves:

  • Tricuspid valve — between right atrium and right ventricle
  • Pulmonary valve — at the exit of the right ventricle into the pulmonary artery
  • Mitral (bicuspid) valve — between left atrium and left ventricle
  • Aortic valve — at the exit of the left ventricle into the aorta

The cardiac wall consists of three layers: the epicardium (outer), myocardium (thick muscular middle layer, especially in the left ventricle), and endocardium (smooth inner lining).

The Cardiac Cycle

The heart's rhythmic contraction originates in the Sinoatrial (SA) node — the natural pacemaker — located where the superior vena cava meets the right atrium. Electrical impulses spread across both atria (causing atrial systole), reach the Atrioventricular (AV) node in the interatrial septum, pass down the Bundle of His and its left and right bundle branches, and then spread through the Purkinje fibres to cause ventricular systole. The cycle:

  1. Atrial systole — atria contract, filling ventricles
  2. Ventricular systole — ventricles contract; tricuspid and mitral valves close (first heart sound); blood is ejected into pulmonary artery and aorta
  3. Diastole — all chambers relax; pulmonary and aortic valves close (second heart sound); atria refill from venous return

Normal resting heart rate: 60–90 beats/minute (average 72). The SA node is regulated by the autonomic nervous system: sympathetic nerves increase heart rate and force; parasympathetic (vagal) nerves reduce both.

Cardiac Output

Cardiac output (CO) = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume. At rest, CO averages 5.5 litres/minute in adult males. Sympathetic activation increases both heart rate and stroke volume; parasympathetic activation reduces them. Venous return — the volume of blood returning to the right heart — is a key determinant of stroke volume (Frank-Starling law: greater stretch = greater force of contraction).

Blood Pressure Regulation

Blood pressure is the force exerted by circulating blood against vessel walls. Normal: systolic 90–140 mmHg, diastolic 60–90 mmHg (average 120/80 mmHg).

Three primary regulatory mechanisms:

  1. Neural regulation (baroreceptors) — Baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch detect changes in arterial stretch. When BP rises, they signal the vasomotor centre in the medulla to reduce sympathetic output: heart rate slows, vessels dilate, BP falls. This is the fastest mechanism (seconds).

  2. Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) — When renal perfusion falls, the kidneys release renin → converts angiotensinogen to angiotensin I → angiotensin II (ACE) → powerful vasoconstriction + aldosterone release (sodium and water retention) → BP rises. This mechanism operates over minutes to hours.

  3. Long-term fluid balance — ADH (vasopressin) regulates water reabsorption in the kidneys; atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) promotes sodium excretion when atria are overstretched.

Coronary Circulation

The heart muscle is supplied by the left and right coronary arteries, the first branches of the aorta immediately above the aortic valve. The left coronary artery divides into the left anterior descending (LAD) branch — supplying the anterior wall and septum — and the circumflex branch (lateral and posterior walls). The right coronary artery supplies the right ventricle, inferior left ventricle, and the AV node.

Anastomotic connections between coronary branches can enlarge when one branch is compromised, providing natural collateral supply — a process yoga therapy supports through vagal activation and improved microcirculation.

Disease Mechanisms in This Category

Cardiovascular diseases share common underlying pathophysiological mechanisms, all amplified by chronic psychosocial stress, sedentary lifestyle, and metabolic dysfunction.

Atherosclerosis

Atherosclerosis — the progressive narrowing of arteries by lipid-laden plaques — is the foundation of most ischaemic cardiovascular disease. The sequence: endothelial injury (from hypertension, smoking, hyperglycaemia, oxidative stress) → LDL accumulation in the intima → macrophage infiltration → foam cell formation → fibrous plaque → calcified, unstable plaque. Plaque rupture triggers thrombosis, which can cause complete arterial occlusion: myocardial infarction in coronary arteries or stroke in cerebral arteries.

Chronic stress accelerates atherosclerosis by promoting endothelial dysfunction, increasing LDL oxidation, and sustaining low-grade systemic inflammation.

Hypertension Pathophysiology

In primary (essential) hypertension — 90–95% of all cases — no single organic cause is found. Multiple converging factors:

  • Increased sympathetic drive → elevated heart rate and peripheral vascular resistance
  • RAAS hyperactivation → sodium retention and vasoconstriction
  • Endothelial dysfunction → impaired nitric oxide production → reduced vasodilation
  • Structural vascular remodelling → reduced arterial compliance
  • Chronic cortisol elevation → sodium retention, increased vascular sensitivity to catecholamines

Cardiac Output Dysregulation

In heart failure, the heart cannot maintain adequate cardiac output for the body's needs. This can result from systolic dysfunction (impaired contractility — post-infarction, cardiomyopathy) or diastolic dysfunction (impaired relaxation — hypertension, hypertrophy). Compensatory mechanisms (RAAS activation, sympathetic stimulation, ventricular remodelling) initially maintain output but ultimately accelerate deterioration.

Peripheral Vascular Disease

Atherosclerosis in peripheral arteries — typically the lower limb — reduces blood supply to skeletal muscle, causing intermittent claudication (pain on walking). Critical ischaemia produces rest pain and tissue necrosis. Venous insufficiency (varicose veins, DVT) impairs venous return and produces leg swelling, skin changes, and ulceration.

Yoga Therapy Rationale for This Category

The Pancha Kosha framework addresses cardiovascular disease at every level of the human system, with particular emphasis on the autonomic nervous system as the key mediating pathway between stress and cardiovascular function.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Dynamic asana practice, especially when performed in a flowing, breath-coordinated manner, provides moderate aerobic stimulation that improves cardiac efficiency, peripheral vascular tone, and venous return. Inversions (Viparitakarni, Sarvangasana) dramatically increase venous return to the right heart, providing the equivalent of autotransfusion and directly training cardiac preload tolerance. However, inversions require caution in uncontrolled hypertension and IHD.

Loosening exercises (Sithilikarana Vyayama) improve peripheral circulation and mobilise the ankle pump, aiding venous return in peripheral vascular disease.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Pranayama is perhaps the most powerful direct intervention in cardiovascular yoga therapy:

  • Nadi Suddhi (alternate nostril breathing) — corrects autonomic imbalance by alternating stimulation of sympathetic (right nostril) and parasympathetic (left nostril) pathways. Documented to reduce heart rate, blood pressure, and sympathetic nervous system reactivity.
  • Bhramari (humming bee breath) — the humming sound generates nitric oxide in the paranasal sinuses, which is inhaled into the pulmonary circulation, causing direct vasodilation. Simultaneously activates the vagus nerve through acoustic stimulation of the brainstem.
  • Slow deep breathing (Dirga Pranayama) — increases respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA), a marker of cardiac vagal tone. Regular slow breathing training produces lasting improvement in heart rate variability (HRV).
  • Kapalabhati — contraindicated in severe hypertension; used cautiously in mild cases.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

Cardiovascular disease has a well-established psychosomatic dimension: chronic mental stress, Type A personality traits, hostility, and anxiety are independent risk factors for both hypertension and coronary artery disease. The mental layer of yoga therapy directly addresses these:

  • Deep Relaxation Technique (DRT) — progressive neuromuscular and mental relaxation reduces sympathetic tone within a single session
  • Cyclic Meditation — documented to reduce blood pressure through its active-passive cycling structure, which produces deep psychophysiological rest
  • OM Meditation — sustained meditative practice reduces cortisol and catecholamine levels, reduces hypothalamic reactivity, and trains the mind toward equanimity

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

Lifestyle counselling — dietary education (low sodium, low saturated fat, high fibre), tobacco cessation, stress management skills — provides the intellectual framework for sustained risk reduction. Yoga philosophy's concept of Vairagya (non-attachment to outcome) is practically relevant for the chronic cardiovascular patient who must learn to engage with life without the chronic urgency that drives hypertension.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

At the deepest level, cardiovascular disease often reflects a life lived under relentless pressure — competitive achievement, financial anxiety, relational tension. Karma Yoga — acting in a state of inner equanimity, without compulsive craving for outcomes — addresses the existential root of the Type A lifestyle. Group devotional practices provide social regulation of the cardiovascular system through positive emotional states.
