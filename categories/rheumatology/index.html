Rheumatology & Musculoskeletal Disorders

The Musculoskeletal System

The musculoskeletal system provides the structural framework of the human body and enables all voluntary movement. It consists of 206 bones, over 600 skeletal muscles, numerous joints, tendons, ligaments, and connective tissues. It is intimately connected to the nervous system (neuromuscular control), the cardiovascular system (bone marrow haematopoiesis, vascular supply), and the immune system (joints as sites of inflammatory attack in autoimmune disease).

Joint Anatomy

A synovial joint — the most common and most clinically relevant joint type — consists of:

  • Articular cartilage — smooth hyaline cartilage covering the bone ends; avascular, nourished by synovial fluid; provides frictionless, shock-absorbing surfaces
  • Synovial membrane — vascular inner lining of the joint capsule; secretes synovial fluid (a viscous, acellular fluid rich in hyaluronic acid that lubricates and nourishes cartilage)
  • Joint capsule — fibrous outer layer providing mechanical stability
  • Ligaments — dense collagenous bands connecting bone to bone; prevent excessive movement and provide proprioceptive feedback
  • Bursae — fluid-filled sacs at points of friction between tendons and bone; reduce wear

Key structural proteins of connective tissue:

  • Collagen — provides tensile strength; Type II collagen predominates in cartilage; Type I in tendons and ligaments
  • Proteoglycans (aggrecan) — large hydrophilic molecules that attract and retain water, providing cartilage with compressive resistance and the ability to spring back after loading

Bone Structure

Bone is a dynamic living tissue continuously undergoing remodelling — the coupled processes of osteoclastic resorption and osteoblastic deposition. This balance is influenced by mechanical loading (weight-bearing exercise promotes deposition), hormones (parathyroid hormone, calcitonin, oestrogen, vitamin D), and nutritional factors.

Cortical bone (compact) forms the dense outer shell — 80% of skeletal mass, providing mechanical strength. Trabecular (cancellous) bone forms the inner spongy network — high metabolic activity; first site of osteoporotic loss. Bone marrow within the medullary cavity produces all blood cells (haematopoiesis).

Spinal Anatomy

The vertebral column consists of 33 vertebrae (7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral fused, 4 coccygeal) and provides axial support, housing and protecting the spinal cord, while allowing the mobility required for upright posture and movement.

Each vertebra consists of a body (weight-bearing cylindrical block), pedicles (projecting posteriorly from the body), laminae (connecting pedicles to form the posterior arch), spinous and transverse processes (muscle attachment points), and articular processes forming the facet joints with adjacent vertebrae.

Intervertebral discs lie between adjacent vertebral bodies. Each disc consists of:

  • Nucleus pulposus — soft, gelatinous centre (80% water in youth), acting as a hydrostatic shock absorber
  • Annulus fibrosus — concentric rings of Type I collagen providing tensile containment of the nucleus

With age and dehydration, the nucleus loses water content, reducing disc height and the disc's ability to distribute loads evenly across the annulus. Repeated asymmetric loading (sitting, bending, twisting) can create annular tears through which nucleus material herniates — compressing adjacent nerve roots and producing radiculopathy.

Paraspinal muscles — the erector spinae group (iliocostalis, longissimus, spinalis), multifidus, and deep stabilisers (transversus abdominis) — provide dynamic spinal support. Weakness or spasm in these muscles is both a cause and consequence of disc pathology and back pain.

Connective Tissue

Connective tissue permeates the entire body: skin, fascia, tendons, ligaments, cartilage, and bone are all variations on the same theme — cells embedded in an extracellular matrix of collagen, elastin, and proteoglycans. The proportions and arrangements of these proteins determine the mechanical properties of each tissue. In connective tissue disorders (Marfan's syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos), mutations in structural proteins produce hypermobility, fragility, and systemic dysfunction.

Disease Mechanisms in This Category

Autoimmune Inflammation (Rheumatoid Arthritis)

In rheumatoid arthritis (RA), self-reactive T lymphocytes and autoantibodies (rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP) target the synovial membrane. The resulting synovitis produces: synovial hyperplasia (pannus formation), release of proteolytic enzymes (matrix metalloproteinases) and cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6) → destruction of articular cartilage and bone erosions → joint deformity. The systemic inflammatory burden also accelerates cardiovascular disease.

The HPA-sympathetic-immune axis is critically relevant: chronic psychological stress maintains elevated cortisol but paradoxically fails to suppress the RA inflammatory cascade because synovial cells become glucocorticoid-resistant over time. Yoga therapy's reduction of the chronic stress burden offers genuine immunomodulatory benefit.

Degenerative Wear (Osteoarthritis)

Osteoarthritis (OA) results from failure of the cartilage repair/degradation balance. Risk factors: increasing age, obesity (mechanical overload), previous joint injury, and genetic predisposition. Progressive cartilage erosion → subchondral bone exposure → subchondral sclerosis and osteophyte formation → joint space narrowing → pain, stiffness, and loss of function.

Unlike RA, OA is not primarily autoimmune; however, low-grade synovial inflammation does occur and contributes to pain. Crucially, obesity is both a mechanical risk factor (load) and a metabolic one — adipose tissue secretes pro-inflammatory adipokines that drive cartilage degradation.

Disc Prolapse and Radiculopathy

Intervertebral disc herniation compresses nerve roots exiting the spinal canal, producing pain, paraesthesia, and weakness in the distribution of the affected root. L4/L5 and L5/S1 are the most common levels. The acute inflammatory response around the extruded nucleus further irritates the nerve root. Management combines pain relief, anti-inflammatory treatment, and crucially — rehabilitation of spinal stability and posture through a graduated programme that directly overlaps with yoga therapy's approach to back pain.

Paraspinal Muscle Spasm

Protective muscle spasm following acute injury or disc prolapse — while initially adaptive — perpetuates the pain cycle by maintaining compression of the disc and sensitising nociceptors. Breaking this spasm-pain cycle through relaxation techniques, heat application, and gentle movement (all components of yoga therapy) is a primary early management goal.

Yoga Therapy Rationale for This Category

The musculoskeletal system is perhaps the most directly addressable by yoga's physical practices — asanas are inherently exercises in joint mobility, muscle strengthening and stretching, proprioception, and postural alignment.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Sakti Vikasaka (dynamic joint exercises) — the systematic series of joint mobilisation exercises that begins every yoga therapy session — directly addresses joint stiffness, synovial fluid circulation, and the fear-avoidance behaviour that leads to progressive deconditioning in musculoskeletal disease. Each joint is taken through its full range of motion in a controlled, pain-free manner.

Asana for spinal health:

  • Flexion postures (Paschimottanasana, Balasana) — stretch posterior spinal muscles, open posterior disc spaces, reduce lordosis
  • Extension postures (Bhujangasana, Salabhasana, Dhanurasana) — strengthen paraspinal muscles, restore lumbar lordosis, encourage anterior disc loading which reduces posterior herniation risk
  • Twisting postures (Vakrasana, Ardha Matsyendrasana) — mobilise facet joints, reduce paraspinal muscle spasm

Weight-bearing practices — promoting bone density through mechanical loading (standing asanas, Surya Namaskara) stimulates osteoblastic activity and reduces osteoporosis risk.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Slow, diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system, reducing the sympathetic-driven inflammatory cascade. In RA, reducing sympathetic tone reduces synovial vascular permeability and inflammatory mediator release. Pranayama also promotes pain tolerance through endorphin release and shifts focus away from pain perception.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

Chronic musculoskeletal pain has a well-established psychological dimension: catastrophising, fear-avoidance, depression, and anxiety all amplify pain perception through central sensitisation. The pain modulation pathway (descending inhibitory system, endorphin-mediated) is directly activated by relaxation.

DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) reduces central sensitisation by promoting profound neuromuscular relaxation. Yoga Nidra has been used clinically to reduce opioid requirements in pain management. MIRT (Mind-body Integration and Relaxation Training) combines progressive relaxation with positive imagery to rebuild confidence in the body's capacity for movement.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

Education about the biomechanics of the spine, joint protection principles, ergonomics, and the role of body weight in joint loading provides patients with the intellectual tools for sustainable self-management. The yoga philosophical framework of Ahimsa (non-violence) applied to the body teaches appropriate practice boundaries — a crucial safeguard against aggravating joint conditions.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Chronic musculoskeletal disease often produces a sense of loss — of function, independence, identity, and capacity for enjoyment. Karma Yoga — finding meaning and purpose in service even within physical limitation — and the cultivation of Santosha (contentment) through meditation provide the deepest psychological support for long-term adaptation to chronic physical conditions.
