Respiratory Disorders

The Respiratory System

The respiratory system enables the exchange of gases — oxygen from the environment into the blood, and carbon dioxide from the blood back out — that sustains every cell in the body. Beyond this primary function, the respiratory system is the most direct physiological interface between the mind and the body. Breath is the only autonomic function that is also fully under voluntary control, making it the central instrument of yoga therapy across all disease categories.

Anatomy: Lungs and Bronchial Tree

The lungs are two large, spongy organs occupying the thoracic cavity, separated by the mediastinum (which contains the heart, great vessels, trachea, and oesophagus). The right lung has three lobes; the left has two (accommodating the heart). Together they contain approximately 300–500 million alveoli — microscopic air sacs where gas exchange occurs — providing a total surface area of 70–100 m².

The bronchial tree is the conducting airway that branches progressively:

  • Trachea (windpipe) — 10–12 cm long, C-shaped cartilage rings maintain patency
  • Primary bronchi — one to each lung
  • Secondary (lobar) bronchi — to each lobe
  • Tertiary (segmental) bronchi — to each bronchopulmonary segment
  • Bronchioles — no cartilage; patency maintained by smooth muscle and elastic tissue
  • Terminal bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles → alveolar ducts → alveoli

The walls of the bronchioles contain smooth muscle innervated by the autonomic nervous system:

  • Sympathetic (β₂ receptors) → bronchodilation (widening)
  • Parasympathetic (muscarinic M₃ receptors) → bronchoconstriction (narrowing)

The airway is lined with ciliated mucous epithelium — the mucociliary escalator. Goblet cells secrete mucus that traps inhaled particles; cilia beat rhythmically to propel this mucus upward for swallowing or expectoration. Disruption of this mechanism (by smoking, inflammation, or dehydration) impairs clearance and promotes infection.

The Diaphragm — Primary Muscle of Respiration

The diaphragm is a dome-shaped musculotendinous partition separating the thoracic and abdominal cavities. During inspiration, it contracts and descends, increasing thoracic volume and generating negative pressure that draws air into the lungs. It accounts for approximately 70% of resting tidal volume. Diaphragmatic breathing is the foundation of all pranayama practice.

Accessory muscles of inspiration: external intercostals, scalenes, sternocleidomastoid. Muscles of forced expiration: internal intercostals, rectus abdominis, obliques.

Gas Exchange: Ventilation-Perfusion (V/Q) Ratio

Gas exchange at the alveoli depends on two matched processes:

  • Ventilation (V) — air reaching the alveoli
  • Perfusion (Q) — blood flowing through alveolar capillaries

Optimal gas exchange requires that ventilated alveoli are adequately perfused (V/Q ratio ≈ 0.8). V/Q mismatch — areas that are ventilated but not perfused (dead space), or perfused but not ventilated (shunt) — impairs oxygenation. This is the primary mechanism of hypoxaemia in asthma, COPD, and pneumonia.

Respiratory Control Centres

Breathing is controlled by rhythmic neural centres in the brainstem:

  • Dorsal respiratory group (medulla) — sets the basic rhythm of inspiration
  • Ventral respiratory group (medulla) — active in forced breathing
  • Pneumotaxic centre (pons, upper) — limits inspiratory duration, regulates breathing rate
  • Apneustic centre (pons, lower) — promotes sustained inspiration; normally inhibited by the pneumotaxic centre

These centres receive input from central chemoreceptors (sensitive to CO₂/pH in cerebrospinal fluid) and peripheral chemoreceptors (carotid and aortic bodies, sensitive to O₂, CO₂, and pH). Rising CO₂ is the primary stimulus for increased breathing rate and depth.

Importantly, voluntary cortical control — breathing at will — can override these automatic centres. This is the physiological basis for pranayama's ability to modify autonomic function.

Bronchial Smooth Muscle: cGMP-cAMP Balance

The contractile state of bronchial smooth muscle is regulated by intracellular cyclic nucleotides:

  • cAMP (cyclic AMP) → muscle relaxation → bronchodilation
    • Raised by: β₂ adrenergic agonists (adrenaline, salbutamol), theophylline, deep breathing
  • cGMP (cyclic GMP) → muscle contraction → bronchoconstriction
    • Raised by: vagal stimulation, histamine, leukotrienes, cold air, exercise (in asthma)

Yoga therapy modulates this balance by training the breath away from sympathetic (constricting) patterns and toward slow, deep, parasympathetically toned breathing — while also reducing the psychological triggers that activate the allergy cascade.

Disease Mechanisms in This Category

Respiratory diseases share several common pathological processes, all of which yoga therapy can influence at various levels.

Bronchospasm

Contraction of bronchial smooth muscle narrows the airway lumen, increasing resistance to airflow and causing the characteristic wheeze of asthma. Triggers include allergens (pollen, dust, animal dander), irritants (smoke, cold air, perfumes), exercise, and psychological stress (the brain-airway connection via the vagus nerve is well established). Beta-2 receptors on smooth muscle cells respond to sympathetic stimulation with relaxation; cholinergic (vagal) stimulation causes contraction.

Mucus Hypersecretion

Chronic airway inflammation stimulates goblet cell hyperplasia and submucosal gland hypertrophy, producing excessive thick mucus that blocks smaller airways. This is prominent in asthma, COPD, and TB-damaged lung. Mucociliary clearance is impaired when mucus is too viscous or cilia are damaged. Yoga kriyas — especially Jala Neti and Kapalabhati — directly support mucociliary clearance.

Airway Remodelling

In persistent asthma, repeated cycles of inflammation and healing produce structural changes: thickening of the basement membrane, subepithelial fibrosis, smooth muscle hypertrophy, angiogenesis, and goblet cell metaplasia. These changes are partially irreversible and cause progressive loss of lung function — underlining the importance of early, consistent management including yoga therapy.

Allergy Cascade

The type I hypersensitivity response involves: allergen exposure → IgE production by B lymphocytes → IgE binding to mast cells → re-exposure to allergen → mast cell degranulation → release of histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins, and cytokines → immediate bronchospasm (minutes) and late-phase inflammatory response (hours). Chronic stress impairs regulatory T cell function, increasing allergy susceptibility.

Yoga Therapy Rationale for This Category

Pranayama is the primary domain of yoga therapy in respiratory disease. The respiratory system is uniquely accessible to yoga intervention because breath is both an autonomic function and a voluntary one — making it the principal gateway to modifying autonomic, immune, and psychological function simultaneously.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Kriyas for mucociliary clearance: Jala Neti (nasal saline irrigation) mechanically clears the nasal passages of allergens, mucus, and irritants — functioning as a form of bronchoprotection. Sutra Neti and Vamana Dhauti assist upper airway and oesophageal clearance. Kapalabhati as a kriya creates forceful exhalations that mobilise mucus in the bronchial tree.

Chest-opening asanas (Bhujangasana, Matsyasana, Ustrasana) expand the anterior thorax, improve rib mobility, and strengthen respiratory muscles. Sarvangasana inverts the lung fields, altering regional ventilation-perfusion relationships and draining postural mucus from dependent zones.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Pranayama is the primary domain for respiratory diseases:

  • Vibhagiya Svasana (sectional breathing) — trains all three respiratory compartments (abdominal, thoracic, clavicular), re-establishing efficient full-lung ventilation in patients who have adopted shallow, accessory-muscle-dependent breathing patterns
  • Nadi Suddhi — balances nostril dominance; the nasal cycle alternates ventilatory advantage between lungs and modulates autonomic tone
  • Bhramari — sound resonance in the sinuses generates nitric oxide (a bronchodilator); sustained exhalation lengthens expiratory time, reducing air trapping in obstructive conditions
  • Ujjayi — partial glottic closure creates controlled expiratory resistance, analogous to pursed-lip breathing in COPD, reducing the dynamic airway collapse that causes air trapping
  • Sitali / Sitkari — cooling, humidifying breath protects against exercise-induced and cold-air-triggered bronchospasm

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

The stress-bronchospasm link is direct and well-established: psychological stress activates the vagus nerve and histamine pathways, both of which trigger bronchoconstriction. Fear of breathlessness itself creates respiratory anxiety that tightens the airways in a vicious cycle. DRT and Cyclic Meditation reduce the hypothalamic-limbic reactivity that mediates this cycle. Breath-awareness meditation (Anapana Sati) teaches the patient to observe breathlessness without catastrophising it — fundamentally restructuring the psychological response to respiratory symptoms.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

Education about allergen avoidance, trigger identification, the role of stress in symptom provocation, and the mechanism of pranayama empowers patients to engage intelligently with their management. Recognition that respiratory health is intimately linked to emotional health enables sustained lifestyle change.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

OM chanting and devotional singing engage the expiratory muscles in sustained, resonant exhalation — providing both respiratory rehabilitation and deep psychophysiological relaxation through sound-mediated vagal activation. The experience of breath as the bridge between the individual self and the universal (Prana) provides a transformative relationship with the breath that sustains practice motivation.
