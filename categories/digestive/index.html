Digestive & Gastrointestinal Disorders

The Digestive System

The digestive system is responsible for the ingestion, digestion, absorption, and elimination of nutrients — transforming food into the molecular building blocks required for energy production, cellular repair, and physiological function. Beyond its metabolic role, the digestive system maintains a complex, bidirectional dialogue with the brain through the gut-brain axis, making it uniquely sensitive to psychological stress and a primary target for yoga therapy.

GI Tract Anatomy

The gastrointestinal tract is a continuous muscular tube approximately 9 metres long, extending from the mouth to the anus. Its wall consists of four layers:

  1. Mucosa — innermost layer; secretes digestive enzymes and mucus, absorbs nutrients
  2. Submucosa — connective tissue with blood vessels, lymphatics, and the submucosal (Meissner's) nerve plexus
  3. Muscularis — circular and longitudinal smooth muscle layers producing peristalsis; contains the myenteric (Auerbach's) nerve plexus
  4. Serosa/Adventitia — outer connective tissue covering

Oesophagus: 25 cm fibromuscular tube connecting pharynx to stomach; the lower oesophageal sphincter (LOS) prevents reflux of gastric contents.

Stomach: J-shaped muscular organ; capacity 1.5–2 litres. Contains:

  • Parietal cells — secrete hydrochloric acid (pH 1.5–2) and intrinsic factor
  • Chief cells — secrete pepsinogen (converted to pepsin by acid)
  • G cells — secrete gastrin (stimulates acid production) The stomach also has a third oblique muscle layer enabling the churning (mastication) that reduces food to chyme.

Small intestine: 5–7 metres; the primary site of nutrient absorption. Three segments:

  • Duodenum (25 cm) — receives pancreatic juice and bile; digestion primarily occurs here
  • Jejunum — primary absorption of carbohydrates, proteins, fats, and vitamins
  • Ileum — absorbs vitamin B₁₂-intrinsic factor complex and bile salts; contains Peyer's patches (lymphoid tissue — gut immunity)

Absorptive surface area is maximised by three structural features: circular folds (valvulae conniventes), villi (finger-like projections), and microvilli (brush border) — together providing ~200 m² of absorptive surface.

Large intestine (colon): 1.5 metres; absorbs water and electrolytes; houses the gut microbiome; produces and excretes faeces. Segments: caecum, ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid colon, rectum, anal canal.

Accessory Digestive Organs

Liver: The largest internal organ (1.5 kg), performing over 500 functions including:

  • Bile production (500–1000 mL/day) — emulsifies dietary fat for absorption
  • Glucose metabolism — glycogen storage and gluconeogenesis
  • Protein synthesis (albumin, clotting factors)
  • Detoxification of drugs, alcohol, and metabolic waste
  • Fat-soluble vitamin storage (A, D, E, K)

Pancreas: Dual function — digestive (exocrine) and hormonal (endocrine). The exocrine pancreas secretes 1.5–2 litres of alkaline pancreatic juice daily via the pancreatic duct into the duodenum, containing:

  • Amylase (digests starch)
  • Lipase (digests fat)
  • Trypsin, chymotrypsin (digest protein, activated in the duodenum)
  • Sodium bicarbonate (neutralises acid from the stomach)

The Gut-Brain Axis

The gut is sometimes called the "second brain." The enteric nervous system (ENS) — a network of approximately 100–500 million neurons embedded in the walls of the GI tract — can function independently of the CNS, governing peristalsis, secretion, and local immune responses. It communicates bidirectionally with the brain via:

  • Vagus nerve — parasympathetic supply to the gut; transmits signals from gut to brain (80% of vagal fibres are afferent, carrying gut-to-brain information rather than brain-to-gut)
  • Sympathetic nerves — originating from the thoracic and lumbar cord; inhibit gut motility during stress
  • Enteroendocrine cells — secrete hormones (serotonin, ghrelin, CCK) that influence appetite, mood, and gut motility centrally

Gut microbiome: The colon houses approximately 38 trillion microorganisms — comparable in number to human cells. This ecosystem (dominated by Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, and Actinobacteria) produces short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), vitamins (K, B₁₂), and neurotransmitter precursors (particularly serotonin — 90% of the body's serotonin is produced in the gut). Microbiome composition influences mood, immunity, metabolic function, and inflammatory tone throughout the body. Chronic stress, poor diet, and antibiotic use disrupt microbiome diversity — a mechanism linking psychological and digestive pathology.

Disease Mechanisms in This Category

Acid Hypersecretion and Peptic Ulcer Disease

The gastric mucosa is protected from its own acid by a "mucosal barrier" — mucus layer, bicarbonate secretion, tight epithelial junctions, and prostaglandin-mediated repair. When aggressive factors (acid, pepsin, H. pylori) overcome defensive factors, mucosal damage occurs. H. pylori (a gram-negative bacterium present in 50% of the world's population) disrupts mucus production, triggers inflammation, and stimulates gastrin release, creating a cycle of acid hypersecretion and mucosal injury. NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin synthesis, removing mucosal protection. Psychological stress directly increases acid secretion via vagal stimulation and cortisol-mediated suppression of prostaglandins.

H. pylori and Gastric Pathology

H. pylori colonises the gastric mucosa, secreting urease (which produces ammonia from urea, neutralising local acid to allow survival), proteases, and phospholipases. The resulting chronic active gastritis precedes peptic ulcer in the duodenum (acid-sensitive) and stomach, and — with prolonged infection — intestinal metaplasia and gastric carcinoma.

IBS: Gut-Brain Dysregulation

Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) is defined by recurrent abdominal pain associated with altered bowel habit (diarrhoea, constipation, or alternating) in the absence of identifiable structural or biochemical pathology. The mechanism involves:

  • Visceral hypersensitivity — lowered pain threshold in the bowel, mediated by sensitised enteric nociceptors
  • Altered gut motility — dysregulated peristalsis producing either diarrhoea (increased) or constipation (decreased)
  • Gut-brain axis dysregulation — bidirectional: psychological stress worsens gut symptoms; gut distress amplifies anxiety
  • Microbiome dysbiosis — altered bacterial composition influences ENS function, serotonin production, and local immune activation
  • Low-grade mucosal inflammation — some patients show increased mucosal mast cells and cytokines even without frank inflammatory bowel disease

Hepatic Inflammation

Hepatitis — inflammation of the liver — has multiple causes: viral (HAV, HBV, HCV), alcoholic, autoimmune, and drug-induced. Chronic inflammation leads to hepatocyte necrosis, stellate cell activation, fibrosis, and ultimately cirrhosis (loss of functional hepatic architecture). Portosystemic hypertension follows, with ascites, variceal bleeding, and hepatic encephalopathy as complications.

Yoga Therapy Rationale for This Category

The digestive system is uniquely positioned for yoga therapy intervention because: (1) abdominal asanas and kriyas directly stimulate digestive organs; (2) pranayama activates the vagus nerve — the primary neural regulator of digestive function; and (3) stress reduction through meditation directly removes the most potent driver of gut-brain dysregulation.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Pavanamuktasana series ("wind-relieving postures") — progressive supine movements (single leg raise, double leg raise, cycling, rocking) systematically mobilise the abdominal organs, stimulate peristalsis, and relieve gas accumulation. Particularly effective for IBS constipation, post-operative ileus, and general digestive sluggishness.

Abdominal compression asanas:

  • Ardha Matsyendrasana, Vakrasana — lateral compression of abdominal viscera massages the transverse colon, liver, and spleen
  • Dhanurasana — prone; anterior abdominal compression against the floor massages small intestine and colon
  • Paschimottanasana — anterior flexion; compresses abdominal organs against thighs; stimulates bowel motility

Kriyas for GI health:

  • Laghu Sankhaprakshalana — saline-assisted intestinal cleansing; resets the GI environment
  • Agnisara — rapid abdominal pumping; massages the viscera and improves hepatoportal circulation
  • Kunjal Kriya (Vamana Dhauti) — voluntary emesis to cleanse the upper GI tract; used in APD management

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Abdominal (diaphragmatic) breathing is the most important pranayama for digestive health:

  • Each breath cycle moves the diaphragm 1.5–7 cm vertically, transmitting mechanical pressure waves through the abdominal viscera
  • This "diaphragmatic massage" stimulates peristalsis, improves hepatoportal circulation, and reduces splanchnic congestion
  • The rhythmic intra-abdominal pressure changes also stimulate the vagus nerve's afferent fibres, sending gut-calming signals to the brainstem

Nadi Suddhi — shifts ANS balance from sympathetic (which inhibits digestion) to parasympathetic (which promotes "rest-and-digest" function): increases gastric acid output appropriately, enhances bile flow, promotes peristalsis.

Kapalabhati — the forceful exhalation creates intense abdominal pressure pulses; used therapeutically to stimulate sluggish digestion and hepatic function.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

The stress-digestion link operates through multiple pathways: sympathetic activation redirects blood away from the gut, inhibits motility, and increases visceral sensitivity. Cortisol suppresses mucosal prostaglandins, reducing protection from acid damage. Anxiety amplifies pain perception from the gut, creating the vicious cycle of IBS.

DRT after meals reduces post-prandial sympathetic activation, allowing the parasympathetic "rest-and-digest" state to facilitate optimal digestion. Yoga Nidra has demonstrated measurable effects on gastric acid reduction in APD patients. Cyclic Meditation reduces cortisol and improves gut-brain axis regulation.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

Dietary education — the satvik diet principle (fresh, light, easily digestible, vegetarian food consumed in appropriate quantity) — directly addresses the dietary causes of acid peptic disease, IBS, and fatty liver. Understanding the connection between emotional states and digestive symptoms empowers self-monitoring and prevents the escalation of flares.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

The yogic concept of Ahara (nourishment) extends beyond food to encompass the quality of all sensory inputs and the mental state in which food is consumed. Eating in a calm, mindful, grateful state — rather than anxiously, hurriedly, or while watching distressing media — is itself a therapeutic intervention with measurable effects on gastric acid, digestive enzyme activity, and post-meal gut comfort.
