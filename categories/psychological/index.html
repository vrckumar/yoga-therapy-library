Brain Neuroanatomy Relevant to Mental Health

Note: PGDYT 104 Block 5 does not include a dedicated anatomy section. The neuroanatomy presented here is synthesised from PGDYT 101 Block 2 (Nervous System) and general neurobiological knowledge to support the clinical yoga therapy rationale.

Mental health is rooted in the neurochemistry and neuroanatomy of the brain. Understanding how yoga therapy modifies brain function requires familiarity with the key structures and systems underlying mood, cognition, stress, and behaviour.

The Limbic System

The limbic system is the brain's emotional regulation centre — a collection of interconnected cortical and subcortical structures that process emotion, memory, motivation, and behaviour. Key components:

Amygdala: An almond-shaped structure in the medial temporal lobe — the brain's "alarm system." It detects potential threats (real or perceived), triggers the fight-or-flight response via the hypothalamus, and forms emotional memories. In anxiety disorders, the amygdala is chronically hyperactivated; in PTSD, it encodes traumatic memories with excessive emotional intensity. Meditation practice measurably reduces amygdala volume and reactivity.

Hippocampus: Adjacent to the amygdala; critical for forming new explicit (declarative) memories and for contextualising the amygdala's threat responses. Chronic stress impairs hippocampal neurogenesis and causes dendritic atrophy — a mechanism linking depression to impaired memory formation. Exercise and meditation promote hippocampal neurogenesis.

Cingulate gyrus: A cortical belt overlying the corpus callosum. The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) is involved in error detection, conflict monitoring, and emotional regulation — it exerts top-down inhibitory control over the amygdala. The ACC is activated by meditation practice, providing the neural substrate for the improved impulse regulation and equanimity seen in long-term practitioners.

Hypothalamus: Master integrator of the limbic system and the autonomic/endocrine systems. Regulates body temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep, and the HPA axis. In chronic stress, hypothalamic dysfunction sustains the pathological cortisol patterns underlying anxiety, depression, and metabolic disease.

Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)

The prefrontal cortex is the brain's executive centre — responsible for planning, decision-making, impulse control, working memory, and the regulation of emotional responses through top-down modulation of the amygdala and limbic system. The PFC is the last brain region to mature developmentally (not fully myelinated until the mid-20s) and the first to show atrophy in chronic stress and depression.

Sustained yoga and meditation practice increases PFC grey matter density, strengthens prefrontal-amygdala connectivity, and shifts the emotional regulation style from reactive (amygdala-driven) to reflective (PFC-mediated).

HPA Axis and Stress Neuroscience

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis is the body's principal stress response system:

  1. Perceived threat → amygdala activation → hypothalamus releases CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone)
  2. CRH → pituitary releases ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)
  3. ACTH → adrenal cortex releases cortisol
  4. Cortisol provides short-term adaptive benefits (mobilises energy, suppresses inflammation, enhances memory consolidation)
  5. Elevated cortisol signals negative feedback to hypothalamus and pituitary to reduce CRH and ACTH production

In chronic stress, this negative feedback loop becomes dysregulated: cortisol remains chronically elevated (or, paradoxically, becomes blunted in conditions of exhaustion), the hippocampus (which mediates negative feedback) atrophies, and the stress response becomes self-perpetuating. This HPA hyperreactivity is the neurobiological foundation of anxiety disorders, depression, PTSD, burnout, and many psychosomatic diseases.

Disease Mechanisms in This Category

HPA Hyperreactivity (Anxiety and Depression)

Generalised anxiety disorder and major depressive disorder — while clinically distinct — share a common neurobiological substrate: dysregulated HPA axis with impaired prefrontal-limbic connectivity. In anxiety, the amygdala is chronically overactive, producing persistent fear, hypervigilance, and somatic symptoms (palpitations, breathlessness, muscle tension). In depression, the failure of the PFC to modulate limbic activity produces anhedonia, cognitive slowing, and motivational collapse — alongside the neurovegetative features of disrupted sleep, appetite, and psychomotor function.

Chronic cortisol excess (anxiety) → hippocampal atrophy → impaired negative feedback → sustained HPA dysregulation. This is not merely psychological: it is a physically measurable change in brain structure that yoga therapy can partially reverse through sustained practice.

Dopaminergic Dysregulation (Schizophrenia)

The dopamine hypothesis of schizophrenia proposes hyperactivity of the mesolimbic dopaminergic pathway (producing positive symptoms: hallucinations, delusions, disorganised thought) alongside hypoactivity of the mesocortical pathway (producing negative symptoms: blunted affect, poverty of speech, social withdrawal). Modern antipsychotics primarily target D2 receptors. The aetiology involves genetic vulnerability, prenatal neurodevelopmental disruption, and environmental stressors that interact to produce clinical expression.

Yoga therapy in schizophrenia requires careful, structured, grounded approaches — avoiding stimulating or confusing practices that might exacerbate psychotic symptoms.

Habit Formation and Neural Circuits (OCD and Addiction)

OCD involves hyperactivity of the orbitofrontal-striatal circuit — a loop connecting the prefrontal cortex, striatum, and thalamus involved in habit formation and error detection. Excessive activation produces the intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and compulsive, repetitive behaviours (compulsions) that temporarily reduce anxiety but perpetuate the pathological loop. Yoga therapy's MEMT (mind-emptying meditation technique) and Vijnanamaya counselling aim to break the compulsive response cycle.

Addiction (substance dependence) involves pathological hijacking of the mesolimbic reward pathway (nucleus accumbens, dopamine). Repeated substance use dysregulates natural dopamine signalling: tolerance develops (requiring more of the substance), withdrawal produces profound negative affect, and cravings are encoded as powerful procedural memories. Nadi Suddhi has documented effects on craving reduction through ANS normalisation.

Maladaptive Neural Networks (Phobias and Eating Disorders)

Phobias involve the amygdala-based fear conditioning system generating excessive, irrational fear responses to specific stimuli or situations, maintained by the avoidance behaviour that prevents extinction learning. Treatment requires graduated exposure combined with relaxation — directly mirroring the VISAK (Visualisation and Imagination with Self-Awareness and Kinaesthetics) approach in yoga therapy.

Eating disorders (anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa) involve complex interactions between body image distortion (distorted interoceptive awareness), reward dysregulation (anorexia as self-rewarding control behaviour), and emotional dysregulation driving binge-purge cycles. The Manomaya Kosha is primary; Vijnanamaya counselling addresses cognitive distortions; Anandamaya work seeks to redirect the search for inner satisfaction away from body control and food restriction.

Yoga Therapy Rationale for This Category

The psychological category is the domain where yoga therapy most powerfully asserts its holistic advantage: conventional psychiatry primarily targets neurotransmitter systems through medication; yoga therapy addresses the entire Pancha Kosha system, with Manomaya Kosha as primary and Vijnanamaya Kosha as the distinctive contribution of the yogic approach.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Gentle, safe physical practice — especially in the early stages of psychiatric yoga therapy — establishes the habit of body awareness and sensory integration. Dynamic loosening exercises reduce psychomotor retardation (depression) and discharge tension (anxiety). Balance practices develop the proprioceptive system as a ground for present-moment awareness. However, strong stimulating practices should be avoided in acute psychiatric presentations.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

The breath-emotion link is direct and bidirectional: emotional states alter breathing patterns (anxiety → rapid shallow breathing; depression → slowed, shallow breathing); conversely, voluntary changes in breathing pattern alter emotional states. This is the primary physiological basis for pranayama in psychiatric practice.

  • Nadi Suddhi — reduces the sympathetic hyperarousal of anxiety; documented to reduce cortisol
  • Bhramari — vagal stimulation; rapid anxiety reduction through sound resonance; the sustained exhalation activates the parasympathetic branch
  • Ujjayi with prolonged exhalation — 1:2 breathing ratio (inspiratory:expiratory) activates the parasympathetic system through prolonged vagal stimulation on exhalation
  • Kapalabhati and Bhastrika — generally avoided in acute anxiety, schizophrenia, and epilepsy due to stimulating/destabilising effects

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body) — PRIMARY DOMAIN

The mental-emotional layer is the primary domain for this category. The entire therapeutic relationship between yoga teacher/therapist and psychiatric patient operates at this level:

  • Cyclic Meditation — active-passive cycling produces deeper relaxation than passive rest alone; normalises cortisol; improves affect
  • Yoga Nidra — deep systematic relaxation at the hypnagogic threshold; increases thalamic GABA; reduces anxiety and depression scores in controlled trials
  • DRT (Deep Relaxation Technique) — accessible to all patients; provides daily experience of a non-distressed somatic state, counteracting the chronic muscular and autonomic tension of anxiety and depression
  • OM Meditation — sustained cultivation of the observer-awareness state; shifts identification from thought-content to awareness itself

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

Yoga philosophy provides a cognitive framework for psychological healing that differs fundamentally from Western cognitive behavioural therapy — though many parallels exist. Key concepts:

  • Witness consciousness (Sakshi Bhava) — cultivating the ability to observe mental events without identification with them
  • Viveka (discrimination) — developing the intellectual capacity to distinguish the transient, conditioned mental activity from the stable, aware self
  • Santosha (contentment) and Vairagya (non-attachment) — philosophical attitudes that restructure the cognitive relationship to life circumstances

This Vijnanamaya work provides the intellectual corrective to the cognitive distortions (catastrophising, all-or-nothing thinking, learned helplessness) that sustain anxiety and depression.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Karma Yoga — engaging in purposeful service without attachment to outcome — is the Anandamaya-level prescription for depression and addictive behaviour, both of which involve a collapse of meaningful engagement with life. The discovery of an unconditional inner satisfaction through sustained meditation practice provides the deepest motivation for sustained recovery from addiction and the deepest antidote to the existential emptiness that underlies many eating disorders.
