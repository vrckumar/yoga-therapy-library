Neurological Disorders

The Nervous System

The nervous system is the body's principal communication, integration, and control network. It receives information from the internal and external environments, processes it, and coordinates appropriate responses — spanning everything from reflex movements and sensory perception to thought, emotion, memory, and consciousness itself.

Structural Organisation

Central Nervous System (CNS):

  • Brain — enclosed in the bony cranium; the seat of all conscious and unconscious processing
  • Spinal cord — within the vertebral canal; a bidirectional conduit for sensory (afferent) and motor (efferent) signals, and the site of spinal reflexes

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):

  • Somatic (voluntary) — sensory neurons (body to brain) and motor neurons (brain to skeletal muscle)
  • Autonomic (involuntary) — regulates visceral function; subdivided into sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions

Neurons and Neurotransmission

The functional unit of the nervous system is the neuron, consisting of:

  • Cell body (soma) — contains the nucleus and primary metabolic machinery
  • Dendrites — branching processes that receive signals from other neurons
  • Axon — a single long projection that transmits electrical impulses (action potentials) to the next cell

The junction between two neurons is the synapse. Electrical impulses cannot cross the synaptic cleft directly; instead, the presynaptic neuron releases neurotransmitters that diffuse across the cleft and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic neuron. Key neurotransmitters:

Neurotransmitter Location General Effect
Acetylcholine (ACh) Neuromuscular junction, parasympathetic, CNS Excitatory/inhibitory (receptor-dependent)
Noradrenaline Sympathetic system, brainstem Arousal, attention, vasoconstriction
Dopamine Basal ganglia, limbic system, frontal cortex Movement coordination, reward, motivation
Serotonin Raphe nuclei, limbic system Mood, appetite, sleep regulation
GABA Widely distributed in CNS Primary inhibitory neurotransmitter
Glutamate Widely distributed in CNS Primary excitatory neurotransmitter

Myelinated axons conduct impulses 50–100× faster than unmyelinated ones. Myelin is produced by Schwann cells (PNS) and oligodendrocytes (CNS). Demyelination — as in multiple sclerosis — slows or blocks neural conduction, producing the wide range of neurological symptoms characteristic of that disease.

Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)

The ANS governs all involuntary visceral functions — heart rate, blood pressure, respiration, digestion, glandular secretion, and smooth muscle tone — and is the principal mediating pathway between psychological stress and physical disease.

Sympathetic division ("fight-or-flight"):

  • Preganglionic neurons exit the spinal cord at thoracic and lumbar levels (T1–L2)
  • Synapse in paravertebral or prevertebral ganglia
  • Postganglionic neurons release noradrenaline onto target organs
  • Effects: increased heart rate and BP, bronchodilation, pupil dilation, reduced digestion, raised blood glucose

Parasympathetic division ("rest-and-digest"):

  • Preganglionic neurons in brainstem (cranial nerves III, VII, IX, X) and sacral cord (S2–S4)
  • The vagus nerve (CN X) provides parasympathetic supply to heart, lungs, and all abdominal organs
  • Postganglionic neurons release acetylcholine
  • Effects: reduced heart rate, bronchoconstriction, enhanced digestion, pupil constriction, glandular secretion

The balance between sympathetic and parasympathetic tone — the autonomic set-point — is the physiological parameter that yoga therapy most consistently and measurably shifts.

The Limbic System

The limbic system — comprising the amygdala, hippocampus, cingulate gyrus, hypothalamus, and related structures — is the brain's emotional processing and memory system. It modulates autonomic output, endocrine function (via the hypothalamus-pituitary axis), and behaviour.

  • Amygdala — fear detection and emotional memory; triggers the fight-or-flight response
  • Hippocampus — spatial memory, contextual learning, stress response regulation (inhibits HPA axis when cortisol is excessive)
  • Hypothalamus — master integrator linking limbic system to autonomic and endocrine systems; controls the HPA axis

Cerebral Blood Flow

The brain receives approximately 750 mL/minute of blood (15% of cardiac output) despite weighing only 1.4 kg. Cerebral autoregulation maintains constant flow over a wide range of systemic blood pressures. The brain is extremely sensitive to hypoxia (ceases functioning within seconds) and hypoglycaemia (irreversible damage within minutes). Meditation increases cerebral blood flow to prefrontal cortical regions associated with executive function, attention regulation, and emotional modulation.

Disease Mechanisms in This Category

Neurological disorders arise from a diverse range of mechanisms, but several common pathophysiological threads run across conditions that yoga therapy is positioned to address.

Neuronal Loss

Progressive, irreversible death of specific neuronal populations underlies neurodegenerative diseases. In Parkinson's disease, dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra are lost, depleting the basal ganglia of dopamine and producing the characteristic triad of tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia. In Alzheimer's disease, cortical and hippocampal neurons are lost with amyloid accumulation. Yoga therapy cannot reverse neuronal loss but can slow progression by reducing neuroinflammation, improving cerebral perfusion, and promoting neuroplasticity in surviving circuits.

Demyelination

Loss of the myelin sheath disrupts or blocks axonal conduction. In multiple sclerosis, immune-mediated attacks on CNS myelin produce relapsing-remitting episodes of motor weakness, sensory disturbance, visual loss, and fatigue. Heat sensitivity (Uhthoff's phenomenon) is characteristic — high body temperature further slows conduction in already-compromised axons — making cooling pranayama (Sitali) a practical therapeutic consideration.

Vascular Insufficiency

Stroke (cerebrovascular accident) results from either ischaemic infarction (80% — arterial occlusion cutting off blood supply) or haemorrhage (20% — arterial rupture). The resulting neurological deficit depends on which brain region is deprived of circulation. Yoga therapy in stroke rehabilitation targets neuroplasticity — the brain's ability to reorganise surviving neurons to compensate for lost function — through bilateral movement practice, breath training, and meditation.

Excitation-Inhibition Imbalance

Normal brain function requires a precise balance between excitatory (glutamatergic) and inhibitory (GABAergic) neural activity. Excessive excitation relative to inhibition underlies epileptic seizures. Migraine involves cortical spreading depression — a wave of neuronal depolarisation followed by suppression — triggered by excitatory-inhibitory imbalance and autonomic dysregulation. Practices that enhance GABA tone (meditation, slow breathing) are beneficial; practices that increase neural excitability (Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, breath retention) are contraindicated in epilepsy.

Yoga Therapy Rationale for This Category

Neurological yoga therapy is grounded in the understanding that the nervous system — particularly the ANS — is the primary mediating pathway between yoga practices and therapeutic benefit across all disease categories.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Balance asanas — Vrikshasana (tree pose), Garudasana, Ardha Chandrasana — require constant proprioceptive feedback, cerebellar integration, and motor correction. They provide graded challenges to the balance and coordination systems that are compromised in Parkinson's, stroke rehabilitation, and multiple sclerosis. Regular practice promotes proprioceptive retraining and stimulates cerebellar neuroplasticity.

Slow, controlled movements — analogous to Tai Chi — have demonstrated neuroprotective effects in Parkinson's disease by reinforcing dopamine-deficient motor circuits through intensive, rhythmic motor practice.

Bilateral movement practices activate both cerebral hemispheres simultaneously, potentially supporting cross-hemisphere compensation in stroke rehabilitation (mirror neuron activation through bilateral asana practice).

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

Pranayama modulates autonomic function directly and powerfully:

  • Nadi Suddhi — alternating nostril breathing produces measurable hemispheric shifts in EEG activity (right nostril → left hemisphere activation; left nostril → right hemisphere) and shifts autonomic balance
  • Bhramari — vagal stimulation through sound resonance; reduces limbic excitability
  • Slow diaphragmatic breathing — increases heart rate variability (HRV), a reliable index of cardiac vagal tone and a predictor of neurological resilience
  • Cooling pranayama (Sitali, Sitkari) — reduces core body temperature; important in heat-sensitive demyelinating conditions

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

Meditation produces structural and functional changes in the brain that are measurable by neuroimaging:

  • Increased grey matter in the prefrontal cortex (attention, impulse regulation) with sustained practice
  • Reduced amygdala reactivity (anxiety, fear response)
  • Strengthened prefrontal-limbic connections (top-down emotional regulation)
  • Enhanced default mode network deactivation (reduced mind-wandering, rumination)

These changes are directly relevant to migraine (reducing cortical excitability), autonomic dysfunction (improving HPA regulation), and neurological rehabilitation (enhancing neuroplasticity).

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

Cognitive reframing through yoga philosophy — particularly the concept of Drashtu (the witnessing awareness) — provides a neurological patient with a framework for observing neurological symptoms without identification with them. This reduces the catastrophising and fear responses that amplify suffering in chronic neurological conditions.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Devotional practices — OM chanting, Nadanusandhana, group bhajan singing — activate bilateral auditory-motor networks and limbic circuits in ways that physical rehabilitation cannot. For stroke patients with speech impairment (aphasia), chanting engages preserved right-hemisphere language processing. For Parkinson's patients, rhythmic vocalisation supports dopamine-depleted timing circuits.
