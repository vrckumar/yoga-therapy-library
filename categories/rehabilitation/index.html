Rehabilitation, Oncology & Geriatrics

Geriatric Physiology and Oncology — Foundational Concepts

This category spans three overlapping clinical domains: obstetric and gynaecological conditions (Block 7), and rehabilitation medicine, geriatric medicine, and oncology (Block 8). The unifying principle is that yoga therapy must adapt its tools and intensity to match the reduced physiological reserve, the altered body schema, or the existential challenges of these patient groups.

Geriatric Physiology

Ageing is associated with progressive, multi-system physiological change that reduces functional reserve and resilience:

Sarcopenia — the age-related loss of skeletal muscle mass, strength, and function. After age 30, muscle mass declines at 0.5–1% per year; after 60, this accelerates to 1–2% annually. Mechanisms include: reduced anabolic hormone production (testosterone, GH, IGF-1), chronic low-grade inflammation ("inflammageing"), reduced satellite cell activity, and progressive motor neuron loss. Sarcopenia is the primary driver of falls, functional dependence, and frailty in older adults. It is reversible — resistance exercise (even gentle yoga-based chair exercises with muscle loading) promotes satellite cell activation and protein synthesis.

Osteoporosis — age and hormonal changes (particularly oestrogen withdrawal at menopause) shift the bone remodelling balance toward net resorption. Bone mineral density (BMD) falls below the fracture threshold (T-score ≤ -2.5 by DXA scan). Trabecular bone is lost first (vertebrae, wrist), then cortical bone (hip). Weight-bearing activity stimulates osteoblastic bone formation — walking, standing asanas, and gentle Surya Namaskara are directly therapeutic; inversions require caution in advanced osteoporosis due to vertebral fracture risk.

Reduced neuroplasticity — the ageing brain shows reduced BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) production, slower synaptic transmission, reduced hippocampal neurogenesis, and impaired cognitive processing speed. Memory, executive function, and balance (due to vestibular and proprioceptive decline) are particularly affected. Meditation and aerobic activity (including dynamic yoga) increase BDNF, promote neurogenesis, and maintain cognitive function.

Cardiovascular deconditioning — reduced maximal heart rate (220 − age formula), decreased cardiac compliance, increased arterial stiffness, and reduced baroreceptor sensitivity all contribute to cardiovascular deconditioning. Exercise tolerance falls, making the pacing and graduated intensity of yoga therapy particularly appropriate.

Polypharmacy — older patients frequently take five or more medications concurrently. Drug interactions, side effects (orthostatic hypotension, sedation, falls risk), and altered pharmacokinetics (reduced hepatic and renal clearance) complicate management. Yoga therapy's non-pharmacological approach is particularly valuable for reducing medication burden.

Oncology: Physiology of Cancer and Treatment

Cancer is a group of diseases characterised by uncontrolled cell proliferation, invasion of adjacent tissues, and potential metastasis to distant organs. Carcinogenesis involves accumulated mutations in genes controlling cell growth (oncogenes), tumour suppression (tumour suppressor genes), and programmed cell death (apoptosis). The hallmarks of cancer include: self-sufficiency in growth signals, evasion of growth inhibition, resistance to apoptosis, limitless replicative potential, angiogenesis, tissue invasion, and immune evasion.

Immune system disruption: The immune system normally performs immunosurveillance — identifying and eliminating mutated cells before they proliferate. Cancer cells evade this through: reduced MHC expression (becoming "invisible" to T cells), production of immunosuppressive cytokines (TGF-β, IL-10), recruitment of regulatory T cells, and PD-L1 expression (checkpoint evasion). Chronic stress suppresses NK cell activity and T cell function, potentially impairing immunosurveillance. Yoga therapy's stress-reduction effects may support immune surveillance.

Treatment side effects:

  • Chemotherapy — fatigue (the most common and debilitating symptom), nausea/vomiting, peripheral neuropathy, bone marrow suppression (anaemia, infection risk, bleeding), hair loss, cognitive impairment ("chemo brain")
  • Radiotherapy — local tissue damage, fatigue, mucositis, radiation fibrosis
  • Surgery — post-operative pain, deconditioning, body schema changes, lymphoedema
  • Hormonal therapy — menopausal symptoms, bone density loss, mood changes

Rehabilitation Principles

Rehabilitation medicine is concerned with restoring function and quality of life after illness, injury, or surgery. The core principles:

  • Graded exercise — progressive increases in intensity and complexity, matched to improving capacity, preventing both under-loading (continued deconditioning) and overloading (injury, fatigue)
  • Neuroplasticity — the brain's ability to reorganise and compensate after injury through use-dependent synaptic strengthening; maximised by intensive, task-specific, repetitive practice
  • Functional retraining — prioritising the specific movements and activities of daily living required for independence
  • Psychosocial support — addressing depression, anxiety, and the existential challenges of disability
  • Interdisciplinary team approach — physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, psychology, yoga therapy, social work

Disease Mechanisms in This Category

Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension (PIH)

PIH includes gestational hypertension, pre-eclampsia (hypertension + proteinuria), and eclampsia (seizures). Pathophysiology involves: defective placentation → impaired trophoblast invasion → uteroplacental insufficiency → placental ischaemia → release of antiangiogenic factors (sFlt-1, sEng) → systemic endothelial dysfunction → hypertension, proteinuria, and multi-organ involvement.

Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR)

IUGR — fetal weight below the 10th centile for gestational age — results from inadequate placental perfusion and nutrient transfer. Causes: maternal (hypertension, diabetes, malnutrition, smoking), placental (structural abnormality, partial abruption), or fetal (chromosomal abnormality). Yoga therapy supports maternal physiology through stress reduction (cortisol reduction improves uteroplacental flow), lateral positioning (left lateral decubitus optimises venous return), and gentle breath practices.

Menstrual Disorders

Dysmenorrhoea (painful periods), menorrhagia (heavy periods), and PCOS (polycystic ovarian syndrome with oligo-ovulation, hyperandrogenism, and insulin resistance) are addressed through yoga therapy's combined hormonal, autonomic, and psychological mechanisms.

Yoga Therapy Rationale for This Category

This category uniquely demands equal engagement of all five Pancha Koshas, with Vijnanamaya Kosha (acceptance, adaptation, meaning-making) holding particular importance.

Annamaya Kosha (Physical Body)

Chair yoga — all standard asanas adapted to be performed seated in a chair — makes yoga accessible to those with severe frailty, post-stroke hemiplegia, advanced COPD, or post-surgical deconditioning. The therapeutic benefits of joint mobilisation, muscular engagement, and proprioceptive challenge are preserved while eliminating fall risk and excessive strain.

Progressive protocol: A structured pathway from chair yoga → loosening exercises → gentle floor asanas → standing asanas → pranayama provides a graded functional recovery trajectory applicable across neurological, cardiac, and musculoskeletal rehabilitation.

Sakti Vikasaka — dynamic joint exercises for all major joints — is the most important physical component in geriatric yoga therapy, directly targeting sarcopenia, joint stiffness, and balance deficits.

Balance practices with support (Vrikshasana with wall support, Garudasana holding a chair) provide progressive proprioceptive challenge within a safe environment.

Pranamaya Kosha (Energy/Breath Body)

PET (Pranayama Energy Technique) — a gentle, accessible breathing practice suited to debilitated or fatigued patients (cancer, post-surgery, elderly). The breath is used to manage cancer-related fatigue by improving oxygenation, reducing anxiety-driven hyperventilation, and promoting energy conservation.

Nadi Suddhi — fundamental pranayama for sleep quality in geriatric insomnia, pre-operative anxiety, post-chemotherapy nausea, and IUGR maternal support.

Lateral Savasana — for obstetric patients, lying on the left side (rather than supine) prevents aortocaval compression and optimises foetal blood flow; pranayama in this position is both safe and therapeutic.

Manomaya Kosha (Mental/Emotional Body)

MSRT (Mind Sound Resonance Technique) — deep relaxation using sound resonance (OM) in a systematic body-awareness sequence; particularly suited to cancer patients for whom touch-based relaxation may be uncomfortable. Provides profound rest and pain relief.

DRT — accessible to even the most deconditioned patients; provides daily experience of deep rest and pain modulation. Documented to reduce the opioid requirement in post-operative pain management.

MIRT (Mind-body Integration and Relaxation Training) — for rehabilitation patients, this practice systematically rebuilds the mental body-map after stroke, amputation, or severe illness, reintegrating the sense of physical self.

Vijnanamaya Kosha (Wisdom/Intellect Body)

This is the uniquely central kosha in rehabilitation and oncology. The existential questions raised by serious illness — "Why me?", "What is my life worth now?", "How do I live with uncertainty?" — are addressed by yoga philosophy:

  • Acceptance (Pratigya) — not passive resignation but the active choice to engage fully with present reality
  • Adaptation — finding new expressions of capability and meaning within changed limitations
  • Impermanence (Anitya) — the yogic framework for facing mortality with equanimity rather than terror

Counselling grounded in yoga philosophy provides what no medication can: a framework for suffering that renders it meaningful and workable.

Anandamaya Kosha (Bliss/Spiritual Body)

Karma Yoga framing — reframing the experience of illness, limitation, and dependence as an opportunity for the deepest human learning (surrender, compassion, resilience) — provides the Anandamaya layer of support that sustains quality of life even when physical function is severely limited.

Group practices — devotional singing, shared meditation, community belonging in a yoga therapy group — provide the social dimension of healing that is particularly vital for isolated elderly patients and cancer patients who face the psychological isolation that illness often brings.
